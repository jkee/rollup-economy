Master-Lease Co-Living

From 4-digit aggregate (5311 Lessors of Real Estate) into the master-lease sub-segment within 6-digit 531110 · Outpost Group / OJH Holdings operator case
4-digit (5311): Strong → Master-lease co-living sub-segment: Pass

The 4-digit aggregate (5311 Lessors of Real Estate) scores Strong on the main map — driven by traditional asset-owning landlords with predictable long-lease revenue, cap-rate-anchored valuations, and decades-long compounding returns through the residential REIT category. The master-lease co-living sub-segment within 6-digit 531110 (Lessors of Residential Buildings and Dwellings) looks superficially similar but is structurally different: operators sign the lease but do not own the asset. They take 100% of the downside on rent obligations with 0% of the real-estate upside. The sub-segment has had near-100% mortality among VC-backed operators since 2018 — Common, Sonder, Selina, Quarters, Bedly, HubHaus, WeLive, The Collective all dead or in Chapter 7 wind-down. Outpost / OJH Holdings is consolidating distressed remnants of this category at a fraction of cumulative invested capital, but no operator in the category has yet returned capital at >1× MoIC.

Why the colors differ

The "extra" in the 4-digit aggregate that the sub-segment loses: asset ownership. Traditional residential lessors in 5311 (Camden, AvalonBay, Equity Residential and the long tail of private landlords) own the underlying real estate. They earn rent and capture appreciation; their cash flows are backed by hard collateral; their multiples are anchored to cap rates that have a floor. Master-lease co-living operators sign a long-term lease with a third-party owner, then sublease by-the-room. They earn the spread between aggregate room rent and master rent — but they carry every dollar of lease obligation contractually regardless of occupancy, and they never own the asset. When occupancy drops below ~85% the model flips negative; when it drops below ~75% the operator is technically insolvent on a cash basis within months. None of this is present in the parent-aggregate economics.

NAICS structure

Per US Census 2022 NAICS, the 4-digit industry group 5311 Lessors of Real Estate contains four 6-digit codes:

  • 531110 Lessors of Residential Buildings and Dwellings — apartment landlords, single-family rental owners, master-lease operators
  • 531120 Lessors of Nonresidential Buildings (except Miniwarehouses) — office, retail, industrial landlords
  • 531130 Lessors of Miniwarehouses and Self-Storage Units
  • 531190 Lessors of Other Real Estate Property — land, mobile-home pads, etc.

Outpost / OJH sits inside 531110 but operates a sub-specialization — master-lease residential aggregation with by-room subletting (commonly marketed as "co-living"). NAICS does not separately code this; the same code houses traditional apartment landlords (~95% of 531110 firm count) and the much smaller master-lease operator pool (~<1% of firms). The verdict difference is between those two operating models within the same NAICS code.

The category graveyard — verified primary sources

Of the 12 VC-backed master-lease co-living operators tracked 2015–2025, 10 are dead or in wind-down, 1 pivoted out of the model (Bungalow), and 1 (PadSplit) survives operating a materially different model (marketplace where landlords retain the lease — not master-lease). The sub-segment has not produced a single confirmed exit at >1× MoIC since 2018.

Why master-lease residential fails structurally

Five mechanics, each verified by primary-source post-mortems:

1. Asymmetric leverage on the lease

The operator signs a long-term master lease (typically 5-15 years) at a fixed monthly rent. The landlord keeps 100% of the real-estate upside (appreciation, refinancing, sale). The operator carries 100% of the rent obligation regardless of occupancy. Every dollar of asset value created by rising rents flows to the landlord; every dollar of occupancy shortfall hits the operator.

2. Fixed-cost rigidity

Community staffing, cleaning, events programming, on-site management — none of this scales down with occupancy. Below ~85% occupancy the unit economics flip negative; below ~75% the operator is technically insolvent on a cash basis within months. Pandemic-era operators (Quarters, Collective, HubHaus) all failed at this threshold.

3. Customer-acquisition mismatch

Average tenancy 3-6 months (by design — flexible terms are the product). Paid acquisition channels (Google, Facebook, broker fees) cost $300-800 per tenant. LTV/CAC structurally weak — by the time CAC is amortized the tenant churns. Acquisition costs disguised in marketing line; true contribution margin per tenant much lower than reported.

4. Regulatory exposure

By-room subletting violates SRO and zoning law in most primary markets including SF and NYC. HubHaus was forced to evict 7 tenants from a single property over SRO violations. Bungalow faced "housing scam" complaints. Compliance is rarely fully achieved at scale; operators run with implicit regulatory risk.

5. Capex disguised as opex

Furniture, conversion costs, community-build all expensed rather than capitalized. Suppresses near-term reported EBITDA, attracts growth-capital narrative, then collapses when growth pauses and the back book has to support itself. Common, Sonder, Quarters all showed this pattern in retrospective audits.

6. Pandemic stress-test verdict + post-pandemic confirmation

Every operator that hit 2020 with debt failed (HubHaus 2020, Quarters 2021, Collective 2021). The recovery cohort that survived through 2022-2023 then ALSO failed (Common 2024, Sonder 2025, Selina 2024) — proving the model is not viable even in "normal" demand environments.

What would have to be true for this to work

🟡 Conditions that could make a selective play investable

  • Pure fee-based property management growth (the 13% PM revenue line), not the 75% master-lease portfolio
  • Asset-light pivot like Bungalow attempted — operator becomes marketplace/management layer, landlords retain lease
  • Workforce / corporate housing contracts with creditworthy tenant counterparties (large employer master agreements) replacing transient room-by-room demand
  • Geographic concentration in markets with structural housing shortage AND no SRO/zoning enforcement risk
  • Conservative valuation at <1× revenue acknowledging category history
  • Capital structure that survives a 15-pp occupancy drop without insolvency

❌ Conditions that confirm Pass verdict

  • Lease tail on existing master-lease portfolio creates years of fixed obligation regardless of operational performance
  • FY2026 EBITDA-positive forecast is budget; T12 actuals trend negative; pre-merger June Homes also negative at 4× larger scale
  • No exit comparable above 1× MoIC in 7 years of category history
  • Strategic acquirer pool effectively zero — residential REITs do not buy operating businesses, hotel chains have exited the category (Marriott terminating Sonder)
  • Roll-up math does not work: tuck-in candidates are all dead or distressed; surviving operators in the category number in the single digits and are not for sale at reasonable multiples

Final thesis

The 4-digit aggregate (5311 Lessors of Real Estate) is Strong because of asset ownership — traditional landlords own the real estate, earn rent, capture appreciation, and trade on cap rates with a hard-asset floor. Master-lease co-living operators within 531110 lose that structural floor: they sign the lease but never own the asset. The 10-year experiment with this model has resulted in near-100% mortality among VC-backed operators — Sonder, Selina, Common, The Collective, Quarters, Starcity, HubHaus, WeLive, Bedly, Roam all dead or in Ch.7 wind-down. Cumulative capital destroyed exceeds $1.5B. Only PadSplit has survived, and only because it operates a different model (marketplace where landlords retain the lease). OJH Holdings — the post-merger Outpost + June Homes entity — is the most rational consolidator left in the category, taking failing competitors' books at zero acquisition cost via direct landlord re-contracting. But the combined entity is still EBITDA-negative ($925K T12), the FY2026 break-even forecast is internal budget not actuals, and pre-merger June Homes was EBITDA-negative at 4× larger scale. The sub-segment scores Pass not because Outpost's execution is poor, but because the operating model itself has been disproven over 10 years and the strategic exit market has been hollowed out by the category's own bankruptcies.
