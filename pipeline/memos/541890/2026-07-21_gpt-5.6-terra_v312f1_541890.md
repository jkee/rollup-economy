# 541890 — Other Services Related to Advertising

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312f1_541890` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

This is a heterogeneous catch-all, but promotional-products distribution is the best-supported working dominant subsegment: Census product detail explicitly includes coordinating logoed advertising-specialty production and delivery [S2], while ASI reports a $26.6bn promo industry [S3]. A $1–10m EBITDA target can harvest meaningful quoting, artwork and order-administration savings, but it is not a clean software-like roll-up: supplier-side revenue is in the trade total, client books can be portable, and physical/event-service work is not replaced. I therefore view the code as a conditional relationship-protected reseller opportunity, not a broad AI windfall.

## How AI changes the work

AI primarily removes information and coordination work: product search, first-pass artwork, mockups, proof routing, quote preparation, store administration, supplier chasing and billing. ASI reports that 50% of extra-large ($5m+) distributors use AI and that creative copy is the most common use among AI users [S4], which supports rapid tool diffusion but not full task replacement at smaller firms. In-person demonstrations, sampling, installation and the physical production and delivery of merchandise remain human or supplier work; salespeople and account leaders still own the brief and relationship.

## Value capture

The economic model is markup resale and managed-program service, not a billable-hour consultancy. 4imprint's 2025 results show $1.347bn revenue, $436m gross profit and $145m operating profit [S5], demonstrating that scaled direct distribution can retain healthy economics. That is a benchmark, not a comp for a small independent: online self-service makes search, artwork and ordering more contestable and is likely to pass a material part of labor savings to buyers. I set capture below one-half on both distribution and moat, because company stores and supplier access help retention but portable sales books and transparent quotes limit it.

## Adoption timing

The reported 50% AI-use rate applies to extra-large distributors, not the full code or the lower-middle market [S4]. I therefore estimate current effective adoption at 35%, with roughly 2.5 years to 50% effective adoption as tools move from copy and mockups into quote-to-cash workflows. This is deliberately slower than casual AI use: integration with supplier data, approval workflows and client systems matters more than access to a general model.

## Consolidation and economics

There is real, current consolidation: HALO acquired What's Happening Promotions in October 2025 [S6], BDA acquired Harper + Scott in September 2025 [S7], and Overture acquired Pride Products in January 2026 [S8]. These facts support active buyers, not a claim that every small distributor is buyable or that deal terms are known. The selected 4.0x buy and 5.0x exit multiples sit inside Windsor Drake's Q1 2026 marketing/digital-agency $3m–$10m EBITDA range of 4.0x–6.5x [S9], but that is an adjacent broad category; a target with concentrated or salesperson-owned accounts warrants the low end.

## Terminal demand

Demand for physical branded merchandise is more durable than the distributor's intermediation. Census describes a 541890 product as coordinating production of logoed specialties and delivering them to the client [S2], and AI cannot itself manufacture or distribute the object. The pressure is that AI-native search and self-service can reduce the value of the independent coordination layer. I classify terminal demand as PRESSURED rather than MELTING: enterprise program management, fulfillment and trusted account service can persist, but a bare-bones reseller has a credible bypass risk.

## Risks and uncertainty

The code includes merchandise coordination, demonstrations and other advertising services, so no single trade statistic maps cleanly to it. ASI's $26.6bn figure covers a broader promotional-products ecosystem [S3], and its 50% AI statistic is for extra-large distributors [S4]. The deterministic OEWS proxy is 4-digit 5418 and imports agency roles, so the role estimate is sensitive. There is no verified, code-specific owner-age survey or disclosed small-target transaction multiple in the reopened sources; those inputs are deliberately low-confidence estimates rather than invented facts.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: 4-digit 5418 is an agency-heavy proxy rather than a clean 541890 staffing census.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The role mix is reinterpreted toward the promo-products dominant-subsegment hypothesis; demonstration/event firms would have lower automation.
- inputs.pi_dist: 4imprint is a large direct operator, not a lower-middle-market independent.
- inputs.pi_moat: No source in this packet measures account portability for 541890 targets.
- inputs.t50_years: The observed 50% figure is only for extra-large distributors and measures use, not effective workflow adoption.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: Not a directly observed full-industry or NAICS adoption rate.
- inputs.historical_analogs: This is an analogue, not a quantified historical study.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: Unobserved estimate; do not treat as a demographic fact.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: Absence of this packet's evidence is not proof of no shortage.
- inputs.active_consolidators: Announcements do not show acquisition cadence, target size, or willingness to buy every small distributor.
- inputs.buy_mult: No disclosed 541890 lower-middle-market transaction multiple was reopened; the cited category is an adjacent proxy.
- inputs.exit_mult: Expansion requires demonstrated retention; recent acquisition announcements disclose no transaction multiples.
- inputs.pricing_structure: The full catch-all code includes service models that differ from promo distribution.
