# 541410 — Interior Design Services

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312p1_541410` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

Interior Design Services is a selective, pressured AI opportunity rather than a clean automation roll-up. The code includes planning/design as well as construction-contract administration, specifications, and furnishings-related resale [S1]. BLS likewise describes a workflow spanning client discovery, layouts, material specification, ordering, installation oversight, contractor coordination, and site visits [S2]. AI can reduce substantial visualization, documentation, proposal, marketing, and administration effort, but the supplied low labor share (0.2359) means that labor savings apply to only part of receipts: physical goods and procurement flow through the business. The practical investment question is whether a target has a transferable commercial client base, procurement system, and senior team, rather than a founder’s residential taste and contacts. I assess reasonable but not exceptional value creation, moderate retention, an already-active adoption curve, and PRESSURED—not melting—terminal demand.

## How AI changes the work

AI exposure is concentrated in creating and revising plans, mood boards, renderings, presentations, marketing, schedules, basic specifications, and office workflows. BLS confirms that designers use CAD for most drawings and BIM for three-dimensional visualization [S2]. Houzz’s 2025 survey of US design-only firms reports that AI is used primarily for document formatting, scheduling, and content generation, while complex client management is less adopted [S3]. That maps to partial, rather than complete, replacement of interior designers: a designer still translates a client brief, checks codes and dimensions, specifies real products, resolves substitutions, coordinates contractors, and visits the site. Graphic-design and administrative roles carry higher task fractions; physical movers and receiving remain low. The four-digit 5414 mix is contaminated by graphic-design activity, so the higher fraction assigned to those supplied roles is a task judgment, not a claim that graphics is proportionally central to a pure interior-design firm.

## Value capture

Whether the firm keeps AI savings depends on its revenue model. Trade reporting describes firms using flat fees, hourly work, cost-plus procurement, and product markups, with no standard fee structure [S4]. For a well-scoped flat-fee design phase, faster production can be retained. For hourly work, fewer hours mechanically reduce revenue; for product-markup and procurement fees, AI drawing speed has limited direct effect while direct-to-consumer shopping can weaken the furniture-margin base. BLS’s task list shows enduring relationship, procurement, and coordination work [S2], which supports a real service moat, but not necessarily a firm-level moat when the principal owns the client relationship. I therefore give a midrange distribution share and moat: the score does not treat the physical work floor as captured value, and it does not use adoption timing to inflate capture.

## Adoption timing

I estimate 32% current effective AI adoption and 2.5 years to 50% effective adoption in the relevant target population. Houzz found nearly one-third of design businesses already using AI, rising to 50% among firms with at least 10 employees [S3]. The frozen target pool begins at the 20–99 employee class, so its firms are plausibly ahead of the all-design average, but the survey measures any everyday business use, not automation of fee-earning design work. Houzz also reports that designers use AI first in routine administration and content, with complex client management lagging [S3]. The selected timing therefore recognizes meaningful current use without equating a chatbot or document tool with full design-process adoption. It is independent of owner age, target count, or M&A evidence.

## Consolidation and economics

This code is fragmented, but the frozen dataset identifies 313 firms in the target EBITDA band, a much more usable starting pool than the many sole practitioners below it. ASID’s 2025 succession research covered 63 architecture/interiors firms under 40 employees and found formal succession plans at only 30% of 25–40 employee firms, 12% of 2–10 employee firms, and under 5% of sole practitioners [S6]. That documents a real transition problem, though it does not give an owner-age rate or a buyer-ready target count. There is at least strategic acquisition activity: Chambers acquired C2 Limited, an interiors/planning firm, while retaining its brand and leadership [S7]. This is not evidence of a broad PE roll-up. A 2025 design-and-build transaction was appraised at 3.2x trailing EBITDA [S8], but it had commercial repeat revenue, approved-vendor status, and remodeling exposure, so it is an optimistic hybrid comp. I use that as a restrained purchase anchor and underwrite a modestly lower exit multiple because acquisition success depends on retaining creative leadership and client relationships.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is PRESSURED, not MELTING. The formal product taxonomy distinguishes full-service design, which includes construction administration and construction documents, from design-only and consulting work [S1]. It also identifies furnishings resale margins as a related product [S1], explaining why labor cost is a low share of receipts and why AI-generated images alone do not erase the business. BLS’s workflow includes product orders, installation oversight, contractor coordination, code awareness, and site follow-up [S2]; those are paid physical-world and accountability functions. Yet client-accessible visual ideation, online product discovery, and low-friction AI content can commoditize the early design phase and erode product-markup transparency. Demand should survive for well-positioned full-service commercial, hospitality, healthcare, and high-touch residential firms, but their mix is unknown and design-fee-only firms face more disintermediation.

## Risks and uncertainty

Evidence is useful but mismatched to the exact acquisition target. Houzz’s sample is US design-only professionals registered on its platform and includes residential and commercial design; it is not a census of 541410 firms or a measure of task-level replacement [S3]. The supplied role mix is the broader 5414 group, so graphic-design wages likely overstate their share in the code. Business of Home articles illustrate practitioner pricing but are not a representative revenue-mix survey [S4]. The specific 3.2x EBITDA transaction is one design-and-build business with favorable commercial characteristics [S8], whereas a broker’s 4–6x general range is marketing guidance rather than disclosed comparables [S9]. Finally, 541410 bundles residential, commercial, contract-administration, decorating, procurement, and resale activity; value capture and terminal demand differ sharply across those lines. Low confidence is therefore intentional, and a real diligence case should reconstruct service fees, product margins, founder-originated revenue, client concentration, trade-account economics, and employee retention before assigning a multiple.
