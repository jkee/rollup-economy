# 811192 — Car Washes

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-sol_v312g1_811192` · gpt-5.6-sol · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

Car washes have durable paid demand and an active consolidation market, but they are a weak pure-AI value-migration thesis. The Census scope is establishments primarily cleaning, washing or waxing vehicles, including detail, mobile and self-service washes [S1]. At the attractive express end, the real assets are the site, tunnel and membership book: Mister reported that subscriptions produced 76% of 2025 wash sales [S2]. AI can improve membership recognition, marketing and overhead, but it does not perform the wash and should not receive credit for decades-old tunnel mechanization. A buyer should underwrite this as a location, subscription and real-estate investment with a modest AI overlay.

## How AI changes the work

The clearest AI-enabled tasks are license-plate recognition, frictionless member access, payment, customer-service triage, marketing segmentation, scheduling and multi-site reporting. Current AI-LPR products automate member signup and transaction processing and analyze usage patterns [S5]. Those tools touch attendants, cashiers, customer-service staff, office clerks and managers, but most of the wage bill remains physical cleaning, loading, maintenance and site supervision. The supplied occupational mix is a 5-digit proxy that includes oil-change businesses, so automotive technicians and tire workers are largely sibling-industry contamination and are assigned little AI replaceability rather than treated as car-wash labor.

## Value capture

Distribution is favorable because customers pay the operator directly, without a payer or administered fee schedule. Retention is less certain: location, visible wash quality and convenience matter, and Mister warns that competition can force price cuts or service changes [S2]. Membership improves recurrence, yet industry data show 7.2% average monthly churn and stronger retention only at larger member bases [S3]. The result is high operator control over realized savings but only a middling moat: nearby tunnel supply, promotions and churn can pass part of any efficiency gain back to consumers.

## Adoption timing

Digital and automated workflows are already visible in scaled express operators, but effective AI adoption is well below 50% across the full NAICS population. AI-LPR is commercially available and is being included in new systems [S5], while the largest public chain already operates a technology-intensive subscription model [S2]. The long independent tail, heterogeneous formats and limited payoff on physical roles slow diffusion. A four-year estimate to 50% effective adoption reflects relatively easy software deployment but not wholesale labor substitution.

## Consolidation and economics

The market is active but more selective than at the 2022 peak. Three directly evidenced acquirers are Mister, Whistle Express and Quick Quack [S7][S8][S9], while the specialist M&A report describes broader buyer participation and a turn toward densification and bolt-ons [S4]. For 5–20-site, real-estate-owned, high-volume portfolios, Car Wash Advisory reported 2025 values around 8–10× EBITDA [S4]. The selected 8.5× entry and 9.0× exit sit inside that same observed range; the exit premium assumes a better diversified, professionally managed portfolio, not a return to peak 10–12× pricing. EBITDA must be normalized for market rent, maintenance capital and membership churn.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is durable: mature AI does not clean vehicles, and recurring road use continues to soil them. The larger risk is economic, not technological elimination. New local supply can impair volume and pricing, customers can cancel subscriptions at any time [S2], and weather causes seasonal variation [S3]. Self-service, mobile detailing and express tunnels all remain within the exact NAICS scope [S1], but their labor intensity and capital requirements differ sharply, so durable category demand does not make every format or site durable.

## Risks and uncertainty

The largest uncertainties are format mix, the 5-digit occupational proxy, real-estate treatment and the transferability of scaled-chain evidence to lower-middle-market targets. A leased tunnel at market rent is not economically comparable with a real-estate-owned portfolio, and the cited 8–10× evidence is specifically for 5–20-site, high-volume, real-estate-owned deals [S4]. Owner-age evidence is a cross-industry employer-business proxy, not car-wash-specific [S6]. AI adoption evidence is product and operator evidence rather than a representative penetration survey. These limitations warrant medium overall confidence and wide ranges around adoption, owner age and multiples.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The role mix is 5-digit NAICS 81119 and includes oil-change/lube operations; automotive technicians and tire workers are sibling-code contamination.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Tunnel mechanization is mature capital equipment and is deliberately not credited as AI replacement.
- inputs.pi_dist: Direct pricing control does not prevent competitive discounting; that separate retention risk is reflected in pi_moat.
- inputs.pi_moat: Mister is a scaled express proxy and may overstate membership resilience for independent or legacy-format targets.
- inputs.t50_years: No representative NAICS-811192 AI-adoption survey was found; the estimate bridges product availability and scaled-operator practice to the full industry.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: Technology availability and chain deployment are proxies for effective labor-task adoption, not measured penetration.
- inputs.historical_analogs: The analog mixes traditional automation with current machine-learning features and is not evidence that AI automates the tunnel itself.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: Cross-industry U.S. employer-business proxy; it is not specific to car washes and reports age 55+, not 60+.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: Absence of verified industry-specific evidence is not proof that individual sellers lack succession pressure.
- inputs.active_consolidators: This is a verified lower bound, not a census of all private-equity platforms or regional acquirers.
- inputs.buy_mult: The source cohort is high-volume, real-estate-owned and larger than many targets; leased, single-site or legacy formats can price materially lower.
- inputs.buy_mult: The multiple must be applied to normalized EBITDA after market rent and recurring maintenance capital.
- inputs.exit_mult: The modest exit premium depends on creating a portfolio-grade asset; no return to peak 10–12× pricing is assumed.
- inputs.exit_mult: Exit value is highly sensitive to real-estate ownership, leverage, local saturation, membership churn and capital-market conditions.
- inputs.pricing_structure: Mister's 76% subscription share is a scaled-chain benchmark and not representative of every NAICS 811192 format.
