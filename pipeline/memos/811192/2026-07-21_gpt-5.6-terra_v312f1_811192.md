# 811192 — Car Washes

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312f1_811192` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

NAICS 811192 comprises establishments primarily cleaning, washing or waxing automotive vehicles, including detail shops, mobile washes and self-service washes [S1]. It is a durable consumer service but a weak AI value-migration thesis: the major historic labor-removal event was tunnel and kiosk capital equipment, not AI. A buyer can use AI around member service, marketing, pricing and multi-site planning, but the likely operating gain is small against a modest wage base and format-specific capex, real-estate and local-competition risks.

## How AI changes the work

The AI surface is limited to customer support, membership billing/retention, marketing, demand analytics, labor planning and centralized management. Physical vehicle cleaning, equipment maintenance, loading and site supervision remain on site. Mister Car Wash's public model illustrates the distinction: it sells express-exterior washes through kiosks and team members and operates a large subscription program [S2]. Existing unattended or automated tunnels should not be credited as new AI labor replacement.

## Value capture

There is no payer or regulated fee schedule above the operator; a direct-to-consumer site initially retains a cost saving. But price capture is local: an express exterior site with a valuable subscription base can defend price better than a legacy full-service site surrounded by new tunnels. Mister's Unlimited Wash Club represented 76% of its wash sales in 2025, showing the economic importance of subscriptions for a scaled express operator [S2]. The selected distribution and moat inputs retain meaningful competition leakage rather than assuming a subscription business automatically keeps all savings.

## Adoption timing

Current effective AI deployment is low because most potential benefit sits in a thin above-site and customer-administration layer, not the wash itself. A six-year path to 50% of that narrow applicable workflow is reasonable for a regional chain as data, membership systems and centralization mature; it is not a claim that half of car-wash labor will be automated. The absence of a disclosed industrywide AI-adoption series is a material uncertainty.

## Consolidation and economics

Consolidation is real but has cooled from its peak and remains format dependent. Car Wash Advisory publishes a 2025 market report based on proprietary transaction experience [S3], and Amplify recorded Driven Brands' agreement to sell its U.S. car-wash business to Whistle Express for $385 million [S4]. A published Q1 2025 small-business table places $1–5M EBITDA express exterior at 5.6x and full service at 4.2x, with $5–10M EBITDA at 7.0x and 5.9x respectively [S5]. These are advisory benchmarks, not directly comparable confidential deals; owner compensation, rent and real estate must be normalized.

## Terminal demand

Demand for paid vehicle washing remains durable because AI does not wash cars and cannot eliminate site convenience, water, equipment and physical cleaning. The terminal issue is not AI substitution but whether an operator owns an attractive trade area, a defendable membership book and a viable lease or real-estate position. Mister's subscription concentration demonstrates that mature express operators have recurring demand, but it also makes retention and churn central rather than automatic [S2].

## Risks and uncertainty

Format heterogeneity is decisive. Express exterior, full-service/flex, self-service, detailing and mobile operators share NAICS 811192 but have very different labor, capex, membership and valuation profiles. The supplied occupational mix is 5-digit 81119 and includes oil-change/lube-shop contamination, so automotive technicians and tire repairers are not treated as car-wash labor opportunity. The target case is a regional express-exterior membership operator; if the available band is instead full-service, self-service or real-estate-heavy, the score and multiples should be rerun.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The supplied 5-digit occupational mix includes oil-change/lube-shop occupations and is a disclosed proxy.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Fractions exclude pre-existing tunnel, gate and kiosk automation that is not AI.
- inputs.pi_dist: No trade-area-level supply, pricing or churn data are available for the target EBITDA band.
- inputs.pi_moat: Mister is a scaled public benchmark, not evidence that a regional target has the same subscription density.
- inputs.t50_years: No representative current AI-adoption survey for NAICS 811192 was located; this is a bounded judgment.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: Do not equate kiosks, gates or tunnel equipment with AI adoption.
- inputs.historical_analogs: This is not evidence of a measured generative-AI adoption curve.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: Validate owner age, real-estate ownership and format locally before underwriting.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: This is not a nationwide owner-survey result.
- inputs.active_consolidators: Buyer activity differs substantially by region, format and real-estate ownership.
- inputs.buy_mult: Published advisory benchmark, not confidential deal comps; EBITDAR and owned real estate require separate treatment.
- inputs.exit_mult: Exit depends on local supply, membership churn and real-estate/lease terms; no multiple expansion is assumed outside the published range.
- inputs.pricing_structure: Mister's 76% UWC sales share is a scaled-public-company benchmark, not an industry average.
