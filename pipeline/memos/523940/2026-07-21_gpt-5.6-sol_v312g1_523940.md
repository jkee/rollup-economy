# 523940 — Portfolio Management and Investment Advice

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-sol_v312g1_523940` · gpt-5.6-sol · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

A wealth-management RIA in the $1-10 million EBITDA band offers a large AI-addressable task pool, but current acquisition pricing leaves little safe multiple arbitrage. Planning drafts, meeting preparation, research synthesis, portfolio monitoring, account operations, billing, client-service workflows, and compliance documentation are tractable; client acquisition, behavioral coaching, fiduciary accountability, and trust remain human-intensive. Schwab found 68% of surveyed RIAs using AI in some way [S1], so the opportunity is no longer speculative, but effective labor substitution is behind tool use. AUM-based fees decouple price from hours and support retention of savings, while documented fee compression and easy access to automated advice limit capture. Paid advice is pressured rather than melting: the market is growing and regulated, but mature conversational AI is a more credible substitute for mass-affluent service than the prior robo-adviser wave.

## How AI changes the work

The operating model can shift from people assembling information to people supervising, interpreting, and communicating it. Advisors can automate plan drafts, meeting briefs and notes, proposal generation, and routine follow-up; analysts can automate screening, research summaries, rebalancing, and performance attribution; operations teams can automate account opening, transfers, billing, reconciliation, RMD workflows, scheduling, and CRM hygiene. The SEC's robo-adviser guidance confirms that algorithmic advice can generate and manage portfolios with limited human interaction, while remaining subject to disclosure, suitability, compliance, and fiduciary obligations [S13]. Those obligations preserve review and accountable supervision, but they do not preserve every current task or headcount position.

## Value capture

Distribution retention is structurally favorable because the dominant price is a percentage of assets, not hours worked: 95.5% of SEC-registered advisers offered AUM-based fees in 2024, although only 17.4% relied on that model alone [S11]. Labor savings therefore do not automatically reprice the client contract. Capture is incomplete. Cerulli reports fee pressure, broader service expectations, and an expected average fee around 66 basis points for clients above $10 million [S10]. Independent books are also portable; Cerulli observed asset losses even in adviser transitions between independent firms [S9]. Institutionalized service teams, next-generation equity, niches, and strong client retention can protect savings, while a founder-centric book or commoditized allocation proposition cannot.

## Adoption timing

Schwab's 2025 benchmark reports 68% of 1,288 custody clients using AI in some way [S1]. That headline includes experiments and low-intensity uses, so the packet estimates only 40% current effective adoption of economically material workflows and a two-year path to 50%. The speed case is strong because meeting tools, custodial APIs, CRM assistants, and research copilots fit existing software. The slower case is driven by privacy, recordkeeping, hallucination risk, integration, and the need for supervised advice. Adoption can reach a majority before the full labor-saving fraction is realized.

## Consolidation and economics

The RIA market is exceptionally active. ECHELON counted 466 wealth-management transactions in 2025 and listed eight strategic RIAs with ten or more acquisitions, led by Wealth Enhancement at 20 and Mercer Advisors at 17 [S8]. Current multiple evidence does not support a cheap-entry story: Advisor Growth Strategies' 2026 report, summarized from 60 actual 2025 transactions, places the median at 11.6x adjusted EBITDA and the average seller around $500 million AUM [S7]. The prior report already showed an 11.0x 2024 median, included sub-$2 million-revenue transactions, and emphasized earnouts and contingent consideration [S6]. The packet therefore uses 11.6x entry and only 13.0x exit; the latter assumes scale and institutionalization but stays far below 20x-plus large-platform headlines. Any real return must come primarily from organic growth and operating improvement, not assumed multiple expansion.

## Terminal demand

Paid demand is pressured. Industry scale and participation continue to grow: IAA reports 16,544 SEC-registered advisers, 73.7 million clients, and record assets in 2025 [S2]. Federal law imposes a fiduciary duty across the adviser-client relationship [S12], and complex tax, estate, behavioral, family, and concentrated-asset decisions benefit from accountable judgment. Yet the SEC has long recognized fully or partly automated advisers under the same regulatory framework [S13], so regulation is not a human-only moat. Basic portfolio construction and routine planning can migrate to cheaper AI-native providers; durable demand is strongest for complex households and firms that sell coordination, judgment, and accountability rather than information access.

## Risks and uncertainty

NAICS 523940 combines wealth advice with portfolio and institutional asset management, whose fee rates, staffing, margins, and automation differ sharply. The deterministic labor share is ancestor-level and the role mix is three-digit 523, including brokerage sales agents and larger-manager software staff. The fallback target count is a deliberately rough AUM-to-revenue-to-margin bridge and excludes state advisers that are mostly below the inferred EBITDA threshold. Transaction multiples are adjusted EBITDA headline values with meaningful equity, earnout, and retention components; owner compensation normalization can move the denominator. Market declines lower AUM fees immediately, founder departures can trigger asset loss, and AI could increase service expectations rather than reduce headcount.
