# 541690 — Other Scientific and Technical Consulting Services

## Executive view

541690 is a heterogeneous residual code, not a clean consulting vertical. The investable $1–10M EBITDA slice is most plausibly specialist technical advisory with repeat compliance, energy, safety, or field-linked work, rather than generic analysis alone. AI can remove meaningful documentation and analytical labor, but owner-expert concentration, scope uncertainty, and hourly billing make the value migration less clean than the high labor share suggests [S1][S5].

## How AI changes the work

AI is most useful in desk work: research synthesis, first drafts of reports and proposals, data cleaning, benchmarking, meeting notes, and routine project administration. It is less substitutive for site inspection, sampling, testimony, accountable professional judgment, and the relationship work that wins specialist referrals. The 4-digit 5416 occupation mix is an imperfect proxy for this catch-all; the residual is deliberately treated as only partly automatable rather than assuming all unlisted specialists are analysts [S1][S2].

## Value capture

The dominant advisory hypothesis is a mix of project and hourly expert work, with a smaller share of recurring compliance, seasonal, or shared-savings engagements. Faster production initially raises margin, but competitive bids and client rescoping can pass much of the saving through when hours are the billing unit. Credentials, liability, proprietary local data, and physical inspection protect selected niches, while a named expert relationship is portable and weakens firm-level moat [S5].

## Adoption timing

Census measured AI use at 17%–20% across U.S. businesses from December 2025 through May 2026, with lower use among the smallest firms [S2]. This is a broad, all-function adoption anchor rather than a 541690 result. A five-year estimate to 50% effective adoption reflects tool availability alongside fragmented owner-operated firms, client confidentiality, validation needs, and physical/credentialed delivery; it does not rely on consolidation or owner age.

## Consolidation and economics

The deterministic dataset identifies 1,139 firms in the modeled EBITDA band, but the code’s large sub-five-employee population means many apparent targets are SDE/key-person practices. Equiteq reports continued buyer appetite for specialist consulting assets with deep expertise, analytics, and scalable delivery, while its report is global and not a 541690 transaction-comps file [S5]. Buyability is therefore real but conditional on transferable clients, second-line talent, and normalized owner compensation. The selected 5.0x buy and 5.5x exit EBITDA anchors are conservative estimates between micro-business cash-flow evidence and specialty-consulting strategic appetite, not observed 541690 comps [S3][S5].

## Terminal demand

Paid demand is PRESSURED rather than melting: a client can increasingly obtain generic analytical output, and AI shortens the billable analytical portion of a project. Demand remains for work whose output must be defended to a regulator, insurer, court, or operating team, and for field observation that a model cannot perform. This is a terminal-demand mechanism distinct from the price-retention estimate; it does not itself increase C.

## Risks and uncertainty

The largest uncertainty is market composition: NAICS 541690 spans economic, energy, agricultural, safety, and other scientific consulting, and adjacent environmental, engineering, and management-consulting codes absorb similar work [S1]. There is no public, code-specific lower-middle-market EBITDA-comp set in the consulted sources. Owner-age evidence and small-business transaction data are broad proxies, so both B and M carry wide ranges. A buyer should underwrite a specific subsegment, contract form, credential requirement, and client-transfer history rather than rely on this residual-code average.
