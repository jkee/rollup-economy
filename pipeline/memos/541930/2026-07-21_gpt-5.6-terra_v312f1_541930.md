# 541930 — Translation and Interpretation Services

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312f1_541930` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

Translation and interpretation services are a heterogeneous, AI-exposed industry rather than a clean cost-savings buy. The exact code includes written translation, speech interpretation and sign-language services [S1]. General written translation is being compressed by AI while health-care, court, regulated and live interpreting work has a demand floor. With only 28 supplied firms in the inferred target EBITDA band, a buyer needs a segment- and contract-specific diligence case, not a generic language-services thesis.

## How AI changes the work

AI changes written translation first: drafting, translation-memory work, quality checks, vendor assignment and project administration can be automated or compressed. Nimdzi reports that traditional translation can rise in volume while declining in revenue and says LLM adoption follows a gradual testing/validation path, especially in low-risk work [S2]. Remote/live interpretation, qualified human review and specialized workflows retain more human execution and liability ownership.

## Value capture

Capture is weak for ordinary per-word translation because client procurement can use lower-cost AI-enabled alternatives. It is better for recurring, regulated contracts where a provider owns compliance, scheduling, qualified interpreter supply and operational integration. HHS confirms that covered health programs have language-assistance obligations [S3], but that demand floor is not evidence that an LSP keeps every automation saving; pi_dist therefore remains low and terminal demand is assessed separately.

## Adoption timing

I estimate 40% current effective AI adoption and 1.5 years to 50% for general translation and back-office workflows, not for all revenue. Nimdzi describes four-to-six years for neural MT to become mainstream and a repeating gradual adoption pattern for LLMs [S2]. This estimate separates operational use from a claim that most live interpretation has already been displaced.

## Consolidation and economics

Consolidation is real: Slator's 2025 report covered 57 language-industry M&A transactions [S4]. However, current valuations depend on recurring revenue, specialization, process depth, customer concentration and AI exposure. A language-industry M&A adviser reports healthy LSPs at 3-6x EBITDA, with firms under $3m revenue at 2-3.5x [S5]; historic scaled platforms were much dearer, as Teleperformance paid $1.522bn for LanguageLine against $147m adjusted EBITDA in 2016 [S6]. The latter is a non-comparable strategic platform, not a small-target comp.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is PRESSURED. HHS identifies interpreter or document-translation needs in covered health programs and the underlying Title VI/Section 1557 language-access requirements [S3]. That protects a portion of healthcare and similarly regulated live service, but it does not protect routine written translation, localization, or all sign-language and remote modalities from automation and procurement repricing. A blended operator's revenue mix is therefore decisive.

## Risks and uncertainty

The supplied 4-digit role mix is materially contaminated by veterinary occupations and is not an actual LSP occupational census. I retain every supplied SOC as required but treat their automation fractions as a disclosed proxy rather than evidence about language-company staffing. Language-industry research also spans technology, dubbing, data/AI services and global providers [S7], so it cannot be mapped mechanically to this US NAICS code. [S7]
