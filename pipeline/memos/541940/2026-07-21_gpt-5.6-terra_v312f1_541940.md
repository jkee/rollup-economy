# 541940 — Veterinary Services

## Executive view

Veterinary Services is a conditional lower-middle-market roll-up: AI can remove meaningful documentation and front-desk work, while the paid clinical product remains tied to licensed, physical care. The important discipline is not to mistake an AI scribe for replacement of a DVM, technician, or animal-care task. The code also includes reference laboratories and specialty hospitals, so this packet is framed around a multi-doctor companion-animal clinic rather than an average establishment. [S1][S2][S3]

## How AI changes the work

The near-term workflow is ambient documentation, record drafting, follow-up messages, scheduling, reminder handling, refill routing, and revenue-cycle administration. AAHA describes AI scribes as tools that create records from veterinarian-client conversations and emphasizes the need to test accuracy; the specialty survey reports routine AI use but also a low self-reported knowledge base. That supports gradual, supervised adoption rather than unattended clinical substitution. [S3][S4]

## Value capture

A clinic has a direct client relationship and mostly clinic-set fee schedule, so some saved administrative time can remain in margin or be reinvested in appointment capacity. That retention is incomplete: staff scarcity, software subscriptions, local competition, and client price sensitivity can absorb savings. Low insurance penetration does not prove unlimited pricing power, but it does mean the standard human-health insurer cost-control channel is limited; NAPHIA reports 6.4 million insured U.S. pets at year-end 2024. [S6][S7]

## Adoption timing

Effective adoption should reach half of relevant administrative and documentation workflows in roughly three years, not immediately. The cited survey is a specialty-membership sample rather than a representative GP-practice census: 42% of ACVIM respondents and 33% of ECVIM-CA respondents routinely used AI, with scribes the most common tool. Clinic implementation also requires PIMS integration, privacy review, clinician verification, and staff training. [S3][S4]

## Consolidation and economics

Buyability is real but the deterministic band count is conservative: it captures only the 100-499 employee class, whereas many practical clinic targets have lower EBITDA after replacing owner-DVM labor. AAHA, citing Brakke, reports substantial corporate ownership and names Mars-owned Banfield, BluePearl, and VCA as major platforms. For multiples, the directly reopened market evidence is a 3.44x median asking-price/SDE multiple for small listed veterinary practices, not an adjusted-EBITDA sale multiple; this packet therefore uses a conservative SDE-to-EBITDA proxy and no expansion thesis. [S5][S7]

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is durable for the clinical core. FDA says prescription animal drugs can be dispensed only by or on the lawful order of a licensed veterinarian, and VCPR requirements apply to important prescribing contexts. AI can triage, document, and assist decisions, but it cannot itself perform surgery, examinations, handling, or the accountable professional role. The residual risk is that AI deflects some low-acuity questions and that telemedicine rules evolve at the state level. [S2]

## Risks and uncertainty

The central risks are owner-DVM key-person dependence, a chronic clinical-labor constraint, the gap between a specialty AI survey and general-practice adoption, and mismatched valuation bases. The selection excludes a claim that every veterinary act requires an in-person visit: FDA notes state rules matter. It also treats the supplied target-band estimate as a screen rather than a count of the actual clinic acquisition universe. [S2][S4][S7]

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The estimate treats AI scribes as documentation augmentation and caps DVM substitution well below administrative work.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The supplied mix is 5-digit 54194 and includes laboratories and specialty care as well as GP clinics; role-level fractions are a companion-clinic investment view.
- inputs.pi_dist: Insured-pet counts are not a clinic-level payer-mix measure and do not establish local pricing power.
- inputs.pi_moat: Federal VCPR rules do not create a universal in-person requirement; state law and the service line matter.
- inputs.t50_years: The survey is a voluntary specialty-membership sample, not a representative general-practice adoption census.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: This is a specialty-survey proxy, not a measured adoption rate for all NAICS 541940 operators.
- inputs.historical_analogs: No audited historical AI adoption curve for independent GP clinics was found in the reopened sources.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: No reopened source provided a current national 541940 owner-over-60 percentage; diligence must replace this estimate with target-level owner age and succession intent.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: Corporate consolidation evidence is not itself proof that each owner lacks an internal successor.
- inputs.active_consolidators: This is a screening count, not a verified current deal pipeline or a count of all buyers.
- inputs.buy_mult: The source reports asking-price multiples of seller's discretionary earnings, not closed transactions or adjusted EBITDA; owner-DVM replacement must be normalized in diligence.
- inputs.exit_mult: The exit multiple is not supported as an EBITDA platform multiple; it deliberately avoids an expansion thesis.
- inputs.pricing_structure: Neither source measures a specific target's realized payer mix or price elasticity.
