## Executive view

Veterinary services offer durable paid demand and real but bounded AI value. The exact code covers licensed animal medicine, dentistry and surgery as well as veterinary testing laboratories [S1]. AI can remove documentation, scheduling, reception, billing, and some diagnostic-review effort, but it cannot replace animal handling, procedures, prescribing responsibility, or most technician work. A disciplined buyer can retain part of those administrative savings because the market is overwhelmingly consumer-paid and locally delivered, yet the large physical and licensed wage base caps V. Attractive acquisition economics therefore depend more on operational execution and doctor retention than on a large labor takeout.

## How AI changes the work

Adoption is already meaningful: a Digitail survey conducted with AAHA found 39.2% of thousands of veterinary-professional respondents used AI tools or software in their setting, and 69.5% of users did so daily or weekly [S2]. Recordkeeping, administrative work, and imaging/radiology were leading use cases [S2]. The most replaceable blocks are phone and text intake, reminders, scheduling, payment collection, records, claims, bookkeeping, and parts of practice reporting. DVM documentation and diagnostic support improve, but a veterinarian still examines the patient, integrates uncertain evidence, performs procedures, communicates difficult decisions, and remains accountable. Technicians, assistants, and animal caretakers remain predominantly hands-on.

## Value capture

Capture is structurally better than in insurer-dominated human healthcare. NAPHIA reported 6.4 million insured US pets at year-end 2024 [S3], and its published penetration rate was only 3.92% of the US dog-and-cat population [S4]. Most clinics therefore set retail fees and collect from owners rather than having a dominant payer audit labor inputs and re-rate reimbursement. Local convenience, records, staff continuity, and clinical reputation provide some competitive moat. Capture is not complete: price-sensitive owners can defer care, local competitors can constrain fee increases, and consolidators may need to reinvest savings in scarce DVM and technician labor.

## Adoption timing

Current effective adoption is set at the observed 39.2% professional-use rate [S2], below the 50% boundary. Because the same survey found another 38.7% interested in adopting soon, crossing 50% effective use is estimated at two years. Depth matters: a clinic using an AI scribe is not yet realizing full front-office or staffing savings, and the survey is vendor-associated rather than a probability sample of establishments. Adoption timing is based only on technology-use evidence, not consolidation or owner demographics.

## Consolidation and economics

The buyer field is deep: current industry directories identify roughly ten major US consolidators, including Mars Veterinary Health, NVA, Mission Pet Health, VetCor, PetVet, Thrive, AmeriVet, Ethos, MedVet, and Veterinary Practice Partners [S9], while AAHA documents continuing corporate and private-equity acquisition activity [S6]. For general practices, a 2025 valuation study reports 8.6x EBITDA for the $1–5M EBITDA band and 11.3x for $5–10M [S7]; legal-market commentary independently describes mid- to high-single-digit practice multiples [S8]. Those size-tier values support the selected buy and exit multiples, but they are not a matched cohort of completed transactions and may embed seller-friendly auction conditions.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is DURABLE. Census defines the core service around licensed practitioners providing medicine, dentistry, and surgery [S1], and AVMA's VCPR policy requires the veterinarian to assume clinical responsibility and have sufficient patient knowledge grounded in examination or medically appropriate visits [S5]. AI can answer low-acuity questions and deflect some visits, while telemedicine laws may evolve, but it cannot vaccinate, restrain, anesthetize, operate on, or physically examine an animal. Paid demand survives because the revenue-bearing product is a local licensed physical act, not merely information.

## Risks and uncertainty

The principal risks are valuation and labor, not terminal substitution. DVM and technician shortages can absorb administrative savings through wage inflation; owner-veterinarian production must be replaced at market compensation before EBITDA is trusted; and corporate buyers may pay materially different multiples by doctor count, specialty, geography, and seller dependence. The code also includes laboratories, livestock practices, specialty and emergency hospitals, so a generic GP thesis does not fit every establishment. Owner-age evidence reports an average rather than an over-60 share, succession shortage is not directly documented, and the deterministic n_band excludes many classic smaller clinic targets under the generic margin assumption.

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The five-digit role mix covers clinics and veterinary laboratories; the selected fractions are oriented to a multi-doctor general practice.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Credentialed labor shortages may convert time savings into capacity rather than headcount reduction.
- inputs.pi_dist: Low insurance penetration is an indirect capture proxy; it does not directly measure clinic price responses to cost savings.
- inputs.pi_dist: Specialty, emergency, wellness-plan, and insured-client mixes can differ materially.
- inputs.pi_moat: VCPR and licensure support terminal demand; they support capture only through scarce clinical capacity and relationship continuity, avoiding double count.
- inputs.pi_moat: Seller-DVM departure and associate turnover can rapidly weaken the moat.
- inputs.t50_years: The vendor-associated survey sampled professionals, not establishments, and adoption depth was not equivalent to labor savings.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: Respondent self-selection and vendor involvement may bias adoption upward.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: Use of any AI tool is broader than effective operating adoption across a clinic.
- inputs.historical_analogs: Generative AI can deflect some low-acuity visits more directly than prior practice software.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: The source was published in 2020 from an earlier AVMA survey; ownership transitions since then may lower the current share.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: A generational category is not an exact current age-band tabulation of controlling owners.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: Corporate buyer demand is not the same as a documented succession shortage.
- inputs.active_consolidators: Directory status does not prove each buyer will bid on every geography or practice type.
- inputs.active_consolidators: Some groups may be integrating, recapitalizing, or selectively acquiring rather than broadly active.
- inputs.buy_mult: The study draws on private networks, databases, and interviews rather than a published list of completed deals.
- inputs.buy_mult: Owner-DVM clinical compensation, doctor concentration, earnouts, and real estate can change the effective multiple.
- inputs.exit_mult: This is size-tier valuation evidence, not a guaranteed same-asset exit after a roll-up.
- inputs.exit_mult: Higher interest rates, buyer distress, clinical turnover, or weaker visit growth could eliminate the spread.
- inputs.pricing_structure: Insurance penetration counts pets, not revenue; insured pets may represent a larger share of high-cost procedures.
