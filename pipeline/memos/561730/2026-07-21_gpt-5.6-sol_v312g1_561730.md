# 561730 — Landscaping Services

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-sol_v312g1_561730` · gpt-5.6-sol · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

NAICS 561730 covers landscape care and maintenance, landscape installation, and businesses that combine those services with design or hardscape work; pure landscape architecture is outside the code [S1]. The attractive acquisition case is a route-dense, maintenance-heavy regional operator, not the whole code indiscriminately. AI can remove a meaningful share of estimating, scheduling, customer-service, sales-support, bookkeeping, and reporting work, but most wage dollars remain in outdoor physical roles. The result is a modest labor-productivity channel paired with a stronger consolidation channel, durable end demand, and real but execution-dependent multiple expansion.

## How AI changes the work

The dominant occupation is grounds maintenance, whose tasks include mowing, edging, pruning, planting, applying treatments, and operating outdoor tools [S2]. Those tasks are not treated as replaceable by software. The modeled AI surface is concentrated in clerical work, bookkeeping, administrative support, sales support, route and crew planning, proposal drafting, customer communications, and parts of management analysis. Some supervisory documentation and field routing can be automated, while in-person quality control, safety, travel, equipment handling, and customer escalation remain human. Robotic mowing or autonomous equipment would be a separate capital-and-field-automation thesis and is deliberately excluded from the AI labor fractions.

## Value capture

BrightView reports recurring annual maintenance contracts that typically run one to three years but can generally be cancelled on 30 to 90 days' notice, alongside noncontract and project revenue [S4]. That structure delays immediate pass-through of back-office savings, but renewal competition, rebids, and low switching frictions limit permanent retention. Route density, local reputation, crew availability, contract quality, and account relationships create some moat; fragmentation and comparatively low entry barriers limit it. The distribution and moat inputs therefore assume partial, not complete, capture of AI-driven labor savings.

## Adoption timing

Aspire's 2025 industry survey says 93% of respondents use some software while more than three quarters do not currently use AI [S3]. That supports a current effective-adoption estimate below one quarter, but the survey is vendor-sponsored, practice definitions are broad, and software usage is not the same as measurable labor substitution. Adoption should arrive through existing field-service, CRM, estimating, accounting, and communication platforms. A five-year base case to 50% effective adoption allows for fragmented buyers, workflow integration, field-data quality, training, and uneven owner sophistication.

## Consolidation and economics

The consolidation thesis is stronger than the direct AI thesis. LM150 regional rankings show a broad set of sizable landscape platforms [S6], and United Land Services reports 25 acquisitions since 2020 while growing from roughly $40 million to $285 million of revenue [S7]. A buyer can create value through route density, shared branches, procurement, fleet utilization, centralized selling and administration, and disciplined tuck-ins. The selected 5.0x entry and 8.0x exit are inside a 2026 secondary-market estimate of roughly 4-8x for private landscape services and 7-10x for PE-backed regional platforms [S5]. Those figures are indications rather than a disclosed transaction dataset, and the exit case requires genuine scale and professionalization.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is DURABLE. Customers still require recurring vegetation control, property appearance, safety, irrigation, seasonal cleanup, and installation work, and BLS projects grounds-maintenance employment growth rather than disappearance [S2]. AI may make contractors more efficient but does not eliminate the physical service. Weather, drought, water restrictions, housing and commercial construction cycles, labor availability, and discretionary enhancement work affect mix and volatility; they do not imply that the underlying maintenance need melts away.

## Risks and uncertainty

The largest uncertainties are the time required for fragmented operators to turn AI features into effective labor substitution, the owner-age and succession profile, and whether a target can earn a platform exit multiple. The deterministic establishment-band count uses a broad margin proxy and excludes 20-99 employee firms, so it is a sizing screen rather than a census of $1-10 million EBITDA candidates. The occupation mix is available only at the five-digit 56173 level. The same route density and centralization mechanisms can improve both operations and exit quality; their use across the operating and consolidation narratives is disclosed to avoid treating one improvement as two independent facts.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The source evidence describes tasks and adoption but does not provide measured occupation-level AI substitution rates.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The supplied OEWS role mix is at five-digit NAICS 56173, not exact six-digit 561730.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Robotic mowing, autonomous vehicles, and other physical-capital automation are excluded.
- inputs.pi_dist: BrightView is a large commercial operator and is not representative of every residential, installation, tree-care, or snow-services target.
- inputs.pi_moat: Moat varies sharply by route density, customer concentration, service mix, and local labor conditions.
- inputs.t50_years: The Aspire survey is vendor-sponsored and does not measure effective labor displacement or provide an exact NAICS 561730 sampling frame.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: Any AI use is broader than effective adoption in the specific replaceable workflows modeled here.
- inputs.historical_analogs: This is a cross-sectional survey comparison, not a measured historical time series.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: The source is not landscaping-specific and retirement intent does not map exactly to age 60 or older.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: No exact NAICS 561730 survey of succession readiness was found, so this is an explicitly declared proxy.
- inputs.active_consolidators: The LM150 is a revenue ranking, not a live transaction database, and some listed groups may not be active acquirers in every period.
- inputs.buy_mult: The source is a secondary advisory estimate, not a disclosed-deal comp set, and its company-size bands are not identical to the $1-10 million EBITDA screen.
- inputs.exit_mult: Exit multiple expansion is not automatic and could disappear if the asset remains owner-dependent, project-heavy, concentrated, or subscale.
- inputs.pricing_structure: BrightView's commercial mix is broader and more scaled than a typical lower-middle-market target.
