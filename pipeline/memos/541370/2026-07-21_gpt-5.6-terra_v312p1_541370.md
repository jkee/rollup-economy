# 541370 — Surveying and Mapping (except Geophysical) Services

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312p1_541370` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

This is a real but narrow AI-value thesis: AI can remove a meaningful share of office processing while field observation, legal judgment, and responsible charge remain paid work. The stated EBITDA band is only 119 firms and is concentrated in 100-plus employee establishments, so it is not a broad small-firm roll-up universe. I therefore view it as a low-confidence, sub-scale platform thesis rather than a broad acquisition screen. [S1][S2][S4]

## How AI changes the work

Technicians both collect measurements outdoors and enter, process, and map the data; BLS explicitly describes the mixed physical and digital workflow. AI and existing geospatial tools should help with data entry, image selection, drafting, map updates, research, and project administration, but they do not eliminate site visits, monument recovery, instrument operation, or accountable interpretation. The supplied OEWS mix is four-digit and engineering-heavy, so the role fractions are deliberately broad estimates rather than a reconstructed six-digit wage survey. [S1]

## Value capture

Boundary work has a license-and-liability gate, but that gate requires responsible charge rather than passive stamping. Louisiana’s rule illustrates both sides: a licensed surveyor or person under responsible charge performs the boundary survey, and individual professional judgment remains required. That supports some retention of office savings on trusted repeat accounts, but bids for standardized work can pass savings to customers and a faster licensed reviewer can also put downward pressure on fees. [S2]

## Adoption timing

There is evidence that drones and other advances improve efficiency while technicians remain needed to review and interpret output for accuracy and completeness. I use a long, uncertain 12-year remaining path to 50% effective AI-enabled workflow adoption from a low current base, not a claim that 50% of firms already use generative AI. This is a judgment about effective workflow penetration across field and office tasks, not a measured survey statistic. [S1]

## Consolidation and economics

The target count is constrained by the frozen size-band derivation. Bowman’s 2026 purchase of Smith & Associates is direct evidence of one active strategic acquirer of land-surveying capacity, and its 2021 filing records an earlier surveying-and-civil-engineering acquisition. Those facts establish buyer interest, not a broad competitive auction market or a transaction-multiple dataset; the selected 5.0x/7.0x planning multiples are conservative estimates with wide ranges. [S4][S5]

## Terminal demand

Paid demand is DURABLE rather than immune. BLS expects technicians to keep gathering and preparing map information despite drones because accuracy and completeness still require review and interpretation. Boundary determination also remains subject to state licensure and responsible-charge rules. Unlicensed aerial mapping, repetitive deliverables, and office processing are materially more exposed, so durable demand may shrink toward field execution, accountable review, and client service rather than preserve today’s revenue per job. [S1][S2]

## Risks and uncertainty

The largest uncertainties are the true six-digit role mix, adoption measurement, owner age, pricing mix, and transaction multiples. NSPS documents an aging profession and a shortage of younger licensed surveyors, but not an owner-age distribution; its evidence supports the succession-shortage flag but not a precise owner percentage. State rules vary, the supplied occupation cell is 5413 rather than 541370, and the market includes boundary, construction, and mapping work with very different licensing and pricing dynamics. [S3]
