## Executive view

HR consulting is heterogeneous: AI materially compresses generalist research, policies, analytics and engagement administration, while proprietary benchmark data, credentialed retirement work and trusted board-level advice retain value. A $1-10M EBITDA acquisition is attractive only where recurring data/plan-cycle work is firm-owned; a generic project-advisory boutique risks fee deflation and key-person leakage. The supplied 5416 occupational mix is management-consulting-heavy, so role conclusions are necessarily rebuilt hypotheses [S1][S3].

## How AI changes the work

SHRM found 39% of surveyed organizations had AI adopted in HR functions, with another 7% intending launch that year; recruiting and HR technology were the most common practice areas. AI can assemble handbooks, analyze survey data, draft job architectures and automate engagement administration, but client negotiation, change facilitation and defensible executive recommendations need accountable humans. WorldatWork likewise describes routine-work automation and role redesign rather than broad replacement [S1][S2].

## Value capture

Capture depends on revenue mix. Annual subscriptions to proprietary compensation panels and recurring plan/compliance cycles should hold price better than hours-benchmarked projects, whereas generic handbook and analysis work is increasingly contestable by clients using HR-tech and AI. I select only partial distribution and moat; credentialed human gates are not used to inflate capture, because they mainly support retained demand [S2][S3].

## Adoption timing

The client function is already adopting AI but is not yet a majority effective user across HR: SHRM reports 39% adopted in HR functions. I therefore set current adoption at 39% and estimate three years to 50% effective adoption for small and midsize consulting providers, allowing for data governance, client confidentiality and integration. This is not an observed consultant-firm penetration rate [S1].

## Consolidation and economics

A recent disclosed US buyout of an HR advisory firm confirms strategic acquisition interest, but terms were not disclosed [S7]. The only earnings-basis-aligned public reference available here is a much larger workforce-development set that reports Korn Ferry at 6.2x TEV/LTM EBITDA [S8]. That is not a small private transaction comparable. Rather than invent an SDE-to-EBITDA conversion or assert a spread, the valuation case holds entry and exit at the same 6.2x EBITDA reference; underwriting assumes no multiple expansion.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is PRESSURED, not melting. IRS describes enrolled actuaries as approved to perform ERISA-required actuarial services, and proprietary data or high-trust advisory can remain paid. Conversely, SHRM reports AI use centered in HR workflows and WorldatWork reports automation of routine work; the broad, unprotected generalist consulting shell faces self-service substitution. The score does not assume the protected segment represents most firms [S1][S2][S3].

## Risks and uncertainty

The code boundary is unusually severe: benefits brokerage, PEO, payroll, staffing and management consulting can appear in acquisition and revenue data but are different businesses. The supplied 4-digit 5416 role mix omits distinctive compensation analysts and actuaries. The disclosed US HR-advisory transaction has no price or earnings, while the 6.2x reference is a larger public comparable; no reliable SDE-to-EBITDA bridge exists without target QoE and owner-compensation data. The sole M conclusion is therefore no-underwritten expansion, not a claim that 6.2x is a small-boutique clearing multiple [S7][S8].
