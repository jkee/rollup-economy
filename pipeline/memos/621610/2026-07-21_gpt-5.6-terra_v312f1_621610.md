# 621610 — Home Health Care Services

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312f1_621610` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

Home health has durable paid demand, but it is not a high-AI-labor-replacement roll-up. The six-digit code covers skilled nursing and a broad mix of personal, therapy and support services delivered in the home [S1]. A buyer can improve the documentation, intake, billing and scheduling wrapper, yet the large aide and clinician wage base is care delivered in person. The investable question is therefore disciplined, payer-and-service-line-specific operational improvement rather than an AI-driven labor reset.

## How AI changes the work

The credible AI surface is administrative and clinical-support work: referral completeness, eligibility, revenue-cycle checks, scheduling, documentation drafts and quality review. An older home-health survey reported AI/ML use in revenue-cycle tasks, while the Alliance's 2026 submission identifies EMR integration, cost, workforce readiness, legal uncertainty and home-setting privacy as adoption constraints [S4][S5]. AI should be treated as supervised assistance; it does not substitute for an aide's physical care, a nurse's home visit, or clinical accountability.

## Value capture

Medicare FFS skilled home health is paid through standardized 30-day bundled periods, so an efficient operator can initially retain lower administrative cost. That spread is not fully durable: CMS finalized both a -1.023% permanent PDGM behavioral adjustment and a -3.0% temporary 2026 adjustment [S2]. MedPAC also found a 24.1% average FFS Medicare margin for home-health stays in 2023 [S3]. Medicaid personal care and MA contract economics differ, and state wage-pass-through or plan repricing can transfer savings away from the agency; the selected capture inputs therefore remain conservative.

## Adoption timing

Effective use should cross 50% of a well-run regional agency's administrative wrapper in roughly four years, not immediately. Scheduling and digital workflow familiarity are improving—49.3% of surveyed caregivers said technology helped most with scheduling and shift management [S5]—but home-based workflows, protected health information, model validation and EHR integration slow deployment [S4]. This estimate concerns useful operational adoption, not replacement of direct-care visits.

## Consolidation and economics

There is a genuine buyer universe but it is uneven by service line and compliance profile. Mertz Taggart recorded 105 home-based-care transactions in 2025, though only three home-health deals in Q4; it also described sponsor-backed acquirers pursuing add-ons [S6]. The available sub-sector benchmark puts Medicare-certified skilled home health at 4.5x–8x EBITDA, whereas broader home care differs materially by payer and service mix [S7]. Underwriting should normalize owner compensation, referral concentration, EVV and compliance costs, and isolate skilled, private-duty and HCBS economics.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is durable because AI cannot independently provide the hands-on home services inside this NAICS scope. Medicare coverage is for homebound beneficiaries needing intermittent skilled nursing or therapy, and the current federal payment system still purchases a bundle of in-home clinical services [S1][S2]. That does not make every agency durable: MA network position, local labor supply, referral relationships and rate-setting determine who gets paid and at what margin. The durable claim is about demand for human-delivered home care, not durable retention of every AI saving.

## Risks and uncertainty

The code is heterogeneous: Medicare-certified skilled care, Medicaid HCBS/personal care and private-pay home care have different payers, margins, licensing and buyers. The supplied 4-digit occupational mix is broader than a single 6-digit operating model, and the selected target population is a regional Medicare-certified skilled provider with some private-pay diversification. Multiples and owner-age evidence are market benchmarks rather than disclosed transaction comps for the $1–10M EBITDA band; both are low-confidence. CMS rebasing, MA bargaining, staffing shortages and non-transferable referral relationships can overwhelm the modest AI benefit.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The supplied 4-digit occupational mix spans service models within 621610.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Fractions measure mature AI task assistance/replacement, not legacy non-AI automation or avoided visits.
- inputs.pi_dist: Applies to a skilled Medicare-oriented target, not Medicaid HCBS rates or all MA contracts.
- inputs.pi_moat: No agency-specific referral concentration or payer-contract evidence is available.
- inputs.t50_years: This estimates adoption of useful administrative/clinical-support AI, not autonomous clinical care.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: No current representative six-digit survey measures effective AI deployment at $1–10M EBITDA agencies.
- inputs.historical_analogs: The sources do not establish a historical causal adoption curve for generative AI.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: Use as a weak buyability assumption; validate owner age locally before underwriting.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: This is a market-structure judgment, not a documented nationwide owner-survey result.
- inputs.active_consolidators: Not a transaction-database count and varies by skilled, private-duty and hospice service line.
- inputs.buy_mult: Advisory benchmark; no disclosed $1–10M EBITDA transaction comp is supplied.
- inputs.exit_mult: Exit assumes no adverse reimbursement or MA-network deterioration and is not a promised multiple.
- inputs.pricing_structure: No payer mix for the target EBITDA band is available.
