# 621610 — Home Health Care Services

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-sol_v312g1_621610` · gpt-5.6-sol · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

Home health has durable paid demand and a real administrative AI surface, but its high labor share should not be mistaken for replaceable care labor. The exact 2022 NAICS scope is establishments primarily providing skilled nursing in the home with related personal care, therapy, medical social work, equipment and other services; establishments primarily providing non-medical home care are cross-referenced to 624120 [S1]. I therefore score Medicare-certified/skilled home health as the dominant model, while disclosing hospice and payer heterogeneity. AI can reduce OASIS/documentation, intake, scheduling, coding, claims and recruiting work, but aides, nurses and therapists still deliver the product. A 5.5x buy and 8.0x scaled exit are supportable estimates within current Medicare-certified and platform ranges [S8], while CMS's recurring rate adjustments sharply limit long-run capture [S2][S3].

## How AI changes the work

AI changes the wrapper around the visit more than the visit. Scheduling a dispersed workforce, referral intake, eligibility, OASIS and medication documentation, coding review, claims follow-up, QA sampling, recruiting and routine communications are strong candidates. A June 2026 survey of more than 400 home-care leaders reported 91% already using or planning to use AI in operations, but did not separate current use from plans [S4]. A 2025 survey of more than 200 home-health clinicians found 50% used AI personally or professionally and 70% were interested in ambient documentation [S6]. These are adoption signals, not evidence that half the industry's work is automated. Physical care, observation, wound care, medication support, transfers, family interaction and escalation remain human.

## Value capture

Medicare prospective payment lets an efficient agency keep some savings inside a rate period, but the public payer has an explicit rebasing channel. CMS's 2026 rule applied a permanent -1.023% adjustment and a -3.0% temporary adjustment tied to differences between assumed and actual PDGM behavior, producing an estimated 1.3% aggregate payment decline from 2025 [S2]. MedPAC reported a 21.2% 2024 FFS Medicare margin, projected 19% for 2026 and recommended a 7% base-rate cut for 2027 [S3]. That is direct evidence that aggregate productivity and coding behavior can be transferred back to Medicare. Private-pay retains more efficiency; Medicaid and managed-care pricing can transfer more to wages or payers. The selected distribution fraction is therefore only 45%, with no use of durable human demand to inflate capture.

## Adoption timing

Effective adoption is below 50% even though awareness and plans are high. The AxisCare statistic combines use with intention [S4], WellSky's 50% includes personal use [S6], and the national trade association describes cost, integration, workforce readiness, privacy, legal uncertainty and limited small-provider resources as active barriers [S5]. I estimate 30% current workflow-weighted adoption and three years to 50%. The range is wide because scaled agencies with modern EMRs can move quickly, while independent agencies face integration, consent, broadband, compliance and change-management constraints.

## Consolidation and economics

The supplied dataset estimates 1,983 firms in the EBITDA band, all in the 100-499 employee class, but it does not split Medicare-certified, mixed-service, hospice or payer models. Mertz Taggart counted 105 home-based-care transactions in 2025; Q4 included three home-health add-ons and named VitalCaring, while its home-care section named Addus and Amivie transactions [S7]. I count three directly observed consolidators as a conservative floor. Current advisory benchmarks put Medicare-certified home health at 3.5x-7.0x EBITDA and multi-service platforms at 6.0x-10.0x-plus [S8]. The selected 5.5x buy and 8.0x exit fit those ranges but require clean OASIS, billing, compliance, referral concentration and management diligence; hospice economics must be separated rather than used to inflate the multiple.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is durable because the covered service is skilled, physical care in the home, not generic information. MedPAC recorded 2.7 million FFS beneficiaries, $16.0 billion of 2024 spending and more than 12,000 Medicare-certified agencies; 97% of beneficiaries lived in a ZIP code served by at least two agencies [S3]. AI may help triage, monitor and document, and payers may reduce visits or rates, but it does not perform wound care, bathing, transfers, medication support or a skilled nursing assessment in the patient's home. The Alliance also notes growing demand and strong preferences for home-based care while emphasizing the distinct, uncontrolled home environment [S5]. The economic risk is rate capture and labor availability, not disappearance of paid demand.

## Risks and uncertainty

The first-order risk is scope and payer heterogeneity. Official NAICS excludes establishments primarily engaged in non-medical home care, while still allowing personal-care services when bundled with skilled home health; it also lists in-home hospice as an example [S1]. The 4-digit OEWS role mix and the dataset's broad firm counts can therefore blend models with different wages, margins and multiples. Adoption evidence is vendor-sponsored and often mixes use, plans, interest, personal use and professional deployment [S4][S6]. Medicare margin evidence is strong but applies to FFS freestanding HHAs, not Medicaid personal care, MA contracts or private pay [S3]. The selected valuation pair uses skilled-home-health and platform ranges, not higher hospice multiples [S8]. Owners over 60 and succession shortage are weakly evidenced, so they are estimated conservatively and shortage is not asserted.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: RN documentation savings may raise capacity or retention rather than remove positions.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The 17.66% residual combines schedulers, coders, recruiters and marketers with very different automation exposure.
- inputs.pi_dist: Strong Medicare evidence does not directly quantify Medicaid, MA or private-pay pass-through; the selected value represents the skilled-home-health base case.
- inputs.pi_moat: Certificate-of-need and licensure barriers vary by state, and personal-care operations outside exact 621610 may have lower entry barriers.
- inputs.t50_years: Surveys do not report a representative six-digit, workflow-weighted effective-adoption measure.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: AxisCare combines current use with plans, WellSky includes personal use, and both are vendor-sponsored.
- inputs.historical_analogs: Mandated EVV and payment-model changes are imperfect analogs for voluntary AI investment.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: Broad employer-business proxy, not 621610-specific and not necessarily weighted by control ownership.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: Active M&A and aging portfolio companies do not by themselves prove an owner succession shortage.
- inputs.active_consolidators: Addus and Amivie examples include home care, which may fall outside exact 621610 when primarily non-medical; the count is a conservative home-based-care buyer floor.
- inputs.buy_mult: Advisory benchmark, not a complete closed-deal tape; quality scores, CON status, payer mix, compliance and earnouts can move price materially.
- inputs.exit_mult: The platform range can include broader service lines; hospice multiples are excluded from the base case, and CMS rate risk can compress exit pricing.
- inputs.pricing_structure: The payer mix of the supplied EBITDA-band firms is not reported, and exact 621610 excludes establishments primarily providing non-medical home care.
