## Executive view

Computer Systems Design Services is a conditional MSP/systems-integration roll-up screen, not a blanket software-services thesis. AI is already changing the routine operating layer: Kaseya's 2026 survey reports 53% of MSPs use AI to automate ticketing, patching and monitoring [S1]. The investable question is whether the operator retains that productivity while clients continue paying a recurring fee for accountable IT, security, documentation and incident response. Current evidence supports durable need and active consolidation, but it also shows smaller contracts and competitive pressure. The selected score is therefore conditional, with resale-heavy, project-led and founder-dependent firms outside the thesis.

## How AI changes the work

The most exposed work is repeatable technical delivery: ticket triage, knowledge retrieval, password and onboarding flows, patching, monitoring, documentation, runbook drafting, configuration and first-pass QA. Kaseya reports current automation of ticketing, patching and monitoring, though more than half of respondents have automated only roughly a quarter of workload [S1]. Systems analysis, solution architecture, migrations, security escalation, field installs and client-specific environment judgment remain partial rather than complete substitutes. The role estimates reflect this task split, not deal activity or subscription economics.

## Value capture

Capture is materially better than in time-and-materials consulting because managed services commonly bill per user, per device, bundled monthly fee or value-based subscription [S2]. That can let a provider keep labor savings until a renewal reprices, particularly for security and business-continuity work; Kaseya describes cybersecurity and backup as the revenue anchors in its current MSP survey [S1]. The counter-channel is explicit: per-user fees fall when a client reduces headcount, per-device fees can fall with a smaller estate, and vendors may monetize the automation layer. I therefore assign only partial distribution capture despite recurring billing and client documentation.

## Adoption timing

Effective operational adoption has crossed the 50% threshold: Kaseya's April 2026 survey of more than 1,000 MSPs reports that 53% are already using AI to automate ticketing, patching and monitoring [S1]. I set current adoption to 53% and t50 to zero under the frozen rule. This is strong business-model evidence but not a U.S. 541512 establishment census; the source's global MSP population also spans systems integrators, outsourced operations and related IT-service codes. It measures deployment in three core operational activities, not autonomous management of an entire client estate.

## Consolidation and economics

Buyability is real but quality-dependent. The immutable target count is large, and a specialist MSP exit adviser describes many firms as small, founder-led businesses launched in the 1990s and 2000s whose owners are now considering transitions [S5]. Solganick's July 2025 market update records a Q2 rebound, active private-equity interest and several MSP platform acquisitions, including a security-focused MSP joining Evergreen's Lyra family [S3]. I count only three demonstrably active platform/strategic buyers from that compact evidence and do not confuse their interest with proof that every low-margin local MSP is saleable.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is DURABLE, though the delivery mix changes. SMBs still need someone accountable for endpoint security, backup, recovery, vendor coordination, privileged access, incident escalation and the business consequences of an outage. Kaseya reports security and business-continuity as the strongest growing MSP revenue categories [S1]. AI makes routine monitoring and service-desk effort cheaper; it does not presently establish that clients can safely replace a managed operator with a generic AI subscription. The key downside remains platform disintermediation or AI-driven client seat reductions, which would pressure fees even if the need for accountable operations persists.

## Risks and uncertainty

The main modelling limitation is frame mismatch. The frozen 541512 data includes systems design, while MSP surveys and deal sources also cover 541513, 541519, custom-development, MSSP and cloud-partner populations. Additionally, blended MSP revenue may include low-margin hardware and software resale, whereas valuation evidence rewards recurring revenue, retention and security/cloud depth [S2][S4]. Owner age and consolidation counts are directional rather than code-specific. The selected buy/exit pair is a modest scale-path assumption within the observed small-MSP valuation range, not a promise that a resale-heavy or weak-contract operator can exit at a platform multiple.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Role mix is 4-digit 5415, while MSP and systems-integration employment spans adjacent NAICS codes.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Kaseya measures automation deployment, not labor hours eliminated by SOC role.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The residual includes unknown proportions of desk, field, infrastructure and administrative work.
- inputs.pi_dist: The 2023 pricing survey predates the current service-desk AI cycle; client headcount and endpoint-base effects could offset rate retention.
- inputs.pi_moat: MSP contracts may be cancellable and platforms can capture value through higher software fees; this is not a claim of proprietary technology.
- inputs.t50_years: Global MSP survey rather than U.S. 541512 census; it does not establish full autonomous delivery.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: Observed value is for a global MSP sample spanning adjacent IT-service classifications, not a NAICS 541512 sample.
- inputs.historical_analogs: No code-specific historical labor or pricing series was located.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: Advisory commentary is not a representative 541512 owner-demographic survey.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: Do not infer the volume, geography or quality of available targets from this qualitative evidence.
- inputs.active_consolidators: Examples are from 1H 2025 and do not enumerate all acquirers or prove interest in every 541512 subsegment.
- inputs.buy_mult: The source's disclosed-deal median and size table are not a direct comp set for each screened target.
- inputs.exit_mult: Aventis's higher transaction data are skewed to larger deals; Solganick reports premium valuations only for differentiated scalable platforms.
- inputs.pricing_structure: The cited pricing mix is from the 2023 benchmark and applies to MSPs, a business-model frame broader than 541512.
