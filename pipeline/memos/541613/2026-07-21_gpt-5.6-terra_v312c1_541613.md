# 541613 — Marketing Consulting Services

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312c1_541613` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

Marketing consulting has a real near-term AI labor dividend, particularly in research synthesis, routine content, reporting, proposals and coordination. It is nevertheless a difficult buyout thesis: client-side marketing teams are adopting the same tools, delivery savings are likely to be competed into renewals, and much of a small firm's revenue follows its principal. Buy only a specialized, retainer-heavy operator with transferable accounts and execution/data integration; otherwise this is a cautious, pressured-demand situation rather than a broad roll-up opportunity. [S1][S2][S4]

## How AI changes the work

Generative AI can accelerate first drafts of positioning, personas, competitive scans, campaign analyses, briefs, presentations and status reporting. The CMO Survey reports generative AI deployed across 15.1% of marketing activities in 2025 and a 10.8% reported reduction in marketing overhead, useful evidence that the work is becoming tool-assisted rather than hypothetical. Client workshops, senior persuasion, accountable recommendations and relationship selling remain human-intensive, so the task judgment is partial rather than a claim that whole occupations disappear. [S1]

## Value capture

The operator should retain only a minority of the gross labor saving. Project fees and retainers can initially preserve a spread when deliverables are produced faster, but buyers increasingly ask consulting firms to demonstrate AI delivery economics and clients can use the same tools internally. I model distribution/pass-through at 0.35 and moat retention at 0.30: vertical expertise, proprietary client data, martech implementation and sticky execution can protect selected firms, while a generalist strategy deck is readily benchmarked and portable with the partner. This is distinct from terminal demand: it measures who keeps a saving if work remains. [S1][S4]

## Adoption timing

Current adoption is below the 50% boundary on the closest directly measured metric: the 2025 CMO Survey says generative AI is deployed across 15.1% of marketing activities and projects 44.2% of all marketing efforts using AI within three years. I therefore use 15.1% as a conservative, scope-mismatched proxy and estimate three years to 50% effective adoption in the target operators. That is an adoption estimate, not an inference from owner age or consolidation. [S1]

## Consolidation and economics

The supplied band has 1,936 estimated $1–10M EBITDA targets, but transaction comparables are imperfect. BizBuySell's full-year 2025 tables are voluntary broker-reported small-business transactions and use cash flow/SDE rather than normalized EBITDA; they show the broad small-business market is liquid enough to inform a low-end entry anchor, not an exact marketing-consulting multiple. Equiteq describes continuing selective consulting M&A but says buyers favor scale, specialization and defensibility. I use a 4.5x normalized EBITDA buy estimate and 5.0x exit estimate only for a qualifying specialist, with modest active-consolidator evidence rather than assuming broad multiple expansion. [S3][S4]

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is PRESSURED, not melting. Marketing leaders still need accountable outside perspective and execution capacity, but the product has no license or physical moat and clients can generate research, planning and creative drafts internally. The same evidence showing AI in marketing operations and buyer scrutiny of AI economics supports pressure on commoditized advisory hours; it does not demonstrate that all external marketing advice vanishes. Durable paid demand is most plausible where the firm owns client-specific data, vertical knowledge, media/martech execution or an embedded retainer. [S1][S4]

## Risks and uncertainty

The role mix is a 4-digit 5416 proxy rather than this marketing-consulting code and the residual creative/content tail may be more exposed than the listed occupations. The adoption survey measures client marketing activity, not AI deployment by US lower-middle-market consultancies. Owner-readiness and transaction evidence are national, cross-industry samples; neither proves owner age, succession scarcity, client transferability or EBITDA multiples for this code. Finally, agency, advertising, market-research and martech benchmarks cross NAICS boundaries. These uncertainties justify MED overall confidence and disciplined deal-level diligence on renewals, client concentration, founder dependence and normalized compensation. [S1][S2][S3][S5]
