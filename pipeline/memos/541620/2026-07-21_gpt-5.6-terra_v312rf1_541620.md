# 541620 — Environmental Consulting Services

## Executive view

Environmental consulting has durable, regulation- and risk-driven work, and AI can improve the document, data, permitting, and project-control layer. It is a weak near-term AI roll-up screen, however: much of the professional product remains site-specific judgment, field execution, and liability-bearing sign-off, while time-and-materials and competitive fixed-fee work share savings with clients. The fragmented target base and active consolidation support a conventional buy-and-build thesis, but not an AI-led underwriting premium [S1][S2][S4].

## How AI changes the work

The addressable work is records review, regulatory-database search, GIS and data reduction, draft permit and monitoring reports, proposal production, invoicing, and project administration. Environmental scientists still collect and analyze evidence, assess hazards, recommend action, and help clients comply with regulation; fieldwork, sampling oversight, agency interaction, and defensible professional conclusions do not disappear [S6]. ACEC describes AI as a time multiplier for proposal formatting, specification drafting, and review of legacy project data, while also describing an incremental, constrained implementation path in engineering [S3].

## Value capture

Savings will not all accrue to the operator. Rate-schedule and time-and-materials work invites clients to demand fewer billable hours for the same deliverable; fixed-fee Phase I and recurring compliance work also faces price competition. A qualified environmental professional must still exercise judgment and sign the required report in the AAI process, which creates some client and liability friction but does not require a large firm [S1]. The selected capture inputs therefore assume partial retention: firm relationships, regulatory credibility, and workflow integration help, but a commodity report can be produced faster and competitively repriced.

## Adoption timing

Effective adoption is materially below 50% for the selected labor-saving workflows in the lower-middle-market target population. The closest evidence is AEC rather than a clean 541620 survey: ACEC reports high expectations for AI but says adoption is incremental and constrained by regulatory, contractual, and cultural issues, with interviewees expecting a five-to-ten-year transformation [S3]. The selected six-year midpoint is a judgment about achieving dependable workflow redesign and client acceptance, not a forecast that field science is replaced.

## Consolidation and economics

The deterministic dataset identifies 764 firms in the modeled $1-10M EBITDA band, a meaningful but not unlimited target pool. EBJ describes a fourth and largest consolidation wave in environmental consulting and engineering, with platforms buying specialized services and geographies [S2]. Equiteq reports vigorous global ESG-consulting M&A, PE/platform participation, a fragmented acquired-firm population, and an 11.3x median private-market valuation; its population is broader and generally larger than a small US environmental-consulting target, so the selected entry and exit multiples are deliberately discounted estimates rather than direct comps [S4].

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is DURABLE, although not uniformly high-margin. EPA's AAI framework requires an environmental professional with defined education, licensing or experience qualifications to develop opinions for relevant inquiries, and environmental work also includes compliance, remediation, permitting, monitoring, and risk management [S1][S6]. BLS projects environmental-scientist employment to grow 4% through 2034 and notes continuing business demand for environmental consulting [S6]. AI reduces the production cost of portions of the service; it does not by itself remove the need for site evidence, professional judgment, or many regulated client decisions.

## Risks and uncertainty

The severe role-mix mismatch is the largest V risk: the supplied 4-digit 5416 mix is dominated by management analysts, whereas 541620 uses environmental scientists, geologists, engineers, technicians, and field staff. Current effective AI adoption, owner age, small-firm entry multiples, and the exact share of T&M, fixed-fee, and cost-plus work have no clean NAICS-specific public measurement in the reopened sources. The packet therefore uses bounded estimates and visible caveats; an acquisition should replace them with target-level billing mix, labor allocation, backlog, client-concentration, and credential data. Policy, CRE transaction volume, and public-enforcement budgets can also shift demand by subsegment [S2].
