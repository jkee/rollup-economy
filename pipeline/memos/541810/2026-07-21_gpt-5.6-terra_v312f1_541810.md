# 541810 — Advertising Agencies

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312f1_541810` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

541810 covers businesses that create campaigns and place advertising, offering creative, account-management, production, media-planning and buying capabilities [S1]. This makes it highly AI-exposed, but that exposure does not create an easy roll-up: labor-based fees and procurement can pass cost savings to clients, while in-housing and platform automation challenge the product itself. The screen therefore gives meaningful task automation but low retained capture and a conservative small-agency multiple bridge.

## How AI changes the work

Generative tools can reduce first-draft copy, asset versions, production design, research synthesis, media plans, reporting, billing support and project coordination. Client judgment, brand accountability, senior selling and relationship continuity remain human-intensive. The supplied 5418 role mix is a four-digit proxy contaminated by media representatives, display and promotional occupations; judgments explicitly discount those lines and use a bounded residual for account service, production and administrative roles absent from the top ten [S1].

## Value capture

ANA reports that labor-based fees remained the dominant agency-compensation form, especially among smaller advertisers, even as fixed/output fees gained use [S3]. Under labor-based scopes, AI hours saved invite procurement repricing rather than pure agency margin retention. Output fees can retain a spread, but that benefit is offset by platforms and in-house teams becoming cheaper substitutes. This is why pi_dist is low and separate from the still-real value of a strong client relationship or specialist positioning.

## Adoption timing

BTOS evidence reports 18% of firms using AI in a business function in late 2025/early 2026, with high use in knowledge-intensive professional services but limited functional breadth among adopters [S2]. Advertising's task profile should progress faster than the economy-wide average, but effective adoption includes workflow, approvals, brand risk and client billing, not merely tool use. Three years to 50% effective operational adoption is a judgment rather than a claim that 541810 already has 50% adoption.

## Consolidation and economics

The frozen dataset estimates 1,175 firms in the $1–10M EBITDA band, largely 20–99-employee agencies. JEGI reports that digital-marketing assets with £4m–£7.5m EBITDA traded at a 10.8x median, while larger assets received still higher multiples [S5]. That supports real deal interest but is neither a U.S. 541810 series nor a comp for $1–4M EBITDA agencies. A 5.5x entry and 7.0x exit reflect a deliberate discount for smaller scale, client concentration, owner dependence and normalized-EBITDA uncertainty.

## Terminal demand

The paid product is pressured, not automatically melting. Agencies retain value where they provide brand judgment, accountable coordination, specialist vertical knowledge and integrated execution; those services sit within the exact NAICS definition [S1]. But ANA reports 82% of members had an in-house agency in 2023 and the cited research reports meaningful internal cost and time advantages [S4]. AI-native creative and platform automation therefore put durable pressure on undifferentiated production and media execution.

## Risks and uncertainty

The key risks are client concentration, principal-led revenue, labor-fee repricing, in-housing and boundary leakage into PR, media buying, marketing consulting and design. The available multiple evidence covers digital marketing and larger assets, not exact NAICS 541810 lower-middle-market transactions [S5]. The target screen should be underwritten on net revenue rather than gross media billings, normalized owner compensation, churn and contracts; no generic agency statistic substitutes for that diligence.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The supplied 4-digit 5418 wage mix includes sibling advertising industries, so the role allocation is an agency-specific adjustment rather than a direct 541810 observation.
- inputs.pi_dist: ANA's compensation evidence is marketer-survey based and does not give an exact small-agency revenue-weighted mix.
- inputs.pi_moat: In-house penetration is advertiser-member evidence and may not map to SMB clients of lower-middle-market agencies.
- inputs.t50_years: BTOS does not publish a direct 541810 effective-adoption series.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: The observed statistic is national, not an exact advertising-agency rate.
- inputs.historical_analogs: The analogy does not prove the pace or magnitude of generative-AI substitution.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: No NAICS- and EBITDA-band-specific owner-age source was located; confirm seller and management succession case by case.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: This deliberately withholds the succession uplift.
- inputs.active_consolidators: JEGI's source covers digital marketing services broadly and not a complete count of 541810 buyers.
- inputs.buy_mult: Neither source is a direct U.S. 541810 $1–10M EBITDA transaction series; buy multiple must be validated with live brokered comps and QoE.
- inputs.exit_mult: Exit multiple depends on net revenue, client retention, data/technology differentiation and normalized owner compensation; it is not an observed agency median.
- inputs.pricing_structure: Compensation structures vary by client size and agency type; media billings must be separated from net revenue.
