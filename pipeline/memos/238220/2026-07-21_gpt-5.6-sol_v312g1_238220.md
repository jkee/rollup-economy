# 238220 — Plumbing, Heating, and Air-Conditioning Contractors

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-sol_v312g1_238220` · gpt-5.6-sol · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

A lower-middle-market plumbing/HVAC contractor is a credible AI value-retention target, but the value pool is narrow rather than transformational. Most payroll belongs to technicians, installers, helpers, and field supervisors whose physical work AI cannot perform. The useful prize is the office layer: call handling, dispatch, scheduling, pricebook upkeep, estimates, invoicing, documentation, and selected management reporting. Residential service and replacement operators using flat-rate pricebooks can retain a meaningful portion of those savings, while competitively bid commercial and new-construction work will pass more through. Paid demand is durable because repair and installation remain physical and, for refrigerant work, federally certification-gated [S2].

## How AI changes the work

AI changes the coordination around the wrench, not the wrench work itself. ServiceTitan's 2025 survey found 46% of more than 1,000 contractors using or experimenting with AI, with administration the leading use area at 59%, marketing and sales at 51%, and customer communication and field operations at 39% [S1]. That is consistent with automating CSR scripts, booking, dispatch suggestions, job summaries, estimate follow-up, invoicing, and permit paperwork. ServiceTitan's pricebook product also exposes AI-supported price comparisons, equipment-document retrieval, troubleshooting guidance, and automated job notes [S8]. These tools raise technician utilization but do not eliminate licensed installation, diagnosis in the building, quality control, or customer-facing accountability.

## Value capture

Residential service/replacement commonly uses an upfront flat-rate pricebook; ServiceTitan states that flat-rate systems dominate among the HVAC contractors it supports [S8]. Because the customer buys an outcome rather than hours, an operator can initially keep much of an administrative productivity gain. Capture is not complete: local competitors buy the same software, dynamic local price comparisons can re-anchor prices, lead-generation costs can absorb savings, and commercial/new-construction work remains bid-intensive. The underwriting therefore assigns strong distribution retention but only a middling moat. Regulatory and physical-work barriers support terminal demand, not the capture score.

## Adoption timing

The broad survey signal is already substantial, but 'using or experimenting' is not the same as effective deployment across target workflows. The packet discounts ServiceTitan's 46% headline to an estimated 30% current effective adoption for the dominant residential/light-commercial service segment and places the 50% threshold about three years away. Embedded features are the likely path: 59% of AI users in the survey reported using features already built into existing software [S1]. Fragmented small shops, poor process data, integration work, and technician/dispatcher trust slow the conversion from experimentation to labor removal.

## Consolidation and economics

The market is unquestionably active: Capstone counted 149 announced or completed HVAC-services transactions through September 2025 and documented Sila, TruTemp, Liberty Service Partners, NexCore, and other acquirers [S5]. Size-tiered valuation evidence matters more than headline transactions. Western Commerce's February 2026 table places residential businesses at 8.4x EBITDA for $1-5 million EBITDA and 9.9x for $5-10 million, with lower commercial-service multiples [S4]. The packet uses 8.0x entry and 9.5x exit to reflect a mixed-code target that can become a scaled, service-heavy platform, while recognizing that contingent consideration, owner dependence, normalization, and technician retention can erase the spread. BizBuySell's much smaller HVAC transactions average 2.75x seller's discretionary earnings, confirming that micro-shop SDE multiples cannot be mixed mechanically with lower-middle-market EBITDA [S3].

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is durable. HVAC and plumbing require an on-site person, tools, parts, and responsibility for building systems; EPA Section 608 requires certification for technicians maintaining, servicing, repairing, or disposing of covered refrigerant equipment [S2]. AI may reduce avoidable visits through remote diagnostics and may shift the customer relationship toward OEMs or national platforms, but it does not install a heat pump, replace a compressor, repair a drain line, or accept job-site liability. PHCC also describes continued skilled-worker demand amid retirements and a tight pipeline [S9]. Those mechanisms preserve paid field demand while simultaneously capping the AI-addressable wage share.

## Risks and uncertainty

The code is heterogeneous: residential service/replacement, commercial maintenance, and mechanical new-construction subcontracting have different margins, pricing, cyclicality, and AI capture. The deterministic labor share is an ancestor-level, mixed-vintage low-quality input, and the target count uses an engineering/construction margin that likely misses profitable 20-99 employee service firms. AI adoption evidence is a vendor-sponsored, cross-trade survey and does not measure labor removed. Multiple evidence is current and size-tiered but still advisory and directional. Founder dependence, license transfer, technician scarcity, customer concentration, seasonality, and the mix of recurring maintenance agreements are acquisition-specific risks that cannot be settled at the NAICS level.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The role mix is at five-digit 23822 rather than exact 238220 and leaves a 26.49% residual whose composition is uncertain.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Vendor survey use cases indicate task direction, not measured labor displacement.
- inputs.pi_dist: The value assumes a residential/light-commercial service-heavy operator; commercial construction would be lower.
- inputs.pi_moat: Licensing supports service availability and demand but is not treated as a complete barrier to price competition.
- inputs.t50_years: Survey usage is cross-trade and includes experimentation rather than scaled labor removal.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: Vendor-sponsored survey of multiple residential and commercial trades is not representative of exact NAICS 238220.
- inputs.historical_analogs: The analogue is qualitative and does not identify a causal labor-reduction rate.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: HARDI survey respondents are contractors/purchasing decision-makers, not a probability sample of exact-code firm owners; plumbing coverage is incomplete.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: FMI covers the broader built environment and PHCC's shortage statement concerns skilled talent, so the exact-code owner-succession bridge is a disclosed proxy.
- inputs.active_consolidators: The count is a documented minimum, not a census, and includes commercial HVAC as well as residential home services.
- inputs.buy_mult: Western's table is illustrative and partly synthesized; BizBuySell is SDE, mostly below the target EBITDA band, and is not converted into EBITDA.
- inputs.exit_mult: Achieving the selected exit requires mix and quality improvement; deal structures and market rates can reduce realized cash value.
- inputs.pricing_structure: No exact-NAICS revenue-weighted pricing-model survey was located; plumbing and commercial-mechanical mixes may differ.
