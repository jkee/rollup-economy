## Executive view

This residual code is heterogeneous and its revenue mix is unmeasured. The best available MSP proxy shows growth in security, cloud management and infrastructure support, while platform tools automate portions of provisioning and recovery. The investable question is therefore not whether managed IT demand vanishes, but whether a target owns recurring accountable outcomes rather than commodity project hours.

## How AI changes the work

AI and automation affect triage, documentation, configuration scripts, monitoring and routine support. Microsoft documents zero-touch device deployment without traditional imaging, and Azure documents managed replication, failover and failback; these are bounded examples of vendor-supplied workflow automation, not evidence that all installation or recovery services disappear. Field deployment, recovery design, security judgment and client escalation remain human work.

## Value capture

A recurring managed-service or DR contract can retain some efficiency, whereas project, incident and resale-plus-services books face competitive pass-through. Vendor platforms package more routine work upstream, so a small provider needs the client relationship and accountability for an outcome to retain value. The available sources do not measure capture for 541519, so this remains target-level diligence rather than a population claim.

## Adoption timing

MSP benchmark evidence shows AI is helping with repetitive tasks, but it does not establish that most residual-code firms have effective delivery-wide adoption. The selected rate remains below 50%, so timing is positive rather than mechanically zero. The proxy and vendor framing make this a low-confidence estimate.

## Consolidation and economics

The 705 estimated targets include businesses that MSP buyers may exclude for low MRR or high resale/project mix. M&A benchmarks reward retention and recurring revenue, so selected multiples are below a high-quality MSP case and assume only limited quality improvement. The selected consolidator count is an unsupported sensitivity assumption, not a buyer tally.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is DURABLE, with important economic pressure. The MSP proxy reports structurally growing managed-IT demand from cybersecurity, cloud adoption and regulation; the Kaseya benchmark also reports revenue growth in security, cloud management and infrastructure support. Zero-touch provisioning and vendor-native DR capabilities can commoditize bounded routine delivery tasks, but these sources do not establish that the residual code's service base is shrinking or that vendors absorb accountable recovery, security and client-governance work. Treat durable demand as a recurring-accountability thesis, not a claim that project hours or margins are durable.

## Risks and uncertainty

MSP evidence is a sibling-code proxy, while 541519 is a catch-all. No code-specific owner-age source or revenue split was found, and vendor documentation demonstrates capabilities rather than displacement rates. Revenue mix, MRR, resale pass-through, and the actual share of DR versus project work remain decisive diligence items; a project/resale-heavy target can still have materially weaker economics than the durable-demand proxy.
