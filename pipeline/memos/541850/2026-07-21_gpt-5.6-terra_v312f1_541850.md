# 541850 — Indoor and Outdoor Display Advertising

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312f1_541850` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

Indoor and Outdoor Display Advertising is an asset-heavy advertising business, not an ad agency: Census defines it as creating display campaigns and/or placing displays on billboards, panels, transit, malls and other structures [S1]. Its durable feature is permitted, leased and built physical inventory. OOH revenue reached $9.46 billion in 2025, grew 3.6%, and digital OOH accounted for 36.3% of revenue [S2]. AI can materially lower sales-support, design and administrative cost but cannot replace structures, permits, landowner leases or field maintenance. I classify terminal demand as DURABLE, while retaining low overall confidence because NAICS 541850 spans billboard plants, transit, indoor, display-design and other formats with very different economics.

## How AI changes the work

The supplied 5418 role mix is agency-weighted, so it overstates PR, research and creative labour and omits much of the installation, printing, lease and field-operation tail. I use it only as the deterministic wage-share shell and judge actual tasks within it. AI can automate prospect lists, rate-card queries, proposal generation, proof-of-performance reports, billing, contract administration and creative iteration. Physical posting, structure maintenance, site permitting, lease negotiation and local selling remain partly or wholly human. BTOS’s 17–20% broad-business AI-use range shows adoption is early across firms, not already at a 50% effective-workflow threshold [S3].

## Value capture

A display operator sells scarce inventory rather than hours. Billboard cash-flow guidance defines the asset cash flow after direct costs such as leases, maintenance, utilities, permit fees and an imputed sales cost [S4]. This supports a high, though not perfect, share of labour savings remaining with the operator: site rents are set by location and demand, not directly by back-office labour. Programmatic buying adds price transparency and a platform relationship, but OAAA data still show a mixed market: programmatic was 24% of DOOH spend in 2024 and outdoor inventory was 51% of spend on the Place Exchange platform in H1 2025 [S5]. The platform channel is a capture risk, not an assumption that it destroys the physical asset.

## Adoption timing

I estimate current effective AI adoption at 25% and time to 50% at four years. BTOS provides a broad lower bound rather than a code-specific measurement [S3]. Adoption should be faster in creative, sales-support and billing teams, but the benefit is limited by smaller operators, disparate CMS and traffic systems, and the fact that most wage cost in a real plant includes field work that AI does not automate. Programmatic OOH is a related automation path, but it is a transaction technology, not proof that the whole operator has adopted AI [S5].

## Consolidation and economics

There is real, current buyer activity. Lamar completed more than 50 outdoor-asset acquisitions for $191.1 million in 2025 and acquired Verde Outdoor for $147.6 million; the filing identifies site locations, structures, contracts and leases as acquired assets [S6]. That supports one documented active consolidator and a credible exit path for quality assets. The multiple benchmark must be handled carefully: Billboard Insider reports 10–12x standardized billboard cash flow for well-maintained plants with a reasonable remaining lease portfolio [S4], while its Clear Channel analysis discusses 12.5–14x billboard cash flow [S7]. Those are cash-flow, not EBITDA, measures. I use 12.0x buy and 13.0x exit as transparent EBITDA proxies with a wide range, not as claimed observed EBITDA comps.

## Terminal demand

The terminal is durable because AI cannot replicate a permitted display location, although media demand can still cycle and formats can lose share. OOH revenue grew for a nineteenth consecutive quarter through 2025, while traditional formats also posted gains and DOOH increased 10.5% [S2]. Lamar’s 2025 acquisitions and its allocation of consideration to site locations and advertising structures reinforce that scarcity has continuing economic value [S6]. A target with short leases, weak permits, poor locations or transit-management contracts is not protected by the industry result; terminal durability must be verified asset by asset.

## Risks and uncertainty

The critical diligence risks are lease duration, permit status, structure condition, local demand, customer concentration, digital conversion capital and which portion of revenue is a physical plant rather than design or transit management. The deterministic target count uses a generic services margin and may undercount mature billboard plants; it is not changed here. The public multiple sources use standardized billboard cash flow rather than EBITDA, and OAAA statistics cover the full OOH medium including large public operators [S2, S4]. Owner age and succession evidence for independents is weak in reopened public sources, so those inputs remain low-confidence estimates.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The supplied 4-digit 5418 workforce omits much installation, print, maintenance, permitting and lease work while overstating agency/PR roles.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Programmatic transaction growth is not counted as a V input; it is addressed in pi_dist and terminal discussion.
- inputs.pi_dist: No reopened source reports the platform take rate or AI savings pass-through for independent 541850 operators.
- inputs.pi_moat: Moat is asset-specific: short leases, non-transferable permits and poor locations can sharply reduce it.
- inputs.t50_years: Neither source is a 541850 workflow-adoption survey.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: BTOS is all-business evidence and Place Exchange is a selected platform, not the full code.
- inputs.historical_analogs: The source covers one platform’s patterns and must not be read as all-industry adoption.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: S4 discusses plant valuation and owner-operated sales expense, not a representative owner-age survey.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: Not documented is not evidence of no target-level succession need.
- inputs.active_consolidators: Count is deliberately limited to an active acquirer documented in a reopened primary filing, not a census of all buyers.
- inputs.buy_mult: S4 and S7 report billboard cash flow, not EBITDA; accounting definitions, lease treatment and sales-cost normalization can make the proxy materially wrong.
- inputs.exit_mult: The source multiple is billboard cash flow, not EBITDA. Exit premium is not used in capture or terminal scoring.
- inputs.pricing_structure: This describes billboard plants; 541850 also includes indoor, transit and display-design activities.
