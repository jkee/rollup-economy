# 541830 — Media Buying Agencies

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312f1_541830` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

Media Buying Agencies have an unusually clear AI efficiency story but a fragile lower-middle-market terminal-demand story. NAICS 541830 is specifically the purchase of advertising time or space from media outlets for resale [S1], not creative advertising or selling-side representation. Platform automation and programmatic execution make execution cheaper, yet those same tools shift bargaining power toward platforms and in-house teams. This is a conditional, diligence-heavy acquisition thesis rather than a straightforward AI roll-up.

## How AI changes the work

Planning, audience research, trafficking, pacing, optimization, reconciliation and reporting are increasingly automated. The IAB Europe survey reports that agencies bought more than half of display budgets programmatically in 2024, with the agency figure at 68% [S2]. IAB's US revenue report also describes AI moving from automation toward models that make autonomous campaign decisions and manage campaigns end to end [S3]. Senior negotiation, cross-platform measurement, vendor relationships and client strategy remain important but are a smaller operational moat than routine execution.

## Value capture

Commission or percentage-of-spend pricing can preserve labor savings better than an hourly-service model, but it also exposes the operator to fee-audit, transparency and self-service pressure. ANA's K2 study found pervasive non-transparent practices and cash rebates across the US media-buying ecosystem [S4]; that supports the need to normalize profit and not treat opaque principal economics as durable savings capture. The selected distribution fraction credits initial margin retention, not unbounded mark-up economics.

## Adoption timing

The 68% agency-side display-programmatic figure exceeds the scorecard threshold, so t50 is zero under the frozen rule [S2]. It is a European, display-focused automation proxy rather than a US NAICS-541830 AI census, and programmatic buying predates generative AI. Still, it is strong evidence that automated execution is already operationally normal enough that a later 50% adoption date would be misleading.

## Consolidation and economics

The target universe is thin in the supplied data, and large independents can dominate the statistics. Marketing Dive reports Empower and Ocean Media merged into a $1.5B-billings independent group, combining resources and buying power while maintaining operations [S5]. That demonstrates strategic scale benefits but is not a comp for a $1-10M EBITDA target. Commercial valuation guidance explicitly values media buyers on EBITDA on fees/net revenue rather than gross media spend [S6], making revenue-quality diligence non-negotiable.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is pressured rather than melting. Brands still need cross-platform allocation, attribution, data governance, negotiation and accountable advice, and US internet ad revenue reached $258.6B in 2024 [S3]. But the independent operator's historic execution layer is being automated by programmatic systems and platforms. The likely survivor is a smaller, data-capable advisory/buying specialist; a generalist whose offer is merely placing media is vulnerable to direct platform tools and fee compression.

## Risks and uncertainty

The supplied 4-digit 5418 occupation mix contains obvious PR, design, promoter and display-role contamination, so the role estimate is explicitly adjusted and carries a large residual. The programmatic statistic is European and measures automation, not US firm-level AI adoption. The multiple source is commercial guidance rather than a disclosed transaction database, and reported receipts can include media pass-through. Diligence must reconcile gross billings to fee revenue, disclose principal/rebate economics, test client concentration and establish whether vendor relationships transfer.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Role mix is 4-digit 5418, not pure 541830, and includes obvious non-media-buying occupations.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The programmatic statistic is Europe-wide and not a direct US firm AI census.
- inputs.pi_dist: Opaque principal economics and client auditing can materially lower realized distribution retention.
- inputs.pi_moat: The consolidation example is vastly larger than lower-middle-market targets.
- inputs.t50_years: This is a Europe-wide display automation proxy, not a US NAICS-specific AI adoption measure.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: The observed measure is European and display-specific; it is not a US 541830 establishment statistic or a pure generative-AI measure.
- inputs.historical_analogs: Analogy does not predict target-level revenue retention.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: No NAICS-specific owner-age study was found in reopened sources.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: This is not proof that no individual target has a succession issue.
- inputs.active_consolidators: Large-scale buyer activity does not prove appetite for every small target.
- inputs.buy_mult: S6 is commercial guidance rather than a disclosed transaction database; EBITDA must be on net fee revenue, not gross billings.
- inputs.exit_mult: Exit value depends on normalization of pass-through and principal/rebate economics.
- inputs.pricing_structure: Reported receipts and EBITDA can be distorted by gross pass-through and rebates.
