# 541618 — Other Management Consulting Services

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312f1_541618` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

541618 is a residual code: it excludes general, HR, marketing and logistics consulting, while expressly including telecommunications and utilities management consulting [S1]. That boundary makes a generic code-level underwriting thesis weak. I use telecom expense management (TEM), utility-management and niche specialty advisory as working subsegments, but do not claim a measured dominant revenue cluster. The attractive contingency-fee TEM slice can retain a share of savings; the same analytical product is also highly susceptible to client self-service AI. With an economically anomalous supplied labor share of 1.3991, this is a low-confidence, pressured terminal-demand screen rather than a platform thesis.

## How AI changes the work

Management analysts gather information, analyze data, develop solutions and prepare reports, while also interviewing people, consulting and maintaining relationships [S2]. In a TEM or utility-audit workflow, invoice ingestion, anomaly detection, benchmarking and draft reports are the most automatable pieces; carrier negotiation, disputed-charge resolution and client accountability remain less so. The supplied 4-digit 5416 role mix is especially weak here because this residual code can contain niches with very different labor and technology intensity.

## Value capture

TEM providers use several fee models. A live provider's pricing description lists contingency arrangements that take 20-50% of recovered savings, as well as fixed-hourly, fixed-license and fixed-scope models [S6]. Contingency work can preserve economics when the provider continues to originate verified savings, but it also makes AI substitution more existential: if the client runs the analysis internally, there may be no fee pool. I therefore give distribution credit only for the contingency/managed component and assign a modest moat because the source is a vendor description, not a code-wide mix survey.

## Adoption timing

Professional services are reported at about 33% firm AI adoption in the BTOS synthesis [S3], while Census reports less than 20% AI use among firms with four or fewer employees in its 2025-26 collection [S4]. The supplied dataset says the great majority of 541618 firms are under five employees, so I use 20% current adoption and four years to 50% effective adoption. This deliberately treats broad-sector adoption as an upper anchor and does not use the small target count, owner age or consolidation to accelerate A.

## Consolidation and economics

The supplied estimate has only 256 firms in the $1-10M EBITDA band, so buyability is not the same as an abundant platform funnel. Professional-services lower-middle-market ranges are 2.5x-4.5x for $1-3M EBITDA and 4.0x-6.0x for $3-10M, with utilization and key-person dependence named drivers [S5]. I use 3.25x entry and 3.75x exit only as a cautious de-risking case within those broad ranges. The micro tier also has a different sale metric: BizBuySell reports an average 2.52 cash-flow multiple for service businesses in 2025 [S7], which is not interchangeable with EBITDA and reinforces the need to normalize owner compensation.

## Terminal demand

The code's telecom and utilities inclusion gives a plausible human-survival channel: ongoing representation, exception handling and carrier or utility negotiation. But the core savings-discovery product is informational, and TEM contingency pricing explicitly pays for billing-error and cost savings [S6]. Mature AI in a client's hands could remove rather than merely cheapen that product. I classify terminal demand as PRESSURED, not MELTING, because specialty representation and managed recurring work may persist, but the code-level evidence cannot establish their prevalence.

## Risks and uncertainty

This is the most uncertain packet in the pair. Official NAICS establishes the residual boundary but not revenue shares [S1]; the selected TEM cluster is a hypothesis, not a demonstrated dominant subsegment. The 1.3991 supplied labor share is economically implausible and likely reflects a cross-vintage/classification mismatch, so V should be treated as a sensitivity rather than a literal labor-cost forecast. National owner-transition evidence and broad professional-services multiple data are disclosed proxies. Diligence must identify the actual subsegment, fee basis, contract recurrence, data assets, client self-service capability and normalized EBITDA before buying.
