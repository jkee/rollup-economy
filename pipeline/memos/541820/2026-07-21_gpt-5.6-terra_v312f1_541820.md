# 541820 — Public Relations Agencies

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312f1_541820` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

Public Relations Agencies remain a real lower-middle-market acquisition universe, but the investable product is narrowing. The official NAICS definition covers campaigns, lobbying, political consulting and PR consulting [S1]; those services share a need for senior judgment and trust, while the production layer is rapidly assisted by AI. A buyer can harvest meaningful internal efficiency, but should underwrite a pressured terminal demand profile rather than a broad, durable retainer book.

## How AI changes the work

AI is compressing drafting, monitoring, research, media-list preparation, repurposing and reporting, not the whole client outcome. Muck Rack survey reporting says 75% of PR professionals had incorporated AI into their work by 2025 [S2], while USC documents agencies and corporate teams using AI to streamline daily work and appointing AI leadership [S3]. Crisis counsel, executive access, relationship management and accountable advice remain human-intensive, so the role estimate does not treat the profession as fully replaceable.

## Value capture

AI savings initially repair unbilled over-service and improve account-team utilization. Retainer pricing can let an agency retain part of that saving, but clients see staffing and deliverables and can reset scope at renewal. The selected distribution and moat fractions are deliberately moderate: media relationships and specialization protect a portion of price, whereas generic release, content and reporting work faces visible deflation. This is a capture judgment, not a claim that retained hours establish terminal demand.

## Adoption timing

The 75% professional-use survey measure is above the scorecard's 50% effective-adoption threshold, so t50 is zero by the frozen rule [S2]. It is not a NAICS 541820 establishment census and does not prove every workflow is automated. The high usage reading nevertheless makes waiting for a distant adoption inflection implausible; implementation quality and client policy, rather than access to tools, are the limiting variables.

## Consolidation and economics

PRovoke tracked 80 PR-sector deals in 2025 and reported a tilt toward larger, integrated sellers; it also identifies PE-backed buyers and data/technology capability as a value driver [S5]. That supports active strategic interest but is not proof that a $1-10M EBITDA generalist agency has the same demand. Founder-linked books remain central: a PR-agency transaction guide describes 60-70% cash at close and 30-40% earnouts tied to client and senior-publicist retention [S4], making headline consideration a weak proxy for immediately transferable EBITDA.

## Terminal demand

Paid demand should survive in specialist, regulated, crisis and high-stakes corporate communications, but the broad generalist product is pressured. Muck Rack's survey reporting highlights media relations as the leading job function and respondents cite low journalist response rates and shrinking newsrooms as obstacles [S2]. That is a non-AI headwind layered on AI's removal of routine production. The likely endpoint is fewer paid senior-counsel hours per account, not simply the same retainer delivered at a lower cost.

## Risks and uncertainty

The deterministic role mix is a 4-digit 5418 proxy that includes advertising and media-representation occupations, so the role model uses a large explicitly uncertain residual. No NAICS-specific owner-age statistic or private lower-middle-market PR multiple database was available in the reopened sources. The multiple is a broad marketing-agency proxy and the ownership, consolidator and capture estimates should be diligence items, especially client concentration, founder dependence, earnout mechanics, renewal language and specialty mix.
