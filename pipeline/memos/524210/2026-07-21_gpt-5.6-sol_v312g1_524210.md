# 524210 — Insurance Agencies and Brokerages

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-sol_v312g1_524210` · gpt-5.6-sol · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

Insurance agencies are unusually fertile for AI-enabled operating improvement but unusually unforgiving on purchase price. Service, quoting, policy checking, renewal preparation and commission reconciliation are automatable, while carrier-paid renewal commissions and commercial-line complexity help the operator retain savings [S2][S5]. The problem is that buyers already pay roughly low-double-digit EBITDA multiples for ordinary firms and about 14x for platforms [S8][S9]. This is therefore a durable operating thesis with weak conventional multiple arbitrage: attractive only when underwriting a commercial-heavy book, measurable service-labor removal and a price below headline market averages.

## How AI changes the work

The near-term prize is not replacing licensed producers; it is shrinking the manual work around them. The 2026 ACT report identifies data entry, quote comparison, proposal assembly, policy checking, contract review and commission reconciliation as highly automatable, while keeping complex-risk judgment, carrier advocacy and client trust with humans [S2]. That supports high fractions for processing, clerical and customer-service roles, moderate fractions for producers, underwriters and adjusters, and a substantial but mixed residual. State producer licensing still requires a human or licensed entity to sell, solicit or negotiate insurance [S4], so the producer role changes toward review and advice rather than disappearing.

## Value capture

Core agency revenue is paid by carriers as fixed percentages of premium on new and renewal policies, with contingent compensation tied to production, retention, growth and profitability [S5]. Because the customer does not usually buy agency labor by the hour, removing internal service cost need not immediately lower revenue; that makes distribution capture relatively strong. Leakage remains material: carriers control commission schedules, direct and embedded channels can bypass the agency, and buyers may pay away the benefit through elevated entry valuations. Licensing and human advice support terminal demand, but they are not counted twice as capture; the capture score rests on commission mechanics, renewal retention and the agency's ability to keep operating savings.

## Adoption timing

AI interest is ahead of operational deployment. ACT reported that 68% of surveyed agencies were somewhat or very likely to increase AI use within twelve months, but only 8% were using it regularly and strategically; disconnected systems, missing processes, security and governance slow the move from experiments to effective workflow replacement [S2]. A three-year estimate to 50% effective adoption is therefore faster than legacy core-system replacement but slower than consumer-LLM use. Carrier APIs and AI features embedded in agency-management systems are the likely transmission mechanism, with commercial-data quality and compliance review setting the pace.

## Consolidation and economics

The market is already crowded. OPTIS counted 695 announced North American agent-and-broker deals in 2025; its seven most active named buyers completed between 22 and 69 deals each, and PE or hybrid buyers represented 73% of transactions [S7]. MarshBerry's 2024 database put the average upfront price for all firms at 11.22x EBITDA and platform firms at 14.02x; its 2026 discussion says private platforms have held near 14x base value while public broker medians fell toward 12x [S8][S9]. I therefore use 11.5x entry and 14.0x exit as transparent estimates, not promises. The spread is thin before integration cost, earnouts and leverage, and both benchmarks skew larger than a $1–10M EBITDA target.

## Terminal demand

Paid demand is durable for a commercial-heavy independent agency, though not invulnerable. The Big I reported that independent agencies placed 87.7% of U.S. commercial-lines premium and 62% of all P&C premium in 2025 [S3], and the ACT report says customers still strongly prefer agent help for purchase and policy management [S2]. Complex carrier appetites, renewals and licensed advice survive mature AI. Personal lines and standardized small-commercial risks face greater direct, embedded and AI-native pressure, so a buyer should underwrite the retained commercial mix rather than assume the entire six-digit code is equally protected.

## Risks and uncertainty

The largest uncertainties are valuation, line-of-business mix and the gap between AI experimentation and actual headcount removal. The exact Census scope includes any establishment acting as agent or broker in selling annuities or insurance policies [S1], while the best technology and demographic surveys focus mainly on independent P&C agencies. The supplied occupation mix is at five digits and includes MGA or delegated-authority roles that may not appear in a retail target. The owner-age proxy is from a broad independent-agency survey, and headline M&A multiples mix roll-ins, stand-alone firms and platforms. Any deal should separately verify book retention, carrier concentration, producer ownership of relationships, normalized contingent commissions and whether quoted EBITDA already reflects outsourced service labor.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The 52421 occupation mix includes MGAs, wholesale brokers and delegated-authority work as well as retail agencies; target books can differ materially.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Fractions describe technically replaceable tasks at mature deployment, not current adoption or immediate headcount reduction.
- inputs.pi_dist: Carrier commission schedules and contingent income are controlled upstream and can change; fee-based large-commercial accounts expose cost more directly.
- inputs.pi_moat: Commercial-heavy agencies have more moat than personal-lines and standardized small-commercial books; licensing is not treated as an exclusive channel franchise.
- inputs.t50_years: The survey measures agency-reported usage rather than verified labor substitution, and target sophistication varies sharply by size.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: National Big I member survey; regular strategic use is a stricter definition than any AI use and does not prove headcount removal.
- inputs.historical_analogs: E-signature is a simpler, lower-liability tool than AI-assisted policy advice or autonomous workflow execution.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: More than 450 principals and owners were surveyed; it is not a Census estimate and may not match the $1–10M EBITDA cohort.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: A documented channel-wide challenge does not mean every target lacks an internal successor.
- inputs.active_consolidators: North American announced-deal data covers insurance distribution broadly and includes transactions outside exact NAICS 524210 or the target EBITDA band.
- inputs.buy_mult: MarshBerry mixes roll-ins, stand-alone firms and platforms and skews larger than many $1–10M EBITDA targets; earnouts and contingent consideration are excluded.
- inputs.exit_mult: Exit requires genuine platform scale, organic growth, retention and infrastructure; a collection of small books may still price as a roll-in, and 2026 public comparables had fallen toward 12x.
- inputs.pricing_structure: The Hanover is one carrier example and does not establish a universal commission percentage.
