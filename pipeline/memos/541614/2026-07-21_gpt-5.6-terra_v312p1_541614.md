# 541614 — Process, Physical Distribution, and Logistics Consulting Services

## Executive view

Logistics consulting offers meaningful AI leverage in analysis, documentation and back-office work, but the investment case depends on commercial structure. Gainshare, proprietary benchmark data and implementation-heavy engagements can retain savings; generic project analysis faces fee compression and client bypass through AI-enabled supply-chain software.

## How AI changes the work

Network modeling, inventory analysis, freight-rate review, forecasting, report drafting and clerical audit work are increasingly machine-assisted. Site observation, facility implementation, stakeholder interviews, operational change and accountability during WMS or TMS deployment remain human-intensive. The role estimates therefore automate task bundles rather than whole engagements.

## Value capture

Consulting supports time-and-materials, fixed-fee and outcome/value-based structures [S3]. AI savings flow most directly to an operator under gainshare or a well-scoped fixed fee; hours-based work and competitively rebid studies pass savings to clients. Proprietary data and recurring platform-enabled delivery can improve retention, but Equiteq's buyer evidence shows those features must be demonstrated rather than presumed [S4].

## Adoption timing

The 2025 MHI-Deloitte survey reports AI in use at 28% of responding supply-chain organizations and a five-year expectation of 82% [S1]. That is client-side rather than consultant-specific evidence, but it indicates adoption below the 50% boundary today and a plausible crossing within a few years. CSCMP's 2026 report confirms that core-workflow use remains uneven [S2].

## Consolidation and economics

The deterministic target pool is real but heterogeneous, and it unusually includes some 500-plus-employee, low-margin firms. Equiteq describes sustained buyer conviction for specialized, data-enabled and platform-like consultancies, while broad generalists face pressure [S4]. Entry and exit multiples are therefore low-confidence estimates conditional on owner independence, vertical depth and recurring or gainshare economics.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is PRESSURED rather than melting. Physical warehouse implementation, field diagnosis, change management and executive accountability remain paid services, especially when disruption makes execution urgent. Pure network-design, benchmarking and forecast studies are more vulnerable because clients can access similar analysis through their own software and AI-equipped teams.

## Risks and uncertainty

No source isolates US NAICS 541614 by fee model, owner age, adoption or transaction multiple. Supply-chain surveys represent buyers and operators, professional-services pricing evidence spans many consulting types, and M&A evidence covers specialty consulting broadly. The four-digit occupational proxy also likely understates logisticians, industrial engineers and field personnel, so implementation-heavy targets may be less automatable than the central case.
