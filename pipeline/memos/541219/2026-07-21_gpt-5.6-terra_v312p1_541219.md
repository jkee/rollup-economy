## Executive view

Payroll processing has abundant AI/RPA task exposure, but local bureaus face scale platforms and embedded fintech. Regulatory liability and money movement preserve an intermediary, though often the platform rather than the acquirable bureau [S1].

## How AI changes the work

Payroll platforms are investing in generative AI and automate routine compliance, calculation and client workflows [S1]. Human exception handling, tax notices and client relationships remain; the supplied CPA-heavy occupation mix omits payroll clerks and is a material proxy caveat.

## Value capture

PEPM recurring pricing can retain automation gains, but ADP’s recurring-revenue platform, long retention and client-funds capabilities show the scale advantage local bureaus face [S1]. Competitive platforms can pass savings through, so capture is moderate rather than assumed.

## Adoption timing

Platform payroll is already digital and ADP reports generative-AI investment [S1]. Effective automation is estimated above 50%, so t50 is zero, although independent bureaus may lag the platforms.

## Consolidation and economics

The deterministic screen has 297 target-band firms, but small bureaus face fixed compliance costs and channel dependence. Active platform consolidation is not used as AI-adoption evidence and multiples remain low-confidence.

## Terminal demand

Paid payroll intermediation is durable because multi-state remittance, custody, reporting-agent responsibility and exception handling remain costly. The local bureau is pressured by platform oligopoly, which is distinct from the survival of payroll processing.

## Risks and uncertainty

Labor share is unusually high and compares different vintages; revenue can net client funds. The supplied 5412 mix is not payroll-specific. No reopened independent-bureau price or transaction census supports precise capture or multiples.
