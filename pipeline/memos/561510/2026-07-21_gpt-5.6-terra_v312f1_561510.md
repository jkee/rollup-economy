# 561510 — Travel Agencies

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312f1_561510` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

The $1–10M EBITDA population is best understood as mid-size managed-travel companies, host agencies, and specialist leisure agencies rather than solo advisors or OTAs. AI can take meaningful work out of itinerary search, booking, service, and back office, but the buyer is acquiring a commission- and relationship-dependent intermediary. Consumer use of AI in travel is already broad [S3], so the operational saving is real but the terminal risk is that the AI interface becomes the customer’s booking intermediary. I therefore see a useful but fragile operational-improvement thesis, not a clean durable roll-up.

## How AI changes the work

BLS describes the core travel-agent workflow as collecting preferences, finding fare and schedule information, calculating costs, booking travel, and making alternative arrangements after changes [S2]. Those are unusually text-, search-, and workflow-heavy tasks. AI should remove much of the transactional work, including first-pass itinerary research, routine booking support, document handling, and communications. It is less substitutive for advisor judgment on complex/luxury/group trips, supplier relationships, exception handling, and the trust required during disrupted travel. The 4-digit role mix also includes OTA/TMC technology labor that is not a clean proxy for the lower-middle-market target population.

## Value capture

I assign only moderate retention of savings. On a commission or service-fee booking that remains with the agency, fewer fulfillment hours should improve the operator’s margin. But BLS notes that agent earnings often depend on commissions and service fees [S2], and the industry’s core classification is agency intermediation rather than principal travel supply [S1]. Consumer AI use for travel planning and booking is rising [S3], creating a credible route for suppliers, platforms, or customers to compress that intermediation. This is distinct from task automation: it affects who keeps the saving and what price the agency can charge, not the fraction of labor tasks AI can perform.

## Adoption timing

The adoption evidence is directional rather than a code-level survey of the $1–10M EBITDA band. Phocuswright-reported consumer usage reached 56% for planning, booking, or in-destination help, while a separate Phocuswright-cited observation says 32% of travel companies were not using agentic AI [S3, S4]. That supports a roughly 3.5-year estimate to 50% effective agency adoption: tools are available and customer expectations are shifting, but integration into GDS, supplier, payment, duty-of-care, and service workflows will be slower than consumer experimentation. This estimate deliberately does not use M&A activity or advisor demographics as adoption evidence.

## Consolidation and economics

Consolidation exists, especially in managed travel: Direct Travel acquired ATPI in 2025 to combine global corporate, leisure, events, and specialty capabilities [S7]. However, the relevant $1–10M EBITDA target is far smaller and more heterogeneous than that transaction. Business Travel News reported TMC sellers seeking 5–7x EBITDA versus a historical 3–5x range [S5], while Travel Weekly described an average TMC at 4–8x profits and a much larger Amex GBT transaction near 10x projected EBITDA [S6]. I use a conservative 4.5x entry and 5.5x exit rather than treating mega-TMC pricing as an achievable lower-middle-market outcome. The owner-age input is deliberately low-confidence because no code-specific ownership-age source was found.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is PRESSURED. The Census definition limits this code to acting as an agent selling travel, tour, and accommodation services, excluding tour operators and other reservation services [S1]. BLS still expects gradual demand for personalized advice and handling travel issues, but explicitly says online research and self-booking can limit demand [S2]. Complex corporate compliance, negotiated programs, and high-touch leisure niches can survive, but they do not eliminate the risk that a capable consumer or corporate AI booking layer disintermediates the paid agency role. That demand risk is kept separate from the margin benefit of automating the agency’s own fulfillment work.

## Risks and uncertainty

The score has material boundary and measurement limits. NAICS 561510 combines leisure agencies, hosts, TMCs, and platform-like firms; gross bookings versus net commission revenue makes margins and multiples difficult to compare. The supplied W-2 labor base excludes much independent-advisor work, while the supplied role mix includes technology labor more typical of larger platforms. TravelAge West’s 2023 survey found 36% of surveyed advisors sold travel part time [S8], illustrating fragmented labor economics but not establishing owner age or target-band succession. The multiple range is commentary-led and exact deal terms are rarely disclosed. These uncertainties support MED overall confidence and a conservative terminal classification.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The 4-digit 5615 role mix includes OTA/TMC platform roles that are imperfect proxies for the $1–10M EBITDA agency population.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Independent advisors and host-agency contractors may sit outside the supplied W-2 wage base.
- inputs.pi_dist: Commission and service-fee economics vary sharply by leisure, corporate, host, and specialty model.
- inputs.pi_moat: This is a retained-savings moat judgment, not a terminal-demand score.
- inputs.t50_years: The sources cover travelers and broad travel companies, not a representative survey of 561510 lower-middle-market employers.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: Not a directly observed code-level adoption rate.
- inputs.historical_analogs: BLS occupation-level evidence does not isolate target-band firm economics.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: No code-specific owner-age source was located; the value is not observed and should not be treated as a statistic.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: Absence of located evidence is not evidence that succession stress is absent.
- inputs.active_consolidators: Evidence is weighted toward larger TMCs and does not establish buyer depth for every agency niche.
- inputs.buy_mult: The cited evidence is business-travel/TMC commentary, not disclosed transactions for small host or leisure agencies; normalize owner compensation and gross-versus-net revenue first.
- inputs.exit_mult: Requires transferable client relationships and normalized EBITDA; it is not applicable to an owner-dependent or commission-book-only agency.
- inputs.pricing_structure: Gross booking and net revenue bases are not comparable across agency disclosures.
