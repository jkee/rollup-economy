# 621340 — Offices of Physical, Occupational and Speech Therapists, and Audiologists

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-sol_v312g1_621340` · gpt-5.6-sol · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

This is a durable-demand, modest-AI-capture thesis rather than a clinical-labor replacement thesis. NAICS 621340 covers independent PT, OT, speech and audiology practices, including practices operating in another facility, while skilled nursing delivered in the home is outside this code [S1]. The investable center is multi-site outpatient rehabilitation. AI can remove a meaningful share of scheduling, eligibility, coding, denial-management and documentation work, but licensed therapist time is usually the paid service. A disciplined buyer can capture back-office savings and throughput, yet recurring payer pressure and weak exclusivity limit retention. Buy at about 6.5x normalized EBITDA and underwrite a 10.0x scaled-platform exit only as a bounded estimate supported by current lower-middle-market and platform ranges [S8][S9], not as guaranteed expansion.

## How AI changes the work

The clean replacement surface is administrative: intake, scheduling, insurance verification, prior authorization, claims follow-up, coding assistance and routine patient communication. AI scribes can also draft notes and compliance checks, but in the licensed roles this primarily returns unbilled time or creates visit capacity rather than eliminating the therapist who performs the billable intervention. WebPT's 2025 industry report identifies documentation, billing, compliance and front-desk workflows as the main AI opportunities [S4]. Digital MSK platforms are a separate substitution channel: Hinge describes AI-guided exercise delivered with licensed clinicians and says digital MSK remains early, so low-acuity exercise episodes face pressure while manual therapy, complex assessment and relationship-intensive care remain human [S5].

## Value capture

Capture is mixed. Medicare therapy remains fee-schedule based, assistant services carrying CQ/CO modifiers are paid at 85% of the otherwise applicable amount, and multiple-procedure payment reduction applies to later same-day therapy services [S2]. The 2026 physician fee schedule raised conversion factors partly through a one-year statutory increase while also applying an efficiency adjustment to certain non-time-based services [S3]; that illustrates annual rate-setting rather than a permanent technology rent. Operators can retain direct overhead savings and use reclaimed documentation time for more visits where demand and therapist capacity allow, but payer contracting, utilization controls and referral concentration share the benefit. Clinic reputation and referral relationships create some local moat, not durable pricing power.

## Adoption timing

Adoption is underway but not yet pervasive enough to call 50% effective use across the relevant workflows. WebPT reports increasing interest and concrete use cases, while Hinge characterizes the broader digital MSK market as early [S4][S5]. I estimate current effective adoption at 20% and four years to 50%, with wide ranges because vendor surveys measure interest or use of particular tools rather than verified, workflow-weighted labor displacement. Integration, HIPAA review, clinician trust and uneven small-practice systems slow deployment; packaged EMR-integrated scribes, RCM tools and scheduling automation accelerate it.

## Consolidation and economics

The target universe is large in the supplied dataset, but the relevant 821-firm EBITDA-band estimate spans 100-499 and 500-plus employee classes and multiple therapy specialties. Current evidence shows several active consolidators: USPH continued buying multi-clinic practices in 2025 [S6], Arete launched with two acquisitions [S12], and PT Solutions acquired OrthoCarolina's therapy branch [S13]; Provident's market update also records ongoing sector transactions [S8]. USPH's partnership model typically leaves founders with material equity and buys remaining interests at the same EBITDA multiple as the original purchase [S7], highlighting both retention alignment and limited automatic multiple arbitrage. I use four visibly active consolidators, a 6.5x buy and 10.0x exit, with owner-comp normalization and referral concentration as core diligence items.

## Terminal demand

Terminal paid demand is durable. The Census scope is independent rehabilitation and audiology practice, not a generic wellness application [S1]. AI cannot perform hands-on examination, manual treatment or the accountable licensed encounter; even Hinge's technology-led model retains licensed physical therapists and launched an in-person provider network [S5]. Digital MSK can divert uncomplicated exercise-led episodes, and payers can reduce authorized visits, so some volume is pressured. The base case nevertheless retains paid human care for post-surgical, neurologic, pediatric, balance, complex musculoskeletal, speech, occupational and hearing needs. This supports DURABLE terminal demand while keeping capture and substitution risk separate.

## Risks and uncertainty

The largest uncertainties are adoption speed, how much documentation time converts into removable labor rather than capacity, and valuation transfer from a clinic group to a scaled platform. The code is heterogeneous: outpatient orthopedic PT, pediatric speech, hand therapy, recreational therapy and audiology have different billing and technology exposure [S1]. OEWS role weights are at 62134 rather than the exact six-digit code, and labor share combines 2024 wages with 2022 receipts. Multiples are especially uncertain: public USPH traded at 17.6x in Provident's Q4 2024 snapshot [S8], whereas the current advisory range for lower-middle-market PT groups is 5.5x-8.0x and a scaled platform 10.0x-14.0x [S9]. The selected 6.5x/10.0x pair deliberately sits inside, not above, those ranges.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Therapist documentation time may convert to capacity or reduced burnout rather than headcount removal.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The 24.53% residual mixes billing, audiology, clinical-adjacent and other roles; its fraction is necessarily broad.
- inputs.pi_dist: Payer mix and contract cycle vary materially by clinic; 2026's statutory conversion-factor increase is not evidence of permanent pricing power.
- inputs.pi_moat: Referral relationships may be personal to clinicians and can weaken after a transaction.
- inputs.t50_years: Available surveys report interest or product use, not verified effective adoption across the 621340 workforce.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: Vendor respondents and digital-MSK customers are unlikely to represent the full six-digit industry.
- inputs.historical_analogs: Teletherapy and digital MSK change delivery channels but remain human-supported, so they are imperfect automation analogs.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: Broad employer-business owner-age proxy, not six-digit therapy-practice ownership data.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: Absence of documented shortage is not evidence that individual practices lack succession risk.
- inputs.active_consolidators: The count is a conservative observable floor, not a complete buyer census, and some transactions span adjacent rehabilitation services.
- inputs.buy_mult: Advisory range rather than a complete closed-deal tape; normalize owner-clinician compensation, rollover, earnouts and referral concentration.
- inputs.exit_mult: Public-company valuation is not directly comparable to a private exit, and USPH's same-multiple partner buyouts show that expansion is not automatic.
- inputs.pricing_structure: Payer and specialty mix vary sharply; audiology retail economics are not well represented by a therapy-unit model.
