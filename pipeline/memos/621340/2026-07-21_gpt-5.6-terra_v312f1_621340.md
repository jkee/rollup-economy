# 621340 — Offices of Physical, Occupational and Speech Therapists, and Audiologists

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312f1_621340` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

This code is a credible outpatient-rehabilitation consolidation market but a difficult AI-retention thesis. The clinic product is licensed, hands-on therapist time; AI documentation and revenue-cycle tools can free non-billable time, but replacing clinical minutes can remove the billable unit rather than create a wage saving. CMS's 85% payment rule for qualifying PTA/OTA services is a concrete reminder that payer rules, not AI, already shape economics [S1]. The practical case is a conditional, management-and-density roll-up with modest AI-enabled throughput rather than a clinical labor-replacement play.

## How AI changes the work

The clean AI surface is scheduling, eligibility verification, prior-authorisation paperwork, claims/denials, intake and documentation support. AI scribes can reclaim therapist note-writing time and potentially add visits if capacity and demand exist; they do not make manual therapy, clinical judgment or licensed accountability disappear. PT, OT, speech and assistant roles are therefore assigned low task-replaceability fractions, while front office and selected management are high. WebPT's 2025 report describes increasing AI interest aimed at reducing operational friction and administrative burden, not clinical replacement [S2].

## Value capture

AI value is hard to retain because payers buy services through fee schedules, contracts, authorisation rules and visit limits. CMS requires reduced payment for qualifying PTA/OTA-furnished services [S1], illustrating a structural payer capture channel independent of AI. A clinic can retain some documentation-driven throughput if it has referral demand, therapist capacity and payer authorization, but the public evidence does not prove a stable price uplift. I therefore use modest capture inputs and put payer capture in C only; the human clinical floor supports terminal demand, not price retention.

## Adoption timing

Effective adoption should become common within about four years, led by documentation, revenue-cycle and workflow products. WebPT's survey release says AI adoption is increasing and respondents see technology as a way to reduce administrative burden [S2]. Current effective adoption is estimated at 20%, not inferred from vendor marketing. The timing estimate is deliberately separate from the attractive consolidation evidence and from therapist-labor scarcity.

## Consolidation and economics

There is clear scale activity: Provident's PT review says fragmentation, supply-demand imbalance and consolidator competition are catalyzing investment and reports historical private PT transactions at 10.4x-16.1x EBITDA for $12M-$175M EBITDA targets [S4]. Those are platform-scale historical comparables, not valid purchase quotes for a $1-10M EBITDA clinic. The 4.5x entry is deliberately well below those historical platform indications, while the 10.0x exit assumes verified multi-site scale and payer diversification. Both are bounded estimates, not observed band-size prints; actual underwriting needs local payer mix, normalized owner-therapist compensation, referral concentration and clinic-level cohort data.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is DURABLE but pressured. Licensed hands-on rehabilitation, referral relationships and in-person clinical judgment remain paid needs; AI cannot itself deliver manual treatment. Conversely, digital MSK programs compete for some low-acuity utilization: Hinge's company-sponsored, validated claims analysis reported lower physical or occupational therapy utilization alongside other cost reductions [S5]. That supports a bounded substitution risk, not a claim that all outpatient therapy melts. It is kept distinct from capture and from V.

## Risks and uncertainty

The code is heterogeneous: orthopaedic PT, pediatric speech, OT, audiology with hearing-aid economics, and mixed clinic groups have different reimbursement and labor economics. The supplied band includes both 100-499 and 500+ employer classes, so it is not a direct map to a single local clinic. APTA reported a 9.5% national vacancy rate in outpatient PT practices in 2024 and roughly 13% of PT/PTA positions open [S3]; this supports labor scarcity but not owner-age succession. The main open questions are payer mix, local referral durability, documentation-to-visit conversion, and whether an exit buyer will credit scale without discounting reimbursement exposure.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Role mix is supplied at five-digit 62134 rather than six-digit 621340.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Clinical-task fractions exclude a claim that AI replaces billable hands-on treatment.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Audiology and other residual subsegments may have different exposure.
- inputs.pi_dist: CMS Medicare mechanics are a direct payer channel but do not describe every commercial or Medicaid contract.
- inputs.pi_moat: Sector consolidation evidence does not prove moat for a clinic whose referrals are physician-personal or payer-concentrated.
- inputs.t50_years: Vendor survey language indicates direction, not a representative effective-adoption series.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: No reopened source provides a code-specific percentage of clinics using AI effectively.
- inputs.historical_analogs: The source is a vendor survey release and not a causal clinical-productivity study.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: Workforce age and owner age are different populations.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: Do not convert therapist vacancy evidence into owner-succession evidence.
- inputs.active_consolidators: Platform count does not establish active acquisition appetite for every local specialty or payer mix.
- inputs.buy_mult: S4 comparables are materially larger and mostly historical, so this is a conservative bounded estimate rather than an observed band-size transaction multiple. Verify local transaction data and normalize owner-therapist pay.
- inputs.exit_mult: The cited transactions had $12M-$175M EBITDA and cannot support a precise multiple for a smaller clinic group.
- inputs.pricing_structure: CMS is not a full description of commercial, Medicaid, school or audiology economics.
