# 812210 — Funeral Homes and Funeral Services

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-sol_v312g1_812210` · gpt-5.6-sol · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

Funeral homes have durable need for licensed, physical and compassionate service, but terminal revenue is pressured by cremation and unbundling rather than by AI. The exact Census scope covers preparing the dead and conducting funerals, including wake facilities, transport and merchandise [S1]; stand-alone cemeteries and crematories sit outside 812210. AI can reduce case administration, communications and obituary drafting, yet most core delivery remains human. The acquisition case is therefore a conventional local-trust and succession roll-up with a modest administrative AI benefit, offset by a long-run shift toward lower-ticket cremation and direct disposition.

## How AI changes the work

AI can draft obituaries, organize case data, triage calls, schedule staff, prepare routine correspondence and support pricing or pre-need administration. An NFDA supplier article documents native AI-assisted obituary workflows but also requires human editing and warns about invented details [S12]. Funeral directors still coordinate transport, preparation, ceremonies, merchandise and legal documentation [S5]. Embalming, removals, crematory operation, service direction and family counsel are physical, regulated or relational. The supplied 4-digit role mix includes cemetery grounds labor, so that occupation is treated as sibling-code contamination rather than a funeral-home automation opportunity.

## Value capture

Families pay funeral providers directly and the FTC Funeral Rule requires itemized General Price Lists and consumer choice [S2], so there is no insurer-style payer automatically reclaiming cost savings. Local reputation, embedded pre-need relationships and the need for guidance support retention: even among online planners, nearly 48% still needed a funeral director [S4]. But capture is not complete. Itemization and digital search make prices more comparable, and the 2025 projected cremation rate of 63.4% materially shifts mix toward lower-ticket offerings [S3]. AI savings are likely retained at first, while competitive transparency and mix deflation limit the moat.

## Adoption timing

Digital interaction is already meaningful—nearly 30% of surveyed families completed all arrangements online—yet that is not the same as effective AI labor replacement [S4]. AI obituary tools exist [S12], but the most valuable tasks require licensed review, judgment and compassionate interaction. Current effective adoption is estimated below 50%, with five years to broad use across the addressable administrative task set. Small independent operators, state-specific workflows and reputational risk should keep deployment slower than generic office software.

## Consolidation and economics

At least four active consolidators are directly evidenced: SCI acquired 22 funeral locations in 2025 [S6], Park Lawn completed repeated 2025–2026 acquisitions [S8], Carriage acquired eight funeral homes and a cremation business in 2025 [S9], and Foundation Partners has fresh institutional capital and more than 250 locations [S10]. Johnson Consulting states that average-to-larger funeral homes trade around 6–7× EBITDA, with higher values when prized real estate is included [S7]. A 6.5× buy and 7.0× exit use that same range, with the small exit step reserved for a diversified, professionally managed portfolio. Pre-need liabilities, owner labor, real-estate rent and trust earnings must be normalized before applying either multiple.

## Terminal demand

Paid demand survives AI because death still requires disposition, transport, paperwork and often ceremonies, and many consumers value professional guidance [S4]. Revenue quality is nevertheless pressured. NFDA projects cremation at 63.4% in 2025 and 82.3% by 2045, while 2023 median prices were $8,300 for viewing and burial versus $6,280 for a funeral with cremation [S3]. SCI's comparable cremation rate reached 64.4% in 2025 even as average revenue per service rose to $5,823 [S6], showing that pricing can partly offset mix but does not remove the structural risk. Terminal class is therefore pressured, not melting.

## Risks and uncertainty

The dominant risks are cremation mix, direct-cremation bypass, online price transparency, licensing and ownership rules, and quality-of-earnings treatment of pre-need contracts. SCI's $17.0 billion pre-need backlog illustrates both locked future volume and significant delivery obligations [S6]. The owner-age estimate is cross-industry and the n-band dataset likely understates qualifying targets because its generic margin is below healthy funeral-home margins. The 6–7× multiple source dates from 2022 and includes a real-estate caveat, so current target-specific bids require fresh local comps. These uncertainties justify medium confidence despite strong evidence on category demand and active buyers.

- inputs.active_consolidators: This is a verified lower bound and excludes smaller regional groups, family offices and buyers without public disclosures.
- inputs.buy_mult: The primary multiple evidence dates from 2022 and says prized firms may be higher when real estate is included.
- inputs.buy_mult: BizBuySell reports seller-discretionary-earnings multiples for much smaller deals and is not directly comparable to EBITDA.

- inputs.exit_mult: The exit premium requires reduced owner dependence and durable local volume; cremation mix or loss of the selling family's goodwill can erase it.

- inputs.pricing_structure: SCI combines funeral, cemetery and cremation activities and is a scaled-company proxy; exact 812210 targets may not own cemeteries or crematories.
