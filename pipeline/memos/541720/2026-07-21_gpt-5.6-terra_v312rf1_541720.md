## Executive view

This is a heterogeneous, low-confidence Conditional screen rather than a roll-up thesis. AI can materially compress research preparation, coding, analysis and reporting, but much of the likely dominant sponsored-research book is effort-priced or cost-reimbursable, so the client captures a substantial share of efficiency. The code also mixes program evaluation and survey work with cultural-resource management (CRM) and behavioral programs; those businesses have different demand, buyers and economics [S1][S3][S4].

## How AI changes the work

AAPOR reports that AI is being used across questionnaire design, interviewing, data processing, analysis and reporting, while also distinguishing synthetic outputs from human research participants [S2]. That maps to substantial assistance for literature review, survey drafting, coding, statistical work, documentation and clerical operations. It does not replace study design accountability, sponsor interpretation, physical fieldwork, human-delivered behavioral services or professional responsibility for archaeological work.

## Value capture

Federal cost-reimbursement contracts pay allowable incurred costs up to an estimated-cost ceiling, so labor-hour reduction commonly reduces the cost base rather than creating a like-for-like supplier margin windfall [S1]. Fixed-price CRM projects can retain more of the saving, but the code-level revenue mix is not observed. Contract vehicles, past performance and qualified staff create some relationship protection, while sponsors can use AI internally for generic analysis; this is why capture is deliberately low rather than treating statutory fieldwork as a capture moat.

## Adoption timing

AAPOR's 2026 responsible-AI report says adoption is accelerating and identifies use across the survey-research lifecycle [S2]. It does not publish a representative adoption percentage for US lower-middle-market NAICS 541720 firms, so the 25% current-use selection and three-year t50 are explicit estimates rather than reported statistics. The central case assumes task-level tooling spreads before firms materially redesign delivery and pricing.

## Consolidation and economics

The deterministic dataset identifies 258 firms in the estimated $1-10M EBITDA band, but it does not tell us whether they are for-profit, founder-dependent, nonprofit-affiliated, or exposed to the same end markets. ACRA confirms that CRM firms are a real, firm-based subsegment, but it does not establish an owner-age or buyer-count statistic for this NAICS code [S5]. Entry and exit multiples are therefore conservative, unsupported-by-a-code-specific transaction database estimates and should not be used to underwrite a deal.

## Terminal demand

Terminal value is PRESSURED. National Park Service standards require qualified archaeological professionals with education and field experience for archaeological activities, preserving a physical and credentialed component of CRM demand [S4]. Conversely, survey/evaluation analysis is exposed to AI-enabled substitution and to volatile federal demand: the Department of Education OIG reports 129 contract terminations totaling $1.3 billion in early 2025, including educational-study contracts [S3]. Neither mechanism proves the revenue-weighted mix, so the terminal classification is cautious.

## Risks and uncertainty

The principal risk is misclassification: NAICS 541720 combines federal and foundation research contractors, CRM archaeology and behavioral-program organizations, while market research is elsewhere. The occupational mix is a five-digit proxy and may include roles not representative of a target firm. There is no fresh code-specific fee-model, owner-age, consolidator, or EBITDA-multiple dataset in this packet; all such selections are plainly low-confidence estimates with wide ranges. A target-level diligence pass should segment revenue, contract type, sponsor concentration, owner dependence and field-labor exposure before treating the screen as actionable.
