# 561492 — Court Reporting and Stenotype Services

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-sol_v312g1_561492` · gpt-5.6-sol · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

Court reporting is a pressured but not disappearing paid service. The exact NAICS scope is verbatim reporting and stenotype recording of live legal proceedings, transcription of recorded material, and real-time live captioning—not general document transcription or prerecorded captioning [S1]. AI and digital capture can compress draft transcription, scoping, proofreading, scheduling and production coordination. Yet the purchased output remains an accurate, certified record with chain-of-custody and jurisdiction-specific officer requirements. The investment case is therefore cost compression inside a durable legal workflow, offset by digital entrants, reporter disintermediation and price pass-through.

## How AI changes the work

The production stack is splitting into capture, machine draft, human review and certification. AAERT-sponsored research describes digital capture and ASR-assisted transcription as tools that reduce cost and turnaround time, and reports that accuracy is the primary selection criterion for 96% of surveyed users [S3]. Digital reporters are trained and certified as accountable professionals rather than being unattended recorders [S4]. AI is strongest in rough drafts, speaker-aided cleanup, scheduling, exhibit handling, QA triage and routine delivery; it is weaker where realtime accuracy, oath administration, contested readback, confidentiality and final certification matter.

## Value capture

Per-page pricing creates a real window for retained savings because automation can lower production cost without immediately changing the billed page. Federal examples price ordinary originals at $4.40 per page and daily originals at $7.30, with distinct copy rates [S5]. The private deposition market also charges for attendance and turnaround, but the exact mix varies by market. Capture is not assured: digital-first competitors can bid lower, law-firm procurement can demand shorter turnaround at the same price, and freelance reporters may retain key client relationships. The operator's moat rests on scheduling reliability, certified coverage, multi-jurisdiction compliance, security and preferred-vendor relationships—not on transcription labor alone.

## Adoption timing

Digital reporting is already an accepted operating model in parts of the system. Florida's court standards direct digital reporting alone for several proceeding types while reserving stenography for capital and certain circuit-criminal work [S8]. Texas still requires certification to engage in shorthand reporting [S6], and California requires a deposition officer to certify the transcript or recording [S7]. I estimate 30% effective adoption of digital/AI-enabled production today and four years to 50%; the range is wide because state rules, realtime requirements, buyer acceptance and the difference between digital capture and autonomous AI all slow a uniform national transition.

## Consolidation and economics

This is a visibly consolidating but tiny target universe. Veritext publicly lists numerous regional court-reporting acquisitions, including a dense 2024 cadence [S9]. A sector M&A adviser lists 2025 acquisitions by Veritext, Magna, Lexitas and Circle City and describes roughly a dozen additional small unannounced deals [S11]; Magna's PE owner separately describes it as an acquisition platform [S12]. Sector valuation guidance updated with 2025 market data places firms over $1 million EBITDA at roughly 4-6x [S10]. I use 4.5x entry and 5.5x exit, but no disclosed recent court-reporting deal supplied a verifiable transaction multiple, so both are bounded estimates rather than observed comps.

## Terminal demand

Paid demand survives, but the human product becomes thinner. Texas requires certified shorthand reporters [S6]; California requires an accountable deposition officer to certify the record [S7]; Florida demonstrates that digital reporting can satisfy the system for many proceedings while still imposing professional standards and retaining stenography for high-stakes matters [S8]. These mechanisms preserve paid capture, monitoring, certification and custody, not necessarily stenographic keystrokes or hours of manual transcription. Agencies that own reliable scheduling and compliance can remain valuable; agencies whose economics depend on marking up freelance pages without workflow differentiation face pressure.

## Risks and uncertainty

The deterministic role mix is an especially severe mismatch: it is 4-digit 5614, is dominated by contact-center occupations, and omits court reporters entirely. NCRA data also show 46% of reporter members are freelancers [S2], so W-2 labor share omits much of the production cost that AI would first affect. The AAERT report is sponsor-aligned toward digital reporting [S3], while NCRA represents stenographic professionals [S2]; their directional overlap on aging supply is more reliable than either side's product claims. Exact digital share, agency-owner age and transaction multiples are not publicly reported. Captioning/CART and deposition reporting also have different demand and adoption paths.
