# 561492 — Court Reporting and Stenotype Services

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312f1_561492` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

This is a conditional, heterogeneous roll-up candidate rather than a clean AI-cost-takeout. NAICS 561492 covers verbatim reporting, stenotype recording of live legal proceedings and subsequent transcription; it also includes realtime captioning [S1]. AI and digital reporting can compress transcription, scheduling and QA cost, but a paid human record-capture, certification and liability function remains in important settings. The attractive targets are agencies with transferable law-firm relationships, a durable reporter roster and a credible digital workflow; a thin freelancer network or one-off client book can lose the very assets a buyer believes it is acquiring.

## How AI changes the work

AI speech-to-text can create a fast first draft, and digital reporting already separates capture from transcription. AAERT's certifications explicitly cover trained electronic and deposition reporters who digitally capture proceedings and monitor audio in real time [S2]. That is meaningful substitution for stenographic production and post-production labor, not proof that the human role disappears: audio quality, speaker management, oath/certification rules, chain of custody, realtime service and legal accountability still require a qualified person. The supplied 4-digit 5614 role mix is badly unrepresentative because it omits court reporters, so V is necessarily a conservative proxy judgment.

## Value capture

Agencies may retain part of the savings through scheduling, workflow, bundled videography and client-service infrastructure, but page-rate and competitive procurement limit the spread. Federal-court examples show per-page pricing and separate rates by turnaround and copy type [S3], illustrating that a production-cost reduction need not automatically raise agency revenue. The durable agency advantage is preferred-vendor access, reliable capture and delivery, not ownership of the underlying speech-recognition technology. I therefore assign moderate distribution and moat factors rather than treating certification as a complete pricing shield.

## Adoption timing

Effective adoption reaches 50% of eligible workflow in about four years from a current 15% base. That reflects widespread digital-capture capability and AI drafting, offset by state-level variation, high-stakes accuracy requirements, reporter acceptance and the difference between a useful draft and a legally defensible record. The source evidence establishes a real digital reporting pathway [S2], but it does not measure AI penetration among 561492 agencies; both current adoption and timing are estimates and must not be read as an industry survey result.

## Consolidation and economics

Buyability is genuine but uneven. The court-reporting advisory source lists 2025 acquisitions by Veritext, Magna and Lexitas and describes a long-running consolidation dynamic [S5]. An independent 2026 valuation guide places $500K-$2M EBITDA regional court-reporting firms at 4.5x-6.5x adjusted EBITDA and larger multi-market platforms at 7x-10x [S4]. The dataset's $1-10M band straddles those categories, so I use 5.0x entry and 5.5x exit only for a quality regional operator that adds a transferable roster and recurring law-firm relationships. Those are source-informed estimates, not disclosed terms for a particular NAICS transaction.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is PRESSURED, not melting. The code's paid output is a verbatim legal record and related live reporting, not merely generic transcription [S1]. AAERT describes digital reporters as a scalable response to reporter shortages while retaining real-time capture, protocols and accuracy responsibilities [S2]. The human production layer can narrow to capture, verification and certification as AI handles more text, and lower-value captioning/transcription may commoditize. However, the residual legal-record and high-stakes realtime service should preserve paid demand in a substantial subset of the market.

## Risks and uncertainty

The dominant risks are scope and asset-transfer risk. The deterministic wage mix is a 4-digit contact-center proxy with no court-reporter SOC, while much agency production may be paid to 1099 reporters outside W-2 wages; the labor-share input therefore understates and misclassifies economics. NAICS also includes realtime captioning, whose demand and pricing may differ from deposition reporting. The valuation range is a commercial guide rather than disclosed target transactions, and each target requires diligence on reporter agreements, client concentration, state rules, WIP/receivables, digital workflow and the share of revenue that remains after AI-assisted transcription.
