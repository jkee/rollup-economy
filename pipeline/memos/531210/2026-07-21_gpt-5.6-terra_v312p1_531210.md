# 531210 — Offices of Real Estate Agents and Brokers

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312p1_531210` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

This is a weak AI-value acquisition despite meaningful workflow automation. The supplied wage base is small relative to gross commissions, while agent splits are contractual pass-throughs rather than controllable payroll. AI can make staff and agents more productive, but brokerages have fragile retention of both agents and leads, and post-settlement compensation remains in flux [S1].

## How AI changes the work

AI is already material in listing copy, marketing, research, follow-up and transaction administration: 41% of surveyed REALTORS® reported using AI/generative AI, but 46% reported no noticeable business impact [S1]. It can compress clerical and drafting work, not eliminate licensed negotiation, supervision, local judgment or trust in a high-stakes transaction. The supplied 3-digit role mix is contaminated by property-management and lessor jobs, so its physical-work shares are deliberately assigned low automation.

## Value capture

A brokerage retains only its company dollar after paying agent splits; productivity in an agent's personal workflow does not automatically enlarge that retained share. Technology can support pricing and service, but digital lead sources and MLS/portal channels limit office-level bargaining power. Consequently savings are likely shared with agents, spent on lead generation, or competed away; pi_dist and pi_moat are both deliberately low rather than treating commission pass-through as labor savings [S1].

## Adoption timing

Current reported AI use is 41%, below the 50% effective-adoption boundary, and the survey's substantial neutral-impact share argues against calling adoption complete [S1]. A three-year estimate to 50% effective adoption allows tools to diffuse through brokerage systems and compliance workflows, while recognizing that individual agent experimentation is already widespread.

## Consolidation and economics

The deterministic screen identifies only 259 firms in the indicated EBITDA band and warns that firm counts are dominated by very small shells. Acquisition economics are therefore thin and heterogeneous: agent books can move, owner compensation complicates EBITDA normalization, and retained-company-dollar economics cannot be compared with gross commission revenue. We estimate a handful of active strategic consolidators but do not use that fact as AI-adoption evidence.

## Terminal demand

Paid representation is pressured, not extinguished. NAR's 2025 buyer/seller survey reports 88% of buyers and 91% of sellers using an agent or broker, chiefly for negotiation, explanation, marketing and pricing [S2]. Those services survive AI, but AI reduces the information and paperwork component and makes a lower-fee licensed-execution model more plausible; this terminal-demand conclusion is separate from capture.

## Risks and uncertainty

The key uncertainty is the mismatch between the 531 proxy occupation mix and a brokerage's actual payroll, compounded by rapidly changing compensation practices. NAR sources represent member respondents, not all brokerages, and current AI use is not the same as effective operating adoption. The model also cannot infer a durable brokerage moat from consumer use of agents; portable agents, portals and franchise arrangements can absorb the value.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The supplied 3-digit 531 mix is materially contaminated by lessors and property managers, not brokerage offices.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Independent-contractor agent commissions are outside W-2 payroll and not included as automatable labor.
- inputs.pi_dist: Commission terms and post-settlement repricing vary by market and brokerage.
- inputs.pi_moat: NAR member evidence measures agent use, not ownership of lead flow or retention of an acquired office.
- inputs.t50_years: Survey use is self-reported and does not establish automation of a brokerage's payroll.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: NAR REALTOR respondent scope is not identical to NAICS 531210 firms.
