# 531210 — Offices of Real Estate Agents and Brokers

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-sol_v312g1_531210` · gpt-5.6-sol · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

A residential brokerage can automate transaction coordination, listing preparation, marketing, bookkeeping and first-pass pricing work, but it is a weak pure AI-labor roll-up. The supplied wage base is only 18.26% of receipts and the largest economic outflow is commission paid to independent-contractor agents, not employee labor. Compass paid 81.1% of stand-alone brokerage revenue as commissions and related expense in early 2026 [S6], and Real's standard model lets agents retain 85% until a cap and 100% thereafter [S7]. AI can make agents more productive without creating much acquirer-owned margin. Buyability exists, but durable value depends on recruiting, lead flow and ancillary services rather than office-cost removal.

## How AI changes the work

NAR's 2025 survey found 41% of REALTORS using AI or generative AI, with 20% using AI daily and 22% weekly [S2]. Listing copy, comparative market analysis, marketing assets, scheduling, form preparation and transaction coordination are already exposed. The human remainder is pricing judgment under local conditions, negotiation, trust, exception handling and licensed accountability. The supplied 3-digit occupation mix is badly contaminated by lessors and property managers, so maintenance, janitorial and property-management roles receive bounded task judgments but are not presented as the actual structure of a 531210 brokerage.

## Value capture

Most automation value initially accrues to the individual agent or upstream platform rather than the acquired brokerage. Compass describes its agents as independent contractors and paid $5.68 billion of $6.96 billion 2025 revenue as commissions and related expense [S6]; Real explicitly offers an 85/15 split followed by 100% to the agent after the annual cap [S7]. A brokerage can retain savings from employee transaction coordinators, office support and management, but those are a small base. It also faces competition through higher splits and recruiting incentives. The NAR settlement made compensation more explicit and negotiable [S4], though observed buyer-agent rates have so far remained sticky [S5].

## Adoption timing

Tool use is already close to the halfway threshold: 41% reported using AI or generative AI, while 42% used it daily or weekly [S2]. That is individual-agent usage, not verified brokerage-wide labor replacement, so current effective adoption is set at 41% with a caveat and time to 50% at 1.5 years. Adoption is likely to be fast because agents buy consumer tools directly, yet operator-level integration into compliance, transaction and accounting systems will lag. The main gating factors are brokerage policy, data quality, fair-housing risk, MLS permissions and whether tools become reliable enough to remove staff rather than merely accelerate drafts.

## Consolidation and economics

There is visible consolidation but no insurance-style deep roll-up market. Compass completed Anywhere and had already acquired other brokerages [S6]; Rocket completed Redfin in 2025 [S10]; Real announced an $880 million RE/MAX transaction in 2026 [S9]. For entry, Axial reports a recently closed $2.0 million EBITDA real-estate-broker transaction at 4.01x, inside the target band [S8]. For exit, the Real-RE/MAX filing states 7x fully synergized 2025 EBITDA, while the transaction also includes a global franchise system and mortgage brand [S9]. I use 4.0x buy and 7.0x exit as transparent estimates, with a very wide range because business mix, agent retention, brand and company-dollar basis dominate value.

## Terminal demand

Full-commission intermediation is pressured, not yet melting. California requires a license before acting as a broker or salesperson [S11], and consumers still value local judgment and negotiation in a high-stakes transaction. But licenses do not require consumers to buy a traditional full-service package, and AI plus portals can unbundle discovery, pricing and paperwork. Post-settlement data show buyer-agent commissions rebounded to roughly pre-change levels in 2025, including 2.52% below $500,000 and 2.34% for $500,000–$999,999 homes [S5]. That supports near-term durability, while explicit negotiation and low-fee licensed wrappers remain credible long-term pressure.

## Risks and uncertainty

The central measurement problem is scope. Census defines 531210 as agents and brokers selling, buying or renting real estate for others [S1], but the deterministic OEWS mix is 3-digit 531 and contains large blocks of maintenance, property management, janitorial and rental-clerk labor from sibling codes. NAR sources cover members rather than brokerage owners, Axial's real-estate-broker category may not perfectly isolate residential 531210, and public transactions include franchising, title, mortgage or portal assets. Gross commission income, company dollar and EBITDA must never be mixed. A buyer must underwrite agent retention, split resets, owner production, lead ownership, franchise royalty obligations and ancillary-service earnings separately.

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The 3-digit 531 role mix is not representative of exact 531210; maintenance, janitorial, rental and property-management roles largely belong to sibling codes.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Most real estate agents are independent contractors, so automation of agent tasks is not equivalent to reducing the supplied W-2 wage base or improving brokerage EBITDA.
- inputs.pi_dist: Compass and Real are large public models, not a direct measure of every private brokerage; savings from automating 1099 agent work are not operator labor savings.
- inputs.pi_moat: A dominant local brand with company-owned leads can be materially stronger; an owner-dependent or agent-portable book can be weaker.
- inputs.t50_years: NAR measures individual REALTOR use, not verified brokerage process coverage or labor replacement.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: The survey had a 2.5% response rate and is member-level rather than firm-level; tool use is a broad proxy for effective adoption.
- inputs.historical_analogs: Generative AI may unbundle advice and discovery more deeply than prior workflow tools, so the analog may understate terminal pressure.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct: NAR members include sales agents, associate brokers and nonowners; member age cannot be treated as direct owner age.
- inputs.owners_60plus_pct.succession_shortage_documented: Firm-level succession planning must be diligenced; member demographics alone do not prove a channel-wide shortage.
- inputs.active_consolidators: These are large strategic transactions, not proof of a deep, repeatable buyer pool for independent $1–10M EBITDA brokerages.
- inputs.buy_mult: Axial's category may include business mixes beyond exact residential 531210; the single comp is anonymized, and EBITDA must be normalized after owner production and agent splits.
- inputs.exit_mult: REMAX includes global franchise and mortgage assets, brand value and $30M expected synergies; it is not a clean independent-brokerage comp, and Axial's observed range is extremely wide.
- inputs.pricing_structure: Owned brokerage, franchise, cap, desk-fee, team and ancillary-service models have different revenue bases; compare valuations only on normalized retained EBITDA.
