# 541350 — Building Inspection Services

## Executive view

Building inspection is a modest AI-efficiency acquisition, not an AI replacement thesis. AI can reduce narrative, photo-labeling, scheduling and follow-up work, but the paid product remains a physical examination and a human written opinion. The 40-firm modeled EBITDA band is especially thin and founder/realtor relationships are often personal; buy only a multi-inspector firm with retained referral channels and credible normalized earnings [S1][S4].

## How AI changes the work

ASHI defines a home inspection around visual examination of accessible installed systems and components, followed by a written report. That makes report drafting and administrative workflow the exposed layer, while site access, observation, testing and professional judgment remain human work. The supplied 5413 role mix is engineering-heavy rather than a faithful 541350 mix, so the residual is used to express the inspector-dominant real industry rather than pretending the parent mix measures it [S1][S2].

## Value capture

Fees are generally a per-inspection service price rather than an hourly report-writing tariff, so a trusted local operator can retain some savings from faster report production. Capture is constrained by referral-driven local competition: standardized reports and readily available AI tools make the efficiency portable, and buyers can comparison-shop. The selected distribution and moat fractions deliberately exclude the physical human floor; that supports terminal demand, not capture [S1][S2].

## Adoption timing

AI-assisted reporting is already commercially available, but broad effective deployment includes reliable field capture, templates, quality assurance and liability-safe review. I estimate current effective adoption below 50%, then a two-year path to half of operators using it on material workflows. This is an estimate rather than a survey of 541350 firms; the cited products establish capability, not penetration [S2].

## Consolidation and economics

The supplied band count is only 40, making a conventional broad roll-up difficult. Franchise systems and platforms can create repeatable workflows, but the central transferable asset is a staffed local brand and realtor/consumer funnel rather than a solo inspector's personal book. Generic service-business sold multiples provide a cautious valuation anchor, not an inspection-specific comp; they are reported as SDE/cash-flow multiples and are materially below a clean EBITDA-market benchmark [S4].

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is DURABLE but transaction-sensitive. ASHI requires the inspector to examine accessible systems and produce a written report, and licensing/certification requirements vary by state; AI cannot itself make the site visit. Yet demand is derivative of housing transactions and buyers sometimes waive inspection contingencies: NAR reported 25% waiving in May 2025, while existing-home sales were depressed. This supports durable paid demand with cyclicality, not immunity [S1][S3].

## Risks and uncertainty

The largest uncertainty is the severe 4-digit role-mix mismatch: construction inspectors are only 2.05% of supplied parent wages despite being the signature occupation here. Other material risks are transaction volumes, waiver behavior, personal referral concentration, state-by-state rules, and whether report-time is nearer one-fifth or one-third of inspector work. Generic service multiples are a broad proxy and should not set a purchase price without local comps and SDE-to-EBITDA normalization [S3][S4].
