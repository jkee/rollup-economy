# 561422 — Telemarketing Bureaus and Other Contact Centers

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-sol_v312g1_561422` · gpt-5.6-sol · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

This is a technically exposed but economically awkward AI target. NAICS 561422 is specifically outsourced communication performed for clients, not the client's in-house contact center and not telephone answering services [S1]. Routine calls, chats, summaries, routing and quality review are increasingly automatable, but the operator usually sells the labor-linked unit that AI removes. Current CX contract research says more than 70% of primary contracts remain unit-based or time-and-materials while only 5% are outcome/gainshare [S5]. The base case is therefore high replaceable labor, weak retained savings and melting terminal demand for undifferentiated human-seat providers.

## How AI changes the work

Customer-service AI has moved quickly from experiment toward deployment: a 2026 global survey reports 66% of service organizations use agentic AI, though that is a broad, vendor-sponsored measure rather than a 561422 statistic [S3]. Voice remains less mature: Gartner's 2024 survey found 5% deployed and 11% piloting customer-facing GenAI voicebots [S2]. The likely operating model is autonomous handling of routine intents and outbound scripts, AI assistance for complex contacts, automated summaries and QA, and a smaller human tier for exceptions, empathy, regulated disclosures and difficult collections. Cisco's forecast of 68% agentic handling by 2028 supports the direction but also finds strong demand for retained human connection [S4].

## Value capture

The central problem is commercial, not technical. Legacy contracts monetize minutes, hours, seats, calls or transactions, so fewer human interactions can mean fewer billed units. HFS reports 36% unit-based and 36% time-and-materials primary CX contracts, versus only 5% outcome/gainshare [S5]. Large operators can redesign around outcomes—Concentrix now markets an outcome-based managed service [S6]—but that capability is itself evidence that buyers can demand the savings. A subscale bureau without proprietary data, integrations or regulated workflow depth should expect substantial pass-through and direct competition from CCaaS and AI vendors.

## Adoption timing

Effective replacement is estimated at 30% today, below the much broader 66% organizational-use statistic because tool adoption includes agent assist, routing and internal workflows that do not remove a seat [S3]. Voice deployment data started much lower in 2024 [S2], while three-year interaction forecasts are aggressive [S4]. A 2.5-year path to 50% effective adoption is therefore plausible, with a 1.5-4 year range driven by data integration, accuracy, consent and sector-specific compliance. The estimate applies to outsourced workflows; in-house buyer adoption can bypass the bureau and may move on a different clock.

## Consolidation and economics

There is an active strategic market, but public precedents are much larger and more diversified than the target band. A 2025 SEC-filed transaction table shows four recent CX transactions with distinct buyers and a 6.8x median adjusted-EBITDA multiple, down from a 10.2x pre-AI median [S7]. Concentrix described its Webhelp price as about 9.5x anticipated EBITDA and 7.6x after expected synergies, a large-platform ceiling rather than a small-bureau comp [S8]. Generic small-deal evidence clusters around 5.5-5.6x, with somewhat higher multiples above that tier [S9]. I use 5.5x buy and 6.8x exit, but client concentration, offshore exposure and AI-driven volume loss could erase the modeled spread.

## Terminal demand

Undifferentiated outsourced human conversation is a melting product because the buyer owns the brand, customer data and reason for contact, while the bureau performs communications for that buyer [S1]. Mature AI can perform routine interactions directly; the surviving paid work is escalations, sensitive or regulated interactions, complex retention and human-supervised AI operations. Cisco's research supports a human residual even under high automation [S4], but that residual should carry lower total volume and intense price competition. Terminal demand could be preserved only if the operator becomes an outcome-owning, integrated workflow provider rather than remaining a seat vendor.

## Risks and uncertainty

The largest evidence mismatch is population: adoption surveys cover global customer-service organizations, including in-house centers, whereas NAICS 561422 covers U.S. establishments communicating for others [S1-S4]. The supplied occupation mix is a 4-digit 5614 proxy that includes sibling-code artifacts and understates frontline agents at the 6-digit industry. Ownership age is a generic employer-business proxy [S10], and the multiple evidence mixes giant strategic deals with generic small transactions [S7-S9]. Offshore delivery is outside U.S. NAICS data, so observed domestic contraction can reflect offshoring as well as AI. These limitations make exact timing, capture and entry valuation more uncertain than the direction of task automation.
