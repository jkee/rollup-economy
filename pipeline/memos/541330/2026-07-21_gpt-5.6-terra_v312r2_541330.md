# 541330 — Engineering Services

*v3.1.2 research memo · run `2026-07-21_gpt-5.6-terra_v312r2_541330` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

Engineering Services is a credible but qualified AI value-migration target. Repetitive production, proposal, document-control, scheduling and review work can be compressed, while professional judgment, responsible charge and site-facing work remain human. The investable question is capture: public cost-accounting and competitive fixed-fee work can return part of the efficiency to clients. I assign only a modest capture moat, based on firm-specific data/workflow integration and client differentiation rather than licensure or accountable review. I would prefer a diversified, second-tier-led firm with recurring private/on-call relationships over a principal-dependent shop. [S1][S2][S4]

## How AI changes the work

AI is already being used across operations and technical disciplines, including proposal writing, document search, code-compliance review, generative design and inspection support [S1]. It should first reduce hours in drafting, data reduction, report assembly, submittals, RFI handling, scheduling and internal knowledge retrieval. It does not replace the engineer of record, liability-bearing review, client/agency negotiation, physical observation or the judgment required to validate a model in a project-specific context. The consequence is capacity expansion before headcount elimination, consistent with surveyed firms expecting additional output at stable staffing. [S1]

## Value capture

I assign only partial distribution retention because government A/E work is exposed to negotiated indirect-cost rates and reimbursable-cost procedures: FAR explicitly uses cost principles for A/E contracts and indirect-rate negotiation [S2], while FHWA maintains AASHTO audit guidance for consultant indirect-cost rates [S3]. This does not prove every public contract claws back AI savings, nor quantify public exposure; it does establish a real pass-through channel. For the remaining lump-sum and relationship-led work, ACEC describes firms using proprietary data, AI integrated with business systems, and faster or higher-value delivery to differentiate with clients [S1]. Those workflow/data integration and client-value channels are independent, limited evidence for retaining part of savings or making a client switch less attractive, but not evidence that licensure, human review, backlog or terminal demand protects price. I therefore use a modest 0.40 moat, with competitive bids and broadly available tools still limiting retention. [S1][S2][S3]

## Adoption timing

Current effective adoption is below the 50% threshold used here. ACEC reports 29% of surveyed member firms already had an AI strategy and another 34% were developing one in January 2025; strategy is a deliberately conservative proxy for operational deployment, not proof that a majority of tasks are transformed [S1]. Its companion evidence describes use as uneven and technical use as materially behind marketing/sales. I therefore use four years to 50% effective adoption, inside the report's three-to-five-year expectation window, rather than treating enthusiasm as realized automation. [S1]

## Consolidation and economics

Buyability is real: ACEC's 2025 review identified 13 member firms that made multiple acquisitions in a May–September window, with eight of the 13 PE-backed, and documented active demand in transportation, site-development, MEP and water [S4]. Valuation evidence is less clean than the deal pace: a 2026 buy-side guide reports 3.5–5.5x EBITDA for $1–3M EBITDA firms and 6–9x for platform-grade $3M-plus firms, but it aggregates A/E and relies partly on published survey anchors and advisor data [S6]. I use a conservative 4.5x buy and 6.0x exit contingent on normalized EBITDA after market-rate principal pay, not SDE or owner bonuses. [S6][S7]

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is DURABLE rather than immune. Construction-related A/E contracts remain subject to cost principles, and buyers specifically diligence who seals work, registrations and transferable prequalifications [S2][S6]. Those requirements preserve demand for accountable firms and senior licensed judgment. Yet the billable production layer is pressured: clients can demand faster delivery and lower fees, and productivity gains may shrink a time-and-materials base. The durable asset is trusted responsibility and integrated delivery—not a permanent claim on every drawing hour. Licensure and human review are treated here as paid-demand mechanisms, not as evidence of savings retention.

## Risks and uncertainty

This is a heterogeneous NAICS: civil/transportation, MEP, water, structural, energy and field-heavy specialties differ sharply in public exposure, field content and pricing. ACEC/BST samples are member-oriented and include broader engineering/design services, so neither the strategy percentage nor task expectations is a NAICS-541330 LMM census [S1]. The ownership-age input is an informed AEC proxy because the accessible Zweig release describes transition benchmarking but does not publish a 60-plus percentage [S5]. Multiples are also sensitive to backlog, principal dependence, client mix and whether reported earnings are SDE, pre-bonus profit or normalized EBITDA. The moat estimate is particularly uncertain because the ACEC evidence describes differentiation and integrated data/workflows, not a measured retained-savings or switching-rate result. [S1][S5][S6]
