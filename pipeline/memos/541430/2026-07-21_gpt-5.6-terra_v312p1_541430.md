## Executive view

Graphic Design Services is a high-AI-exposure, poor-roll-up screen rather than a consolidation thesis. The frozen labor model is broad and materially contaminated by sibling interior-design employment, but even a conservative reading leaves much of the actual layout, image, copy-adjacent and production workflow exposed. Graphic designers themselves report the highest daily AI use among the creative roles in Adobe's June 2026 study (40.9%), and BLS explicitly says automated design tools may reduce demand for freelance designers [S1][S2].

## How AI changes the work

Generative tools compress briefing, moodboarding, concept variants, image manipulation, layout iteration and production adaptation. BLS describes the core work as creating visual concepts, layouts, logos and final production using software, with client revisions and error review [S2]. The surviving work is more likely to be client interpretation, choice among alternatives, brand-system stewardship, approval management and accountability; it is not a reason to assume the original production hours remain billable.

## Value capture

There is a narrow service layer around trusted client relationships, proprietary brand systems and senior art direction, but it is weakly portable in the small-firm population. Upwork's marketplace evidence shows worldwide graphic-design rates around $15–$35 per hour and frequent fixed-price work, indicating transparent comparison and price pressure rather than a protected, recurring asset base [S3]. A portfolio and client trust can matter, but neither is a durable code-wide moat when customers can generate a first draft or source freelancers globally.

## Adoption timing

I estimate effective adoption has already crossed 50%, so t50 is set to zero under the frozen rule. That is an inference, not a code-specific census: Adobe surveyed nearly 2,000 creatives, reports 38% daily use overall, 40.9% daily use among graphic designers, and a large pragmatic group that is mostly weekly users [S1]. Adoption is uneven by task and buyer, but the commercial workflow has already crossed the screen's 50% timing boundary; the remaining uncertainty concerns depth of substitution and price pass-through, not first use.

## Consolidation and economics

The supplied band contains 370 firms but the underlying firm distribution is overwhelmingly below five employees. That makes founder/key-person dependence, customer concentration and individual portfolios central diligence risks. The available independent-creative-agency transaction report is a broad, upper-market comparator, not a 541430 lower-middle-market comp; it says traditional service models command lower values than technology-driven agencies and describes selective strategic acquisitions [S4]. I therefore use one minimally confirmed strategic consolidator and set buy/exit assumptions conservatively rather than importing the report's broad-sector headline multiples.

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is MELTING. Clients will still need decisions, brand governance, high-stakes launches and accountable creative leadership, but the code's core deliverable—layouts, logos, graphics and production assets—is increasingly obtainable through software, templates or global freelancers. BLS's projection explicitly notes that automated design tools such as AI may reduce companies' need to contract with freelance graphic designers [S2]. This is product replacement risk, not merely a temporary labor-efficiency cycle.

## Risks and uncertainty

The score is intentionally fragile. The immutable role mix is at 4-digit 5414 and its largest listed occupation, Interior Designers, is identified in the brief as sibling-code contamination; I do not reweight it. Adobe is a multi-country survey of creatives rather than a NAICS revenue study, marketplace rates are not audited firm economics, and the M&A report covers independent creative agencies rather than small pure-play graphic-design firms [S1][S3][S4]. These limitations lower confidence and favor a kill rather than a precision valuation claim.
