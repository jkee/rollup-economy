## Executive view

Landscape Architectural Services has real AI leverage in research, reports, concepts and RFP work, but the sector is still experimenting rather than operating AI as a mature production system. Current acquisition evidence shows strategic buyer interest, not a liquid small-firm platform market. The investable core is licensed, accountable site work; discretionary design and principal-held relationships make the rest pressured. [S1][S2][S9]

## How AI changes the work

The best directly observed landscape-architecture evidence is task-specific: ASLA reports AI use in writing reports (45%), conceptual design (41%) and RFP responses (29%), while IFLA reports research/writing/predesign as the leading uses. That supports partial automation of documentation and visualization, not autonomous stamping, agency coordination, site observation or design accountability. The supplied 5413 occupation mix remains an engineering-heavy proxy. [S1][S2]

## Value capture

ASLA's standard forms explicitly contemplate both a landscape architect-client agreement and a landscape architect-consultant agreement. That establishes two economically different routes to market but does not quantify their revenue mix. I therefore treat retention as a bounded judgment: direct-client firms can retain some early productivity, whereas consultant work and competitive proposals can reprice it. Licensure protects who may perform regulated work in practice-act jurisdictions, not the price of every design output. [S3][S4][S5]

## Adoption timing

I define effective adoption as routine applied use that has produced a reported workflow benefit, rather than any use in teaching, research or experimentation. In ASLA's more-than-300-response 2024 survey, 55% reported some AI use but only 27% reported time saved; the respondent pool was mixed, with 51% at landscape-architecture firms, and only 15% of firms allowed billable experimentation. I select 25% effective adoption after excluding the broad-use and experimental component, and estimate four years to 50%; this is a transparent mapping, not an observed forecast. [S1]

## Consolidation and economics

There are three directly documented U.S. buyer/target transactions inside the 24 months before this run: Greenprint acquired Omni Workshop (January 2025), ISG acquired Lift Environmental Design (July 2025), and Populous acquired OJB Landscape Architecture (May 2026). Populous had disclosed a Providence Equity Partners minority investment before the OJB deal; I do not infer PE backing for the other buyers. Consideration and target EBITDA are undisclosed, so the entry and exit multiples are deliberately bounded screening estimates anchored only loosely to ROG's mixed A/E transaction distribution. [S6][S7][S8][S9][S10]

## Terminal demand

Terminal demand is PRESSURED, not melting. ASLA states that title acts restrict the title while practice acts restrict unlicensed performance of the work, and CLARB notes overlapping or incidental practice with architecture, engineering, interior design and surveying. This supplies an accessible, authoritative explanation for why accountable regulated work can persist while discretionary or adjacent-scope work remains contestable. No source located here quantifies the fee pool inside that protected perimeter, so this is not treated as a measured moat. [S3][S4]

## Risks and uncertainty

The core uncertainties are the contaminated 5413 role mix, the absence of a landscape-specific effective-adoption time series, unknown direct-client versus consultant revenue mix, undisclosed consideration in the three landscape deals, and no reopened owner-age/succession dataset. The score therefore gives no owner-age or succession bonus and uses low-confidence, explicitly bounded valuation estimates rather than importing unsupported A/E precision. [S1][S6][S7][S9][S10]
