## Industry-specific research guidance

### Role hints

- **Home health and personal care aides** — ~40% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 31-1120 Home Health and Personal Care Aides 40.2% of wage bill (60.1% of employment)); automatable: no. In-home physical care — bathing, transfers, meal prep, companionship. The product itself; mature AI does not replace it. Sensor/remote-monitoring tech can trim visit frequency at the margin but the binding industry constraint is aide supply, not aide productivity.
- **Registered nurses** — ~20% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 29-1141 Registered Nurses 19.6%); automatable: partial. Skilled visits are physical, but a large slice of RN time is OASIS assessments, documentation, care-plan paperwork and coding-adjacent work — AI documentation tools reclaim hours per episode; the visit itself persists. Set the within-role fraction from documentation-time evidence, not from the visit.
- **LPNs and nursing assistants** — ~9% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 29-2061 Licensed Practical/Vocational Nurses 5.7% + 31-1131 Nursing Assistants 3.4%); automatable: no. Hands-on clinical support in the home.
- **Therapists (PT/OT/SLP on home visits)** — ~6-7% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 29-1123 Physical Therapists 3.9% + 29-1122 Occupational Therapists 1.65% + SLP tail); automatable: no. Physical home visits; PDGM removed therapy-volume payment incentives in 2020 — visit counts already fell for payment reasons, not technology; don't misread that trend as AI substitution.
- **Clinical management and care coordination** — ~6% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-9111 Medical and Health Services Managers 3.6% + 21-1022 Healthcare Social Workers 2.2%); automatable: partial. QA review, scheduling oversight, utilization reporting automate substantially; family communication and clinical escalation persist.
- **Office, intake, scheduling, billing and coding** — ~7-10% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-9061 Office Clerks 1.1% + 43-6013 Medical Secretaries 1.0% + schedulers/coders/intake in the unlisted tail, est. ~5-8% more); automatable: yes. Intake/referral processing, eligibility checks, scheduling a distributed field workforce, OASIS coding review, claims and EVV compliance — the clean AI surface, plus it leverages the biggest ops pain (scheduling churn).
- **Remainder / long tail** — ~8-12% of wage bill (OEWS basis: unlisted tail (HR/recruiting, marketing liaisons, chaplains/hospice-adjacent roles)); automatable: partial. Recruiting is a huge cost center given aide turnover — AI-assisted recruiting/retention tooling is a real but partial lever; hospital-liaison marketing persists as relationship work.

### Capture questions

- Revenue arrives via Medicare PDGM 30-day episodic prospective payments, Medicaid HCBS hourly rates, MA negotiated per-visit rates, and private-pay hourly fees — under prospective/episodic payment an agency keeps efficiency gains until CMS rebases rates (and CMS demonstrably does, via recurring PDGM behavioral-adjustment cuts flagged by MedPAC's margin reports); under Medicaid hourly rates, states set (and some statutorily pass through to wages) the rate. For each payer line, who ends up with AI-driven savings after one rate cycle — and what is the payer mix of band-size agencies?
- Referral flow is owned by hospital discharge planners, ACO/health-system networks and MA plan preferred networks — is an acquired agency's census durable, or does it hinge on individual liaison relationships and network contracts that reprice or churn post-acquisition?
- The product is physically delivered in the home, so clients cannot bypass with generic AI — but entry barriers in non-medical personal care are low in many states (licensure only) while Medicare-certified home health requires certification and in some states a Certificate of Need or licensure moratorium: which segment is the target population in, and how real is the entry moat there?
- Aide labor supply is the binding constraint (turnover routinely 60-80% in home care): AI scheduling/recruiting tools address churn cost, but do wage-pass-through laws (e.g. state Medicaid rate rules) and minimum-wage floors convert any labor-cost efficiency straight into mandated wages rather than margin?

### Terminal-value question

The product is a human caregiver in the patient's home — aides, nurses and therapists are roughly 75% of the wage bill and mature AI does not replace the visit (remote monitoring trims frequency at the margin). Paid demand is demographically expanding. The mechanism that decides operator value is payer rate-setting: MedPAC documents persistently high Medicare margins and CMS responds with recurring PDGM rebasing/behavioral-adjustment cuts, while Medicaid rates are state-set with wage-pass-through strings — so the question is not 'does demand survive AI' but 'is an AI-driven cost advantage durable, or a temporary spread that the next rate rebasing and MA network repricing transfers to payers?' Answer separately for Medicare-certified skilled care, Medicaid personal care, and private-pay — the clawback mechanics differ across all three.

### Special notes

- Heterogeneity is the first-order trap: 621610 blends Medicare-certified skilled home health, Medicaid HCBS/personal care, and private-duty non-medical home care — different payers, margins, licensure moats and buyers. n_band 1,983 (the largest in this batch, all in the 100-499 class) spans all three; identify the dominant sub-segment among band-size firms, score that, and set heterogeneous: true.
- Pricing clawback channel (model in pi_dist): prospective payment is efficient-operator-friendly only between rate cycles — CMS PDGM behavioral adjustments and MedPAC-recommended cuts are a documented, recurring mechanism transferring measured savings back to the payer; Medicaid wage pass-through laws (e.g. New York wage parity) do the same on the cost side.
- MA penetration shifts volume from high-margin Medicare FFS to lower negotiated MA rates — a secular margin squeeze independent of AI; don't let it contaminate the AI-effect estimate in either direction.
- EBITDA normalization: owner comp at small agencies; EVV (electronic visit verification) compliance costs; hospice (621610-adjacent but coded 621610 only when mixed) carries different cap-driven economics — quality-of-earnings must separate service lines.
- Labor-share note: at 0.6718, this is among the highest labor shares in the screen, but most of it is the product (care hours), not overhead — high labor share here does NOT mean high AI-automatable share; the automatable slice is the ~15-20% office/coordination/documentation wrapper plus RN documentation time.
- Consolidation and strategic interest are heavy (Optum/Amedisys, Addus, BrightSpring, Help at Home; tracked by Mertz Taggart) — admissible for B and M only, never as V or A evidence (rule 2).
