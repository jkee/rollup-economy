### Capture questions

- Fees are lump-sum or hourly, and a large share of firms work as sub-consultants to architects/engineers/developers who hold the prime contract: when AI cuts design-production hours, does the prime simply re-scope or re-bid the LA sub-fee downward (pass-through), or do prime-held relationships and stamped-document requirements let the LA firm keep the saving? Split the answer by prime vs. sub revenue.
- Is the firm prime to the owner/municipality, or a swap-able sub-consultant — who owns the client relationship, and does it survive a principal's exit?
- Does residential and commercial design-build by landscape contractors bypass landscape architects except where stamped grading/stormwater documents are required — and can clients generate discretionary amenity/planting design with AI tools directly?
- What share of fees is code-required work (stormwater, ADA, public procurement, entitlement drawings) versus discretionary design — i.e., how big is the regulated core that AI cannot bypass?

### Terminal-value question

Discretionary planting and amenity design is highly AI-exposed and, in title-act states, not even legally reserved to licensees — the work may be done unlicensed as long as the title 'landscape architect' is not used. Durable paid demand concentrates in stamped, code-required site work (grading, stormwater, public projects). The deciding mechanism is the practice-act vs. title-act split by state: how much of the current fee pool sits inside a genuine practice-act monopoly, and does the rest survive mature AI as a paid service at all? Distinguish AI cutting the firm's production cost from AI (plus contractor design-build) replacing the discretionary product.
