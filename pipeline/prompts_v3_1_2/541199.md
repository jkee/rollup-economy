## Industry-specific research guidance

### Role hints

- **Owner-operators (process-serving firm owners, doc-prep operators, patent agents)** — ~30-35% (hypothesis; heavy SDE component) of wage bill (OEWS basis: role_mix is 4-digit 5411 (Lawyers 62.15%, Paralegals 13.12%) — a law-office mix that does not describe this catch-all code; structure below is hypothesis. 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 2.29% at 4-digit is the only directly relevant line); automatable: partial. Mix of relationship/dispatch management and hands-on production; the production half (document assembly, filings, affidavits) is AI-exposed, the client-relationship half less so.
- **Field process servers** — ~20% (hypothesis) of wage bill (OEWS basis: not in the 5411 top-10 (often coded 33-9021 Private Detectives or 43-5021 Couriers); hypothesis ~20% of this code's wages); automatable: no. Physical service of process cannot be done by AI — but it can be abolished by court e-service rules, a regulatory rather than technological substitution. Track both.
- **Legal document preparers / independent paralegals** — ~20-25% (hypothesis) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 23-2011 Paralegals and Legal Assistants 13.12% at 4-digit is mostly law-office paralegals; within this code, doc preparers are plausibly ~20-25% of wages (hypothesis)); automatable: yes. Form completion and filing packets are precisely what generic consumer AI does — the most exposed role in legal services, with no licensure protecting it.
- **Patent agents (non-attorney USPTO practitioners) and specialty practitioners** — ~10% (hypothesis) of wage bill (OEWS basis: not in the 5411 top-10; hypothesis ~10%); automatable: partial. AI drafts specifications and claims; USPTO registration and prosecution signature stay human.

### Capture questions

- Pricing is per-unit flat fees (per serve, per document packet, per filing) in a fragmented, licensure-free market: when AI cuts the cost of producing a document packet or managing a serve, does per-unit competition pass the saving straight to the client, or do court-integration, e-filing credentials and law-firm dispatch relationships let incumbents hold price?
- Can consumers and small businesses go straight to generic AI for the forms and filings that document preparers sell, given no licensure protects the niche — and does UPL enforcement (which both caps what non-lawyers may sell and creates their niche) tighten or loosen as AI legal tools spread?
- For process serving, who owns the relationship — law firms, collections agencies and insurers dispatch the work, increasingly via national platforms — and do court e-service rule changes eliminate the product by statute regardless of AI?
- Is there any transferable client book at all in the doc-prep segment, or is each transaction one-shot consumer demand won by search/ads (i.e., marketing spend, not a moat)?

### Terminal-value question

Legal document preparation is among the most directly AI-substitutable paid services anywhere — no license, no stamp, no physical act protects it. The sharp question: once consumer AI matures, does anything in this code besides physical service of process (itself exposed to e-service rules, not AI) and USPTO-registered patent prosecution retain a reason to be paid — and what share of the code's current revenue do those two protected slivers represent? Score the dominant sub-segment, not the protected edge cases.
