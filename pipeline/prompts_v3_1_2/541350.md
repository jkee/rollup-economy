- **Building and home inspectors (owners and staff)** — ~65-75% (hypothesis; record's 4-digit mix unrepresentative) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 47-4011 Construction and Building Inspectors 2.05% of the 4-digit 5413 wage bill — but 5413 is engineering-dominated; 47-4011 is this code's signature occupation and within 541350 proper inspectors are plausibly ~65-75% of wages (hypothesis; 6,523 of 7,623 firms are under-5-employee owner-operator shops)); automatable: partial. ### Capture questions

- Pricing is a flat per-inspection fee (a few hundred dollars) paid by the homebuyer in a one-shot transaction: when AI cuts report-writing time roughly in half, does the operator keep that as margin (fee is set by local going rates, not hours) — or does inspector oversupply and realtor fee-shopping pass it through as lower fees? Note the fee was never hours-priced, which argues for margin retention.
- Who owns demand — the buyer hires once, but the realtor referral relationship drives most flow (subject to state RESPA-adjacent rules on referral steering): is the durable asset the firm's realtor network, and does it survive an acquisition and the founding inspector's exit?
- What stops buyers from relying on AI photo/video analysis plus seller disclosures instead of a paid inspection — is the moat the physical access and testing, the liability-backed written opinion (E&O), state licensing requirements, or lender/insurer requirements, and which of those actually binds in the dominant residential segment?
- Is inspection volume itself the bigger risk — waived inspections in competitive markets and falling existing-home sales — such that AI economics are second-order to transaction-volume exposure?

### Terminal-value question

A physical human walk-through cannot be done by AI, and most states license home inspectors — but the paid product is an opinion attached to a real-estate transaction, and demand is set by whether buyers (and their agents and insurers) still commission it. The deciding mechanisms: state licensing plus contract contingency norms hold the human requirement in place, while inspection waivers, iBuyer/institutional purchasing and AI-assisted seller disclosure could shrink the commissioned base. Does mature AI cut the inspector's report-production cost (margin gain on an intact product), or erode the share of transactions that buy a human inspection at all? Answer with the waiver-rate and transaction-volume trend lines, not capability speculation.

### Special notes

- Role-mix warning: the provided role_mix is 4-digit 5413 (engineering-dominated; 47-4011 only 2.05% at parent level) and misstates this code — inspectors dominate the real wage bill; rebuild and flag as hypothesis.
- Demand is derivative of existing-home transaction volume — the 2021-2022 boom then 2023-2025 slump moved inspection counts massively for reasons unrelated to AI; normalize any revenue evidence against NAR existing-home sales, and treat inspection-waiver rates as a separate structural headwind.
- EBITDA normalization: overwhelmingly SDE owner-operator businesses (6,523 of 7,623 firms under 5 employees); n_band is just 40 firms — the acquirable pool is multi-inspector regional firms and franchise operators, and single-inspector books have minimal transferable value since the realtor relationships are personal.
- Sub-segment mix: residential home inspection (dominant, licensed in most states, consumer-paid) vs. commercial property condition assessment (ASTM E2018, lender-driven, engineering-adjacent) vs. specialty niches (radon, sewer scope, energy audits — often upsells). Exposure and pricing differ; score the dominant residential segment and note the mix.
- Boundary confusion: municipal/code-enforcement inspectors are government employees (not in this code); engineering firms doing structural inspections are 541330; pest/termite inspection is 561710; home energy raters may sit in 541690 — association data (InterNACHI) spans licensed individuals, not firms, so firm-level economics need care.
- Watch for state licensing variation: a handful of states still have no home-inspector license — the human-requirement moat is contract-and-custom in those markets, not statute.
