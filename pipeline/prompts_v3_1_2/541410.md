## Industry-specific research guidance

### Role hints

- **Interior designers (core fee earners)** — ~30-40% (scaling the 4-digit share to 6-digit) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 27-1025 Interior Designers 18.4% of wage bill (record is 4-digit 5414 but this occupation is this code's own core; at true 6-digit its share is plausibly 2x higher, est. ~30-40%)); automatable: partial. Space planning drafts, renderings, mood boards, FF&E schedules and spec documents are heavily AI-exposed (generative rendering already mainstream); client taste translation, site visits, showroom sourcing relationships and project babysitting persist. Commercial (contract) design also carries code-compliance work that automates.
- **Design-adjacent creative staff** — ~5% (record shares are mostly sibling contamination) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 27-1024 Graphic Designers 15.4% + 27-1011 Art Directors 3.0% in the 4-digit record — largely contamination from 541430 firms in the 5414 cell; some visualization/branding staff genuinely exist in interior firms, est. true share ~5%); automatable: yes. Presentation decks, renderings and marketing collateral — near-fully automatable production work.
- **Owners and management** — ~12-15% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General & Operations Managers 12.4%); automatable: partial. Principal-client relationships are the business in residential design; internal ops, purchasing admin oversight and reporting automate.
- **Sales and business development** — ~6% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-3091 Sales Representatives of Services 3.7% + 13-1161 Market Research/Marketing 2.0%); automatable: partial. Lead generation content and proposal production automate; relationship selling and showroom/trade networks persist.
- **Project management and expediting** — ~4-5% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1082 Project Management Specialists 3.5%); automatable: partial. Order tracking, vendor follow-up, punch lists and budget reporting automate substantially; site coordination with contractors persists.
- **Procurement, warehousing and admin** — ~15-18% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 53-7062 Laborers & Material Movers 1.2% + 43-9061 Office Clerks 1.8% + 43-6014 Admin Assistants 1.3% + purchasing/receiving/bookkeeping tail, est. ~10-15% more); automatable: partial. Purchase orders, invoice reconciliation and expediting paperwork are near-fully automatable; physical receiving, inspection and installation logistics for furnishings are not — this code moves real goods.
- **Unallocated tail** — ~15-20% of wage bill (OEWS basis: remainder of the wage bill beyond listed occupations, est. ~15-20%); automatable: partial. Junior designers, CAD/Revit techs, bookkeepers — split between the design-production (automatable) and physical-logistics (not) patterns above.

### Capture questions

- Residential interior design monetizes through a mix of design fees (hourly or flat) plus markup on furnishings procured at trade discount — when AI collapses design-production hours, hourly billers lose revenue directly, flat-fee billers keep the savings, and markup-based firms are indifferent to hours but exposed to clients buying product direct. What is the actual revenue split across these three models in the target size class, and which way is it trending?
- Who owns the client relationship — the firm or the principal designer whose taste and social network is the brand? In residential design the book is almost entirely personal; what, if anything, survives an acquisition?
- What stops clients from using generative design tools (room visualization, AI mood boards) plus retail purchasing to bypass designers entirely — is the moat the trade-only showroom/discount ecosystem, the contractor coordination, or nothing durable?
- For the commercial/contract segment: fees often run as % of FF&E budget or fixed fee under competitive pitches to corporate clients — does AI-driven efficiency get competed away in the pitch process?

### Terminal-value question

The labor_share here is only 0.2359 because receipts include resold furnishings — the 'service' is partly a retail margin on goods moved through trade channels. Mature AI can generate the design artifacts (renderings, layouts, spec lists) that justify the fee, and e-commerce erodes the trade-discount arbitrage — does paid demand survive as project management of physical procurement and installation (which endures), or was the fee always for the design artifacts AI now makes free? Score the goods-logistics revenue and the design-fee revenue separately; conflating them is the failure mode.

### Special notes

- Labor share 0.2359 is the lowest in the 5413/5414 group — product pass-through (furniture, fixtures resold at markup) sits in receipts. EBITDA margins on receipts will look thin while margins on service fees are fat; normalize before comparing multiples, and expect SDE-basis figures in any small-firm listing data.
- Role-mix contamination: role_mix is 4-digit 5414 — the graphic designer (15.4%) and art director shares belong mostly to 541430 firms in the same cell. Scale interior designers up, graphics down when building the 6-digit structure.
- Extreme fragmentation: n_total 16,137 with 13,213 firms under 5 employees; n_band 313 concentrated in the 20-99 class. Most of the industry is sole practitioners below any acquirable threshold; no documented consolidation wave exists — verify rather than assume active_consolidators > 0.
- Key-person portability is near-total in residential design: the client book is the principal's taste and network, walking out the door at close — a first-order M discount.
- Sub-segment split matters: residential (taste-driven, markup monetization) vs. commercial/contract interiors (corporate clients, competitive fees, often inside architecture firms) have different capture and terminal profiles — identify which dominates the $1-10M EBITDA band; commercial studios are the more institutional, buyable half.
- Boundary confusion: interior decorating (no design credential) blurs into this code from below; architecture firms doing interiors book in 541310; kitchen/bath design attached to retailers books in retail codes. Industry surveys (ASID, Houzz) span all of these — check the sample frame.
