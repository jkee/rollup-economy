## Industry-specific research guidance

### Role hints

- **HR/benefits consultants and analysts (core billable staff)** — ~22-28% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1111 Management Analysts 15.5% + 13-1071 Human Resources Specialists 1.7% + 13-1199 Business Operations Specialists 2.9% at 4-digit 5416 — at true 6-digit the HR-specialist and compensation-analyst (13-1141) shares are certainly higher; est. ~22-28% combined); automatable: partial. Comp benchmarking runs, survey data analysis, job architecture documentation, handbook/policy drafting and benefits-plan comparison decks are heavily AI-exposed; client advisory judgment on pay decisions, negotiation support and change-management facilitation persist.
- **Partners/principals and firm management** — ~14-16% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General & Operations Managers 13.5% — largely the principal layer in boutiques); automatable: no. Client-executive and board (comp-committee) relationships persist — comp consulting to boards is trust work; only internal admin automates.
- **Actuarial and technical benefits staff** — ~5-10% (hypothesis) of wage bill (OEWS basis: not in the listed 4-digit top-10 — actuaries (15-2011) and pension/benefits technicians sit in the unlisted tail for the retirement/benefits sub-segment; est. ~5-10%); automatable: partial. Valuation runs, compliance filings (Form 5500), testing and disclosure production automate substantially; credentialed actuarial sign-off (Enrolled Actuary for ERISA plans) is a licensed gate that persists.
- **Marketing and sales** — ~8% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1161 Market Research & Marketing 4.1% + 41-3091 Sales Reps 3.6%); automatable: partial. Content, proposals and survey-product marketing automate; relationship selling persists.
- **Project management and client service** — ~5-6% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1082 Project Management Specialists 4.2% + 43-4051 Customer Service Representatives 1.4%); automatable: partial. Engagement admin, data collection chasing and status reporting automate; client-committee coordination persists.
- **Technology staff** — ~3-4% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 15-1252 Software Developers 3.5%); automatable: partial. Survey platforms and analytics tooling — AI-augmented; concentrated in firms with data products.
- **Admin and unallocated tail** — ~30-35% of wage bill (OEWS basis: remainder beyond the listed top-10 (top-10 sums to ~53%), plus 13-2011 Accountants 2.2%, est. ~30-35% total); automatable: partial. Survey operations staff, junior analysts and back office — mostly analyst-pattern (high AI exposure) and admin (high); allocate to the patterns above.

### Capture questions

- HR consulting bills as project fees, retainers, per-participant/per-plan fees (benefits/actuarial work), and survey-data subscriptions — for each, when AI collapses the analysis and drafting hours, does the fee hold (clients pay for defensibility and the data asset) or reprice to the reduced effort? Comp-survey subscription revenue is data-product revenue — the most capture-positive stream — while project advisory is effort-benchmarked; what is the actual mix in $1-10M EBITDA firms?
- Who owns the client relationship — the firm (recurring plan/comp-cycle work, proprietary survey panels) or the individual consultant trusted by the CHRO/comp committee? Annual-cycle recurring work (comp reviews, plan renewals, 5500 filings) is the institutional revenue; how much of a typical boutique is recurring vs. one-off projects?
- What stops clients from self-serving with AI plus HR-tech platforms — Workday/Rippling/Payscale-class products already embed comp benchmarking, policy generation and analytics; is the boutique's surviving moat proprietary survey data, ERISA/actuarial credentials, or litigation-defensibility (pay-equity analyses under privilege), and which client sizes still pay?
- Where regulation anchors demand (ERISA, Enrolled Actuary sign-offs, pay-transparency and pay-equity statutes, AA/OFCCP-type compliance), does it name credentialed humans in ways AI cannot satisfy — and what share of the code's revenue sits behind those gates?

### Terminal-value question

HR consulting splits into a data/credential-gated core (comp survey panels, ERISA/actuarial sign-offs, privileged pay-equity work — where AI cuts the operator's costs and the fee anchors survive) and an open advisory shell (policies, handbooks, org design, generalist HR advice — where AI embedded in HR-tech platforms replaces the product for most mid-market clients). The deciding mechanisms are concrete: proprietary benchmarking data nobody can prompt into existence, statutes naming credentialed signatories, and legal privilege. What fraction of the code's actual revenue sits on the protected side — and is a researcher scoring 'HR consulting' as one thing missing that the unprotected half is most of the firm count?

- Comp-survey data products are the industry's genuine moat and the most misread asset: firms with proprietary panels have SaaS-like revenue inside a consulting wrapper — segment any target-economics evidence by whether a data product exists.
- EBITDA normalization: principal comp inside SDE in boutiques; retainer vs. project mix drives revenue quality; comp-committee advisory work is small-dollar but extremely sticky — headline revenue multiples mislead without the mix.
- role_mix is 4-digit 5416 (management-consulting dominated): compensation analysts (13-1141), actuaries (15-2011) and benefits specialists — this code's distinctive occupations — are absent from the listed top-10; rebuild their shares as hypothesis and say so.
