- **M:** buy and exit EBITDA multiples. Do not use multiple expansion to
  justify C or terminal value.
- **Terminal demand:** whether paid demand for the operator survives AI. This

## Industry-specific research guidance

### Role hints

- **Accountants and auditors (staff through senior manager, CPA-track)** — ~35-40% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-2011 Accountants and Auditors 34.2% of wage bill (4-digit 5412 mix; within CPA offices proper this share is likely higher since siblings 541213/541214/541219 dilute it)); automatable: partial. Audit fieldwork, workpaper preparation, tie-outs, sampling, tax return preparation and technical memo drafting are heavily AI-exposed; professional-skepticism judgment calls, client walkthroughs and materiality decisions less so. The run should justify a within-role fraction from the fieldwork/judgment task split — plausibly 0.3-0.5.
- **Partner / management layer** — ~15% (of wages; true partner economics larger via draws) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-3031 Financial Managers 10.32% + 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 5.1%; note partner profit draws in LLPs sit largely outside QCEW wages); automatable: partial. Audit opinions and return sign-off are licensure-gated; client relationships and pricing judgment are the durable human layer. Internal reporting and review workflow automates.
- **Bookkeeping and client accounting services (CAS) staff** — ~5-6% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-3031 Bookkeeping, Accounting, and Auditing Clerks 5.49%); automatable: yes. Write-up, reconciliation and monthly close are near-fully automatable by platform-native AI — the fastest-compressing cost line.
- **Non-CPA tax preparers** — ~4% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-2082 Tax Preparers 4.2%); automatable: yes. Form-driven preparation automates; review and signature migrate up to CPAs.
- **Advisory / consulting specialists** — ~4% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1111 Management Analysts 4.24%); automatable: partial. The growing CAS/advisory arm; AI-augmented rather than replaced near-term.
- **Administrative and office staff** — ~7% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-6014 Secretaries 2.02% + 43-9061 Office Clerks 1.44% + 43-3021 Billing Clerks 1.29% + 43-1011 Admin Supervisors 1.88%); automatable: yes. Scheduling, return assembly, e-file transmittal, engagement letters, billing.
- **Long tail (IT, HR, other financial specialists, marketing)** — ~25-30% of wage bill (OEWS basis: not in listed top-10; top-10 covers ~70% of the wage bill, est. remainder ~30%); automatable: partial. Mixed exposure; allocate rather than ignore — this tail is large enough to move the V total.

### Capture questions

- Billing is a mix of hourly and fixed-fee compliance engagements with realization tracked against hours: when AI collapses fieldwork and prep hours, do competitive fee pressure and client fee-benchmarking pass the saving through as lower audit/tax fees, or do the CPA pipeline shortage and value-pricing adoption let firms hold price and keep the spread as margin? What does rate/realization evidence since 2023 actually show for small and mid-size firms?
- Is audit/attest demand payer-mandated (lenders, bonding companies, regulators, grant agencies require a signed opinion) such that clients cannot bypass the firm no matter how capable AI gets — and what share of a typical small firm's revenue is attest vs. bypassable tax/CAS work?
- For tax and client accounting services, do Intuit (TurboTax full-service, QuickBooks Live), IRS Direct File expansion and generic AI let SMB and consumer clients self-serve — where is the complexity/liability line above which they keep paying?
- How much revenue is partner-relationship advisory versus commodity compliance, and does the relationship survive partner retirement and an acquisition — what do retention outcomes in the 2021-2025 PE deals show?
- Does the alternative-practice-structure model (attest entity CPA-owned, ServiceCo investor-owned) hold up under state board and independence scrutiny, or is there regulatory risk to the consolidation structure itself?

### Terminal-value question

Attestation is a legally required human signature — audit opinions, comfort letters and many state/federal filings must be signed by a licensed CPA firm, so a demand floor exists by statute. The terminal question is whether AI collapses the billable hours underneath faster than the CPA shortage and value pricing hold fees up: does audit and complex tax become a smaller-headcount stamp-and-judgment business with preserved or improved per-partner economics (operator cost cut), or does fee benchmarking hand the hour collapse to clients (product shrinkage)? Answer separately for attest, tax and CAS revenue lines — they have different floors, and only attest has the statutory one.

### Special notes

- Pricing-model deflation candidate: hourly and hours-anchored fixed fees convert AI efficiency into lower bills where clients benchmark fees; model pi_dist per revenue line (attest / tax / CAS-advisory) rather than blended, and cite realization trends.
- Ownership/structure: state boards require CPA ownership of attest practices — PE deals use alternative practice structures (attest entity stays CPA-owned; non-attest ServiceCo investor-owned). The wave since 2021 is large and well documented; use it for B and M only (rule 2), and verify current deal pace rather than assuming 2021-2023 tempo.
- EBITDA normalization: small-practice comps from brokers (Accounting Practice Sales, Poe Group) quote multiples of gross revenue with owner compensation inside — normalize to market-rate partner comp before comparing to PE platform EBITDA multiples; partner draws also sit outside QCEW wages, so the record's labor_share understates true labor cost at partnership firms.
- The 150-hour rule and the CPA pipeline shortage currently support pricing — a documented counterweight to AI deflation; track whether states' 120-hour alternative pathways (in progress 2024-2026) relieve it.
- Independence rules restrict cross-selling to attest clients — limits the consolidation synergy story; peer review and state board oversight apply to acquired practices.
- Boundary confusion: the provided role_mix is 4-digit 5412, blending in tax prep chains (541213), payroll bureaus (541214) and bookkeeping firms (541219) — CPA-office shares of accountants are higher and of clerks lower than the listed mix; adjust and say so. Association data (AICPA, Rosenberg) covers CPA firms specifically and is the better anchor.
- Seasonality: tax-season revenue concentration distorts quarterly staffing and any part-year financials; use full-fiscal-year figures.
