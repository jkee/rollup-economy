## Industry-specific research guidance

### Role hints

- **Travel agents / advisors** — ~20%; note many leisure advisors are 1099 independent contractors under host agencies and sit outside the W-2 wage bill of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-3041 Travel Agents 19.8% of wage bill (24.5% of employment)); automatable: partial. Itinerary research, fare/rate searching, booking execution, rebooking and disruption handling are squarely within current AI capability; the persistent slice is trusted-advisor curation for complex/luxury/group trips and supplier-relationship leverage. The run should split transactional vs advisory work when setting the fraction.
- **Reservation, ticketing and customer service staff** — ~11% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-4181 Reservation and Transportation Ticket Agents 6.3% + 43-4051 Customer Service Representatives 4.9%); automatable: yes. Fulfillment and servicing work — queue management, ticketing, schedule changes — is the most directly automatable line; large TMCs are already automating it.
- **Management** — ~10% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 10.0%); automatable: partial. Supplier negotiations and client account management persist; ops reporting automates.
- **Sales and marketing** — ~8-9% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-3091 Sales Representatives of Services 4.6% + 13-1161 Market Research Analysts and Marketing Specialists 3.9%); automatable: partial. Corporate-account selling is relationship work; marketing content and campaign ops automate heavily.
- **Software developers and tech staff** — ~6% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 15-1252 Software Developers 5.7% (concentrated in OTAs and TMC platforms, not in retail agencies)); automatable: partial. A signature of the code's heterogeneity: the developer share belongs to the OTA/TMC-platform tier, not the small-agency band the screen targets.
- **Tour and travel guides** — ~3% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 39-7010 Tour and Travel Guides 3.0%); automatable: no. Physical, in-person guiding — not AI-replaceable.
- **Admin and back office** — ~30-35% remainder incl. unlisted tail of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-1011 First-Line Supervisors of Office Workers 2.8% + 43-9061 Office Clerks 1.1% + long tail, est. remainder); automatable: yes. Invoicing, commission tracking/reconciliation (a notoriously manual task in host agencies), and document handling are near-fully automatable.

### Capture questions

- Agency revenue is mostly supplier-paid commission (cruise/tour ~10-16%, hotel ~10%, air near zero) plus modest client service fees — if AI cuts an agency's fulfillment labor, the commission percentage is untouched and the saving is operator margin; but the controlling question is upstream: do suppliers keep paying commissions at current rates once AI booking channels let them acquire the same customer directly, or does the air-commission precedent (cut to zero in the early 2000s) repeat in cruise and hotel?
- Who owns the client: leisure books are personal to the advisor, and a large share of advisors are 1099 ICs affiliated with host agencies who can re-affiliate freely — in an acquisition of an agency or host network, what actually transfers, and what does advisor churn do to it?
- What stops the client from bypassing the agency with consumer AI trip-planners plus direct booking — and on the corporate side, do duty-of-care obligations, policy enforcement, negotiated rates and expense integration keep TMC contracts mandatory even when AI does the booking?
- Gatekeepers: GDS contracts, ARC/IATA accreditation, and consortia (Virtuoso, Signature, Ensemble) hold the negotiated commission tiers and supplier relationships — does AI-era value accrue to those upstream aggregators rather than to the individual agency an acquirer would buy?

### Terminal-value question

This industry has already been melted once: online self-serve booking eliminated the commodity retail agency in the 2000s, and the survivors are complexity niches — cruise/luxury/group leisure advisors and corporate managed travel. The mature-AI question is whether those niches survive a booking agent that can search, compare, book, and rebook directly with suppliers: specifically (a) do suppliers keep paying intermediary commissions when AI channels let them go direct (the air-commission zero-out is the historical mechanism to cite), and (b) does corporate duty-of-care/compliance keep TMC intermediation contractually required? Distinguish AI cutting an agency's fulfillment costs (margin gain on unchanged commissions) from AI becoming the booking intermediary (product replacement) — a diligent researcher can get this wrong by scoring the first and missing the second.

### Special notes

- Severe heterogeneity: 561510 spans mega-OTAs and corporate TMCs (48 firms in the 500+ class averaging $559M revenue — they dominate the $35.6B receipts) down to 6,236 micro leisure agencies. The $1-10M EBITDA band (n_band 250: the 20-99 and 100-499 classes) is mid-size TMCs, host agencies and niche specialists — score that population, not Expedia and not the solo advisor; set heterogeneous thinking accordingly.
- Gross-vs-net revenue trap: agencies variously report gross bookings vs net commission revenue — margins, revenue/employee and multiples are incomparable across sources unless the basis is stated; SUSB receipts for this code mix both conventions.
- 1099 host-agency structure: tens of thousands of independent advisors sit outside SUSB employment and QCEW wages — firm counts and the 0.2099 labor_share (IPS-based, HIGH quality) describe the employer tier only.
- COVID distortion: 2020-22 series (including the 2022 receipts and IPS 2022 labor share in this record) sit on partial recovery — check 2023-24 levels before treating them as steady-state.
- Boundary confusion: 561520 Tour Operators (package assembly, principal risk) and 561599/561591 (other reservation services, CVBs) are siblings; 'travel agency' surveys and market sizings frequently blend them — check coverage before quoting.
- EBITDA normalization: owner-operator comp dominates at the small end; host-agency P&Ls are thin-spread intermediary books where commission-split changes swing margins.
