## Industry-specific research guidance

### Role hints

- **Field crews (landscaping, groundskeeping, tree care, construction labor)** — ~61% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 37-3011 Landscaping and Groundskeeping Workers 53.5% + 37-3013 Tree Trimmers and Pruners 5.2% + 37-3012 Pesticide Handlers/Sprayers 1.25% + 47-2061 Construction Laborers 1.1%); automatable: no. Physical outdoor work — mowing, planting, pruning, installation. Mature AI does not replace it; robotic mowers substitute some mowing hours but that is robotics capex with its own economics, distinct from the AI-labor channel, and adoption in commercial contracting is early. Do not let 'automation' claims about robots inflate the AI-automatable fraction.
- **Crew supervisors / foremen** — ~11% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 37-1012 First-Line Supervisors of Landscaping Workers 10.9%); automatable: no. On-site crew leadership; AI scheduling and job documentation widen span of control only marginally.
- **Management** — ~7.5% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 7.6%); automatable: partial. Job costing, scheduling, fleet reporting automate; client relationships and labor management persist.
- **Sales and estimating** — ~3.5% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-3091 Sales Representatives of Services 3.4%); automatable: partial. AI property-measurement/takeoff from aerial imagery and automated quoting compress estimating labor substantially; relationship selling on commercial accounts persists.
- **Office and back office** — ~6-8% incl. tail of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-9061 Office Clerks 2.7% + 43-6014 Secretaries/Admin 1.0% + 43-3031 Bookkeeping Clerks 1.0% + admin tail); automatable: yes. Invoicing, scheduling, dispatch, payroll for a seasonal hourly workforce — near-fully automatable, but note how small this base is relative to field labor.
- **Remainder / long tail** — ~8-10% of wage bill (OEWS basis: unlisted tail (mechanics, drivers, irrigation techs, designers)); automatable: partial. Equipment maintenance and driving are physical; landscape design/CAD work is AI-exposed.

### Capture questions

- Commercial maintenance contracts are re-bid annually or tri-annually on competitive tender against low switching costs, while residential recurring and design-build work is quoted per job — under each pricing mode, when AI (or robotics) cuts a bidder's cost, does the operator keep it as margin or does the next bid cycle hand it to the property manager? Estimate the revenue mix across these modes for band-size firms, and note BrightView's chronically thin margins as evidence about commercial pricing power.
- Route/branch density: margin uplift in landscape roll-ups comes primarily from densifying crews, routes and equipment utilization — how much of documented acquisition value creation is density versus back-office removal, and does AI change that ratio at all given the ~15% non-field wage share?
- Entry barriers are near zero (a truck, a trailer, a crew) and the binding constraint is field labor supply (H-2B caps, wage inflation) — if AI back-office tools become cheap and universal, do they lower entry barriers further and *increase* fragmentation/price competition rather than helping incumbents hold price?
- Who owns the customer: commercial accounts sit with property/facility managers (often national accounts centrally procured), residential with the local brand — what does churn evidence after landscape acquisitions show about which books actually transfer?

### Terminal-value question

Grass is cut and beds are planted by crews physically on site — mature AI does not replace the ~72% of the wage bill that is field labor and supervision, so paid demand survives; the AI-automatable surface is the thin office/sales/estimating wrapper (~15-20%). The question that decides operator value is therefore not product replacement but capture: with annual competitive re-bids on commercial maintenance and near-zero entry barriers, is there any mechanism by which an operator retains AI or robotic cost savings rather than passing them to property managers at the next bid — and separately, keep robotic mowing (capex substitution for field hours, uncertain timeline) strictly apart from the AI-labor channel so neither inflates the other.

### Special notes

- Largest V trap in this batch: 60%+ of the wage bill is physical field labor AI does not touch. Robotic/autonomous mowers are a robotics-capex story with separate adoption economics — cite them under demand/pricing context if at all, and do not fold them into the AI-automatable fraction.
- EBITDA normalization: owner comp/SDE at the small end; heavy equipment means the EBITDA-to-free-cash-flow gap (maintenance capex on trucks/mowers) is material — EBITDA multiples overstate comparability with asset-light services; seasonality plus snow-removal revenue (high-margin but weather-volatile, common in northern firms) distorts single-year figures.
- Mix matters: maintenance (recurring, values at higher multiples) vs design-build/installation (project-based, cyclical, construction-adjacent) vs tree care (specialized, higher margin) all sit inside 561730 — identify a target population mix before applying one margin or multiple; the n_band chain's 15.65% margin is generous for commercial maintenance (BrightView runs high-single-digit EBITDA margins), so treat n_band 593 as sensitive to the margin assumption.
- H-2B guest-worker dependence: labor supply, not demand, is the binding constraint; visa-cap outcomes move costs industry-wide and are unrelated to AI — don't attribute margin swings to technology.
- Boundary confusions: 561710 handles structural pest; lawn *chemical* application straddles the two; site-prep/hardscape work bleeds into NAICS 238 contractors; interior plantscaping (561730 includes it) is a niche. Surveys labeled 'green industry' often include nurseries (444/111) — check scope.
- Consolidation (BrightView, Yellowstone, Sperber, Mariani plus many PE platforms; aggressive since 2020) — B/M evidence only, never V/A (rule 2).
