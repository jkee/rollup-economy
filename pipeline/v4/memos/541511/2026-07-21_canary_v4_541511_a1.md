# 541511 — Custom Computer Programming Services

*v4.0 research memo · run `2026-07-21_canary_v4_541511_a1` · gpt-5.6-terra · 2026-07-21*

## Executive view

This screen concerns customer-specific software work, a labor-intensive service where AI tools are already common among developers but where tool use alone does not establish durable savings or customer willingness to keep outsourcing. The record therefore separates observed workflow change from the currently unmeasured commercial outcomes. [S1][S2][S3]

## Target archetype

The selected archetype is a US customer-relationship-owned custom-development studio with repeat application support: it writes, modifies, tests, and supports software for particular customers, rather than selling packaged software or operating client facilities. Census's definition supports that activity boundary, but no defensible public source splits the supplied $1–10M EBITDA band into this narrower recurring-support archetype. [S1]

## How AI changes the work

AI is most relevant to coding, test creation, documentation, routine analysis, status work, and support triage; it is less complete for requirements discovery, legacy-context judgment, architecture tradeoffs, client escalation, and production accountability. Stack Overflow reports frequent tool use among professional developers, while DORA reports broad use and productivity gains alongside material distrust of generated code. [S2][S3]

## Value capture

Gross workflow compression is not the same as retained operator value. The selected studio may keep savings where it sells a fixed scope, production accountability, or ongoing support; it may pass them through where a client buys hours, named FTE capacity, or a competitively rebid scope. No reopened public source measured the 24-month retained-savings share for this lower-middle-market archetype, so it is left unmeasured.

## Adoption timing

The evidence supports widespread developer AI-tool use, not the required point at which half of this specific archetype materially uses AI across the tasks represented in the labor estimate. DORA also conditions outcomes on practices such as platforms, testing, version control, and feedback loops. Accordingly, no target-archetype t50 estimate is asserted. [S2][S3]

## Buyability

The deterministic band is large, but the record does not equate all of it with a relationship-owned recurring-support studio. No reopened evidence established the owner's succession posture for this archetype or a reproducible count of active strategic and financial buyers for it. The seller signal is consequently neutral/unknown, and the buyer count is left unmeasured.

## Valuation

No public, reopened transaction set supplied comparable entry and exit EBITDA-multiple ranges for US $1–10M EBITDA custom-programming studios after separating staff augmentation, fixed-scope development, and recurring support. A market multiple cannot be inferred from the existence of large public IT-services firms, so both multiple inputs remain unmeasured rather than using an invented spread.

## Operator survival

At mature adoption, the studio is plausibly pressured rather than automatically eliminated: customer-specific software still requires modification, testing, support, and accountable production operation under the NAICS definition, yet AI can reduce the labor scarcity that historically supported outsourced delivery. DORA's finding that generated code still attracts limited or no trust for many respondents supports continued review and accountability, not a guarantee of independent-studio demand. [S1][S3]

## Risks and uncertainty

The key unresolved questions are the target-archetype share of the band, whether contracts decouple revenue from hours, the timing of material adoption rather than tool use, buyer crowding, and lower-middle-market valuations. Survey and technology-team evidence are broad proxies, not evidence for US owner-operated studios; the 2022 receipt base also predates the recent coding-assistant adoption wave. [S2][S3]
