## Executive view

The selected opportunity is a recurring-revenue SMB managed-service provider (MSP), rather than all computer-systems-design businesses. AI is already being used to automate ticketing, patching, and monitoring, so it can remove meaningful delivery work. The economics remain evidence-limited: recurring per-user/per-device and bundle billing can initially retain savings, but smaller customer deals, price pressure, vendor costs, and an unmeasured seat-count effect can pass savings through. Consolidation creates a credible add-on-to-platform multiple path, while also making targets competitive. [S1][S3][S4]

## Target archetype

The archetype is a US regional MSP serving SMB customers through recurring managed IT, endpoint, helpdesk, cybersecurity, backup, and advisory contracts, with enough adjusted EBITDA to fall in the supplied $1–10M band. It excludes custom-development shops, pure VAR/resale books, one-off project integrators, and outsourced operation of a client-owned data center. MSP surveys and deal reports are business-model samples that cross NAICS 541512, 541513, and 541519, so no source maps their share of the supplied NAICS band; the coverage range is deliberately an unsupported assumption rather than a claim about all 3,524 firms. [S1][S4]

## How AI changes the work

The operational mechanism is specific to managed delivery: AI can triage and answer common tickets, draft or execute routine remediation, assist patching and monitoring, prepare documentation, and reduce project and account-management administration. Kaseya reports that 53% of surveyed MSPs already use AI for ticketing, patching, and monitoring, but more than half have automated only about one-quarter of workload. That supports partial rather than wholesale task replacement: architecture, client-environment knowledge, incident accountability, field work, sales, and trusted-advisor work remain. [S1]

## Value capture

Near-term capture is possible because MSPs commonly bill with recurring per-user, per-device, tiered-bundle, or fixed-fee subscription structures rather than pure hourly labor. The billing structure can decouple a lower service-delivery cost from revenue through a contract period. It is not proof of 24-month capture: the same survey shows substantial month-to-month and one-year contracting, and the 2026 survey reports smaller typical customer spending and intensified customer-acquisition difficulty. The retained-savings range therefore explicitly allows competitive repricing, higher platform/vendor costs, and client headcount reductions to absorb much of the gross saving. [S1][S3]

## Adoption timing

The available current survey is a global MSP proxy rather than a census of US lower-middle-market targets. It reports 53% already using AI to automate ticketing, patching, and monitoring and measurable operational improvements, which supports a base case that majority material use in these delivery tasks has been reached. Because more than half have automated only around a quarter of workload and the survey population is broader than the archetype, the range retains a two-year possibility for the target population rather than treating tool availability as complete operational adoption. [S1]

## Buyability

The supplied band has 3,524 firms before archetype coverage, but the actual MSP portion is unmeasured. Buyer competition is plainly material: a 2025 MSP deal report records 466 deals, roughly 85% PE participation, 16 Evergreen MSP acquisitions in Q4, and transactions by Thrive, The 20 MSP, HarborIT, Corporate Tech, and Omega. The seller-supply signal is kept MISSING because no reopened source measures succession-driven supply for the selected US target tier; the record does not infer it from the deal wave or founder anecdotes. [S4][S7][S8]

## Valuation

The valuation bridge is conditional on buying a recurring-service add-on rather than a resale-heavy or project-heavy systems integrator. Solganick reports 5x–8x EBITDA for smaller MSP add-ons and 11x–20x for $10M-plus EBITDA platforms; Aventis independently describes 5x–8x for smaller MSPs while warning that its disclosed-transaction median reflects much larger deals and public comparables are imperfect. These are market proxies, not a measured entry or exit quote for this target archetype, so the ranges are broad and do not assume a realized spread. [S4][S5]

## Operator survival

The mature outcome is assessed as PRESSURED, not as disappearance of client IT demand. Cybersecurity and backup remain important revenue lines, and Kaseya says 71% of respondents reported year-over-year cybersecurity revenue growth; ScalePad reports compliance-oriented MSPs have higher ARPU and recurring revenue. Those facts support a continuing accountable service role, particularly for security and compliance. Pressure is still substantial because vendors are embedding automation, only 13% of MSPs report meaningful revenue from AI services despite client demand, and the routine helpdesk/endpoint layer can be automated or bundled upstream. [S1][S6]

## Risks and uncertainty

The largest uncertainties are the share of the deterministic NAICS band that is genuinely recurring MSP, seller availability, and the 24-month share of AI savings retained after renewals and vendor pricing. MSP data are mostly global business-model surveys rather than six-digit US statistics, while the deterministic labor and firm-count inputs are NAICS based. A resale-heavy, project-heavy, or owner-dependent operator can have materially different margins, contract portability, and valuation from the selected archetype. The survey evidence also does not quantify AI-driven changes in client seat counts, so it cannot establish whether per-seat billing protects or shrinks the revenue base. [S1][S3][S4]

## Declared caveats

- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: The supplied 5415 occupation mix understates a support-desk-heavy MSP mix and includes adjacent systems-design business models.
- inputs.ai_replaceable_share: Fractions measure work potentially removed or materially compressed, not gross headcount reduction or revenue retention.
- inputs.target_archetype.coverage: The coverage estimate needs a NAICS-coded establishment sample, business-description tagging, or a target screen before it can be treated as evidence.
- inputs.retained_savings_share_24m: No reopened source quantifies gross AI savings, renewal repricing, vendor pass-through, or client-seat changes for the selected US target tier.
- inputs.retained_savings_share_24m: The value is distinct from whether an MSP remains a paid operator at mature adoption.
- inputs.t50_years: The survey does not isolate the US, the $1–10M EBITDA band, or a common threshold for material workload share.
- inputs.current_adoption_pct: It records operational use in named tasks, not the share of total workload automated or the share of each target's revenue exposed.
- inputs.historical_analogs: Differences in survey wording, respondents, and definitions preclude calculating a precise adoption rate from the two percentages.
- inputs.seller_supply_signal: A target-level broker pipeline or owner-demographic study is required to replace UNKNOWN.
- inputs.active_consolidators: The number is a count of demonstrably or plausibly active platform buyers, not a full count of all PE sponsors, strategic buyers, or brokers.
- inputs.buy_mult: No reopened source discloses an entry multiple for a matched US regional target in the exact $1–10M EBITDA band.
- inputs.exit_mult: Platform multiples are not directly comparable to add-ons; exit requires integration, retention, recurring revenue quality, and buyer-market conditions not established here.
- inputs.pricing_structure: A target screen must verify MRR mix, renewal dates, pass-through clauses, and exposure to client user/device counts.
- inputs.operator_survival: No long-horizon evidence directly measures whether SMBs retain an independent MSP versus consume vendor-managed automation.
- inputs.operator_survival: This mature-demand assessment is separate from the 24-month retained-savings estimate.
