## Executive view

The investable unit is a non-PEO independent payroll bureau serving small and midsize employers under recurring processing agreements. AI can compress its exception, inquiry, reconciliation, and document work, but the observable AI investment and distribution power sit chiefly with scaled platforms. A local bureau may retain some near-term process savings, yet there is no public independent-bureau evidence that quantifies that retention. The mature risk is not that payroll tax work vanishes; it is that the platform, embedded accounting system, bank, or POS owner controls the customer relationship and captures the economics [S1][S2][S3][S4][S5].

## Target archetype

The named archetype is a US independent payroll bureau with recurring managed payroll, payroll-tax filing/remittance support, and service staff for SMB and lower-middle-market employers. It excludes PEO/co-employment businesses, staffing, employer-of-record operations, pure payroll software vendors, and national public HCM platforms. This is the relevant acquirable operator rather than the whole code, but the supplied 100–499-employee count cannot be allocated to independents versus platform, software, or other operations from published sources. Coverage is therefore deliberately MISSING rather than asserted as a fraction of the supplied band.

## How AI changes the work

AI and workflow automation can reduce repetitive payroll checking, anomaly detection, data gathering, client inquiries, report creation, and implementation support. ADP describes agentic AI for payroll roles, payroll anomaly validation, missing-tax-registration resolution, service, implementation, and development work [S1]. Those are meaningful task analogs, but the supplied 4-digit occupational mix is CPA-firm-heavy and omits the payroll-clerk occupation; the role fractions are bounded assumptions, not a measurement of the target archetype.

## Value capture

Payroll fees are commonly structured as recurring processing fees with a base component and a per-employee or per-transaction component; Paychex describes that structure and Gusto publicly posts per-person pricing [S2][S4]. Recurrence may let a bureau keep efficiency initially, but it does not establish a 24-month retained-savings share. Platforms can package payroll with HR, accounting integrations, financing, and partner-channel incentives, while ADP and Paychex also earn material client-fund interest that a typical bureau may not replicate [S1][S2][S3]. The packet therefore leaves retained savings MISSING rather than converting this adverse competitive evidence into unsupported capture precision.

## Adoption timing

ADP's current product roadmap documents AI-enabled payroll workflows and internal service and implementation use, demonstrating that the relevant capabilities are already commercial at a scaled provider [S1]. It does not measure effective AI use among independent bureaus, nor the time at which half of them will materially change the tasks represented in the labor estimate. Tool availability is not effective adoption. Both present adoption and time to 50% are consequently MISSING pending a bureau-specific survey or operating dataset.

## Operator survival

At mature AI adoption the base survival class is DISINTERMEDIATED for the acquired independent bureau: payroll remains a paid and accountable function, but product, data, compliance automation, and distribution can migrate to platforms. ADP serves over 1.1 million clients, is building AI agents for payroll work, operates broad SMB platforms, and earns interest on held client funds [S1]. Gusto's accountant program combines a multi-client dashboard, migration support, revenue sharing, and a large partner directory [S3]. IRS reporting-agent rules preserve a legitimate intermediary function but do not reserve it for a local bureau [S5]. PRESSURED and MELTING remain plausible alternatives because local niche service may persist while direct/embedded platforms could also remove the bureau's role.

## Risks and uncertainty

The principal uncertainty is archetype scope: the supplied role mix and implied EBITDA band are not a bureau census. A second uncertainty is whether a rolled-up bureau can preserve local-accountant referrals, state-compliance expertise, and client trust despite platform migration. Public evidence is strongest for the platform side, not for target-bureau economics. The extreme supplied labor-share figure has vintage and revenue-netting limitations, and the role allocation has an unusually large residual. These limitations widen the usefulness of the result and require target-level diligence before any investment decision.
