## Executive view

The screen is for a US institutional language-services provider whose recurring healthcare, government, or court-access work is predominantly live interpretation, with managed written translation as a secondary service. This is deliberately not a screen for a translation-heavy localization agency: written translation has a much more direct AI substitution path, whereas qualified live interpretation remains tied to statutory access obligations and real-time coordination. The operator can still face material price, platform, and procurement pressure, so a persistent underlying need does not by itself establish durable local-operator economics.

## Target archetype

The named archetype is a 100–499-employee US institutional interpreting-led language-services provider serving healthcare systems, public agencies, courts, insurers, or similarly regulated enterprises through OPI, VRI, scheduled on-site interpretation, dispatch, and compliance administration. It includes firms with recurring institutional accounts, a managed interpreter network, and some certified or regulated document translation. It excludes translation/localization-first agencies, freelance-only linguist marketplaces, sign-language/VRS specialists, software-only platforms, and general staffing firms. The supplied firm band does not disclose this revenue mix, so no defensible share of its 28 firms can be assigned to the archetype.

## How AI changes the work

For written translation, machine translation, post-editing, terminology handling, file preparation, QA triage, and project coordination reduce labor per word and make commodity output easier to buy from a platform or perform in-house. For interpreting, AI can automate intake, routing, scheduling, documentation, and some remote speech workflows, but live healthcare and court interactions still require a qualified, accountable human interpreter in many situations. The relevant distinction is therefore not whether AI is used: it is whether the revenue is live qualified interpretation or commoditized written translation, and whether the LSP retains the institutional relationship and operating layer.

## Value capture

Near-term savings may arise in interpreter matching, appointment administration, invoicing, workflow management, translation production, and project management. ## Operator survival

The operator is assessed as pressured rather than automatically melting. HHS identifies oral language assistance and written translation as means of meaningful access for covered health programs, and federal courts maintain qualification categories and contract-interpreter rates. Those requirements support continued paid demand for qualified interpretation, especially live encounters, but do not guarantee that a local LSP keeps the relationship: larger platforms can centralize scheduling, data, compliance, and procurement, while written translation can be bypassed or repriced. At mature AI adoption, an accountable institutional operator can survive, but its mix must be overwhelmingly live, qualified, and contract-controlled rather than commodity document translation.

## Risks and uncertainty

The largest uncertainties are the archetype share of the 28 deterministic firms, 24-month retained savings after client repricing, actual buyer and seller supply, and private EBITDA multiples. The supplied wage mix is a 4-digit 5419 proxy dominated by veterinary occupations and is not representative of an LSP; it is used only because the frozen dataset requires every supplied SOC. Freelance linguists also commonly sit in cost of goods sold, so wage-based V can understate language-production exposure while still failing to measure margin capture. Translation and interpretation must be diligence-separated in any real target, including revenue, pricing, renewal, qualification, and client concentration by line of service.
