Every evidence-bearing input separates:

## Frozen research rules

1. PE/M&A activity informs only B and M, never V or A.
2. Multiples are EV/EBITDA after market-rate owner compensation. State unit, target size, source population and every normalization.
3. A zero consolidator count is an absence-of-evidence estimate, not a directly sourced fact. State the searches performed; do not cite a page as proving zero.
4. `current_adoption_pct` means the share of US firms in this NAICS materially using AI in automatable functions. Individual tool use and broad-sector use are different populations and require a visible derivation.
5. `t50_years` means years from today until 50% material firm adoption. If current adoption is already at least 50%, set `t50_years.value` to `0`; otherwise it must be positive.
6. Payer/regulatory repricing belongs in C when it determines who retains cost savings. Terminal class separately answers whether paid end demand survives mature AI; do not count the same clawback twice.
7. A statutory human-in-the-loop boundary affects terminal class only to the extent it preserves paid demand. It does not make automatable tasks non-automatable.
8. If a W-2 dataset labor share is distorted by 1099-heavy labor, use the injected value unchanged, flag `DATASET_MISMATCH`, lower confidence as appropriate and show sensitivity. Do not silently adjust it.
9. Catch-all codes: identify the top three revenue subsegments, score the dominant subsegment, set `heterogeneous=true`, and disclose the boundary.

## Industry-specific guidance — hints, not facts

**Likely role structure:**
- **Lawyers (partners and associates combined)** — ~62% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 23-1011 Lawyers 62.15% of wage bill (4-digit 5411 mix; for 541110 proper the lawyer share is likely even higher since siblings 541191/541199 dilute it)); automatable: partial. OEWS does not split partners from associates, and partner profit draws often sit outside QCEW wages entirely. Research, memo and brief drafting, doc review, first-draft contracts and discovery are heavily AI-exposed; court appearances, depositions, negotiation, client counseling, signing pleadings and bar-licensure liability are not. The run should split this role by seniority/task mix when setting the within-role fraction — plausibly 0.25-0.45 blended, but justify from task evidence.
- **Paralegals and legal assistants** — ~13% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 23-2011 Paralegals and Legal Assistants 13.12%); automatable: partial. Document assembly, cite-checking, discovery review, e-filing prep and docketing are directly automatable; witness/client coordination and courthouse logistics less so. One of the highest within-role fractions in the firm.
- **Legal secretaries and office administrative staff** — ~9% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-6012 Legal Secretaries 5.51% + 43-6014 Other Secretaries 0.77% + 43-9061 Office Clerks 1.00% + 43-4171 Receptionists 0.73% + 43-1011 Admin Supervisors 1.32%); automatable: yes. Scheduling, dictation/formatting, intake paperwork, filing and phone triage — near-fully automatable; this layer has been shrinking for a decade already.
- **Firm management and billing/finance staff** — ~3% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 2.29% + 43-3031 Bookkeeping Clerks 0.98%); automatable: partial. Time-entry, prebill and collections workflow automates; HR, vendor and partner-compensation judgment persists.
- **Title examiners and abstractors** — ~1% (likely near zero in 541110 proper) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 23-2093 Title Examiners, Abstractors, and Searchers 0.82% — this occupation is 541191 contamination bleeding into the 4-digit 5411 mix; largely absent from pure law offices); automatable: yes. Treat as a sibling-code artifact; do not build a role around it for law offices.
- **Long tail (law clerks, IT, marketing/BD, records, litigation support)** — ~11% of wage bill (OEWS basis: not in listed top-10; est. remainder ~11% of wage bill); automatable: partial. Records, litigation-support processing and marketing content automate; business development is relationship work.

**Suggested sources:**
- (adoption) ABA Legal Technology Survey Report (ABA Legal Technology Resource Center, annual) — Primary lawyer-population data on AI tool adoption by firm size — directly usable for current_adoption_pct.
- (adoption) Clio Legal Trends Report (annual) — Utilization, realization and AI-adoption data drawn from small/mid firm practice-management telemetry — the segment that matches the $1-10M EBITDA band.
- (adoption) Thomson Reuters Future of Professionals Report (annual) — Cross-professional AI adoption and expected-impact survey covering law firms.
- (capture_pricing) Thomson Reuters / Georgetown Law State of the Legal Market report (annual) — Rate, realization and demand-hours trends — the evidence base for whether hourly billing passes AI savings to clients.
- (capture_pricing) Legal Services Corporation Justice Gap Report — Latent unmet consumer demand — relevant to whether volume expansion offsets price deflation at the consumer end.
- (succession_owners) ABA Profile of the Legal Profession (annual) — Lawyer age distribution (large 60+ cohort) for owners_60plus_pct; solo/small-firm demographics.
- (consolidators_multiples) BVR DealStats and BizBuySell Insight Report — The sparse small-practice comp set (consumer practices, PI books); the thinness of arms-length law-firm comps is itself a finding.
- (consolidators_multiples) Trade coverage of MSO and Arizona ABS deals (ALM / Above the Law / Law.com reporting) — The only real windows into PE structures for law — Rule 5.4 bans non-lawyer ownership except AZ ABS and UT sandbox; verify current status of both programs.

**Capture questions:**
- The dominant billing model is the hourly rate: when AI cuts the hours a matter takes, does the saving flow to the client as a smaller bill (hours-billed deflation) or can firms hold effective price via flat/value fees and higher rates? What does rate-vs-realization evidence show since 2023, and how far along is the shift away from hourly in the small-firm segment? Note contingency work (plaintiff PI) is structurally immune — fee is % of recovery.
- Is the client relationship owned by the firm or portable with the individual partner — what share of small-firm revenue survives a rainmaker's exit or an acquisition, and what does that do to transferable enterprise value?
- For consumer/SMB matters (wills, LLC formation, uncontested divorce, demand letters), how much volume can bypass lawyers entirely via generic AI plus e-filing before UPL enforcement bites — and are corporate clients pulling routine work in-house (GC plus AI)?
- What fraction of billings rests on procedural mandates (court appearances, rights of audience, signed and attorney-certified filings) that AI cannot legally perform regardless of capability?

**Terminal-value question:**
Licensure, rights of audience and malpractice liability mean a human lawyer must still sign and appear — but the billable-hour base underneath is what clients actually pay for. In 5-10 years, does paid demand persist at today's scale because bar rules and courts require human counsel, or does the industry compress to a stamp-appear-and-judgment layer with far fewer billed hours, with justice-gap latent demand only partially offsetting hourly-fee deflation? Distinguish AI cutting the firm's cost of production from AI shrinking the product (hours) the firm sells.

**Known traps:**
- Pricing-model deflation candidate: hourly billing converts AI efficiency directly into fewer billed hours unless firms reprice to flat/value fees — model this explicitly in pi_dist, and treat contingency (plaintiff) practices as a separate, deflation-resistant sub-segment.
- Ownership restriction is the central B constraint: ABA Model Rule 5.4 (adopted in nearly every state) bans non-lawyer ownership and fee-sharing; exceptions are Arizona's ABS licenses and Utah's sandbox. PE entry elsewhere uses MSO structures whose economics and enforceability the run should verify — do not assume clean buyouts.
- EBITDA normalization: small-firm 'profit' is overwhelmingly owner/partner compensation (SDE); partner draws are distributions, not QCEW wages, so both the labor_share figure and any quoted practice-sale multiples need owner-comp normalization before use.
- Key-person portability: client books travel with partners; covenants-not-to-compete are ethically restricted for lawyers (Model Rule 5.6), making retention riskier than in other professions.
- Segment economics diverge sharply — plaintiff contingency, insurance defense (hourly, carrier rate-capped, audited legal bills), corporate transactional, consumer flat-fee. Score the dominant small-firm mix and note heterogeneity rather than blending incompatible fee models.
- Boundary confusion: 4-digit data (including the provided role_mix at 5411) blends in 541191 title/settlement and 541199 process servers/doc preparers; ABA and Clio data cover lawyers, not those siblings — check coverage before quoting.
- The record's size-class data show 89% of the 156,583 firms have <10 employees and implied EBITDA far below $1M — the acquirable band (n_band 7,193) sits almost entirely in the 20-99 employee class; evidence about BigLaw does not transfer to these targets.

## Frozen formulas — Python calculates them

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`
- `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)`
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = 10` when `t50_years=0`, otherwise `min(10, 10 / t50_years)`
- `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)`
- `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; add 0.1 for documented succession shortage, cap at 1
- `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
- `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`
