## What you author

You author evidence and judgment inputs in a `research_draft_v3_1` JSON object. You do **not** author deterministic dataset objects, role wage shares, contributions, factor scores, `S`, `uplift_pp` or a verdict. Plain Python injects and calculates those fields.

Every evidence-bearing input separates:

- `value` — selected value used by the formulas;
- `provenance_type` — `DIRECT`, `DERIVED` or `ESTIMATE`;
- `evidence_quality` — `HIGH`, `MED`, `LOW` or `NONE`;
- `source_fact` — only what fetched citations actually establish;
- `derivation` — the complete bridge from source fact to selected value;
- `plausible_range`;
- `citations[]` — atomic fetched citations with URL, title, exact short quote, access date and scope.

`provenance_type` and `evidence_quality` are independent. An unsourced estimate must use `ESTIMATE` + `NONE` + `citations: []`. A proxy with a strong source is usually `DERIVED` plus an evidence quality reflecting the source and scope fit.

## Frozen research rules

1. PE/M&A activity informs only B and M, never V or A.
2. Multiples are EV/EBITDA after market-rate owner compensation. State unit, target size, source population and every normalization.
3. A zero consolidator count is an absence-of-evidence estimate, not a directly sourced fact. State the searches performed; do not cite a page as proving zero.
4. `current_adoption_pct` means the share of US firms in this NAICS materially using AI in automatable functions. Individual tool use and broad-sector use are different populations and require a visible derivation.
5. `t50_years` means years from today until 50% material firm adoption. If current adoption is already at least 50%, set `t50_years.value` to `0`; otherwise it must be positive.
6. Payer/regulatory repricing belongs in C when it determines who retains cost savings. Terminal class separately answers whether paid end demand survives mature AI; do not count the same clawback twice.
7. A statutory human-in-the-loop boundary affects terminal class only to the extent it preserves paid demand. It does not make automatable tasks non-automatable.
8. If a W-2 dataset labor share is distorted by 1099-heavy labor, use the injected value unchanged, flag `DATASET_MISMATCH`, lower confidence as appropriate and show sensitivity. Do not silently adjust it.
9. Catch-all codes: identify the top three revenue subsegments, score the dominant subsegment, set `heterogeneous=true`, and disclose the boundary.

## Industry-specific guidance — hints, not facts

**Likely role structure:**
- **Consultants — process, supply chain and logistics analysts** — ~28-32% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1111 Management Analysts 15.5% of wage bill + 13-1199 Business Operations Specialists 2.9%; note oews_level is 4-digit 5416 proxy — for this code the tail includes 13-1081 Logisticians and 17-2112 Industrial Engineers, est. ~8-12% more); automatable: partial. Network modeling, freight-rate analysis, inventory optimization studies, current-state assessments and report writing are heavily AI-exposed; shop-floor observation, stakeholder interviews and change management on site persist. Plausible within-role fraction 0.3-0.5.
- **Owners and management layer** — ~16-18% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 13.5% + tail managers est. ~4% more); automatable: partial. Client relationships with VPs of supply chain persist; internal coordination and reporting automate.
- **Project and engagement management** — ~4-5% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1082 Project Management Specialists 4.2%); automatable: partial. Implementation programs (WMS/TMS deployments, network redesigns) need on-site coordination; status and schedule paperwork automates.
- **Sales and business development** — ~5% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-3091 Sales Representatives of Services 3.6% + 43-4051 Customer Service Representatives 1.4%); automatable: partial. Proposal production automates; relationship selling to operations executives does not.
- **Software and analytics staff** — ~6-7% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 15-1252 Software Developers 3.5% + data/analytics tail est. ~3-4% more); automatable: partial. Larger firms embed configuration/integration work for supply chain software; AI-augmented rather than eliminated.
- **Back office and admin** — ~12-14% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-2011 Accountants and Auditors 2.2% + 13-1071 HR Specialists 1.7% + office/admin long tail est. ~8-10% more); automatable: yes. Freight-audit clerical work, invoicing, data entry and document handling are the most directly automatable lines.
- **Remaining long tail (engineering, field, misc.)** — ~20-25% of wage bill (OEWS basis: not in the listed top-10; 4-digit top-10 covers only ~53% of wage bill — remainder includes industrial engineers, logisticians and field/implementation staff); automatable: partial. Facility layout, time studies and site work have a physical component; analysis and documentation automate.

**Suggested sources:**
- (adoption) CSCMP Annual State of Logistics Report (with Kearney) — The standard logistics-market reference; recent editions discuss AI/digitization in logistics services — context for adoption pace among advisors and their clients.
- (adoption) MHI Annual Industry Report (with Deloitte) — Tracks adoption of AI and automation technologies across supply chain operations — usable for current_adoption_pct direction.
- (adoption) Gartner supply chain technology surveys — Client-side AI adoption in supply chain planning — evidence for both adoption and the bypass question.
- (capture_pricing) SPI Research Professional Services Maturity Benchmark (annual) — Billing-model and realized-rate data across consulting — evidence for whether hourly/project fees get rescoped as hours fall. *(existence unverified — confirm this source still exists/publishes before relying on it)*
- (consolidators_multiples) Equiteq specialty consulting M&A reports (semiannual) — Consulting-sector deal counts and EV/EBITDA multiples, including operations/supply-chain consultancies.
- (consolidators_multiples) BizBuySell Insight Report (quarterly) — Small-business sale multiples (SDE basis) for the micro tier — 5,724 of 7,630 firms have <5 employees.

**Capture questions:**
- Logistics consulting spans hourly/project fees and gainshare contracts (freight audit and cost-reduction work often priced as a % of realized savings) — under gainshare the operator keeps AI savings by construction, under T&M the client claws them back at rescope. What share of revenue sits under each fee mechanism?
- Who owns the client relationship — the firm's framework agreements with shippers/3PLs, or individual principals whose books walk?
- What stops clients from using AI-native supply chain software (network design, rate benchmarking, forecasting tools) plus internal staff to bypass consultants entirely — is advice being productized into software this operator doesn't own?
- Do implementation and change-management components (WMS/TMS deployment, warehouse redesign) anchor engagements against pure-advice AI substitution, and how much revenue is implementation vs analysis?

**Terminal-value question:**
Nothing statutory protects logistics advice; the mechanisms that could hold demand are physical implementation (facilities, equipment, labor on the floor) and proprietary benchmark data. When mature AI plus supply-chain software does network design, rate analysis and forecasting natively, does paid demand persist for the physical-implementation slice only — and is this operator's revenue actually in that slice, or in the analysis slice AI replaces? Distinguish AI cutting delivery cost from AI (embedded in software) replacing the advisory product.

**Known traps:**
- Pricing-model split is the capture crux: gainshare/contingency work (common in freight audit and cost recovery) is capture-friendly; T&M/project work is a deflation candidate — model pi_dist by fee-mechanism mix rather than one number.
- EBITDA normalization: micro-firm profit is SDE with owner comp inside; note also that class 500+ firms (151) sit IN the $1-10M band per the record's chain — the band here includes some large-headcount, low-margin firms, unusual vs sibling codes.
- Boundary confusion: 541611 (general management consulting) absorbs many ops-consulting firms; freight brokers/3PLs are 488510/493, and freight-audit-and-payment firms straddle 541614/561499 — market reports on 'supply chain consulting' rarely isolate this code.
- Role-mix caveat: the record's role_mix is the 4-digit 5416 proxy; expect this code to carry more logisticians and industrial engineers and fewer marketing occupations than the listed shares.
- Cyclicality: demand tracks freight cycles and supply-chain disruption waves (post-2020 bulge, 2023-2024 freight recession) — single-year revenue and margin data can badly misstate trend.

## Frozen formulas — Python calculates them

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`
- `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)`
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = 10` when `t50_years=0`, otherwise `min(10, 10 / t50_years)`
- `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)`
- `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; add 0.1 for documented succession shortage, cap at 1
- `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
- `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`
