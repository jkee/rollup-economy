Every evidence-bearing input separates:

- `provenance_type` — `DIRECT`, `DERIVED` or `ESTIMATE`;
- `evidence_quality` — `HIGH`, `MED`, `LOW` or `NONE`;

`provenance_type` and `evidence_quality` are independent. An unsourced estimate must use `ESTIMATE` + `NONE` + `citations: []`. A proxy with a strong source is usually `DERIVED` plus an evidence quality reflecting the source and scope fit.

## Frozen research rules

1. PE/M&A activity informs only B and M, never V or A.
2. Multiples are EV/EBITDA after market-rate owner compensation. State unit, target size, source population and every normalization.
3. A zero consolidator count is an absence-of-evidence estimate, not a directly sourced fact. State the searches performed; do not cite a page as proving zero.
4. `current_adoption_pct` means the share of US firms in this NAICS materially using AI in automatable functions. Individual tool use and broad-sector use are different populations and require a visible derivation.
5. `t50_years` means years from today until 50% material firm adoption. If current adoption is already at least 50%, set `t50_years.value` to `0`; otherwise it must be positive.
6. Payer/regulatory repricing belongs in C when it determines who retains cost savings. Terminal class separately answers whether paid end demand survives mature AI; do not count the same clawback twice.
7. A statutory human-in-the-loop boundary affects terminal class only to the extent it preserves paid demand. It does not make automatable tasks non-automatable.
8. If a W-2 dataset labor share is distorted by 1099-heavy labor, use the injected value unchanged, flag `DATASET_MISMATCH`, lower confidence as appropriate and show sensitivity. Do not silently adjust it.
9. Catch-all codes: identify the top three revenue subsegments, score the dominant subsegment, set `heterogeneous=true`, and disclose the boundary.

## Industry-specific guidance — hints, not facts

**Likely role structure:**
- **Consultants and analysts (core billable staff)** — ~22-25% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1111 Management Analysts 15.5% of wage bill + 13-1199 Business Operations Specialists 2.9% + 13-2011 Accountants & Auditors 2.2% + 13-1071 HR Specialists 1.7% (role_mix is 4-digit 5416)); automatable: partial. Research, benchmarking, data analysis, slide/deliverable production and report drafting — the analyst workload — is among the most AI-exposed white-collar task sets anywhere; client interviews, workshop facilitation, political navigation and judgment on ambiguous problems persist. Within-role fraction plausibly 0.4-0.6 for delivery work.
- **Partners, principals and firm management** — ~14-16% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General & Operations Managers 13.5% — in consulting this occupation largely IS the partner/principal layer); automatable: no. Rainmaking, client-executive trust and engagement leadership — the relationship layer that constitutes the firm's revenue engine; only its internal reporting/admin slice automates.
- **Marketing and business development** — ~8% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1161 Market Research Analysts & Marketing Specialists 4.1% + 41-3091 Sales Representatives of Services 3.6%); automatable: partial. Thought-leadership content, proposals and RFP responses automate heavily; relationship-based selling persists.
- **Project/engagement management** — ~4% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1082 Project Management Specialists 4.2%); automatable: partial. Workplans, status decks and budget tracking automate; client steering-committee management persists.
- **Technology staff** — ~3-4% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 15-1252 Software Developers 3.5%); automatable: partial. Implementation tooling and analytics builds — AI-augmented; concentrated in ops/digital-transformation practices.
- **Client service and support staff** — ~4-5% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-4051 Customer Service Representatives 1.4% + admin tail); automatable: yes. Scheduling, engagement admin, document production — near-fully automatable.
- **Unallocated tail** — ~40-45% of wage bill (OEWS basis: remainder beyond the listed top-10 (top-10 sums to ~53% of wages), est. ~40-45% — junior consultants, researchers, back office); automatable: partial. The tail is large because consulting titles map poorly to SOC codes; most of it is additional analyst-pattern delivery labor (high AI exposure) plus back office (high) — the blended within-role fraction for the tail should track the analyst role.

**Capture questions:**
- Boutique management consulting bills as daily/hourly rates or fixed fees benchmarked to expected effort — when AI cuts analyst hours per deliverable by half, do clients accept unchanged fixed fees (paying for judgment, not hours), or do procurement departments and competing boutiques reprice engagements down? Is there any documented shift to value/outcome-based pricing at the sub-$25M-revenue tier, or only at the strategy-brand tier that can command it?
- Who owns the client relationship — the firm or the founding partner(s)? In boutiques the book is personal; earnouts and lockups in consulting M&A exist precisely because value walks. What does deal structure evidence say about how much survives a sale?
- What stops clients from using generic AI directly for the analysis/benchmarking/deck work they currently buy — is the boutique's residual product accountability, industry-specific data, facilitation, or executive cover ('we hired outside experts'), and which of those survives when the client's own staff wield equivalent AI?
- Does the massive solo/micro tail (80k of 93.5k firms under 5 employees) armed with AI become the price-setting competition for the $1-10M EBITDA boutiques — a single senior ex-partner with AI leverage underbidding a 30-person firm?

**Terminal-value question:**
General management consulting has no license, no statute, no physical act — its paid demand rests on judgment, trust and executive cover, wrapped around an analyst pyramid whose output AI now generates. The mechanism to interrogate: the leverage model itself (partners selling junior hours at markup) — if AI deletes the pyramid, does a paid market remain for senior judgment alone at boutique scale, or do clients' own AI-equipped staff plus brand-name strategy firms squeeze the middle out entirely? Distinguish AI cutting delivery cost (bullish only if fees hold) from AI dissolving the thing fees were anchored to — billable analyst effort.

**Known traps:**
- Effort-anchored pricing is the deflation channel: fees (hourly, daily, or fixed-but-effort-benchmarked) reprice downward as AI compresses effort, unless the firm sells outcomes — model in pi_dist and look for actual repricing evidence, not vendor claims.
- Fragmentation extreme even by sector-54 standards: n_total 93,514, of which 79,966 are sub-5-employee firms; n_band 3,853 sits in the 20-99 (2,992) and 100-499 (861) classes. The code also spans strategy, operations, PMO, compliance and generalist advisory — treat as highly heterogeneous and identify what the in-band firms actually sell before scoring.
- Key-person portability is the M-side trap: consulting acquisitions price with heavy earnouts because the client book is personal; quoted headline multiples overstate cash-at-close economics — normalize deal-structure evidence.
- EBITDA normalization: partner/owner draws inside SDE; boutique 'EBITDA' often excludes market-rate partner comp — normalize before comparing to platform multiples.
- Boundary confusion: HR consulting is 541612, marketing consulting 541613, process/logistics 541614, environmental 541620, 'other' consulting 541618/541690, IT-strategy work drifts into 541512 — surveys of 'consulting' (Source, Hinge) span all of these plus the strategy giants; check the frame, and beware Big-4/MBB dynamics being generalized to the boutique tier this screen targets.
- Cyclicality: consulting demand is discretionary and cyclical; 2022 receipts sit at a post-COVID spending peak — check 2024-2026 direction before trusting level-based inputs.

## Frozen formulas — Python calculates them

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`
- `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)`
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = 10` when `t50_years=0`, otherwise `min(10, 10 / t50_years)`
- `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)`
- `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; add 0.1 for documented succession shortage, cap at 1
- `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
- `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`
