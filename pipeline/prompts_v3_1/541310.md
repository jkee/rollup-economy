Every evidence-bearing input separates:

## Frozen research rules

1. PE/M&A activity informs only B and M, never V or A.
2. Multiples are EV/EBITDA after market-rate owner compensation. State unit, target size, source population and every normalization.
3. A zero consolidator count is an absence-of-evidence estimate, not a directly sourced fact. State the searches performed; do not cite a page as proving zero.
4. `current_adoption_pct` means the share of US firms in this NAICS materially using AI in automatable functions. Individual tool use and broad-sector use are different populations and require a visible derivation.
5. `t50_years` means years from today until 50% material firm adoption. If current adoption is already at least 50%, set `t50_years.value` to `0`; otherwise it must be positive.
6. Payer/regulatory repricing belongs in C when it determines who retains cost savings. Terminal class separately answers whether paid end demand survives mature AI; do not count the same clawback twice.
7. A statutory human-in-the-loop boundary affects terminal class only to the extent it preserves paid demand. It does not make automatable tasks non-automatable.
8. If a W-2 dataset labor share is distorted by 1099-heavy labor, use the injected value unchanged, flag `DATASET_MISMATCH`, lower confidence as appropriate and show sensitivity. Do not silently adjust it.
9. Catch-all codes: identify the top three revenue subsegments, score the dominant subsegment, set `heterogeneous=true`, and disclose the boundary.

- (other) DBIA / FMI design-build market studies — Design-build growth shifts the client relationship to contractors — the structural capture threat.

**Capture questions:**
- Architecture fees are commonly a percentage of construction cost or a lump sum benchmarked to it, with construction-document production the largest phase: when AI collapses CD production hours, does the percentage-of-cost convention let firms keep the spread as margin — or do owners and design-builders renegotiate fee percentages downward once production cost visibly drops, and how fast has that repricing happened in past productivity shifts (CAD, BIM)?
- Do owners/developers hire the architect for design judgment and brand, or only because permitting requires a stamped set — and how much of the fee sits in automatable production phases versus relationship phases (SD, CA)?
- Does design-build growth shift the client relationship to contractors, reducing architects to subcontracted stamp providers with fee-taker economics?
- In residential and light-commercial work that is often exempt from architect requirements, does AI design-to-permit tooling bypass architects entirely — and what share of small-firm revenue sits in those exempt segments?

**Terminal-value question:**
If AI can generate code-compliant permit sets, architecture bifurcates into a liability/stamp utility plus a taste-and-brand layer at the high end. The deciding mechanism is the permit counter: do state licensure laws and building departments keep requiring a licensed architect in responsible charge for the building types small firms serve (noting broad residential exemptions), preserving a toll even as production fees deflate — or does the exempt-plus-plan-stamp perimeter expand until the paid product shrinks to judgment and liability at a fraction of today's fee pool? Distinguish AI cutting the firm's production cost from AI shrinking the stamped-set product the firm sells.

**Known traps:**
- Role-mix warning: the provided role_mix is 4-digit 5413 and engineering-contaminated — Civil Engineers (12.8%) outrank Architects (5.82%) only because 541330 dwarfs 541310 at the parent level; rebuild shares for an architecture office and say so.
- Pricing-model deflation candidate: percentage-of-construction-cost and lump-sum fees are anchored to historical effort norms — past tooling shifts (CAD, BIM) eventually repriced fees downward; model pi_dist on that precedent rather than assuming the percentage convention protects margin.
- Ownership restrictions: several states restrict architecture-firm ownership to licensed professionals (New York notably strict; rules vary widely) — check the state mix before assuming clean PE buyouts; A&E consolidators do buy architecture bolt-ons, so structures exist.
- EBITDA normalization: principal comp inside small-firm profit; Zweig-style multiples often pre-owner-comp — normalize before comparing (rule 3). ABI-tracked cyclicality is severe; do not score off a single boom or bust year.
- Residential and interiors segments sit partially outside licensure — different exposure profile within the same code; note the mix rather than applying one fraction.
- Boundary confusion: engineering is 541330, landscape architecture 541320, drafting services 541340, interior design 541410, building inspection 541350 — A&E surveys (Deltek, Zweig, Morrissey Goodale) span the group; check whose members a benchmark covers before quoting it for architecture alone.

## Frozen formulas — Python calculates them

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`
- `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)`
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = 10` when `t50_years=0`, otherwise `min(10, 10 / t50_years)`
- `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)`
- `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; add 0.1 for documented succession shortage, cap at 1
- `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
- `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`
