## What you author

Every evidence-bearing input separates:

- `value` — selected value used by the formulas;
- `provenance_type` — `DIRECT`, `DERIVED` or `ESTIMATE`;
- `evidence_quality` — `HIGH`, `MED`, `LOW` or `NONE`;
- `source_fact` — only what fetched citations actually establish;

- `plausible_range`;

`provenance_type` and `evidence_quality` are independent. An unsourced estimate must use `ESTIMATE` + `NONE` + `citations: []`. A proxy with a strong source is usually `DERIVED` plus an evidence quality reflecting the source and scope fit.

## Frozen research rules

1. PE/M&A activity informs only B and M, never V or A.
2. Multiples are EV/EBITDA after market-rate owner compensation. State unit, target size, source population and every normalization.
3. A zero consolidator count is an absence-of-evidence estimate, not a directly sourced fact. State the searches performed; do not cite a page as proving zero.
4. `current_adoption_pct` means the share of US firms in this NAICS materially using AI in automatable functions. Individual tool use and broad-sector use are different populations and require a visible derivation.
5. `t50_years` means years from today until 50% material firm adoption. If current adoption is already at least 50%, set `t50_years.value` to `0`; otherwise it must be positive.
6. Payer/regulatory repricing belongs in C when it determines who retains cost savings. Terminal class separately answers whether paid end demand survives mature AI; do not count the same clawback twice.
7. A statutory human-in-the-loop boundary affects terminal class only to the extent it preserves paid demand. It does not make automatable tasks non-automatable.
8. If a W-2 dataset labor share is distorted by 1099-heavy labor, use the injected value unchanged, flag `DATASET_MISMATCH`, lower confidence as appropriate and show sensitivity. Do not silently adjust it.
9. Catch-all codes: identify the top three revenue subsegments, score the dominant subsegment, set `heterogeneous=true`, and disclose the boundary.

**Capture questions:**
- Translation is priced per word and interpreting per minute/hour — MTPE discount schedules (30-50% off per-word rates) already show AI savings being passed to clients as lower prices, not kept as margin; what evidence shows any LSP holding price while its production cost collapses, and does interpreting's per-minute pricing follow the same deflation path as AI speech translation matures?
- Who owns the client relationship — the LSP (contracted language-access programs with hospital systems, courts, insurers) or the freelance linguist the end user actually sees? Which contracts (statewide court contracts, health-system language-access agreements) are institutional and transferable in an acquisition?
- What stops enterprise clients from bypassing LSPs with LLM translation plus an in-house reviewer — and for which revenue lines (certified translations for USCIS/courts, sworn documents, regulated medical device labeling under ISO 17100-type requirements) does a certification or liability gate genuinely block the bypass?
- In mandated interpreting (Section 1557 healthcare, court access), the payer often reimburses per encounter at set rates — when AI cuts the LSP's delivery cost, do public payers and hospital procurement re-rate the contracts downward at renewal, clawing back the savings?

**Terminal-value question:**
This is the canonical melter test: LLMs produce the product itself — translated text — at near-zero marginal cost, so the question is precisely which revenue survives as paid demand: statutorily mandated live interpretation (Section 1557 healthcare encounters, court certification requirements, ADA/deaf services), certification-gated document translation (USCIS, sworn/legal), and liability-bearing regulated content — versus ordinary business translation, which is already collapsing into MT-with-review at falling per-word rates. A researcher who scores this code on current LSP revenue growth (interpreting demand is still rising on demographics and enforcement) without separating the mandate-protected floor from the substitutable bulk will get terminal value wrong in either direction. What fraction of this operator's revenue sits behind a statute or certification, and what happens to the rest at mature AI?

**Known traps:**
- Role-mix contamination is total: the 4-digit 5419 proxy is veterinary-dominated (541940 is the giant sibling); 27-3091 Interpreters and Translators shows only 2.1%. Rebuild the V breakdown from LSP structure (linguists/PMs/scheduling), and note that most production linguists are 1099 freelancers — their cost sits in COGS, not the wage bill, so the 0.4178 labor share understates the true language-labor content AI attacks. This materially changes V: AI savings land partly in gross margin (freelance spend), not just opex.
- Pricing-model deflation is documented, not hypothetical: per-word pricing with MTPE discount tiers is the industry standard, and per-word rates have been falling for a decade. Model pi_dist with the per-word/per-minute rate-card channel explicit; the operator keeps savings only where contracts are fixed-fee or mandate-protected.
- Segment split is everything: interpreting (esp. healthcare OPI/VRI and court) is growing, mandate-protected and physically/real-time delivered; written translation is the melting half. Blended firm-level financials mask this — insist the run separates the two before scoring V, A or terminal value.
- Consolidation is active and well covered (Propio, Sorenson Communications, Big Language Solutions, TransPerfect, LanguageLine/Teleperformance, court-contract roll-ups) — usable for B and M only, never as evidence for V or A (rule 2). Slator reports deal multiples; use those rather than generic services comps.
- EBITDA normalization: LSP gross margins run 40-50% over freelance COGS; small-agency 'profit' includes owner comp (SDE). Also watch revenue recognition on interpreting minutes vs. translation projects when comparing margins across firms.
- Boundary confusion: sign-language interpreting providers (Sorenson) straddle this code and telecom relay services funded by the FCC TRS fund; localization technology platforms (TMS vendors) are software 511210/513210; captioning/subtitling straddles 541930 and 512191 — Nimdzi/Slator market sizes include tech revenue; strip it before mapping to the code's $6.1B receipts.
- Structure note: 2,193 of 2,674 firms are <5 employees and n_band is only 28 (the 100-499 class) — the roll-up universe is thin, and the biggest firms (15 firms, 500+, avg $211M revenue) are already consolidator-owned. Golden-set melter anchor: be conservative about cost-savings framing; substitution dominates.

## Frozen formulas — Python calculates them

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`
- `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)`
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = 10` when `t50_years=0`, otherwise `min(10, 10 / t50_years)`
- `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)`
- `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; add 0.1 for documented succession shortage, cap at 1
- `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
- `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`
