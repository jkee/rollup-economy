## What you author

You author evidence and judgment inputs in a `research_draft_v3_1` JSON object. You do **not** author deterministic dataset objects, role wage shares, contributions, factor scores, `S`, `uplift_pp` or a verdict. Plain Python injects and calculates those fields.

Every evidence-bearing input separates:

- `value` — selected value used by the formulas;
- `provenance_type` — `DIRECT`, `DERIVED` or `ESTIMATE`;
- `evidence_quality` — `HIGH`, `MED`, `LOW` or `NONE`;
- `source_fact` — only what fetched citations actually establish;
- `derivation` — the complete bridge from source fact to selected value;
- `plausible_range`;
- `citations[]` — atomic fetched citations with URL, title, exact short quote, access date and scope.

`provenance_type` and `evidence_quality` are independent. An unsourced estimate must use `ESTIMATE` + `NONE` + `citations: []`. A proxy with a strong source is usually `DERIVED` plus an evidence quality reflecting the source and scope fit.

## Frozen research rules

1. PE/M&A activity informs only B and M, never V or A.
2. Multiples are EV/EBITDA after market-rate owner compensation. State unit, target size, source population and every normalization.
3. A zero consolidator count is an absence-of-evidence estimate, not a directly sourced fact. State the searches performed; do not cite a page as proving zero.
4. `current_adoption_pct` means the share of US firms in this NAICS materially using AI in automatable functions. Individual tool use and broad-sector use are different populations and require a visible derivation.
5. `t50_years` means years from today until 50% material firm adoption. If current adoption is already at least 50%, set `t50_years.value` to `0`; otherwise it must be positive.
6. Payer/regulatory repricing belongs in C when it determines who retains cost savings. Terminal class separately answers whether paid end demand survives mature AI; do not count the same clawback twice.
7. A statutory human-in-the-loop boundary affects terminal class only to the extent it preserves paid demand. It does not make automatable tasks non-automatable.
8. If a W-2 dataset labor share is distorted by 1099-heavy labor, use the injected value unchanged, flag `DATASET_MISMATCH`, lower confidence as appropriate and show sensitivity. Do not silently adjust it.
9. Catch-all codes: identify the top three revenue subsegments, score the dominant subsegment, set `heterogeneous=true`, and disclose the boundary.

## Industry-specific guidance — hints, not facts

**Likely role structure:**
- **Advertising and services sales (space/location selling)** — ~12-15% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-3011 Advertising Sales Agents 8.2% of wage bill + 41-3091 Sales Reps of Services 3.0% — note the role_mix is 4-digit 5418, dominated by ad agencies (541810); for actual OOH operators sales is the core commercial function); automatable: partial. Prospecting, proposal decks, rate-card quoting and availability lookups automate substantially; local advertiser relationships and programmatic-platform negotiations persist. Programmatic DOOH is already shifting the transaction to platforms, which is a distribution question, not an automation one.
- **Management and lease/real-estate operations** — ~15-20% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General & Operations Managers 9.3% + 11-2021 Marketing Managers 5.2%; site-lease acquisition and permit management staff are not in the listed top-10 (long tail, est. ~5-10% for actual OOH firms)); automatable: partial. Lease renewals, landowner relations and permit fights are relationship/legal work that persists; internal reporting, billing and inventory management automate.
- **Marketing, research and PR-type staff (mostly 5418 contamination)** — ~5% in a real OOH firm (record proxy says ~15%) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 27-3031 Public Relations Specialists 8.3% + 13-1161 Market Research Analysts 6.6% — these shares reflect ad agencies and PR firms inside 4-digit 5418, not billboard operators; treat as overstated for 541850); automatable: yes. Audience measurement packaging (impressions, Geopath data), campaign reporting and proof-of-performance decks are near-fully automatable where they exist.
- **Creative and design** — ~4-7% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 27-1024 Graphic Designers 3.3% + 27-1011 Art Directors 4.1% (again 5418-agency-weighted; OOH operators carry a thinner version)); automatable: yes. Billboard/display creative and copy layout is among the most directly AI-automatable work in the firm; many operators already outsource it.
- **Installation, printing and field operations** — ~25-35% of wage bill (OEWS basis: not in the listed top-10 — billposters, sign installers (47-xxxx/49-xxxx), large-format print operators and vehicle crews; long tail, est. ~25-35% for actual display-advertising operators); automatable: no. Physically hanging vinyls, servicing digital boards at height, and maintaining structures cannot be automated by current AI; this is the largest AI-resistant block in a real OOH firm and the record's agency-flavored proxy hides it.
- **Admin and back office** — ~15-20% of wage bill (OEWS basis: long tail of office/admin occupations not in the listed top-10, est. remainder ~15-20%); automatable: yes. Billing, contract admin, scheduling of postings and collections automate near-fully.

- (succession_owners) OAAA membership / independent operator profiles and Billboard Insider operator interviews — Independent billboard plants are frequently multi-generation family businesses; succession evidence is anecdotal but consistent — verify rather than assume. *(existence unverified — confirm this source still exists/publishes before relying on it)*

**Capture questions:**
- OOH space is sold off rate cards priced on location scarcity and audience impressions, not on the operator's cost base — when AI cuts sales, design and back-office labor, does the operator keep the savings as margin (location rents are set by demand), or does programmatic DOOH price transparency compress rates toward marginal cost?
- Programmatic DOOH platforms (Vistar, Place Exchange, Broadsign) increasingly own the advertiser transaction — what take-rate do they extract, and does the independent operator become a passive inventory landlord with the AI-era margin accruing to the platform?
- Who owns the two relationships that matter — the landowner lease (typically firm-held, long-term, transferable) and the local advertiser account (often personal to the owner-operator) — and what fraction of revenue survives the founder's exit?
- Can advertisers bypass the operator with generic AI at all, given that the scarce asset is a permitted physical location rather than a service — i.e., is the AI-bypass threat effectively zero here, and should pi_moat reflect asset scarcity rather than service defensibility?

**Terminal-value question:**
The billboard operator's product is a permitted physical location — a zoning-scarce real asset AI cannot replicate — so mature AI cuts this operator's costs rather than replacing its product; the real terminal question is whether advertiser demand for OOH holds as AI-optimized digital channels compete for the same budgets, and whether permit scarcity (many jurisdictions cap or ban new billboards, grandfathering existing ones) keeps location rents intact. Which is it: cost-cutter on a protected asset, or slow demand melt of the medium itself?

**Known traps:**
- Role-mix contamination: the record's role_mix is 4-digit 5418 and is dominated by ad-agency/PR occupations (PR specialists 8.3%, market research analysts 6.6% of wage bill) that barely exist inside a billboard/display operator. Real 541850 firms are sales + lease management + physical installation/print crews. Re-weight before setting within-role fractions.
- This is an asset business wearing a services NAICS code: value sits in permitted structures and site leases, not labor. Labor share is only 0.3265 and the n_band derivation applies a generic 15.65% Business & Consumer Services margin — actual billboard operators run far higher EBITDA margins (often 30-50% on mature plants), so the record's n_band (112) likely undercounts band-eligible firms. Note this when scoring N.
- Multiples quirk: billboard plants trade on cash-flow multiples closer to real-estate/infrastructure than to services (Lamar and OUTFRONT are REITs). Do not import professional-services multiples; use plant-sale comps (Billboard Insider, public tuck-in disclosures).
- Boundary confusion: ad agencies are 541810, media buying is 541830, media reps are 541840, direct mail is 541860, sign manufacturing is 339950, and transit-ad operators sometimes classify under 541850 or 541890 — association revenue figures (OAAA) span the whole OOH medium including the big-three public companies; strip them before reasoning about the independent-operator segment.
- Size structure: 1,543 of 2,083 firms are <5 employees (tiny local plants), and the big three control a large share of national inventory — the roll-up universe is the middle (20-99 employee class, 84 firms) plus sub-scale plants valued on cash flow rather than EBITDA-band logic.
- Consolidation is old news here — Lamar/OUTFRONT/Clear Channel have been tucking in independents for decades. Usable for B and M only; never as evidence for V or A (rule 2).

## Frozen formulas — Python calculates them

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`
- `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)`
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = 10` when `t50_years=0`, otherwise `min(10, 10 / t50_years)`
- `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)`
- `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; add 0.1 for documented succession shortage, cap at 1
- `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
- `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`
