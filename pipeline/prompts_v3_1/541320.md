## What you author

You author evidence and judgment inputs in a `research_draft_v3_1` JSON object. You do **not** author deterministic dataset objects, role wage shares, contributions, factor scores, `S`, `uplift_pp` or a verdict. Plain Python injects and calculates those fields.

Every evidence-bearing input separates:

- `value` — selected value used by the formulas;
- `provenance_type` — `DIRECT`, `DERIVED` or `ESTIMATE`;
- `evidence_quality` — `HIGH`, `MED`, `LOW` or `NONE`;
- `source_fact` — only what fetched citations actually establish;
- `derivation` — the complete bridge from source fact to selected value;
- `plausible_range`;
- `citations[]` — atomic fetched citations with URL, title, exact short quote, access date and scope.

`provenance_type` and `evidence_quality` are independent. An unsourced estimate must use `ESTIMATE` + `NONE` + `citations: []`. A proxy with a strong source is usually `DERIVED` plus an evidence quality reflecting the source and scope fit.

## Frozen research rules

1. PE/M&A activity informs only B and M, never V or A.
2. Multiples are EV/EBITDA after market-rate owner compensation. State unit, target size, source population and every normalization.
3. A zero consolidator count is an absence-of-evidence estimate, not a directly sourced fact. State the searches performed; do not cite a page as proving zero.
4. `current_adoption_pct` means the share of US firms in this NAICS materially using AI in automatable functions. Individual tool use and broad-sector use are different populations and require a visible derivation.
5. `t50_years` means years from today until 50% material firm adoption. If current adoption is already at least 50%, set `t50_years.value` to `0`; otherwise it must be positive.
6. Payer/regulatory repricing belongs in C when it determines who retains cost savings. Terminal class separately answers whether paid end demand survives mature AI; do not count the same clawback twice.
7. A statutory human-in-the-loop boundary affects terminal class only to the extent it preserves paid demand. It does not make automatable tasks non-automatable.
8. If a W-2 dataset labor share is distorted by 1099-heavy labor, use the injected value unchanged, flag `DATASET_MISMATCH`, lower confidence as appropriate and show sensitivity. Do not silently adjust it.
9. Catch-all codes: identify the top three revenue subsegments, score the dominant subsegment, set `heterogeneous=true`, and disclose the boundary.

**Capture questions:**
- Fees are lump-sum or hourly, and a large share of firms work as sub-consultants to architects/engineers/developers who hold the prime contract: when AI cuts design-production hours, does the prime simply re-scope or re-bid the LA sub-fee downward (pass-through), or do prime-held relationships and stamped-document requirements let the LA firm keep the saving? Split the answer by prime vs. sub revenue.
- Is the firm prime to the owner/municipality, or a swap-able sub-consultant — who owns the client relationship, and does it survive a principal's exit?
- Does residential and commercial design-build by landscape contractors bypass landscape architects except where stamped grading/stormwater documents are required — and can clients generate discretionary amenity/planting design with AI tools directly?
- What share of fees is code-required work (stormwater, ADA, public procurement, entitlement drawings) versus discretionary design — i.e., how big is the regulated core that AI cannot bypass?

**Terminal-value question:**
Discretionary planting and amenity design is highly AI-exposed and, in title-act states, not even legally reserved to licensees — the work may be done unlicensed as long as the title 'landscape architect' is not used. Durable paid demand concentrates in stamped, code-required site work (grading, stormwater, public projects). The deciding mechanism is the practice-act vs. title-act split by state: how much of the current fee pool sits inside a genuine practice-act monopoly, and does the rest survive mature AI as a paid service at all? Distinguish AI cutting the firm's production cost from AI (plus contractor design-build) replacing the discretionary product.

**Known traps:**
- Role-mix warning: the provided role_mix is 4-digit 5413 and omits 17-1012 Landscape Architects from the top-10 entirely — it describes the engineering-dominated parent, not this code; rebuild shares and flag as hypothesis.
- Licensure nuance is the moat question: nearly all states license LAs, but a material subset have title acts only (the work itself can be done unlicensed) — materially weaker than 541310/541330 practice acts; verify the practice-act vs. title-act state split before scoring capture.
- Sub-consultant economics: where the architect/engineer/developer holds the prime, the LA firm is a fee-taker — score C on who owns the prime contract, not on end-client demand.
- Very small firms (3,549 of 5,041 under 5 employees) with thin transferable EBITDA; n_band is only 46, and the record's 500+ class shows LOWER revenue/firm ($9.1M) than the 100-499 class ($23.6M) — a suppression/noise artifact in SUSB data; treat size-class tails cautiously.
- Demand rides construction and municipal spending cycles — normalize any single-year evidence.
- Boundary confusion: landscape contractors (design-build installers) are 561730, architecture 541310, civil engineering 541330 — surveys and comps frequently blend LA firms into 'A&E' or into landscaping services; check the sample frame.

## Frozen formulas — Python calculates them

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`
- `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)`
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = 10` when `t50_years=0`, otherwise `min(10, 10 / t50_years)`
- `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)`
- `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; add 0.1 for documented succession shortage, cap at 1
- `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
- `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`
