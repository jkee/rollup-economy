## Industry-specific guidance

### Role hints

- **Staff interpreters and translators** — ~20-30% of W-2 wages (hypothesis; freelance linguists sit in COGS) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 27-3091 Interpreters and Translators 2.1% of wage bill in the record's 4-digit 5419 mix — but that mix is dominated by veterinary occupations from sibling 541940 (veterinarians 17.1%, vet techs 9.2%) and is unusable as a proxy; within an actual LSP the linguist share of the W-2 wage bill is far higher, and most production linguists are 1099 freelancers who never appear in OEWS wages at all); automatable: partial. Written translation is the most AI-displaced task in the economy — MT post-editing (MTPE) is already the default workflow at documented 30-50% per-word discounts; what persists is certified/sworn translation, high-liability legal/medical work, and transcreation. On-site and live interpretation is physical/real-time and holds longer, though AI speech translation is closing in on remote modalities.
- **Project managers and localization engineers** — ~25-35% (hypothesis) of wage bill (OEWS basis: not in the contaminated top-10 — PMs are the operational core of every language services company; hypothesis est. ~25-35% of W-2 wages); automatable: partial. - **Interpretation scheduling and operations (OPI/VRI/on-site dispatch)** — ~10-15% (hypothesis) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-4051 Customer Service Representatives 2.3% in the contaminated mix; real interpreting firms carry large scheduling/dispatch teams — hypothesis est. ~10-15%); automatable: yes. ### Capture questions

- Translation is priced per word and interpreting per minute/hour — MTPE discount schedules (30-50% off per-word rates) already show AI savings being passed to clients as lower prices, not kept as margin; what evidence shows any LSP holding price while its production cost collapses, and does interpreting's per-minute pricing follow the same deflation path as AI speech translation matures?
- Who owns the client relationship — the LSP (contracted language-access programs with hospital systems, courts, insurers) or the freelance linguist the end user actually sees? Which contracts (statewide court contracts, health-system language-access agreements) are institutional and transferable in an acquisition?
- What stops enterprise clients from bypassing LSPs with LLM translation plus an in-house reviewer — and for which revenue lines (certified translations for USCIS/courts, sworn documents, regulated medical device labeling under ISO 17100-type requirements) does a certification or liability gate genuinely block the bypass?
- In mandated interpreting (Section 1557 healthcare, court access), the payer often reimburses per encounter at set rates — when AI cuts the LSP's delivery cost, do public payers and hospital procurement re-rate the contracts downward at renewal, clawing back the savings?

### Existing terminal question — reinterpret at operator level

This is the canonical melter test: LLMs produce the product itself — translated text — at near-zero marginal cost, so the question is precisely which revenue survives as paid demand: statutorily mandated live interpretation (Section 1557 healthcare encounters, court certification requirements, ADA/deaf services), certification-gated document translation (USCIS, sworn/legal), and liability-bearing regulated content — versus ordinary business translation, which is already collapsing into MT-with-review at falling per-word rates. A researcher who scores this code on current LSP revenue growth (interpreting demand is still rising on demographics and enforcement) without separating the mandate-protected floor from the substitutable bulk will get terminal value wrong in either direction. What fraction of this operator's revenue sits behind a statute or certification, and what happens to the rest at mature AI?
