## Industry-specific guidance

### Role hints

- **Bench and field testing staff (inspectors, testers, samplers)** — ~22-26% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 51-9061 Inspectors, Testers, Sorters, Samplers & Weighers 9.2% of wage bill + 19-4042 Environmental Science & Protection Technicians 1.9% + field sampling/materials techs in the unlisted tail, est. ~10-15% more); automatable: no. Physically running tests, collecting samples, operating instruments — the core product is a physical act. Lab automation and robotics compress it slowly; LLM-class AI does not replace it. AI-exposed slice is limited to their data entry and worksheet documentation.
- **Lab scientists and chemists** — ~9-10% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 19-2031 Chemists 5.2% + 19-4031 Chemical Technicians 4.3%); automatable: partial. Method development, result review and sign-off persist (accreditation requires qualified reviewers); data reduction, QC flagging and report narrative drafting automate substantially.
- **Engineering and technical specialists** — ~6-7% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 17-2112 Industrial Engineers 4.0% + 17-3029 Engineering Technologists & Technicians, All Other 2.3%); automatable: partial. Test-protocol design and certification engineering judgment persist; calculation packages, compliance matrices and report assembly automate.
- **Management layer** — ~11-13% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General & Operations Managers 7.9% + lab directors/quality managers in the tail, est. ~3-5% more); automatable: partial. Client relationships and accreditation accountability (ISO 17025 technical-manager roles) persist; scheduling, capacity planning and internal reporting automate.
- **Project management** — ~3% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1082 Project Management Specialists 2.6%); automatable: partial. Sample tracking, client status reporting and coordination paperwork automate; client-facing escalation persists.
- **Admin, clerical and reporting staff** — ~11-14% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-6014 Secretaries & Admin Assistants 1.4% + 43-9061 Office Clerks 1.3% + admin/customer-service tail, est. ~8-12% more); automatable: yes. Sample login, chain-of-custody paperwork, certificate generation, invoicing and LIMS data entry — the most directly automatable cost lines in a lab.
- **Remaining technical tail** — ~25-30% of wage bill (OEWS basis: unlisted remainder across lab-specific technician occupations (bio techs, materials scientists, calibration techs, couriers/drivers), est. ~25-30%); automatable: partial. Mixed physical/documentation work; allocate between the physical-testing and reporting patterns above rather than applying one fraction.

### Capture questions

- Testing is sold per-test off published fee schedules against accredited, standardized methods (ASTM, EPA, ISO) — when AI cuts the reporting/admin layer, does per-test pricing hold (clients buy accreditation, not hours), or does lab-vs-lab bid competition on large environmental/materials contracts pass the savings straight through? Which pricing channel dominates the target firms' revenue mix?
- Who owns the client relationship — the lab (accreditation scope, proximity, turnaround) or specific certifying individuals? Labs look institutionally sticky; verify switching behavior on commodity tests where the nearest accredited lab wins.
- Clients cannot self-serve with generic AI — the product is a physical, accredited test — so the bypass risk is different: can clients bring testing in-house with automated bench instruments, and for which test categories (routine QC yes, regulated compliance testing mostly no)?
- What fraction of a typical small lab's cost base is actually AI-exposed (reporting, LIMS, scheduling, certificates) versus physical bench/field labor? The labor_share is 0.68 but most of that wage bill is physical work — capture upside is real but bounded; force an explicit split.

### Existing terminal question — reinterpret at operator level

Paid demand here is about as durable as it gets — regulation and accreditation mandate physical tests that AI cannot perform, so mature AI cuts the operator's admin/reporting costs rather than replacing the product. The sharp question is the opposite of the usual one: with the AI-exposed slice of the cost base bounded (~quarter of wages, not three-quarters), is the automation upside big enough to justify an AI-premium roll-up thesis at all, or is this just a conventional TIC consolidation play whose economics the global majors (Eurofins, SGS, BV, Intertek) already arbitrage?

### Special notes

- Cost-structure trap: high labor_share (0.6827) does NOT mean high AI exposure — the wage bill is dominated by physical bench and field labor. Score V off the automatable slice (reporting, admin, data reduction, scheduling), not off total labor.
- This is one of the most consolidator-dense codes in 54: global TIC majors plus PE-backed US platforms (UES in construction materials/geotech, Alliance Technical in environmental/emissions, others in food and pharma testing) have been rolling up labs for a decade — strong evidence for B and M, but rule 2 applies: none of it supports V or A.
- n_band structure: 187 firms, all from the 100-499 employee class — the 20-99 class (749 firms, avg implied EBITDA $0.99M) sits just below the band cutoff, so the practical target pool is materially larger than n_band suggests; note the sensitivity to the assumed 15.65% margin.
- Sub-segment heterogeneity: environmental, construction materials/geotech, food, pharma/bioanalytical, electrical/product-safety and calibration labs have different accreditation regimes, margins and consolidation maturity — identify the mix and avoid one-size scoring; construction-materials testing overlaps 541330 (engineering firms with CoMET divisions) in survey data.
- Boundary confusion: medical/diagnostic labs are 621511, veterinary labs 541940, R&D labs 5417, engineering firms with in-house labs 541330 — 'lab M&A' data frequently mixes these; confirm scope before using deal comps or market sizes.
- EBITDA normalization: labs are capex-heavy relative to other 54 codes (instruments, facilities) — EBITDA multiples flatter labs vs. asset-light services; check maintenance-capex burden before treating quoted multiples as comparable.
