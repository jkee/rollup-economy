## Industry-specific guidance

### Role hints

- **Vehicle cleaners (hand wash, prep, detailing)** — ~39% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 53-7061 Cleaners of Vehicles and Equipment 39.0% of wage bill); automatable: no. Physical hand labor concentrated in full-service, flex-serve and detailing formats. The historical labor replacement here — express exterior tunnels replacing wash crews with machinery — is decades-old capital equipment, not AI; do not credit AI with tunnel automation.
- **Attendants and guest-facing site staff** — ~14% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 53-6031 Automotive and Watercraft Service Attendants 10.7% + 41-2011 Cashiers 3.0%); automatable: partial. Loading/guiding, prep, membership selling at the pay station — license-plate recognition, kiosk/app payment and automated gates (adjacent automation, some AI content) are already shrinking this role at express sites.
- **Equipment maintenance and mechanical staff** — ~13% in the proxy; true car-wash-only share is lower (tunnel equipment techs) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 49-3023 Automotive Service Technicians 9.0% + 49-1011 Supervisors of Mechanics 2.3% + 49-3093 Tire Repairers 1.5% — NOTE: role_mix is 5-digit 81119, which includes 811191 oil-change/lube shops; the auto-tech and tire occupations are largely sibling-code contamination); automatable: no. Physical equipment upkeep; strip the lube-shop contamination when modeling car-wash labor.
- **Site supervision and management** — ~19.5% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 53-1047 First-Line Supervisors of Transportation/Material Moving Workers 9.5% + 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 10.1%); automatable: partial. Multi-site chains centralize scheduling, pricing, membership analytics and labor planning — AI compresses the above-site layer; on-site supervision persists.
- **Office and customer service** — ~4-6% incl. tail of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-4051 Customer Service Representatives 1.2% + 43-9061 Office Clerks 1.3% + admin tail); automatable: yes. Membership billing support, chargeback handling, bookkeeping — small base, cleanly automatable.
- **Remainder / long tail** — ~8-10% of wage bill (OEWS basis: unlisted tail (marketing, HR, drivers)); automatable: partial. Mixed overhead roles.

### Capture questions

- Pricing is direct-to-consumer retail: single washes and unlimited monthly memberships, with no payer, fee schedule or auditor above the operator — so any cost saving is retained margin unless competition takes it. The real pricing question is tunnel oversupply: with heavy new-build through 2019-2023, are trade areas seeing membership price wars and discounting that competes away site-level gains, and what do MCW/Driven membership ARPU and churn disclosures show?
- Membership economics are the moat claim: what share of revenue is subscription (unlimited plans), what are churn and ARPU trends, and does the subscription habit plus location convenience actually hold customers when a competitor tunnel opens across the street?
- What does the acquirer of a band-size operator ($1-10M EBITDA, roughly the 100-499 and upper 500+ classes — n_band 186) actually buy: express tunnels with membership books, or legacy full-serve/flex sites whose labor-heavy model is being competed out by express formats? The two have opposite trajectories and different multiples.
- How much of the value sits in real estate rather than operations — sale-leaseback prevalence, cap-rate-driven site values, rent burdens left behind after PE sale-leasebacks — and is the operating company worth much once market rent is charged?

### Existing terminal question — reinterpret at operator level

A car wash is a machine plus a site: AI does not wash cars, and the big labor-removal event (express tunnels replacing wash crews) is mature machinery capex that already happened — so the honest AI question is whether there is any material AI-labor channel at all beyond site-level admin, membership analytics and above-site management (perhaps 15-25% of an already modest wage bill), or whether this is a consumer-subscription/real-estate business whose roll-up math must stand entirely without AI cost extraction. Demand survives trivially; the failure mode to guard against is back-filling an AI thesis onto a non-AI consolidation — if the AI-attributable EBITDA uplift rounds to a small number, the score should say so.

### Special notes

- Sibling contamination: role_mix is 5-digit 81119, which folds in 811191 oil-change/lube shops — automotive service technicians and tire repairers in the mix are largely not car-wash staff; strip them before setting the V structure.
- Format heterogeneity drives everything: express exterior (low labor ~10-15 FTE/site, membership-led), full-service/flex (labor-heavy, declining share), self-serve bays (coin-op, near-zero labor), and detailing — labor share, automation exposure and multiples differ by format; identify which formats populate the $1-10M EBITDA band before scoring.
- Real estate dominates the capital story: sale-leasebacks are pervasive — EBITDA vs EBITDAR (rent-adjusted) comparisons are decisive; a site 'multiple' often embeds land value and a below/above-market lease. Owner-operated legacy sites also carry owner comp and historical cash revenue in their P&Ls — normalize both.
- Membership accounting: unlimited-wash plans create deferred revenue and churn sensitivity; 'revenue growth' can be member-count growth with falling ARPU — check cohort disclosures where available (MCW filings).
- The consolidation wave (Mister, Take 5/Driven, Mammoth, GO, ZIPS, Express Wash Concepts, PE platforms) peaked around 2021-22 and cooled sharply with interest rates and oversupply — verify current deal pace and multiples rather than assuming peak-era figures; B/M use only (rule 2).
- n_band note: unusually, the 500+ class (62 firms, avg revenue $60M) lands IN the $1-10M EBITDA band at the assumed 15.65% margin — the band here includes sizable regional chains, not just the 100-499 class; express-format EBITDA margins (25-35% site-level) are well above the generic margin used, so band membership is margin-sensitive in both directions.
