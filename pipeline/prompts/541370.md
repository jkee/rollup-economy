## Industry-specific guidance (verify everything; these are hints, not facts)

**Likely role structure (for the V breakdown):**
- **Licensed professional surveyors (PLS)** — ~20-25% (hypothesis — record is 4-digit) of wage bill (OEWS basis: role_mix is 4-digit 5413 (engineering-services dominated); 17-1022 Surveyors is not in the listed top-10 — sits in the unlisted tail. Hypothesis for the 6-digit code: est. ~20-25% of wage bill); automatable: partial. The PLS stamp on boundary surveys, plats and legal descriptions is a statutory monopoly and not automatable; but deed research, legal-description drafting, plat preparation and computations are heavily AI-exposed — one PLS can plausibly stamp far more throughput.
- **Survey field crews and technicians** — ~25-35% (hypothesis) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 17-3031 Surveying & Mapping Technicians not in the listed top-10 — unlisted tail; hypothesis est. ~25-35%); automatable: no. Physical occupation of the ground — monument recovery, instrument setup, construction staking. GNSS, robotic total stations and drones already shrank crew sizes from 3 to 1-2; further compression is incremental, not elimination.
- **Office processing: CAD, plat drafting, point-cloud and photogrammetric processing** — ~12-18% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 17-3011 Architectural & Civil Drafters 3.3% in the record + cartographers/photogrammetrists (17-1021) and processing techs in the tail, est. ~10-15% total); automatable: yes. Point-cloud classification, feature extraction, drawing production and deed plotting are the most directly automatable cost lines — much is already semi-automated software work.
- **Management and project management** — ~15% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General & Operations Managers 5.7% + 11-9041 A&E Managers 5.5% + 13-1082 Project Management Specialists 6.5%); automatable: partial. Client relationships (developers, title companies, DOTs, engineers) persist; proposal, scheduling and coordination paperwork automates.
- **Engineers and architects listed in the 4-digit record** — likely <8% at true 6-digit of wage bill (OEWS basis: 17-2051 Civil Engineers 12.8% + 17-1011 Architects 5.8% + 17-2141 Mechanical 3.8% + 17-2071 Electrical 3.0% — contamination from 541330/541310 firms in the 4-digit OEWS cell); automatable: partial. Treat as an artifact of the 4-digit role_mix — survey firms employ few design engineers. Reallocate to surveyor, crew and processing roles.
- **Admin and back office** — ~10-15% of wage bill (OEWS basis: long tail of office/admin occupations, est. remainder ~10-15%); automatable: yes. Research requests, invoicing, records management, client intake — near-fully automatable.

**Capture questions to answer with evidence (for C):**
- Boundary and ALTA surveys are sold as fixed fees per job in locally competitive markets — when AI collapses office processing hours (deed research, drafting, point-cloud work), does per-job pricing hold because the fee reflects the stamp and liability, or does local bid competition pass the savings to developers and title companies? Construction staking and DOT work are often unit-price or low-bid — treat those channels separately.
- Who owns the client relationship — the firm (repeat developer/title/municipal accounts, on-call contracts) or the individual licensed surveyor whose stamp and county-level records knowledge is personal and portable?
- What actually stops clients from bypassing firms with AI: state statutes reserve boundary determination to a licensed PLS in responsible charge — verify whether 'one PLS stamping AI-produced surveys at scale' is permitted in practice, because that pattern deflates price without removing the license gate.
- Aerial mapping/lidar (the non-boundary half of the code) has no equivalent licensure lock in many uses — is that segment already being commoditized by drone operators and software, and what share of a typical firm's revenue does it represent?

**Terminal-value question (for cross-check 2):**
Boundary surveying survives mature AI because it is a statutory license plus a physical act (monument recovery, ground occupation) — but the office half of every job collapses, and mapping work without the licensure lock may go to software entirely. Does paid demand per firm shrink to 'field crew + stamp,' and does fixed-fee pricing in locally competitive markets let owners keep the office savings or hand them to clients? A researcher who scores this code on the license alone, without splitting boundary work from unlicensed mapping revenue, gets it wrong.

**Known traps for this industry:**
- Role-mix data gap: role_mix is 4-digit 5413 — surveyors (17-1022) and surveying/mapping technicians (17-3031), the code's actual core occupations, are absent from the listed top-10 while civil engineers and architects (contamination from 541330/541310) top it. Rebuild the role structure; do not compute within-role fractions off the listed engineering occupations.
- Fragmentation trap: n_total 6,927 but n_band only 119, and the band is filled ONLY by the 100-499 (76 firms) and 500+ (43 firms) classes — the modal survey firm (<20 employees, 6,339 firms) is far below $1M EBITDA. A roll-up thesis here is buying the industry's top tier, not its long tail; check whether those 119 are already AE-platform subsidiaries.
- Succession tailwind is real and documented: the licensed-surveyor pipeline has been shrinking for years (aging PLS population, low exam throughput) — verify with NCEES/NSPS data rather than asserting it.
- Boundary confusion: geophysical surveying is 541360; engineering firms with in-house survey departments book in 541330; construction staking by contractors sits in 23. 'Geospatial' market reports often include GIS software and government contractors far outside this code — check coverage before quoting market size or adoption numbers.
- EBITDA normalization: small survey firms are owner-operator businesses — SDE with a working-owner PLS salary inside; normalize before comparing to AE-platform acquisition multiples.
- Drone/lidar deflation already happened once: aerial mapping pricing collapsed as drones displaced manned flights — useful historical evidence for how fast this industry passes technology savings to clients.

## Step 1b — Declare judgment inputs

These are **declared opinions** — the only place judgment enters the record. Each must be bounded, with its own rationale and plausible range.

- `within_role_automatable_fraction` per role (using the provided `role_mix`): share of that role's labor cost in tasks current AI can substantially do. Licensed/judgment/relationship/physical work = not automatable; a small automatable sliver inside professional roles (drafting, research, documentation) is allowed if justified. → `ai_replaceable_share` = Σ(role share × fraction).
- `pi_dist` ∈ [0,1] — client-relationship control: who owns the client — the firm, an upstream gatekeeper, a platform, a procurement department? **Pricing-model deflation must be reflected here with the mechanism stated** (cost-plus/audited/hourly billing hands AI savings to clients).
- `pi_moat` ∈ [0,1] — barrier strength: licensure, switching costs, proprietary data, physical presence, and the realistic threat that clients use generic AI to bypass the firm entirely.
- `t50` — years from today until 50% of firms materially adopt AI in the automatable functions; derive from `current_adoption_pct` + `historical_analogs` and show the derivation. **t50 must appear in `top_uncertain_inputs` with its range and score impact — always.**

## Step 2 — Compute scores (show full arithmetic; no rounding anywhere)

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`; `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)` — anchor: freeing ≥25% of revenue = 10.
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = min(10, 10 / t50)`
- B subfactors (all continuous):
  `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)` — 10,000+ targets → 1.0
  `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; +0.1 if documented succession shortage, cap 1.0
  `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
  `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
  (No price term in B — pricing signal lives only in M.)
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`

## Step 3 — Mandatory cross-checks (answer each explicitly)

1. **Uplift consistency.** Convert V_raw into expected EBITDA uplift (pp). If uplift < 3pp while A ≥ 5, explain or revisit t50.
2. **Terminal value.** Answer the industry-specific terminal-value question. Distinguish: AI cutting the operator's costs vs AI replacing the operator's product. Classify: `DURABLE` (demand survives mature AI), `PRESSURED` (demand shrinks but persists), `MELTING` (the product itself is being replaced).
3. **No-proxy audit.** Confirm in writing that V and A were justified without reference to deal activity or consolidation trends.
4. **Pricing-model deflation.** State the billing mix and whether AI efficiency flows to clients as lower fees rather than to the operator as margin; confirm how this is reflected in `pi_dist`/`pi_moat`.
