## Industry-specific guidance (verify everything; these are hints, not facts)

**Likely role structure (for the V breakdown):**
- **Financial advisors (client-facing wealth management)** — ~21% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-2052 Personal Financial Advisors 21.3% of wage bill — note role_mix is 3-digit 523 (LOW quality), so brokerage-side occupations bleed in); automatable: partial. Financial-plan drafting, meeting prep/notes, proposal generation and client reporting automate heavily (AI meeting-scribes and planning copilots are live in the RIA stack); the trust relationship, behavioral coaching and rainmaking do not. Within-role fraction should come from task mix, plausibly 0.2-0.35.
- **Securities and financial services sales agents (brokerage-side contamination + hybrid reps)** — ~10-15% for a real 523940 firm (proxy says 15.5%) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-3031 Securities, Commodities, and Financial Services Sales Agents 15.5% — largely reflects sibling brokerage codes inside 3-digit 523; present in 523940 firms as hybrid RIA/BD reps); automatable: partial. Same split as advisors: relationship persists, paperwork/prospecting support automates.
- **Investment analysts and portfolio managers** — ~8-10% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-2051 Financial and Investment Analysts 7.7% + portfolio-manager tail not separately listed); automatable: partial. Research notes, screening, rebalancing and performance attribution are heavily automatable (much already rules-based); final allocation judgment and investment-committee accountability persist. Model-portfolio outsourcing (TAMPs) has been shrinking this role for a decade independent of AI.
- **Financial and operations management** — ~17% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-3031 Financial Managers 10.1% + 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 7.0%); automatable: partial. Compliance-adjacent reporting, billing (AUM fee calculation), and operations reporting automate substantially; supervisory principal duties (SEC/state registration obligations) require named humans.
- **Client service and operations staff** — ~12-15% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-4051 Customer Service Reps 1.3% + 43-9061 Office Clerks 1.2% + 43-6011 Executive Assistants 1.6% + ops/client-service tail est. ~8-12%); automatable: yes. Account opening/transfers (ACAT paperwork), RMD processing, money movement requests, scheduling and CRM hygiene — the classic RIA back office — automate near-fully with custodian API + AI workflows.
- **Technology and accounting** — ~6% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 15-1252 Software Developers 3.2% + 13-2011 Accountants and Auditors 2.5%); automatable: partial. Concentrated in larger asset managers within the 3-digit proxy; small RIAs buy rather than build. Reconciliation and reporting automate.

**Capture questions to answer with evidence (for C):**
- Fees are ~1% of AUM, priced on assets rather than hours or cost — so AI cost savings do not mechanically flow to clients; but is the fee itself compressing (robo precedent pushed simple allocation to 25-30bps, and AI-native advice could re-anchor what '1%' buys), and what evidence shows realized fee rates holding vs. declining in Schwab/Cerulli benchmarking data?
- Who owns the client — the firm (institutionalized service teams, next-gen advisors under agreements) or the founding advisor personally? RIA books are famously portable (protocol departures, breakaways); what do acquirers' retention data show about revenue that survives founder exit, since that determines whether the arbitrage is real?
- What stops clients from replacing the advisor with AI directly — is the moat behavioral (trust, accountability, estate/tax complexity, fiduciary liability) or informational (already commoditized) — and does the demonstrated robo-advisor plateau (Betterment/Wealthfront never displacing human advice at scale) bound the bypass risk, or did robos simply arrive before capable AI?
- Force the multiple spread with sources: what do DeVoe/ECHELON/Advisor Growth Strategies actually report for sub-$500M-AUM seller multiples (historically ~5-8x EBITDA/EBOC) versus platform-level exits (double digits) — and does the entry-exit spread survive the documented post-2022 rate-driven cooldown in RIA deal pricing? Do not assert an M without quoting these trackers' current numbers.
- Consolidators (Focus, Creative Planning, Mercer, Mariner, Wealth Enhancement, CI/Corient, Captrust) now bid competitively for quality sellers — has buyer competition already arbitraged away the small-firm discount that a new roll-up's M depends on?

**Terminal-value question (for cross-check 2):**
Separate AI cutting the advisor's costs (ops, planning prep, reporting — real and large) from AI replacing the advisor's product (paid human judgment and accountability over someone's life savings): the deciding mechanism is fiduciary trust plus regulatory structure — advice is sold under SEC/state RIA registration with a named human fiduciary, and two decades of cheap automated alternatives (index funds, robos) grew AUM-based human advice rather than killing it — but mature AI is the first substitute that can hold a conversation; does the 1%-of-AUM relationship survive when an AI fiduciary-equivalent is free, or does human advice bifurcate into a shrinking high-net-worth priesthood while the mass-affluent base melts to AI-native platforms?

**Known traps for this industry:**
- CONTROL-CODE WARNING on M: the v2 record for this code carried an inflated M relative to its own cited multiples. The run must source buy and exit multiples from named trackers (DeVoe RIA Deal Book, ECHELON, Advisor Growth Strategies Deal Room) with the reported size-tier numbers quoted, and must not average headline mega-deal multiples into small-firm entry multiples. If quoted multiples are on EBOC/revenue bases (RIA deals often are), state the basis and convert explicitly.
- EBITDA normalization: owner-advisors pay themselves variably (salary vs. distributions); RIA deal metrics are frequently EBOC (earnings before owner comp) or revenue multiples — normalize to market-rate advisor comp before any multiple comparison, or the arbitrage math silently inflates.
- Heterogeneous code: 523940 (2022 NAICS) merges portfolio management (old 523920 — asset managers, hedge fund managers, mutual fund advisers) with investment advice (old 523930 — wealth managers, financial planners). Economics differ radically (institutional asset management vs. retail advice). Identify the dominant sub-segment by firm count (wealth-advice RIAs, by far) and score that, setting heterogeneous: true; do not let hedge-fund/asset-manager revenue distort per-firm math.
- Dataset caveats: n_total (88,068) is QCEW establishments — a proxy that overcounts multi-unit firms and includes state-registered micro-RIAs; labor_share (0.3907) is computed at ancestor 5239 and marked LOW; role_mix is 3-digit 523 including brokerage occupations. Treat all three as weak priors and lean on Form ADV-derived counts (IAA Snapshot) for structure.
- Fee-structure nuance for pi_dist: AUM fees decouple price from cost — the deflation channel here is not bid-based clawback but fee-rate compression and unbundling (clients questioning 1% when AI does the visible work); planning-fee-only and subscription models are the leading edge to watch.
- Regulatory: acquisitions require client consent to assignment of advisory contracts (Advisers Act) — retention risk is contractual, not just relational; supervisory/compliance infrastructure (CCO) is a real integration cost line.
- Boundary confusion: broker-dealers are 523150-elsewhere in 5231, trust companies 523991, TAMPs/turnkey platforms straddle codes, and insurance-based advisors sit in 524210 — Cerulli/Schwab datasets cover channels, not NAICS codes; map carefully before quoting firm counts or margins.

## Step 1b — Declare judgment inputs

These are **declared opinions** — the only place judgment enters the record. Each must be bounded, with its own rationale and plausible range.

- `within_role_automatable_fraction` per role (using the provided `role_mix`): share of that role's labor cost in tasks current AI can substantially do. Licensed/judgment/relationship/physical work = not automatable; a small automatable sliver inside professional roles (drafting, research, documentation) is allowed if justified. → `ai_replaceable_share` = Σ(role share × fraction).
- `pi_dist` ∈ [0,1] — client-relationship control: who owns the client — the firm, an upstream gatekeeper, a platform, a procurement department? **Pricing-model deflation must be reflected here with the mechanism stated** (cost-plus/audited/hourly billing hands AI savings to clients).
- `pi_moat` ∈ [0,1] — barrier strength: licensure, switching costs, proprietary data, physical presence, and the realistic threat that clients use generic AI to bypass the firm entirely.
- `t50` — years from today until 50% of firms materially adopt AI in the automatable functions; derive from `current_adoption_pct` + `historical_analogs` and show the derivation. **t50 must appear in `top_uncertain_inputs` with its range and score impact — always.**

## Step 2 — Compute scores (show full arithmetic; no rounding anywhere)

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`; `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)` — anchor: freeing ≥25% of revenue = 10.
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = min(10, 10 / t50)`
- B subfactors (all continuous):
  `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)` — 10,000+ targets → 1.0
  `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; +0.1 if documented succession shortage, cap 1.0
  `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
  `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
  (No price term in B — pricing signal lives only in M.)
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`

4. **Pricing-model deflation.** State the billing mix and whether AI efficiency flows to clients as lower fees rather than to the operator as margin; confirm how this is reflected in `pi_dist`/`pi_moat`.
