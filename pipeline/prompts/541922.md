## Industry-specific guidance (verify everything; these are hints, not facts)

**Likely role structure (for the V breakdown):**
- **Photographers (commercial shooters + post-production)** — ~50% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 27-4021 Photographers 50.7% of wage bill (5-digit 54192 mix, shared with 541921)); automatable: partial. On-location and studio capture (products, food, architecture, corporate) is physical; but retouching, compositing, background work and batch editing — a large share of billed hours — is already heavily AI-automated, and generative AI attacks the deliverable itself (product shots, lifestyle imagery) which belongs in terminal value.
- **Video and production crew** — ~5% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 27-4031 Camera Operators, Television, Video, and Film 2.6% + 27-2012 Producers and Directors 2.5%); automatable: partial. Corporate/brand video shoots are physical; editing, cutdowns and versioning automate heavily.
- **Management** — ~10% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 10.3%); automatable: partial. Production scheduling, bid assembly and studio ops automate; agency/brand relationships persist.
- **Sales and client service** — ~6% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-3091 Sales Reps of Services 3.0% + 43-4051 Customer Service Reps 1.9% + 41-2031 Retail Salespersons 1.9% (retail share likely reflects sibling 541921 in the shared mix)); automatable: partial. Quoting, usage-license paperwork and delivery automate; winning agency/brand accounts stays relationship work.
- **Processing and production support** — ~3% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 51-9151 Photographic Process Workers 3.1%); automatable: yes. Digital asset processing, file prep and delivery pipelines automate near-fully.
- **Admin and back office** — ~20% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-9061 Office Clerks 4.3% + 43-3031 Bookkeeping Clerks 2.1% + unlisted admin/production-assistant tail est. ~15%); automatable: yes. Booking, invoicing, licensing renewals and asset management automate near-fully.

**Suggested sources (start here, prefer primary; mark secondary republication as such):**
- (adoption) ASMP (American Society of Media Photographers) business surveys and advocacy materials — The commercial-photography trade body; has published member research and position papers on generative AI displacing assignment work.
- (adoption) PPA (Professional Photographers of America) research — Overlapping membership with commercial shooters; AI-tool adoption and pricing benchmark data.
- (capture_pricing) Getty Images / Shutterstock investor disclosures on generative AI and stock-imagery pricing — The stock-photo platforms' own generative pivots quantify how fast synthetic imagery substitutes for commissioned/stock photography — the substitution benchmark for this code's product.
- (capture_pricing) Wonderful Machine / commercial photography pricing surveys and 'pricing & negotiation' case archives — Publishes real commercial assignment budgets (day rates + usage licensing) — the concrete pricing-model evidence. *(existence unverified — confirm this source still exists/publishes before relying on it)*
- (consolidators_multiples) BizBuySell Insight Reports (photography/media services listings) — The only realistic multiple source; commercial studios trade as main-street SDE deals — no institutional comp universe exists.

**Capture questions to answer with evidence (for C):**
- Commercial work is priced as day rates plus usage licensing — when AI cuts retouching/production hours and clients can generate 'good enough' imagery in-house, does the licensing component (paying for rights to human-made images) collapse first, and can any operator hold day rates against procurement teams who now have a free substitute as leverage?
- Who owns the client relationship — the studio brand, or the individual photographer whose portfolio the agency/brand hired? Commercial books are notoriously personal; what does an acquirer retain when the shooter leaves?
- What genuinely stops a brand from generating product/lifestyle imagery with AI instead of commissioning a shoot — is it physical-product accuracy (e-commerce shots must match the real item), advertising-claim substantiation, or just habit, and how fast is each eroding?
- E-commerce volume photography (catalog/product-on-white) is high-throughput contract work — do those contracts already show AI/automation clawbacks in per-image pricing, the deflation channel a researcher would miss by looking only at creative assignment rates?

**Terminal-value question (for cross-check 2):**
Melter candidate, sharper than portrait: the deliverable is an image whose end use is commercial persuasion, and generative AI produces that deliverable without a camera — the surviving mechanism is physical-truth requirements (e-commerce images that must depict the actual product, regulated categories where depictions must be genuine, live corporate events) versus synthetic-acceptable uses (lifestyle, concept, advertising backgrounds) where paid demand evaporates. Score by segment share: how much of this code's revenue requires a photograph OF something real, versus imagery that merely needs to look real?

**Known traps for this industry:**
- n_band is 6 firms — and the record's derivation has the odd artifact that only the 500+ class lands in band (avg $13.1M revenue, implied $2.0M EBITDA) while the 100-499 class falls below it; the roll-up universe here is essentially nonexistent. Score N honestly rather than inventing a target set.
- EBITDA normalization: 4,417 of 4,902 firms are <5 employees — SDE with the owner-shooter's labor inside; replacement-cost adjustment typically erases margin. Nonemployer freelancers (large multiple of employer firms) set market prices but sit outside SUSB counts.
- Role-mix caveat: the 5-digit 54192 mix is shared with (and numerically dominated by) portrait studios 541921 — retail-sales and process-worker shares reflect portrait operations more than commercial studios; commercial firms carry more production assistants, digital techs and producers in the tail.
- Boundary confusion: portrait studios are 541921, video production companies are 512110 (many hybrid photo/video shops classify there), stock-photo platforms are 519130-adjacent, and real-estate media companies straddle 541922/541990 — trade data (ASMP, industry surveys) spans freelancers across all of them.
- Pricing-model note: usage-based licensing is a % -of-value structure that historically let photographers capture upside; generative AI turns it into the most deflation-exposed line because the client's alternative cost dropped to ~zero. Model pi_dist with the licensing collapse explicit rather than treating day-rate stability as the whole story.

## Step 1b — Declare judgment inputs

These are **declared opinions** — the only place judgment enters the record. Each must be bounded, with its own rationale and plausible range.

- `within_role_automatable_fraction` per role (using the provided `role_mix`): share of that role's labor cost in tasks current AI can substantially do. Licensed/judgment/relationship/physical work = not automatable; a small automatable sliver inside professional roles (drafting, research, documentation) is allowed if justified. → `ai_replaceable_share` = Σ(role share × fraction).
- `pi_dist` ∈ [0,1] — client-relationship control: who owns the client — the firm, an upstream gatekeeper, a platform, a procurement department? **Pricing-model deflation must be reflected here with the mechanism stated** (cost-plus/audited/hourly billing hands AI savings to clients).
- `pi_moat` ∈ [0,1] — barrier strength: licensure, switching costs, proprietary data, physical presence, and the realistic threat that clients use generic AI to bypass the firm entirely.
- `t50` — years from today until 50% of firms materially adopt AI in the automatable functions; derive from `current_adoption_pct` + `historical_analogs` and show the derivation. **t50 must appear in `top_uncertain_inputs` with its range and score impact — always.**

## Step 2 — Compute scores (show full arithmetic; no rounding anywhere)

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`; `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)` — anchor: freeing ≥25% of revenue = 10.
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = min(10, 10 / t50)`
- B subfactors (all continuous):
  `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)` — 10,000+ targets → 1.0
  `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; +0.1 if documented succession shortage, cap 1.0
  `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
  `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
  (No price term in B — pricing signal lives only in M.)
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`

## Step 3 — Mandatory cross-checks (answer each explicitly)

1. **Uplift consistency.** Convert V_raw into expected EBITDA uplift (pp). If uplift < 3pp while A ≥ 5, explain or revisit t50.
2. **Terminal value.** Answer the industry-specific terminal-value question. Distinguish: AI cutting the operator's costs vs AI replacing the operator's product. Classify: `DURABLE` (demand survives mature AI), `PRESSURED` (demand shrinks but persists), `MELTING` (the product itself is being replaced).
3. **No-proxy audit.** Confirm in writing that V and A were justified without reference to deal activity or consolidation trends.
4. **Pricing-model deflation.** State the billing mix and whether AI efficiency flows to clients as lower fees rather than to the operator as margin; confirm how this is reflected in `pi_dist`/`pi_moat`.
