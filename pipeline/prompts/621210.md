## Industry-specific guidance (verify everything; these are hints, not facts)

**Likely role structure (for the V breakdown):**
- **Dentists (generalists + specialists in tail)** — ~28-30% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 29-1021 Dentists, General 25.3% of wage bill at 9.8% of employment; specialist dentists (29-1022/1023/1024/1029 orthodontists, oral surgeons, etc.) sit in the unlisted tail, est. ~3-6% more); automatable: no. Licensed clinical procedures — restorations, extractions, surgery — are physical and legally reserved to dentists. AI augments diagnosis (FDA-cleared radiograph analysis) and treatment planning; whether that changes dentist hours or just case detection is an evidence question for the run, not an assumption. Note owner-dentists may take income as distributions rather than W-2 wages, so this share may be understated.
- **Dental hygienists** — ~24.5% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 29-1292 Dental Hygienists 24.5%); automatable: no. Licensed, physical chairside care (prophylaxis, perio maintenance); state practice acts set supervision rules. Not an AI-substitution surface.
- **Dental assistants** — ~21% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 31-9091 Dental Assistants 21.0%); automatable: no. Chairside physical assistance and sterilization; charting/notes dictation is the AI-touchable slice inside the role.
- **Front office and billing** — ~17.5% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-6013 Medical Secretaries 6.3% + 43-4171 Receptionists 4.6% + 43-1011 Office Supervisors 3.0% + 43-9061 Office Clerks 1.9% + 43-3021 Billing Clerks 1.0% + 43-6014 Admin Assistants 0.7%); automatable: partial. Scheduling, recall, insurance verification, claims submission/appeal, patient communication — the principal AI-exposed cost base in a dental office. In-person reception and chair-side handoffs persist; the run should set the fraction from task mix, not assume the whole desk disappears.
- **Practice management** — ~1.5% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-9111 Medical and Health Services Managers 1.3%); automatable: partial. Reporting and revenue-cycle oversight automate; HR and payer contracting persist.
- **Remainder / long tail** — ~6-8% of wage bill (OEWS basis: unlisted tail (lab technicians, sterilization techs, marketing)); automatable: partial. In-house lab work (crowns/aligners) is being reshaped by CAD/CAM and milling — a capex channel distinct from AI labor substitution.

**Suggested sources (start here, prefer primary; mark secondary republication as such):**
- (succession_owners) ADA Health Policy Institute (HPI) — dentist workforce, age distribution and practice ownership research — The authoritative source on dentist age, retirement timing, and the ownership-vs-employment trend (including DSO affiliation rates by age cohort).
- (adoption) ADA Health Policy Institute economic outlook polls and dental technology surveys — Periodic practice-level polling that has covered technology and AI tool use in dental offices.
- (adoption) FDA 510(k) clearance records and vendor disclosures for dental AI (e.g. Overjet, Pearl radiograph analysis) — Verifiable evidence that clinical dental AI exists and is deployed — adoption claims can be checked against cleared indications and announced payer/DSO deployments.
- (capture_pricing) ADA HPI dental payer-mix and fee survey data (Survey of Dental Fees / payer-mix research) — Establishes the FFS vs PPO vs Medicaid revenue mix and fee-schedule structure that determines who keeps any cost savings. *(existence unverified — confirm this source still exists/publishes before relying on it)*
- (consolidators_multiples) Group Dentistry Now (DSO industry news and annual DSO landscape reports) — Tracks DSO formation, affiliation counts and deal activity for the B and M inputs.
- (consolidators_multiples) Provident Healthcare Partners dental services M&A updates — Investment-bank coverage of dental practice/DSO transaction multiples by size tier.

**Capture questions to answer with evidence (for C):**
- Dental revenue arrives through PPO negotiated fee schedules (the largest share), Medicaid schedules, and fee-for-service self-pay — under fixed fee schedules a practice keeps any cost saving as margin in the short run, but do insurers ratchet reimbursement down over time (and do PPO leasing networks compress fees further), and what is the actual payer mix for band-size practices? Answer per payer line rather than in aggregate.
- Most states' dental practice acts require practices to be owned by licensed dentists; DSOs operate through management-services agreements — what does an acquirer legally own, what friction/risk does the MSO structure add (state enforcement actions exist), and does it change what the equity is worth?
- Patient relationships attach to the practice location and the treating dentist — what does volume retention look like after the selling doctor departs or reduces hours, and how dependent is the book on the owner's personal production (often 60-80% of collections in a solo practice)?
- Is there any channel by which patients substitute away from the operatory visit — teledentistry triage, AI-guided oral health monitoring, mail-order aligners (note the SmileDirectClub failure as evidence in either direction) — or is demand fully visit-bound regardless of AI capability?

**Terminal-value question (for cross-check 2):**
Dental care is delivered chairside by licensed clinicians — roughly 75% of the wage bill (dentists, hygienists, assistants) performs physical procedures that mature AI does not replace, and state practice acts legally reserve them. The terminal-value question is two-sided and should be answered from evidence, not direction: (a) how much of the ~18-20% front-office/billing wage share and claims overhead does AI actually remove, and under which payer arrangements does the practice keep that saving rather than ceding it at the next fee-schedule negotiation; and (b) does AI diagnostics change visit volume in either direction — earlier caries/perio detection increasing treated volume, or triage and remote monitoring reducing low-value visits? A researcher who assumes either direction without utilization evidence has failed the check.

**Known traps for this industry:**
- EBITDA normalization is the defining trap: solo-practice 'profit' is SDE with owner-dentist clinical compensation inside it. Normalize by subtracting market-rate associate compensation (roughly 28-32% of the doctor's own collections) before quoting margins or comparing solo-practice asking multiples to DSO exit multiples — the same practice can show a 35% 'margin' on an SDE basis and 10-15% EBITDA after normalization.
- The record's 15.80% margin (Damodaran hospitals bucket) used for n_band is a rough proxy for dental practice economics; n_band 369 (100-499 class only) likely understates the band since normalized-EBITDA dental groups in the 20-99 employee class (6,486 firms, avg revenue $4.8M) can clear $1M EBITDA at typical group-practice margins — treat 369 as a floor and note the sensitivity.
- Payer-mix heterogeneity: FFS/PPO-heavy suburban practices, Medicaid-heavy pediatric clinics, and specialty practices (ortho, OMS, endo — all inside 621210) have materially different margins, AI-relevant admin loads, and multiples; state the mix assumed when scoring.
- Corporate-practice-of-dentistry statutes vary by state (ownership, fee-splitting restrictions); DSO structures are standard but carry documented enforcement/litigation risk in some states — a B-side note, not a disqualifier; verify the state pattern rather than assuming either way.
- DSO consolidation (Heartland, Aspen, PDS, specialty ortho/OMS platforms) is a large and well-documented wave — admissible only for B (consolidator counts) and M (multiples); do not use DSO activity or DSO technology marketing as evidence of AI automatability or adoption (rules 2 and 3).
- role_mix is at 4-digit 6212, which is essentially identical in scope to 621210 — no sibling contamination issue here; the larger measurement caveat is owner-dentist compensation form (W-2 vs distributions) blurring the dentist wage share.

## Step 1b — Declare judgment inputs

These are **declared opinions** — the only place judgment enters the record. Each must be bounded, with its own rationale and plausible range.

- `within_role_automatable_fraction` per role (using the provided `role_mix`): share of that role's labor cost in tasks current AI can substantially do. Licensed/judgment/relationship/physical work = not automatable; a small automatable sliver inside professional roles (drafting, research, documentation) is allowed if justified. → `ai_replaceable_share` = Σ(role share × fraction).
- `pi_dist` ∈ [0,1] — client-relationship control: who owns the client — the firm, an upstream gatekeeper, a platform, a procurement department? **Pricing-model deflation must be reflected here with the mechanism stated** (cost-plus/audited/hourly billing hands AI savings to clients).
- `pi_moat` ∈ [0,1] — barrier strength: licensure, switching costs, proprietary data, physical presence, and the realistic threat that clients use generic AI to bypass the firm entirely.
- `t50` — years from today until 50% of firms materially adopt AI in the automatable functions; derive from `current_adoption_pct` + `historical_analogs` and show the derivation. **t50 must appear in `top_uncertain_inputs` with its range and score impact — always.**

## Step 2 — Compute scores (show full arithmetic; no rounding anywhere)

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`; `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)` — anchor: freeing ≥25% of revenue = 10.
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = min(10, 10 / t50)`
- B subfactors (all continuous):
  `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)` — 10,000+ targets → 1.0
  `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; +0.1 if documented succession shortage, cap 1.0
  `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
  `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
  (No price term in B — pricing signal lives only in M.)
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`

## Step 3 — Mandatory cross-checks (answer each explicitly)

1. **Uplift consistency.** Convert V_raw into expected EBITDA uplift (pp). If uplift < 3pp while A ≥ 5, explain or revisit t50.
2. **Terminal value.** Answer the industry-specific terminal-value question. Distinguish: AI cutting the operator's costs vs AI replacing the operator's product. Classify: `DURABLE` (demand survives mature AI), `PRESSURED` (demand shrinks but persists), `MELTING` (the product itself is being replaced).
3. **No-proxy audit.** Confirm in writing that V and A were justified without reference to deal activity or consolidation trends.
4. **Pricing-model deflation.** State the billing mix and whether AI efficiency flows to clients as lower fees rather than to the operator as margin; confirm how this is reflected in `pi_dist`/`pi_moat`.
