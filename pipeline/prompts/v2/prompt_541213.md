## Core rules

1. **No score may be set by judgment.** Every score is the mechanical output of its formula applied to recorded inputs. Your judgment goes into finding good inputs and into the `reviewer_note` field — never into overriding arithmetic. If a formula result contradicts your intuition, report the formula result as the score and describe the disagreement in `reviewer_note`.
2. **PE/M&A deal activity (add-ons, consolidation waves) may only inform B and M.** It is never evidence for V (AI value) or A (AI adoption speed).
3. **EBITDA normalization.** All margins and multiples must be on an EBITDA basis after market-rate owner/partner compensation. If a source reports SDE or pre-owner-comp profit, convert and state the conversion. In professional services owner draws routinely distort margins — normalize explicitly.
4. **Arithmetic**: round half up. Show every calculation.
5. **Catch-all codes** ("Other …", "All Other …"): first identify the top-3 revenue sub-segments inside the code, then score the DOMINANT sub-segment and say so; record heterogeneity in `reviewer_note`.

## Industry-specific guidance (verify everything; these are hints, not facts)

**Likely role structure (for V breakdown):**
- Seasonal tax preparers — ~55% of labor, automatable: True. Interview-driven 1040 prep on top of software is the canonical AI-automatable task; already largely software-guided.
- Owners / franchisees (incl. draws) — ~18% of labor, automatable: partial. Local marketing, hiring and multi-unit operations; the client file often belongs to the brand/franchise system.
- Office managers / seasonal supervisors — ~12% of labor, automatable: partial. Quality review and exception handling; AI review tools compress this layer.
- Receptionists / intake staff — ~15% of labor, automatable: True. Appointment setting, document intake and status calls are fully automatable.

**Suggested sources (start here, prefer primary; mark secondary republication as such):**
- Labor & roles: BLS OEWS, NAICS 541213 detail where published (else 5412) — annual; NATP Tax Professional Fee Study — National Association of Tax Professionals, periodic; NSA Income and Fees Survey — National Society of Accountants, biennial
- Capture / market structure: IRS e-file and Direct File usage statistics — annual filing-season data; H&R Block 10-K and investor materials (assisted vs DIY client counts); Intuit 10-K (TurboTax, full-service vs DIY mix)
- AI adoption: IRS Return Preparer Office PTIN statistics; H&R Block and Intuit investor-day disclosures on AI-assisted prep
- Firm counts & sizes: IRS PTIN holder counts by preparer type; Census Bureau SUSB / CBP, NAICS 541213; Franchise Disclosure Documents (H&R Block, Liberty Tax, Jackson Hewitt) for unit counts
- Multiples / M&A: Accounting Practice Sales (tax practice listings and multiples); Franchise resale data from FDDs and franchise brokers; BizBuySell Insight Report

**Capture questions to answer with evidence (for C):**
- What share of clients are EITC/refund-advance filers who come for the money product, not the prep — and does that embedded-lending relationship insulate the storefront from free AI filing?
- Does IRS Direct File plus generic consumer AI eliminate the paid simple-1040 segment, and how fast is state-level adoption of direct filing expanding?
- In franchise systems, who owns the client file — brand or franchisee — and what does that do to a local acquisition's durability?

**Terminal-value question (for cross-check 2):**
Simple-return preparation is arguably the most AI-terminal paid service in this whole set: within 5-10 years, does storefront prep survive only as embedded refund lending (transfers/advances) plus complex, small-business, immigrant and non-filer-resolution niches — and what fraction of today's revenue do those represent?

**Known traps for this industry:**
No federal licensure — a PTIN suffices; only a few states (CA via CTEC, OR, MD, NY) regulate preparers, so barriers are minimal and the moat is brand plus foot traffic. Extreme seasonality (most revenue Jan–Apr) distorts labor and margin comparisons. Reported revenue often bundles financial products (refund transfers, advances) — separate them when scoring deflation. Do not blend with 541211: complex/business returns behave like CPA work; this code is dominated by consumer 1040 volume. Franchise royalty loads change unit economics versus independents.

## Step 1 — Collect inputs (each with source name, URL, and the specific figure quoted)

Prefer primary sources: Census CBP/SUSB (firm counts, size classes, payroll), BLS OEWS industry table for the closest NAICS (role mix and wages), BLS QCEW, trade-association benchmark surveys. For multiples: industry-specific M&A reports and broker data. Mark any input you could not source as `"quality": "ESTIMATE"` with your basis stated.

**For V (value AI frees up):**
- `labor_share` — total labor cost (employee payroll + benefits + imputed market-rate owner labor) as share of revenue.
- `ai_replaceable_share` — share of that labor cost in tasks current AI can substantially do. Itemize by role using the OEWS role mix: share of labor cost, automatable flag, within-role automatable fraction. Licensed/judgment/relationship/physical work = not automatable; a small automatable sliver inside professional roles (drafting, research, documentation) is allowed if justified.

**For C (share of freed value the operator keeps):**
- `pi_dist` ∈ [0,1] — client-relationship control. Who owns the client: the firm, an upstream gatekeeper, a platform, or a procurement department? Use the capture questions above.
- `pi_moat` ∈ [0,1] — barrier strength: licensure/certification, switching costs, proprietary data/records, physical presence, and the realistic threat that clients use generic AI to bypass the firm entirely.

**For A (speed of AI adoption by the market):**
- `current_adoption_pct` — share of US firms in this industry already materially using AI in the automatable functions (industry surveys; be explicit what counts as material).
- `historical_analogs` — years this industry took to reach ~50% on comparable prior technology.
- `t50` — years from today until 50% of firms materially adopt AI in the automatable functions; derive from the two inputs above and show the derivation.

**For B (can a fund actually buy in):**
- `n_total` — number of firms (Census CBP/SUSB).
- `n_band` — estimated number of firms with $1–10M EBITDA. Derive from SUSB size distribution (employment or revenue classes → revenue → normalized margin). Show the chain.
- `owners_60plus_pct` — share of owners aged 60+ (association workforce data; else derive and mark ESTIMATE).
- `active_consolidators` — count of PE-backed platforms actively doing add-ons in this specific industry in the last 24 months.

**For M (multiple arbitrage):**
- `buy_mult` — median EV/EBITDA actually paid for single-location/small firms (normalized per rule 3).
- `exit_mult` — EV/EBITDA for $10M+ EBITDA platforms in recent transactions or credible comps.

## Step 2 — Compute scores (show arithmetic for each)

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`; `V = round(10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25))` — anchor: freeing ≥25% of revenue = 10.
- `C = round(10 × pi_dist × pi_moat)`
- `A = min(10, round(10 / t50))`
- B subfactors:
  `TD` from n_band: <50 → 0.1; 50–500 → 0.3; 500–2,000 → 0.6; 2,000–5,000 → 0.8; >5,000 → 1.0
  `OW` from owners_60plus_pct: <15% → 0.2; 15–25% → 0.4; 25–35% → 0.6; >35% → 0.8; +0.1 if documented succession shortage, cap 1.0
  `PD = max(0, (exit_mult − buy_mult) / exit_mult)`
  `CFD` from active_consolidators: ≤2 → 0.1; 3–10 → 0.3; 11–30 → 0.6; >30 → 0.9
  `B = round(10 × (TD × OW × PD)^(1/3) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD))`
- `M = min(10, max(0, round((exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult × 4)))`
- `S = V × C × A × B × M`

## Step 3 — Mandatory cross-checks (answer each explicitly)

1. **Uplift consistency.** Convert V_raw into expected EBITDA uplift (pp). If uplift < 3pp while A ≥ 5, explain or revisit t50.
2. **Terminal value.** Answer the industry-specific terminal-value question above. Distinguish: AI cutting the operator's costs vs AI replacing the operator's product.
3. **No-proxy audit.** Confirm in writing that V and A were justified without reference to deal activity or consolidation trends.
4. **Pricing-model deflation.** How does this industry price (hourly, retainer, fixed fee, % of value, per unit)? If hourly/per-unit: assess whether AI-driven efficiency cuts billable volume so savings flow to clients as lower fees rather than to the operator as margin. If material, reflect it in `pi_dist`/`pi_moat` evidence and state the mechanism.
