## Industry-specific guidance (verify everything; these are hints, not facts)

**Likely role structure (for the V breakdown):**
- **Property-management occupations (likely 3-digit proxy contamination)** — ~37% of the proxy — treat as contamination, not brokerage structure of wage bill (OEWS basis: 49-9071 Maintenance and Repair Workers 12.9% + 11-9141 Property/Real Estate/Community Association Managers 15.5% + 37-2011 Janitors 2.4% + 41-2021 Counter and Rental Clerks 3.7% + 49-1011 Supervisors of Mechanics 2.8% — role_mix is 3-digit 531 (quality LOW), so lessors (5311) and property managers (53131) dominate the mix; these occupations are largely NOT present in 531210 brokerage offices); automatable: no. Physical building operations from sibling codes. Do not build the 531210 V breakdown on these; note the contamination explicitly in the output.
- **Real estate sales agents and brokers (W-2 slice only)** — ~12-14% of W-2 wages — but the vast majority of agents are 1099 independent contractors outside the wage bill entirely of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-9022 Real Estate Sales Agents 8.7% + 41-9021 Real Estate Brokers in the unlisted tail (est. ~3-5%)); automatable: partial. AI already does much of the agent's task list (listing prep, CMA/pricing, marketing copy, scheduling, disclosure paperwork); what it does not do is hold the state license required to be paid a commission, or the trust/negotiation relationship. Crucially, agent commission splits are a revenue pass-through, not an employee cost the operator can harvest.
- **Brokerage management** — ~8% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 8.3%); automatable: partial. Managing-broker compliance supervision is statutory (a licensed broker must supervise agents); recruiting and reporting automate.
- **Office, transaction coordination and bookkeeping** — ~10-12% incl. tail of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-9061 Office Clerks 3.3% + 43-6014 Secretaries/Admin Assistants 2.1% + 43-3031 Bookkeeping Clerks 2.4% + admin tail); automatable: yes. Transaction coordination, listing administration, commission disbursement accounting — the genuinely automatable W-2 cost inside a brokerage, and it is a small base.
- **Remainder / long tail** — ~25-30% of wage bill (OEWS basis: unlisted tail of the 3-digit proxy (marketing, IT, appraisal-adjacent, leasing staff)); automatable: partial. Mixed; at 531210 proper this is mostly marketing and support staff.

**Capture questions to answer with evidence (for C):**
- Brokerage revenue is a percentage commission on transaction value, but 70-90%+ of gross commission passes straight through to 1099 agents under commission splits (100%-split and cap models like eXp/RE-MAX leave the brokerage a fee, not a margin) — if AI removes labor from the transaction, trace precisely who keeps the saving: the brokerage (whose retained 'company dollar' is thin), the individual agent (whose split is contractual), or the consumer via post-settlement commission-rate compression. Which line item on a brokerage P&L actually gets bigger?
- Who owns the client and the lead flow: the agent's book is personal and fully portable across brokerages, and upstream, Zillow/Realtor.com/Homes.com and the MLSs control search traffic and increasingly sell the leads back to agents — when AI improves search, matching and transaction automation, does the value accrue to portals and MLS platforms rather than to any brokerage an acquirer could buy?
- What does an acquirer of a brokerage own after the deal — agents can leave with their books the next day, the brand is often franchised (a separate royalty stream), and the office itself is increasingly virtual. Is there any durable asset here beyond a recruiting treadmill?
- What stops sellers and buyers from bypassing the traditional agent entirely — flat-fee MLS listing plus AI-drafted disclosures plus a transaction attorney — and has the post-2024 commission decoupling measurably increased that bypass or compressed effective commission rates?

**Terminal-value question (for cross-check 2):**
The brokerage's product is licensed intermediation of a home sale: access, pricing judgment, negotiation, and paperwork shepherding, paid as a percentage commission. Mature AI makes listing data, valuation, marketing, scheduling and document preparation self-serve; the statutory holdout is that state license law requires a licensed agent/broker to collect a commission, and the practical holdout is consumer trust in the largest transaction of their lives. Does paid full-commission intermediation survive — or does it compress to a low-fee licensed-execution wrapper while portals and AI capture the discovery and decision value? Answer with the post-settlement commission-rate evidence, and keep separate the two ways this fails: AI cutting brokerage office costs (a small W-2 base) versus AI plus fee compression shrinking the commission pool that is the industry's entire revenue.

**Known traps for this industry:**
- Structural mis-fit the run must confront head-on: the operator's biggest 'cost line' — agent commission splits — is not employee labor and cannot be harvested by AI; the W-2 wage bill (labor_share 0.1826, one of the lowest in the screen) is the only automatable base, and it is small. An AI-labor thesis mechanically over-scores this code if applied to receipts.
- Revenue-basis trap: gross commission income vs retained company dollar differ by 4-10x; the record's 3.96% Damodaran margin on receipts reflects pass-through economics. Brokerage valuations (RTC-style) are multiples of company dollar or EBITDA after splits — never compare across bases.
- role_mix is a 3-digit 531 proxy (quality LOW) contaminated by lessors and property managers — maintenance workers and property managers are not brokerage staff; rebuild the V structure for 531210 proper and flag it.
- The 2024 NAR settlement decoupled buyer-agent compensation — commission structure is in live flux; single-year pricing or margin data (especially pre-2024) does not represent the forward state.
- Boundary confusions: 531110/531120 lessors, 531311/531312 property managers (recurring-fee economics, the actual target of much 'real estate services' PE activity), 531320 appraisers — surveys and comps labeled 'real estate services' usually blend these; check scope before quoting.
- EBITDA normalization: broker-owners take income as commission on their own deals plus profit; franchise royalties and referral fees distort comparability; count of 150,892 firms is dominated by 140,557 sub-5-employee shells (single-broker entities), so n_total wildly overstates acquirable businesses — n_band is only 259.

## Step 1b — Declare judgment inputs

These are **declared opinions** — the only place judgment enters the record. Each must be bounded, with its own rationale and plausible range.

- `within_role_automatable_fraction` per role (using the provided `role_mix`): share of that role's labor cost in tasks current AI can substantially do. Licensed/judgment/relationship/physical work = not automatable; a small automatable sliver inside professional roles (drafting, research, documentation) is allowed if justified. → `ai_replaceable_share` = Σ(role share × fraction).
- `pi_dist` ∈ [0,1] — client-relationship control: who owns the client — the firm, an upstream gatekeeper, a platform, a procurement department? **Pricing-model deflation must be reflected here with the mechanism stated** (cost-plus/audited/hourly billing hands AI savings to clients).
- `pi_moat` ∈ [0,1] — barrier strength: licensure, switching costs, proprietary data, physical presence, and the realistic threat that clients use generic AI to bypass the firm entirely.
- `t50` — years from today until 50% of firms materially adopt AI in the automatable functions; derive from `current_adoption_pct` + `historical_analogs` and show the derivation. **t50 must appear in `top_uncertain_inputs` with its range and score impact — always.**

## Step 2 — Compute scores (show full arithmetic; no rounding anywhere)

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`; `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)` — anchor: freeing ≥25% of revenue = 10.
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = min(10, 10 / t50)`
- B subfactors (all continuous):
  `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)` — 10,000+ targets → 1.0
  `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; +0.1 if documented succession shortage, cap 1.0
  `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
  `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
  (No price term in B — pricing signal lives only in M.)
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`

## Step 3 — Mandatory cross-checks (answer each explicitly)

1. **Uplift consistency.** Convert V_raw into expected EBITDA uplift (pp). If uplift < 3pp while A ≥ 5, explain or revisit t50.
2. **Terminal value.** Answer the industry-specific terminal-value question. Distinguish: AI cutting the operator's costs vs AI replacing the operator's product. Classify: `DURABLE` (demand survives mature AI), `PRESSURED` (demand shrinks but persists), `MELTING` (the product itself is being replaced).
3. **No-proxy audit.** Confirm in writing that V and A were justified without reference to deal activity or consolidation trends.
4. **Pricing-model deflation.** State the billing mix and whether AI efficiency flows to clients as lower fees rather than to the operator as margin; confirm how this is reflected in `pi_dist`/`pi_moat`.
