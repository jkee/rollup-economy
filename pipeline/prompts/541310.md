**Capture questions to answer with evidence (for C):**
- Architecture fees are commonly a percentage of construction cost or a lump sum benchmarked to it, with construction-document production the largest phase: when AI collapses CD production hours, does the percentage-of-cost convention let firms keep the spread as margin — or do owners and design-builders renegotiate fee percentages downward once production cost visibly drops, and how fast has that repricing happened in past productivity shifts (CAD, BIM)?
- Do owners/developers hire the architect for design judgment and brand, or only because permitting requires a stamped set — and how much of the fee sits in automatable production phases versus relationship phases (SD, CA)?
- Does design-build growth shift the client relationship to contractors, reducing architects to subcontracted stamp providers with fee-taker economics?
- In residential and light-commercial work that is often exempt from architect requirements, does AI design-to-permit tooling bypass architects entirely — and what share of small-firm revenue sits in those exempt segments?

**Terminal-value question (for cross-check 2):**
If AI can generate code-compliant permit sets, architecture bifurcates into a liability/stamp utility plus a taste-and-brand layer at the high end. The deciding mechanism is the permit counter: do state licensure laws and building departments keep requiring a licensed architect in responsible charge for the building types small firms serve (noting broad residential exemptions), preserving a toll even as production fees deflate — or does the exempt-plus-plan-stamp perimeter expand until the paid product shrinks to judgment and liability at a fraction of today's fee pool? Distinguish AI cutting the firm's production cost from AI shrinking the stamped-set product the firm sells.

**Known traps for this industry:**

- Ownership restrictions: several states restrict architecture-firm ownership to licensed professionals (New York notably strict; rules vary widely) — check the state mix before assuming clean PE buyouts; A&E consolidators do buy architecture bolt-ons, so structures exist.
- EBITDA normalization: principal comp inside small-firm profit; Zweig-style multiples often pre-owner-comp — normalize before comparing (rule 3). ABI-tracked cyclicality is severe; do not score off a single boom or bust year.

- Boundary confusion: engineering is 541330, landscape architecture 541320, drafting services 541340, interior design 541410, building inspection 541350 — A&E surveys (Deltek, Zweig, Morrissey Goodale) span the group; check whose members a benchmark covers before quoting it for architecture alone.

## Step 1b — Declare judgment inputs

These are **declared opinions** — the only place judgment enters the record. Each must be bounded, with its own rationale and plausible range.

- `within_role_automatable_fraction` per role (using the provided `role_mix`): share of that role's labor cost in tasks current AI can substantially do. Licensed/judgment/relationship/physical work = not automatable; a small automatable sliver inside professional roles (drafting, research, documentation) is allowed if justified. → `ai_replaceable_share` = Σ(role share × fraction).
- `pi_dist` ∈ [0,1] — client-relationship control: who owns the client — the firm, an upstream gatekeeper, a platform, a procurement department? **Pricing-model deflation must be reflected here with the mechanism stated** (cost-plus/audited/hourly billing hands AI savings to clients).
- `pi_moat` ∈ [0,1] — barrier strength: licensure, switching costs, proprietary data, physical presence, and the realistic threat that clients use generic AI to bypass the firm entirely.
- `t50` — years from today until 50% of firms materially adopt AI in the automatable functions; derive from `current_adoption_pct` + `historical_analogs` and show the derivation. **t50 must appear in `top_uncertain_inputs` with its range and score impact — always.**

## Step 2 — Compute scores (show full arithmetic; no rounding anywhere)

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`; `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)` — anchor: freeing ≥25% of revenue = 10.
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = min(10, 10 / t50)`
- B subfactors (all continuous):
  `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)` — 10,000+ targets → 1.0
  `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; +0.1 if documented succession shortage, cap 1.0
  `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
  `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
  (No price term in B — pricing signal lives only in M.)
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`

## Step 3 — Mandatory cross-checks (answer each explicitly)

Classify: `DURABLE` (demand survives mature AI), `PRESSURED` (demand shrinks but persists), `MELTING` (the product itself is being replaced).
3. **No-proxy audit.** Confirm in writing that V and A were justified without reference to deal activity or consolidation trends.
4. **Pricing-model deflation.** State the billing mix and whether AI efficiency flows to clients as lower fees rather than to the operator as margin; confirm how this is reflected in `pi_dist`/`pi_moat`.
