## Industry-specific guidance (verify everything; these are hints, not facts)

**Likely role structure (for the V breakdown):**
- **Frontline agents (customer service, telemarketing, collections)** — ~35% as listed; likely 55-70% at the 6-digit code — verify against any 6-digit staffing evidence of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-4051 Customer Service Representatives 25.9% of wage bill + 41-9041 Telemarketers 4.4% + 43-3011 Bill and Account Collectors 4.9%; note the record's role_mix is 4-digit 5614, which dilutes agent share with other business-support codes — at 561422 proper, agent occupations plausibly run 55-70% of the wage bill); automatable: yes. This is the core exposure: AI voice and chat agents now handle routine inbound/outbound interactions end-to-end (order status, collections reminders, tier-1 support, outbound qualification). The residual is escalation, empathy-critical, and complex multi-system cases — the run should size that residual from deflection-rate evidence, not assume it.
- **Supervisors and QA** — ~5-6% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-1011 First-Line Supervisors of Office and Administrative Support Workers 5.5%); automatable: partial. QA scoring and coaching analytics are already AI-saturated (100% interaction scoring vs sampled); span-of-control widens. Live escalation handling persists.
- **Management** — ~7-8% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 7.5%); automatable: partial. Workforce management, forecasting and reporting automate substantially; client relationship management persists.
- **Sales (client acquisition for the bureau itself)** — ~4% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-3091 Sales Representatives of Services 4.0%); automatable: partial. Enterprise BPO selling is relationship/RFP work; proposal production automates.
- **IT and support** — ~3-5% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 15-1232 Computer User Support Specialists 1.6% + tech tail); automatable: partial. Desktop/telephony support automates; CCaaS platform integration work grows with AI deployment.
- **Other admin and proxy-artifact occupations** — remainder ~10-15% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-2021 Counter and Rental Clerks 1.7% + 31-9094 Medical Transcriptionists 1.25% + 43-9051 Mail Clerks 1.5% — these look like artifacts of the 4-digit 5614 proxy (document prep, other business support), not real 561422 staffing); automatable: yes. Treat with caution: the proxy mixes in sibling-code occupations. Routine clerical work is near-fully automatable wherever it genuinely exists in contact centers.

**Suggested sources (start here, prefer primary; mark secondary republication as such):**
- (adoption) ContactBabel US Contact Center Decision-Makers' Guide (annual) — The standard operational benchmark for US contact centers; recent editions carry AI/automation deployment and deflection-rate data usable for current_adoption_pct.
- (adoption) CCW Digital (Customer Contact Week) market study series — Practitioner surveys on AI agent deployment and human-agent headcount plans.
- (capture_pricing) Everest Group CXM services research (PEAK Matrix and market reports) — Tracks outsourced CX pricing models (per-FTE, per-transaction, outcome-based) and the shift under gen AI — directly evidences who keeps the savings.
- (consolidators_multiples) Public CX/BPO comparables: Teleperformance, Concentrix, TTEC filings and disclosed deal multiples — Exit-tier multiples and, in their own disclosures, evidence of AI cannibalization of per-hour revenue; TTEC/Concentrix trading multiples show how the market prices the melt.
- (succession_owners) Site Selection Group contact center labor and site reports — Domestic facility and operator landscape context; small-operator demographics are thin — expect to estimate owner age from generic small-business data. *(existence unverified — confirm this source still exists/publishes before relying on it)*
- (other) NelsonHall customer experience services research — Independent tracking of CX outsourcing volumes and AI-driven contract restructuring.

**Capture questions to answer with evidence (for C):**
- Outsourced contact-center work is billed per agent-hour, per FTE, per minute, or per transaction — when AI deflects contacts or cuts handle time, the billed unit itself disappears. Is there evidence operators are successfully re-pricing to outcome/resolution-based contracts and keeping the spread, or do competitive rebids pass essentially all AI savings (and the revenue base) to clients?
- Enterprise clients run multi-vendor RFPs on 2-3 year contracts and own the customer data and telephony stack — what stops a client from licensing AI voice agents directly from CCaaS/AI vendors (Genesys, NICE, Five9, or model providers) and cutting the bureau out entirely?
- Does an incumbent bureau own anything defensible for the AI era — domain-trained workflows, integrations, compliance certifications (HIPAA, PCI), licensed collections operations — or is its asset simply trained labor that AI replaces?
- The size distribution is barbell-shaped (315 firms at 500+ employees vs 926 micro firms): do small bureaus serving SMB clients have stickier relationships and less sophisticated clients (slower bypass), or are they simply subscale and first to lose volume?

**Terminal-value question (for cross-check 2):**
A telemarketing/contact-center bureau's product is human conversation sold by the hour or seat — no license, statute, or physical act stands between the client and an AI voice agent that does the same job. At mature AI capability, does paid demand for outsourced *human* agents survive at all, and at what volume — i.e., is AI here a tool that cuts the operator's delivery cost, or is the AI agent the direct replacement of the product the operator sells? Force the analysis to size the residual (escalation, empathy-critical, regulated-disclosure calls) and to say what happens to per-unit pricing on that residual; note also that domestic bureaus already lost the cost-arbitrage war to offshore once.

**Known traps for this industry:**
- Pricing-model deflation is the central mechanism, not a side effect: per-hour/per-FTE/per-minute billing converts every AI efficiency gain into fewer billed units. Model this explicitly in pi_dist; an operator 'adopting AI' here is often shrinking its own revenue.
- Melting-base check: verify QCEW employment and SUSB firm-count trends for 561422 before assuming a stable acquisition universe — n_total is only 2,299 firms and the band (201, all in the 100-499 class) sits exactly where AI-driven volume loss hits hardest.
- Boundary confusion: 561421 Telephone Answering Services is a sibling with different (small-account, subscription) economics; in-house corporate contact centers are outside this code entirely — most 'contact center AI adoption' surveys cover in-house centers, so check whether a statistic describes buyers of BPO or the BPO operators themselves.
- The record's role_mix is a 4-digit 5614 proxy — occupations like medical transcriptionists and mail clerks are sibling-code artifacts; re-weight toward agent occupations for V.
- Offshore delivery (Philippines, India, LatAm nearshore) competes with domestic seats and is invisible in US NAICS data; domestic revenue can melt via offshoring as well as AI — do not attribute all decline to AI.
- EBITDA quirks: facility leases for seat capacity, high attrition/training costs, and client-concentration risk (one lost logo can be 20%+ of revenue for a mid-size bureau) — quality-of-earnings matters more than the multiple.

## Step 1b — Declare judgment inputs

These are **declared opinions** — the only place judgment enters the record. Each must be bounded, with its own rationale and plausible range.

- `within_role_automatable_fraction` per role (using the provided `role_mix`): share of that role's labor cost in tasks current AI can substantially do. Licensed/judgment/relationship/physical work = not automatable; a small automatable sliver inside professional roles (drafting, research, documentation) is allowed if justified. → `ai_replaceable_share` = Σ(role share × fraction).
- `pi_dist` ∈ [0,1] — client-relationship control: who owns the client — the firm, an upstream gatekeeper, a platform, a procurement department? **Pricing-model deflation must be reflected here with the mechanism stated** (cost-plus/audited/hourly billing hands AI savings to clients).
- `pi_moat` ∈ [0,1] — barrier strength: licensure, switching costs, proprietary data, physical presence, and the realistic threat that clients use generic AI to bypass the firm entirely.
- `t50` — years from today until 50% of firms materially adopt AI in the automatable functions; derive from `current_adoption_pct` + `historical_analogs` and show the derivation. **t50 must appear in `top_uncertain_inputs` with its range and score impact — always.**

## Step 2 — Compute scores (show full arithmetic; no rounding anywhere)

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`; `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)` — anchor: freeing ≥25% of revenue = 10.
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = min(10, 10 / t50)`
- B subfactors (all continuous):
  `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)` — 10,000+ targets → 1.0
  `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; +0.1 if documented succession shortage, cap 1.0
  `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
  `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
  (No price term in B — pricing signal lives only in M.)
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`

## Step 3 — Mandatory cross-checks (answer each explicitly)

1. **Uplift consistency.** Convert V_raw into expected EBITDA uplift (pp). If uplift < 3pp while A ≥ 5, explain or revisit t50.
2. **Terminal value.** Answer the industry-specific terminal-value question. Distinguish: AI cutting the operator's costs vs AI replacing the operator's product. Classify: `DURABLE` (demand survives mature AI), `PRESSURED` (demand shrinks but persists), `MELTING` (the product itself is being replaced).
3. **No-proxy audit.** Confirm in writing that V and A were justified without reference to deal activity or consolidation trends.
4. **Pricing-model deflation.** State the billing mix and whether AI efficiency flows to clients as lower fees rather than to the operator as margin; confirm how this is reflected in `pi_dist`/`pi_moat`.
