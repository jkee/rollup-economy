## Industry-specific guidance (verify everything; these are hints, not facts)

**Likely role structure (for the V breakdown):**
- **Producers / insurance sales agents** — ~35% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-3021 Insurance Sales Agents 34.6% of wage bill (5-digit 52421 mix — clean, code-specific)); automatable: partial. Quoting, application data entry, comparison shopping across carriers and proposal assembly automate heavily (AI quoting/submission tools are live); relationship selling, complex commercial risk placement and renewal retention conversations persist. Personal-lines producers are far more exposed than middle-market commercial producers — the within-role fraction should be split by line of business.
- **Account managers / CSRs (service staff)** — ~15-19% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-4051 Customer Service Representatives 7.3% + account-manager roles in the unlisted tail, est. ~8-12% more); automatable: partial. Certificates of insurance, endorsements, policy checking, renewal remarketing and carrier correspondence are the industry's biggest AI target and are automating fast; client advocacy in claims and complex service stays human.
- **Policy processing and claims support clerks** — ~7% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-9041 Insurance Claims and Policy Processing Clerks 4.3% + 43-9061 Office Clerks 1.8% + 43-6014 Secretaries 1.1%); automatable: yes. Pure document/data workflow — near-fully automatable with current AI plus carrier API connectivity.
- **Claims adjusters and examiners (in-house at larger agencies/MGAs)** — ~4% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1031 Claims Adjusters, Examiners, and Investigators 4.0%); automatable: partial. Desk adjudication of routine claims automates substantially; field inspection and negotiation persist. Mostly present in MGA/TPA-flavored firms within the code.
- **Underwriters (MGA/program business)** — ~3-4% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-2053 Insurance Underwriters 3.5%); automatable: partial. Signals the MGA/MGU sub-segment holding delegated underwriting authority; risk triage and file assembly automate, appetite judgment and carrier accountability persist.
- **Management and supervision** — ~10% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 8.2% + 43-1011 First-Line Supervisors of Office/Admin 2.0%); automatable: partial. Book analytics, carrier-relationship reporting and ops management automate substantially; carrier contract negotiation and producer recruitment persist.
- **Marketing and remaining back office** — ~20% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1161 Market Research Analysts and Marketing Specialists 1.5% + long tail of admin/accounting occupations, est. remainder ~18-22%); automatable: yes. Lead generation, cross-sell campaigns, accounting/commission reconciliation and compliance filings automate near-fully.

**Capture questions to answer with evidence (for C):**
- Agency revenue is a carrier-set commission (roughly 10-15% of premium) — the client pays the carrier, not the agency, so AI cost cuts cannot be competed away through client fee pressure in the core model: does the operator therefore keep AI savings as margin dollar-for-dollar, and what are the two leaks — carriers trimming commission rates over time, and fee-based large-commercial accounts where clients do see and negotiate the cost?
- Who owns the client — the agency (it legally owns expirations/renewals, the industry's core asset doctrine) or the individual producer (books walk despite non-piracy agreements)? What do acquirer retention rates say about how much of a purchased book actually renews?
- What stops insureds from bypassing the agent — direct carriers (GEICO/Progressive precedent in personal lines) and embedded/AI-native distribution already exist, so the question is line-specific: why has independent-agency share held in commercial lines, and does AI finally give carriers/insurtechs the tool to underwrite small-commercial direct?
- Do carriers capture the AI margin upstream — cutting commission schedules or pushing service work down to agencies without compensation — as their own AI reduces what they need agents for (data collection, first-line underwriting)?
- Recurring renewal commissions make agency EBITDA unusually durable — but with 3,236 band firms and a decade-long buyer frenzy (Acrisure, Hub, BroadStreet, World, Inszone, PCF et al.), have entry multiples already converged toward platform exit multiples, killing the arbitrage the roll-up model needs? Quote MarshBerry/OPTIS current numbers rather than assuming a spread.

**Terminal-value question (for cross-check 2):**
Distinguish AI cutting agency costs (service/processing labor — big, real) from AI replacing the agency's product (matching insureds to carriers): the mechanism holding paid demand is the independent agency's ownership of renewals plus licensed-intermediary requirements and carrier appetite fragmentation — but none of that is a statute protecting the channel itself; personal lines already proved direct distribution wins when the interaction is simple enough (GEICO), so the terminal question is whether AI makes small-commercial and middle-market placement simple enough for carriers and AI-native platforms to go direct, collapsing the commission pool the roll-up is buying. What share of the target book is complexity-protected commercial risk vs. commoditizable personal/small-commercial lines?

**Known traps for this industry:**
- This is the most consolidator-crowded services vertical in America (Acrisure, Hub, BroadStreet, Gallagher, World, Inszone, PCF, Keystone, plus PE-backed platforms in the hundreds) — usable for B and M only, never as evidence for V or A (rule 2). The strategic implication for B is crowding: verify how many of the 3,236 band firms remain independent and unapproached.
- Multiples discipline: MarshBerry/OPTIS document that agency multiples rose for a decade (base multiples for quality mid-size agencies reached low-double-digit EBITDA at peak, with earnouts on top) — the buy-side is NOT cheap; source current-tier multiples and model the entry-exit spread honestly rather than assuming main-street discounts. Small (<$1M revenue) agencies still trade lower — tier the multiple by size with sources.
- EBITDA normalization: owner comp inside SDE at the small end; contingent/profit-sharing commissions (carrier bonuses) are volatile and buyers haircut them; premium-market hardness/softness swings commission revenue with pricing cycles — normalize contingents and note where we sit in the P&C rate cycle before trusting TTM EBITDA.
- Heterogeneity inside the code: retail P&C agencies, benefits/employee-benefits agencies, life/annuity shops, personal-lines-only agencies, MGAs/MGUs and wholesale brokers all sit in 524210 — economics and AI exposure differ (MGAs carry underwriters; benefits agencies face different disruption). Score the dominant sub-segment (retail independent P&C/benefits agencies) and note the mix; the 4-digit-level surveys (Big 'I', Reagan) cover exactly the retail-agency slice.
- Boundary confusion: direct carriers and their captive-agent forces are in 5241 (carriers), TPAs/claims administrators are 524292, and title agencies are 524127 — 'insurance distribution' market sizes often blend them; also QCEW/SUSB place some captive-agent operations here. Check coverage of any benchmark against the independent-agency definition.
- Winner-anchor discipline: the wage bill is white-collar and heavily automatable on paper (~30%+ pure processing/service), which tempts an inflated V — but revenue is commission on premium (price set upstream), so the value of automation is margin expansion on a fixed revenue pool, not capacity to sell more hours. Keep the V story consistent with the commission mechanics.
- Licensing: every producer must hold state licenses (NIPR); AI cannot legally bind coverage or advise on it in most states without a licensed human — a real but shallow moat (carriers hold licenses too and can go direct); note it in pi_moat without overweighting.

## Step 1b — Declare judgment inputs

These are **declared opinions** — the only place judgment enters the record. Each must be bounded, with its own rationale and plausible range.

- `within_role_automatable_fraction` per role (using the provided `role_mix`): share of that role's labor cost in tasks current AI can substantially do. Licensed/judgment/relationship/physical work = not automatable; a small automatable sliver inside professional roles (drafting, research, documentation) is allowed if justified. → `ai_replaceable_share` = Σ(role share × fraction).
- `pi_dist` ∈ [0,1] — client-relationship control: who owns the client — the firm, an upstream gatekeeper, a platform, a procurement department? **Pricing-model deflation must be reflected here with the mechanism stated** (cost-plus/audited/hourly billing hands AI savings to clients).
- `pi_moat` ∈ [0,1] — barrier strength: licensure, switching costs, proprietary data, physical presence, and the realistic threat that clients use generic AI to bypass the firm entirely.
- `t50` — years from today until 50% of firms materially adopt AI in the automatable functions; derive from `current_adoption_pct` + `historical_analogs` and show the derivation. **t50 must appear in `top_uncertain_inputs` with its range and score impact — always.**

## Step 2 — Compute scores (show full arithmetic; no rounding anywhere)

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`; `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)` — anchor: freeing ≥25% of revenue = 10.
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = min(10, 10 / t50)`
- B subfactors (all continuous):
  `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)` — 10,000+ targets → 1.0
  `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; +0.1 if documented succession shortage, cap 1.0
  `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
  `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
  (No price term in B — pricing signal lives only in M.)
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`

## Step 3 — Mandatory cross-checks (answer each explicitly)

1. **Uplift consistency.** Convert V_raw into expected EBITDA uplift (pp). If uplift < 3pp while A ≥ 5, explain or revisit t50.
2. **Terminal value.** Answer the industry-specific terminal-value question. Distinguish: AI cutting the operator's costs vs AI replacing the operator's product. Classify: `DURABLE` (demand survives mature AI), `PRESSURED` (demand shrinks but persists), `MELTING` (the product itself is being replaced).
3. **No-proxy audit.** Confirm in writing that V and A were justified without reference to deal activity or consolidation trends.
4. **Pricing-model deflation.** State the billing mix and whether AI efficiency flows to clients as lower fees rather than to the operator as margin; confirm how this is reflected in `pi_dist`/`pi_moat`.
