# 533110 — Lessors of Nonfinancial Intangible Assets (except Copyrighted Works)

**Driver:** The principal driver is recurring royalty revenue supported by AI-assisted contract, reporting, audit, monitoring, renewal, and licensee-support workflows.
**Weakness:** The principal weakness is an uncertain and potentially miscounted transferable-firm pool built on rights whose value can be concentrated, disputed, expiring, passive, or dependent on a few licensees.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market operating licensors that repeatedly assign external rights to patents, trademarks, brand names, and franchise agreements in exchange for royalties or license fees and maintain transferable licensing, administration, compliance, audit, enforcement, or franchise-support operations.
- Excluded: Copyrighted-work producers and distributors, independent creators, real-property and tangible-asset lessors, franchisors whose classification follows required product or service sales elsewhere, passive one-asset or litigation-only shells without transferable operations, captive internal IP holders, non-control financing situations, and firms outside the target normalized-EBITDA band.
- Customer and revenue model: Licensees and franchisees pay fixed fees, minimum guarantees, upfront fees, per-unit or usage fees, and recurring royalties often tied to licensee product sales or franchisee system sales; operating licensors may also receive audit recoveries, renewal or territory fees, and related support or technology fees.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Intangible-asset lessors can have recurring, asset-light royalty economics and document-heavy workflows that benefit from AI, but the industry mixes patent, trademark, brand, and franchise models and includes passive holders. The frozen firm count is unusually fragile because it uses a low real-estate-services margin that does not fit many royalty businesses.

## How AI changes the work
AI can assist portfolio and licensee research, contract review, disclosure drafting, royalty ingestion, anomaly detection, audit selection, renewal administration, brand monitoring, and support. Humans remain responsible for rights strategy, title, valuation, negotiation, legal judgment, disclosures, enforcement, franchise relationships, and accountable approvals.

## Value capture
Fixed-fee and sales- or usage-based royalty agreements can preserve savings over multi-year terms. Renewals, concessions, litigation, audits, court or regulatory rate setting, franchise support, and dependence on licensee sales limit retention.

## Firm availability
Only operating licensors with defensible rights, recurring third-party contracts, clean title, durable counterparties, transferable staff and systems, and target-band earnings qualify. Passive shells, expiring portfolios, litigation vehicles, and concentrated or owner-dependent businesses materially shrink the pool, while the margin bridge may undercount it.

## Demand durability
New technologies, brands, and franchise formats continue to create licensing opportunities, but asset expiration, invalidity, open standards, poor franchise economics, licensee concentration, and adverse legal outcomes can impair demand abruptly.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are the mismatched firm-count margin, passive-entity prevalence, uncertain title or validity, asset expiration and obsolescence, customer concentration, holdout and litigation, court or regulatory royalty setting, franchise disputes, disclosure failures, key-person negotiation, and dependence on underlying licensee sales.

## Caveats
- a: No public occupation or task mix is available specifically for NAICS 533110.
- a: Patent licensors and franchisors have materially different legal, commercial, support, and enforcement workflows.
- a: Large public-company disclosures may overstate specialization and technology relative to LMM firms, while outside counsel work is not captured by payroll.
- rho: No representative source measures AI adoption or realized labor substitution in 533110.
- rho: AI may reduce outside professional fees or improve coverage rather than reduce employee labor.
- rho: High-stakes legal, valuation, negotiation, and disclosure decisions require review even when drafting is automated.
- e: The frozen n value uses a real-estate operations and services margin of 3.96%, which is economically mismatched to potentially high-margin royalty and licensing firms and may materially undercount firms in the target EBITDA band.
- e: The NAICS code combines patents, trademarks, brands, and qualifying franchise agreements with different asset lives and operating requirements.
- e: Some establishments are passive asset holders or litigation vehicles rather than recurring outsourced operating businesses.
- s5: Gallup covers all U.S. employer-business owners and measures intentions rather than completed qualifying control transfers.
- s5: IP assets may be sold separately from operating-company control, and internal transfers or portfolio purchases do not qualify.
- s5: Institutional, strategic, and passive-entity ownership patterns are not represented by the proxy.
- q: No representative source measures automation-benefit retention in 533110.
- q: Patent royalty terms can be reset through negotiation, arbitration, courts, or regulation, while franchise royalties depend on franchisee sales and relationships.
- q: Large public licensors may have stronger bargaining power and more concentration than LMM firms.
- d5: Trademark applications measure potential asset creation, not licensing demand or royalty-bearing activity.
- d5: One large patent licensor's agreements and revenues are not representative of all U.S. LMM licensors.
- d5: Royalty revenue changes combine price, underlying licensee sales, litigation catch-up, concentration, and quantity.
- o: Rights-holder accountability does not guarantee a staffed independent LMM operating company; platforms, pools, law firms, or acquirers can administer assets.
- o: Patent pools and standardized licenses are more digitally scalable than bespoke brand and franchise relationships.
- o: A passive asset holder may remain legally necessary while falling outside the fixed operating lens.
