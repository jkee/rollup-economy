# 334111 — Electronic Computer Manufacturing

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `334111-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 7.73 · L 3.79 · interval CONDITIONAL → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Digitized engineering, test, support, and supply-chain data make multiple high-wage workflows AI-addressable.
**Weakness:** Transparent components and fierce hardware pricing can pass efficiency gains through before the operator recovers implementation cost.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent U.S. firms repeatedly designing, configuring, assembling, integrating, testing, and selling electronic computers, including servers, workstations, rugged or specialized computers, to external customers.
- Excluded: Captive manufacturing units, pure resellers and retailers, software-only companies, component makers, repair-only firms, embedded-computer machinery manufacturers, passive entities, and firms without transferable operations.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat hardware sales and configured system projects to enterprises, governments, data centers, distributors, and specialist users, priced per unit or configuration with component, warranty, support, volume, and channel terms.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Computer makers have unusually digital engineering, test, commercial, and supply-chain workflows, creating meaningful AI opportunity. Yet hardware competition, component transparency, rapid obsolescence, and an LMM population partly composed of thin-margin assemblers limit retention and transferability.

## How AI changes the work
AI can support configuration, design documentation, testing, inspection, failure diagnosis, sourcing, inventory, quoting, sales support, and service. Physical integration, rework, certification, security, and accountable product release remain.

## Value capture
Specialization, ruggedization, certifications, custom integration, delivery, and support protect gains; standard components, competitive bids, channel bargaining, and frequent repricing pass much of them through.

## Firm availability
The 212 establishments include large OEM units and assembly-heavy businesses as well as plausible independent specialists. The high margin bridge may overstate how many of the frozen 61 firms truly fall in the EBITDA band.

## Demand durability
AI servers, edge systems, secure computing, and refresh cycles support demand, while PCs remain cyclical and cloud concentration and imports can detach U.S. consumption from domestic production. Nearly all remaining systems require an accountable maker.

## Risks and uncertainty
Major risks are component and customer concentration, inventory obsolescence, cybersecurity, export controls, warranty failures, supply disruptions, offshore competition, rapid platform shifts, working-capital intensity, and misclassified resellers.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS combines computers with storage and peripheral equipment.
- a: Large OEM occupation mixes may differ from LMM rugged, custom, and specialist system builders.
- rho: Revenue or throughput gains are not all labor substitution.
- rho: Implementation economics differ sharply between high-volume PCs and low-volume custom servers or rugged systems.
- e: Establishments are not firms.
- e: The 25.32% Computers/Peripherals margin bridge may overstate EBITDA for assembly-heavy or competitively priced hardware firms.
- s5: The source is not industry-specific and does not measure completed control transfers.
- s5: Acqui-hires and product-line sales may not preserve the lens's operating business.
- q: Dell is far larger and has different purchasing power and channel economics.
- q: Commodity PCs, AI servers, rugged computers, and bespoke systems have materially different retention.
- d5: BLS output includes storage and peripherals and is value rather than physical units.
- d5: Dell's AI-server growth is not representative of all LMM domestic manufacturers.
- o: Cloud services and imports can displace domestic manufacturers without making software the physical supplier.
- o: Operator requirement does not imply unchanged employment.
