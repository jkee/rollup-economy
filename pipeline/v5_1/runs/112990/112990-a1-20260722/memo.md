# 112990 — All Other Animal Production

**Base tier:** no base (evidence-first) · A — · L — · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness STRESS_TEST_ONLY · action EVIDENCE_FIRST

**Driver:** Customer-dedicated colonies combine recurring operational complexity with data-rich planning, recordkeeping, and coordination workflows.
**Weakness:** The eligible service subset is tiny and unmeasured, while policy and technology are actively reducing the underlying use of research animals.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market laboratory-animal producers that repeatedly manage customer-dedicated breeding colonies or provide contract breeding and cohort-production services for external biomedical-research customers.
- Excluded: Companion-animal, bird, deer, llama, worm, and mixed-animal farms selling animals or animal products; ordinary laboratory-animal inventory sales without a recurring customer-dedicated production service; captive institutional vivaria, hobby breeders, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Recurring or repeat project fees for customer-directed colony establishment, breeding, husbandry, genotyping coordination, health-status control, and delivery of specified research cohorts, typically under a statement of work or dedicated-supply arrangement.
- Deviation from default lens: The NAICS code combines unrelated animal-production models ranging from pet and bird breeding to deer, worms, mixed farms, and laboratory animals. It is narrowed to customer-dedicated laboratory-animal breeding and colony production because that is the identifiable recurring outsourced-service model; combining it with consumer and commodity animal sellers would make one screen incoherent.

## Executive view
All Other Animal Production is exceptionally heterogeneous and mostly describes raising animals for sale, not outsourced services. The coherent service lens is the narrow laboratory-animal subset providing customer-dedicated breeding and colony production. That niche has a real administrative AI surface and high operator accountability, but eligibility is sparse and regulatory efforts to replace animal testing create a direct demand headwind.

## How AI changes the work
AI can assist breeding-plan analysis, animal and cage record reconciliation, scheduling, inventory forecasting, customer reporting, SOP retrieval, and exception prioritization. Physical husbandry, observation, sanitation, animal handling, veterinary judgment, and controlled procedures remain human- and facility-dependent. Existing portals and colony-management systems mean the estimate counts only incremental exposure beyond current automation.

## Value capture
Complex genotypes, health status, switching risk, and project continuity can protect value during an engagement. Retention is less secure at renewal, when sophisticated customers can benchmark per-animal and per-cage costs or benefit directly from reduced excess breeding. Contract detail, not public service descriptions, will determine whether productivity accrues to the operator or customer.

## Firm availability
The NAICS population spans pet, bird, deer, llama, worm, mixed-animal, and laboratory-animal producers, and only a small unmeasured portion appears to offer qualifying recurring colony services. Transferability further depends on facilities, licenses, compliance history, key scientific and veterinary staff, and assignable customer relationships; no defensible LMM count or observed transaction rate is available.

## Demand durability
Demand that remains animal-based will still require accountable breeding and husbandry, and specialized models can remain scientifically valuable. However, FDA has moved from roadmap to implementation of in-vitro, computational, AI, and human-derived alternatives, making a five-year decline in current animal-service quantity a central case rather than a remote risk.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main risks are classification mismatch, missing firm and labor denominators, undocumented contract economics, customer insourcing, scientific substitution away from animals, and compliance or biosecurity failures. A bad outcome is that apparent targets are principally animal-product sellers or captive facilities, while the truly outsourced operators face shrinking volumes and customer capture of efficiency gains.

## Caveats
- a: No occupation mix specific to NAICS 112990 contract laboratory-animal producers was found.
- a: BLS agricultural-worker duties cover many animal-production settings and underrepresent regulated laboratory quality work.
- a: AI assistance to decision support is not equivalent to removal of accountable labor.
- rho: No longitudinal AI implementation study for commercial laboratory-animal breeding was found.
- rho: Customer and institutional validation requirements may vary by study type.
- rho: Implementation is applied only to the exposed opportunity in a, not to all labor.
- e: No measured firm share for laboratory-animal production or contract colony services within 112990 was found.
- e: A vendor may combine animal sales, breeding services, testing, and other research services across multiple industry codes.
- e: The frozen dataset provides no defensible LMM firm count for this activity.
- s5: No observed control-transfer rate for eligible contract laboratory-animal producers was found.
- s5: License or registration changes need not represent a sale of control.
- s5: Large integrated vendors and nonprofit institutions are not representative of LMM owner succession.
- q: Public vendor materials describe service scope but not contract economics or pass-through.
- q: Pricing power differs between rare custom models, standard cohorts, academic customers, and large pharmaceutical buyers.
- q: Reduced animal use is demand loss in d5 when caused by research substitution, but lower excess production within a contract can also reduce billable volume and retention.
- d5: FDA-regulated drug development is only part of laboratory-animal demand.
- d5: Mice and rats bred for research are not comprehensively captured in Animal Welfare Act reporting.
- d5: New genetically engineered models could offset part of the reduction in routine toxicology animals.
- o: Animal Welfare Act coverage excludes some commonly used research animals and does not define the commercial lens.
- o: Operator requirement does not guarantee that the current independent firm survives; work can be insourced or consolidated.
- o: The estimate applies only to quantity remaining after the d5 reduction.

- **S3** — [USDA Animal Care Public Search Tool](https://www.aphis.usda.gov/animal-care/awa-services/usda-animal-care-public-search-tool) (accessed 2026-07-22): Explains Animal Welfare Act licensing and registration, inspections of animals, records and facilities, periodic updates, and public access to licensee and research-facility records.
