# 713920 — Skiing Facilities

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.72 · L 2.26 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Direct advance pass revenue and unavoidable physical operator responsibility support retention of targeted digital-workflow benefits.
**Weakness:** Most labor is seasonal, physical and safety-critical, while weather and climate create unusually wide demand and investment risk.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market ski-area operators providing repeat lift access and related rentals, lessons, food service, snowmaking, grooming and guest services to external customers.
- Excluded: Government or nonprofit facilities, captive amenities within larger lodging or real-estate businesses, shells, internal operating units, non-control financing vehicles, and firms outside the lower-middle-market band.
- Customer and revenue model: Direct consumer revenue from day tickets and advance season passes, with repeat ancillary spending on lessons, rentals, dining and other mountain services; operations are highly seasonal and weather-dependent.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Ski areas pair direct advance pricing with a service that remains physically operator-dependent, but the labor base is dominated by seasonal frontline work and the demand base is unusually exposed to weather and climate. AI can remove administrative friction and improve forecasting, yet it cannot replace lift, patrol, grooming, instruction and hospitality operations. The eligible independent universe appears meaningful but is not directly measured.

## How AI changes the work
The strongest applications are customer questions, marketing, ticket and pass administration, rental and lesson reservations, demand forecasting, staffing plans, reporting, food ordering and snowmaking optimization. Integrated systems already let small areas manage products that would be impractical manually. Safety-critical field work remains human-led, and some important snowmaking automation is already in place rather than new opportunity.

## Value capture
Day tickets and season passes are paid directly, often in advance, so there is no contractual pass-through of savings. Advance commitment also cushions weather variability. Independent resorts still compete against well-capitalized multi-mountain passes and may share benefits through discounts, service investment or more reliable snow rather than keeping all savings as margin.

## Firm availability
The industry includes family independents, public and nonprofit areas, captive destination assets and concentrated multi-resort groups. Succession and strategic consolidation create transfers, as the Black Mountain case demonstrates, but lifts, permits, water, land arrangements, deferred capital and climate risk make transactions complex. No public source provides a qualifying transfer denominator.

## Demand durability
National skier visits remain close to record levels, but yearly outcomes vary materially by snowfall and region. A near-flat five-year base is defensible because strong recent participation and pass adoption offset some weather exposure. Lower-elevation and warmer markets carry greater downside from shrinking snowmaking windows, while high-elevation western areas are more resilient but not immune.

## Risks and uncertainty
The evidence is weakest on ski-specific occupation wages, representative AI adoption, eligible firm ownership and transfer frequency. Broad recreation data and Vail's scale may both overstate administrative opportunity for small areas. A bad target can be overwhelmed by lift capital, employee housing, energy and water costs, insurance, permits, wildfire, warming winters or reliance on one short peak season.

## Caveats
- a: NAICS 713900 includes many non-ski recreation businesses.
- a: Vail is a multinational platform with centralized functions unlike an LMM independent operator.
- a: Digitization and rules-based automation are not necessarily AI, and already-automated tasks must not be counted.
- rho: Published examples are selected for technology adoption and may not generalize.
- rho: Safety-critical tasks require human override and formal operating procedures.
- rho: Limited capital and shoulder-season cash flow can delay implementation at smaller areas.
- e: Facility, establishment and firm counts do not align one-for-one.
- e: The frozen LMM firm count is an estimate based on a sector margin bridge.
- e: Management contracts and public-land permits can make operational control transferable even when land ownership is not.
- s5: The owner-age proxy covers all US employer businesses and uses 2018 data.
- s5: Public sources emphasize notable transactions and omit quiet small-area transfers.
- s5: Sale of mountain assets or land does not always constitute transfer of an eligible operating firm.
- q: No source directly measures retained AI benefit.
- q: Capture varies with local capacity, snow quality, destination status and competing pass access.
- q: Benefit reinvested in snowmaking or staffing improves resilience but is not retained as immediate cash.
- d5: Visits are realized utilization rather than a constant-price demand curve.
- d5: National totals mask major regional and elevation differences.
- d5: Climate projections cited are primarily for mid-century, so five-year impacts are inferred rather than directly measured.
- o: This measures the need for an operator, not the number of employees required.
- o: Automation can make a facility more self-service while leaving legal and safety accountability intact.
- o: Facility closure from climate or economics belongs in demand, not operator substitution.
