# 315990 — Apparel Accessories and Other Apparel Manufacturing

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `315990-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 5.03 · L 1.82 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** A wage-significant design, quoting, order-management, and production-control layer around repeat custom programs.
**Weakness:** Transparent unit pricing and a weak domestic output baseline limit retention and durable growth.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market manufacturers that repeatedly provide OEM, private-label, custom, uniform, corporate, promotional, or program-based production of hats, caps, belts, neckties, non-medical/non-safety/non-sport gloves, and other apparel accessories to external business or institutional customers.
- Excluded: Own-brand and direct-to-consumer accessory companies driven mainly by brand, merchandising, licensing, or retail; jobbers marketing their own finished goods; captive plants; one-off artisan work; work solely on customer-owned materials classified as contractors; excluded medical, safety, and sporting products; shells; and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat business-to-business custom or private-label manufacturing, typically quoted by design, quantity tier, and unit, with sampling, decoration, labels, packaging, quality control, and recurring reorders or replenishment programs.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 315990 combines own-account manufacturers and jobbers with external-customer custom and private-label suppliers across diverse accessory products. The lens is narrowed to repeat OEM, private-label, uniform, promotional, and program manufacturing because brand/product economics are incoherent with outsourced-service economics.

## Executive view
The coherent opportunity is the custom, OEM, private-label, uniform, and promotional subset rather than the whole accessories code. Wage-weighted AI exposure is meaningful in design, quote, proof, order, scheduling, and customer workflows, while most production remains physical. The commercial case is weakened by transparent quantity pricing, a contracting apparel-output baseline, recent direct 315990 nominal-output declines, and uncertainty over how many of the 31 estimated LMM firms actually fit the lens.

## How AI changes the work
AI can structure customer artwork and specifications, produce design variants, draft quotes and proofs, route approvals, forecast capacity, set up repeat orders, automate customer updates, and organize quality evidence. Computer vision may reduce inspection work. Cutting, sewing, shaping, attaching closures, decorating, finishing, handling variable materials, and rework remain much less exposed, so end-to-end labor displacement is limited.

## Value capture
Custom accessory pricing is visibly tiered by quantity and frequently quoted per design or program. Buyers can compare suppliers and expect savings at scale, giving them leverage to capture direct productivity. Operators have a better chance of retaining value when AI reduces sampling and proof cycles, supports low minimums, accelerates reorders, increases on-time delivery, or avoids defects rather than only reducing unit labor.

## Firm availability
The code includes product companies and jobbers as well as repeat external suppliers, so eligibility requires firm-level screening. Broader manufacturing ownership is old and clothing/fabric transactions are visible, but public sales are generally much smaller than the target band. Transferability depends on customer programs, licenses, sourcing relationships, documented specifications, working capital, and whether design and sales knowledge survives the owner.

## Demand durability
Physical accessories still require a responsible manufacturer, and custom programs have recurring uses in brands, uniforms, teams, events, corporations, and replenishment. Yet broader real apparel output is projected to shrink, and direct 315990 nominal output declined in both 2024 and 2025. Domestic operators need customization, speed, low minimums, compliance, or program reliability to resist imports and commodity pricing.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main evidence bridges are material: occupation and forecast data cover all apparel, ownership and deals cover broader manufacturing, and vendor pricing may include offshore or decoration-heavy models. Product heterogeneity is substantial across hats, belts, gloves, neckties, and miscellaneous accessories. Import competition, customer concentration, licenses, fashion cycles, inventory, material cost, labor compliance, and owner dependence can overwhelm AI savings.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS is NAICS 315, not 315990 or the narrowed custom/private-label subset.
- a: Employment shares are converted judgmentally into wage-weighted task exposure.
- a: Generic GenAI exposure does not establish reliable substitution in manufacturing workflows.
- rho: No representative 315990 adoption survey measures five-year implementation.
- rho: Some gains will increase throughput or service rather than avoid payroll.
- rho: Different products, such as belts, hats, gloves, and neckties, have different automation constraints.
- e: Vendor evidence proves that the model exists but not its prevalence among 315990 firms.
- e: Firms may mix owned brands, blank wholesale, decoration, and private-label programs.
- e: The frozen n=31 is a margin-bridged estimate using broader manufacturing economics, not an observed count.
- s5: Neither source isolates 315990, the narrowed lens, or the target EBITDA band.
- s5: BizBuySell's clothing-and-fabric median cash flow was only $100,000 in 2025.
- s5: Older ownership does not establish intent, timing, transferability, or a control sale.
- q: Public vendor prices may combine imported blanks, decoration, and manufacturing outside 315990.
- q: Commodity promotional programs likely pass through more benefit than specialized or rapid-turn programs.
- q: This estimate excludes demand changes and implementation costs.
- d5: The forecast is NAICS 315, while the direct 315990 series is current-dollar sectoral output rather than constant-price demand.
- d5: Two declining nominal observations may reflect inventory, prices, mix, or sourcing rather than end demand.
- d5: The code spans several products with different demand and trade patterns.
- o: Operator requirement does not mean the operator remains in the US or employs the same labor.
- o: Simple, standardized cap or belt operations may automate faster than complex, high-mix work.
- o: Customers can self-source or vertically integrate even though software cannot fulfill the physical service alone.
