## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market manufacturers of gears, speed changers, and non-hydrostatic industrial high-speed drives that repeatedly supply external OEM, distributor, maintenance, repair, and industrial customers.
- Excluded: Captive internal plants, non-control investment vehicles, motor-vehicle and aircraft-specific gearing classified elsewhere, hydrostatic drives, pure distributors, and repair-only businesses without manufacturing are excluded.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat business-to-business sales of catalog, configured, engineered, replacement, and spare gearing and drives, priced by unit, drawing, program, or supply agreement, sometimes with engineering and aftermarket support bundled into the product relationship.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Gear and industrial-drive manufacturing combines repeat, qualified customer relationships with precision physical production and a large engineering and planning layer. AI can affect design, quoting, CAM, scheduling, inspection, maintenance, and documentation, while actual machining, heat treatment, metrology, assembly, and warranty accountability remain operator-required.

## How AI changes the work
The clearest opportunities are specification extraction, design reuse, quotation, process planning, CNC assistance, in-process monitoring, inspection triage, root-cause analysis, scheduling, and maintenance. Mixed legacy equipment, low-volume complexity, traceability, and severe failure consequences require machine-specific data and supervised validation.

## Value capture
Qualification, embedded designs, reliability history, urgent spares, engineering support, and switching cost can preserve savings. Standard drawing-to-print work remains exposed to rebids, should-cost procurement, dual sourcing, and customer demands for productivity givebacks.

## Firm availability
The injected LMM estimate of 56 firms suggests a usable starting pool, but ownership independence, recurring customer economics, and true EBITDA require verification. No six-digit transfer cohort was available, so succession and deal-flow estimates remain judgmental.

## Demand durability
A broad installed base and diverse industrial applications support recurring OEM, replacement, and aftermarket demand. BLS expects modest real output growth for the broader four-digit industry, but cyclicality, trade, end-market concentration, and drivetrain redesign warrant a wide range.

## Risks and uncertainty
The central gaps are six-digit task data, firm eligibility, actual AI implementation, pass-through, and transfers. Precision failures, warranty exposure, skilled labor, customer concentration, long qualification cycles, equipment capex, tariffs, imports, and industrial downturns can overwhelm software benefits.

## Caveats
- a: The occupational data are four-digit and include turbine and engine manufacturers with a different engineering mix.
- a: NIST documents technical opportunities in machine tools, not the share of 333612 wages that can be substituted.
- rho: No representative 333612 adoption or realized-savings dataset was found.
- rho: Implementation here means operational capture of exposed labor opportunity, not general equipment productivity.
- e: The injected n of 56 is an ESTIMATE based on receipts and a sector margin rather than observed 333612 EBITDA.
- e: Industry establishment counts can include plants inside multi-industry machinery groups.
- s5: Neither the qualifying transfer numerator nor eligible denominator is directly observed.
- s5: Replacement-parts specialists and large OEM-program suppliers likely have different buyer pools and succession dynamics.
- q: No representative contract-level pass-through evidence was available.
- q: Retention should be higher in proprietary or urgent replacement work and lower in drawing-to-print production runs.
- d5: The projection is four-digit and combines materially different turbines, engines, and power-transmission products.
- d5: Industry production can diverge from customer demand because of trade, inventory, and market-share changes.
- o: This is bounded judgment rather than a measured operator-required share.
- o: Automation may reduce staffing intensity substantially without eliminating the accountable operator, which this primitive distinguishes.
