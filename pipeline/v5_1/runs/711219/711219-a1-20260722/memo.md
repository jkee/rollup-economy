# 711219 — Other Spectator Sports

**Driver:** AI can scale the analysis, documentation, communication, and administration surrounding high-value human coaching and athlete support while fixed-price recurring revenue preserves part of the gain.
**Weakness:** The eligible population is only an unmeasured subset of a mixed code, and founder reputation and client trust can make apparent firms nontransferable.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Privately controlled U.S. multi-person sports-training, coaching, scouting, athlete-performance, and competition-support firms whose recurring external service prepares or supports participants in spectator sports events or competitions.
- Excluded: Independent athletes whose revenue depends on their own performance, owners of cars, horses, dogs, or other racing participants, single-person practices without transferable operations, sports instruction unconnected to spectator competition, agents and career managers, captive team departments, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat outsourced support sold to athletes, families, teams, schools, clubs, and competition organizations through monthly memberships, session packages, season or team contracts, retainers, assessments, and specialized project fees. Delivery combines recurring coaching or training with performance analysis, program design, documentation, travel, and event support.
- Deviation from default lens: The code combines independent athletes, owners of racing participants, and establishments supplying specialized support to sports participants. Their labor, transferability, and revenue models are incompatible, so the lens is narrowed to multi-person sports-training and competition-support service firms for coherence, without selecting them for attractiveness.

## Executive view
The usable opportunity is the specialized service-firm slice of a highly heterogeneous code, not independent athletes or racing-participant owners. These firms have a meaningful information and administration layer that AI can compress, while their direct coaching, physical evaluation, motivation, emergency response, and trusted judgment remain difficult to remove. Participation and occupation forecasts support modest demand growth, but the eligible firm count and control-transfer history are not directly observed and personal goodwill is a central acquisition risk.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate performance-video review, opponent and recruit research, individualized plan drafts, session notes, progress reports, scheduling, billing, CRM, marketing, and routine client communication. It can also standardize knowledge across coaches and reduce avoidable administrative hiring. Humans remain responsible for live observation, technique correction, relationship and motivation, clinical assessment, emergency care, safety, complex program adjustment, and final selection or return-to-play decisions.

## Value capture
Monthly memberships, packages, and seasonal contracts give operators a window to retain productivity improvements because invoices are generally fixed rather than cost-plus. The protection is incomplete: renewal cycles are frequent, prices are transparent, clients can follow a coach, and firms may share savings through discounts or add service to retain athletes. Institutional contracts may expose savings more quickly through rebids.

## Firm availability
Census combines three economically different populations in 711219. Independent athletes and owners of racing participants can have substantial receipts but do not represent recurring outsourced service firms, so the frozen whole-code count materially exceeds the coherent pool. Among support firms, only businesses with a team, repeat customers, documented methods, and relationships that survive the founder qualify as transferable operations.

## Demand durability
Athletic-trainer and coach or scout employment projections are positive, NCAA participation reached a record 554,298 championship-sport athletes, and broad activity participation also rose. These are proxies rather than firm revenue, but they support modest growth in the need for athlete preparation and support. Software-only plans and team insourcing can take some quantity; the remaining individualized, safety-sensitive, and live-delivery demand still requires an accountable operator.

## Risks and uncertainty
The six-digit code lacks a clean occupation mix, service-firm share, transfer history, and pricing panel. Classification leakage into sports instruction, fitness centers, healthcare, and agents is substantial. The frozen compensation ratio uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and includes personal-performance businesses, while the frozen firm count is an estimate across the entire code. A bad case is a founder-led company whose clients and coaches leave after sale while generic training and analysis migrate to software.

## Caveats
- a: There is no six-digit occupation table for the narrowed service-firm population.
- a: O*NET establishes task content but not time shares, wage weights, technical exposure, or the current automation baseline.
- a: The frozen compensation ratio includes independent athletes and racing-participant businesses whose compensation economics may differ sharply from support firms.
- rho: This is bounded judgment rather than an observed implementation rate.
- rho: Fragmented software, inconsistent video and sensor data, client consent, and professional-liability concerns may slow adoption.
- rho: Implementation will differ materially between scouting analysis, strength coaching, clinical athletic training, race-animal support, and event logistics.
- e: The frozen firm count covers the full code, while this lens excludes independent athletes and racing-participant owners.
- e: High-earning personal athletes can enter the frozen economic band without creating a transferable service business.
- e: Some apparent sports-performance firms may instead be classified as sports instruction, fitness centers, or healthcare practices.
- s5: Owner-age evidence is broad, dated, and does not imply a sale or a five-year event probability.
- s5: BizBuySell data are voluntarily reported and do not identify 711219 or lower-middle-market transactions.
- s5: Personal goodwill can disappear at retirement even when a legal entity changes hands.
- q: The pricing source is one branded program and may fall in an adjacent healthcare or fitness classification.
- q: Institutional team and school contracts may be bid, hourly, or labor-cost-linked rather than membership-priced.
- q: Retention differs between scalable analysis and administration versus coach time saved in a high-touch membership.
- d5: Employment growth is not a constant-quality service-volume measure and can reflect insourcing rather than outsourced demand.
- d5: NCAA athletes and broad recreation participants are larger populations than paying customers for the narrowed professional-support lens.
- d5: Aggregate growth can conceal contraction in individual sports, racing support, geographies, or customer income segments.
- o: Operator requirement varies substantially between physical athletic care, live coaching, video scouting, and generic programming.
- o: Remote delivery can still require an accountable operator even when it removes local labor and facility use.
- o: The estimate concerns service quantity requiring a lens-type operator, not preservation of current jobs or delivery channels.
