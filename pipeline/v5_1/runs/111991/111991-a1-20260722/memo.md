**Driver:** Recurring processor demand for domestic sugar beets within a coordinated and policy-supported sugar supply chain.
**Weakness:** Processor crop contracts do not establish an outsourced operating-service model, and no defensible LMM firm population is available.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market firms, if any, providing repeat outsourced operation and management of sugar-beet production for external landowners or program customers while materially operating the farm.
- Excluded: Owner-operated beet farms selling their own crop under purchase or marketing contracts, passive land and financing vehicles, manage-only agricultural services classified in NAICS 115, beet processing and sugar manufacturing, non-sugar beet farming, and one-off custom fieldwork.
- Customer and revenue model: The qualifying model would use recurring farm-operation, crop-share, acreage, or management agreements with responsibility for a full beet-growing program. Sugar-beet production commonly uses processor-linked crop sales contracts, but those contracts coordinate commodity sale and are not evidence of outsourced operating-service revenue.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed to repeat outsourced farm operation because 111991 is a crop-production industry, while the fixed lens requires recurring outsourced service. Processor production contracts are common but do not by themselves convert a grower into a service provider.

## Executive view
Sugar-beet farming is tightly coordinated with processors and remains physically operator-dependent, but it is crop production rather than an established outsourced-service industry. Missing labor and firm-count inputs, plus no verified service population, prevent a transferable-firm or value-capture assessment.

## How AI changes the work
AI can improve agronomy, harvest and processor scheduling, input decisions, compliance, records, storage monitoring, and maintenance support. Specialized physical fieldwork and hauling remain central, with some precision automation already embedded.

## Value capture
No defensible commercial-retention range is available. Processor production and purchase contracts govern commodity delivery, not necessarily the sharing of productivity gains under a farm-operating service agreement.

## Firm availability
The frozen LMM count is missing, and public sources do not identify a population of independent outsourced beet-farm operators. Farm succession, acreage, and cooperative membership cannot substitute for a transferable firm roster.

## Demand durability
Domestic sugar use and a policy-managed market support continuing production, but 2026 acreage and projected beet-sugar output are lower. Processor capacity, imports, weather, and crop economics create material downside uncertainty.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are absence of a scalable service target pool, missing labor and firm-count inputs, processor and cooperative dependence, acreage contraction, weather and storage losses, specialized equipment, policy changes, nontransferable delivery rights, and classification leakage to agricultural support services.
