## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent U.S. contractors providing repeat exploration drilling, mine development, blasting, excavation, dewatering, equipment operation and maintenance, sampling, surveying, geological field support, reclamation, and related services to metal-mine owners.
- Excluded: Captive mine-owner crews; mineral extraction for the firm's own account; commodity traders; equipment-only dealers; pure laboratories or one-off consultants without recurring field delivery; shells; and firms dominated by coal, oil, gas, or nonmetallic-mineral support.
- Customer and revenue model: Recurring or repeat project, footage-, unit-, day-rate, and time-and-materials services sold to external mine developers and operators, limited to transferable firms plausibly producing $1-10 million of normalized EBITDA.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Metal-mining support combines essential physical delivery with a more data- and engineering-rich layer than coal support. Strategic-mineral investment can expand demand, but the target universe is small and exposed to extreme project and commodity cycles.

## How AI changes the work
High-value applications include proposal and scope review, geologic-data synthesis, drill-log coding, scheduling, shift reporting, maintenance prediction, remote-sensor anomaly triage, safety-document preparation, and regulatory search. Drilling, blasting, excavation, repair, sampling custody, equipment operation, and accountable field supervision remain physical.

## Value capture
Specialized rigs, technical reputation, safety performance, local knowledge, and scarce crews can preserve gains through a contract term. Sophisticated mining customers, rate transparency, competitive rebids, and project pauses transfer benefits outward over time.

## Firm availability
The frozen estimate suggests a very limited lower-middle-market pool. Establishment-level diligence must verify independence, metal-mining revenue, normalized earnings, equipment ownership and condition, bonding, customer diversity, and project backlog.

## Demand durability
Demand is supported by copper and critical-mineral needs, precious-metals economics, domestic supply-security initiatives, and continuing exploration and reclamation. It remains highly cyclical because permitting, financing, grade, commodity price, and single-project decisions can rapidly change contractor volume.

## Risks and uncertainty
Major risks include commodity and project concentration, permitting delays, safety incidents, environmental and reclamation liabilities, equipment capex, remote labor shortages, mine-owner insourcing, and a small pool of transferable firms.
