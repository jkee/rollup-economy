## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market manufacturers of instruments for measuring and testing electricity and electrical signals that repeatedly serve external customers, including circuit testers, multimeters, wattmeters, signal and semiconductor test equipment, related software, calibration, configuration, and support.
- Excluded: Captive internal laboratories or plants, shells, financing vehicles, firms outside the EBITDA band, communications test equipment classified elsewhere, pure calibration services or software publishers classified elsewhere, and one-off operations without repeat external revenue.
- Customer and revenue model: Instrument and system sales to electronics and semiconductor manufacturers, utilities, laboratories, OEMs, distributors, field technicians, and industrial users, with repeat demand from technology upgrades, fleet replacement, calibration, accessories, software, service contracts, and support.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Electrical-test instrument manufacturing combines durable physical metrology with meaningful engineering and software workflows. The recurring lens fits firms with installed test programs, calibration, upgrades, accessories, and technical support better than one-off custom equipment makers.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate firmware and test-program development, documentation, proposals, application support, planning, and defect analysis. Precision analog hardware, calibration, traceability, qualification, and final metrology accountability remain difficult to substitute.

## Value capture
Proprietary test programs, calibration, validation, application expertise, and switching costs protect differentiated suppliers. Catalog comparison, distributor pressure, major OEM negotiations, and rapid feature competition share savings.

## Firm availability
The sizable estimated LMM population may include independent specialists and strategic subsidiaries across very different product complexities. Recurring eligibility and qualifying transfer flow are not directly observed.

## Demand durability
Semiconductor complexity and domestic investment increase metrology needs, while grid modernization requires higher-fidelity sensing and monitoring. Software-defined instruments and embedded self-test can remove some standalone equipment, but physical signal generation and calibrated measurement persist.

## Risks and uncertainty
Four-digit proxies, semiconductor cyclicality, export controls, rapid obsolescence, customer concentration, calibration liability, component sourcing, cybersecurity, and wide product heterogeneity create material uncertainty.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation mix is four-digit rather than six-digit.
- a: AI task exposure is inferred.
- a: The supplied compensation ratio has a wage/receipts vintage mismatch and may vary sharply between handheld-instrument and semiconductor-test firms.
- rho: NIST evidence describes manufacturing and metrology needs rather than realized labor substitution.
- rho: Implementation barriers are much higher in semiconductor automated test equipment than in basic handheld instruments.
- e: No public source measures lens eligibility.
- e: NAICS is establishment-based and mixes basic meters with highly complex semiconductor test systems.
- e: The supplied firm count uses a margin bridge that may poorly fit diverse product economics.
- s5: The owner evidence is cross-industry, owner-level, and dated 2018.
- s5: Age does not establish sale intent or transferability.
- s5: Corporate carve-outs and internal transfers have different dynamics and are excluded unless they qualify.
- q: No source directly measures AI-benefit retention.
- q: Capture differs materially between commodity bench instruments and proprietary semiconductor test platforms.

- o: Value may migrate from instrument hardware to software, probes, or integrated semiconductor equipment.
