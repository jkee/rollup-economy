# 921120 — Legislative Bodies

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `921120-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** no base (evidence-first) · A — · L — · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness STRESS_TEST_ONLY · action EVIDENCE_FIRST

**Driver:** Research, drafting, summarization, transcription, and constituent-information workflows create a meaningful implementable AI assistance opportunity inside legislative operations.
**Weakness:** Government legislative establishments are neither transferable LMM firms nor commercial outsourced-service operators, leaving eligibility, transfer, and retention undefined.

## Business-model lens
- Included: No qualifying lower-middle-market outsourced-service firms were identified; the correctly classified US population consists of government legislative establishments and their advisory committees and commissions.
- Excluded: Congress, state and local legislative bodies, advisory and study legislative commissions, captive government offices, political campaigns, private lobbying and government-relations firms, govtech vendors, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Legislative bodies exercise public authority and are funded through governmental appropriations rather than recurring fees paid by external commercial customers; constituents are represented citizens, not customers under an outsourced-service contract.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Legislative bodies have a substantial AI-assistable research and document workload, and adoption is already visible among legislative staff. They are government institutions rather than transferable outsourced-service firms, however, so the commercial acquisition lens has no defensible eligible population or value-capture construct.

## How AI changes the work
Generative AI is already used for first drafts, email, data comparison, transcription, research, reports, resolutions, and bill summaries. It can reduce staff effort in these workflows, but source verification, confidentiality, public records, legal precision, democratic judgment, negotiation, voting, and final accountability require strong human control.

## Value capture
Commercial retention is undefined because legislative bodies receive appropriations rather than customer fees. Efficiency can create fiscal capacity or budget savings, but those outcomes cannot be translated honestly into contract pass-through, renewal repricing, or operator margin.

## Firm availability
The official industry population consists of government legislative establishments and commissions. Elections and reorganizations do not constitute control transfers, the frozen LMM firm count is missing, and private vendors serving legislatures belong in other industries.

## Demand durability
The number of US local governments was broadly stable from 2022 to 2025, while federal and state legislative institutions persist by law. Workload may shift with policy complexity and digital channels, but an accountable legislature remains necessary even if staffing and workflow change.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest analytical limitation is the empty investment lens. For operational AI, uncertainty centers on the unobserved staff occupation mix, small survey samples, accuracy and citation failures, confidential information, records and procurement rules, political variation, and the distinction between tool use and realized labor avoidance.

## Caveats
- a: O*NET includes only elected legislators and omits most legislative staff occupations.
- a: NCSL's survey is a small, non-wage-weighted staff sample and reports uses, not avoidable labor share.
- a: The frozen labor-share input is missing because government activity has no comparable receipts denominator.
- rho: Current tool use is not equivalent to sustained workflow implementation or avoided hiring.
- rho: The 68 responses span 35 states, Puerto Rico, and the US Virgin Islands but are not a probability sample of all legislative bodies.
- rho: Implementation excludes demand, pricing, and valuation effects.
- e: The official definition identifies government establishments rather than private firms.
- e: Govtech, lobbying, and government-relations vendors are outside this NAICS code and the frozen lens.
- e: The frozen LMM firm-count input is also missing.
- s5: Political turnover is not a control transaction under the screen.
- s5: Government reorganizations do not create transferable equity ownership.
- s5: A probability conditional on an empty eligible population is undefined.
- q: Appropriation outcomes and budget clawbacks are not customer pricing mechanisms.
- q: No eligible commercial operator was identified.
- q: Using a generic public-sector savings-retention assumption would not answer the primitive.
- d5: Government-unit counts are a stock proxy, not constant-price legislative-service quantity.
- d5: Special districts drove much of the reported increase and may not map to this NAICS code.
- d5: Federal, state, county, and municipal legislative workloads differ substantially.
- o: The estimate concerns operator requirement, not internal labor exposure.
- o: Some information and constituent-service tasks may shift to self-service portals without eliminating the legislature.
- o: Institutional and constitutional requirements differ across jurisdictions.
