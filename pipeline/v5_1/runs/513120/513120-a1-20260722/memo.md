# 513120 — Periodical Publishers

**Base tier:** no base (evidence-first) · A — · L — · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → PRIORITY · readiness STRESS_TEST_ONLY · action EVIDENCE_FIRST

**Driver:** Repeat editorial, audience, subscription, advertising, and administrative workflows are unusually amenable to AI assistance.
**Weakness:** Missing firm and transfer denominators combine with heterogeneous business models and ongoing distribution substitution.

## Business-model lens
- Included: United States lower-middle-market trade, professional, scholarly, newsletter, and paid specialist periodical publishers with recurring subscriber, advertiser, sponsor, licensing, data, event, or institutional relationships and transferable editorial brands and operations.
- Excluded: Mass consumer entertainment and comic-only publishing, captive member publications not classified as publishers, one-off publications, printing-only operations, shells, inactive titles, non-control financing vehicles, and owner-personality publications that cannot continue after a control transfer.
- Customer and revenue model: Readers, professionals, institutions, libraries, and enterprises buy recurring subscriptions, access, licenses, or publication services; advertisers and sponsors buy recurring access to defined audiences; some publishers add events, research, data, custom content, and lead-generation services. The publisher controls the editorial product in print, electronic, or internet-only form.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 513120 mixes consumer magazines, comics, scholarly journals, newsletters, and trade journals whose demand, customer, and accountability structures are too different for one coherent screen. The lens is narrowed to trade, professional, scholarly, newsletter, and paid specialist publishers with recurring external commercial relationships; mass consumer entertainment and comic-only publishing are excluded for coherence, not attractiveness.

## Executive view
The narrowed trade, scholarly, newsletter, and specialist periodical segment has a large AI-assistable editorial and commercial workflow base and recurring customer relationships, but exact eligible-firm and transfer denominators are absent and demand continues to migrate toward platforms, creators, and digital formats.

## How AI changes the work
AI can assist research, abstracts, transcription, first drafts, copy editing, metadata, tagging, newsletters, personalization, archive retrieval, ad proposals, customer service, and multi-format production. Expert commissioning, peer or editorial judgment, author relationships, verification, rights, brand voice, and final approval remain accountable human work.

## Value capture
Subscriptions, licenses, sponsorships, and fixed advertising packages can retain near-term savings, while renewals, performance pricing, contributor safeguards, software costs, free alternatives, and reinvestment in differentiated content share away benefits over time.

## Firm availability
Transferable value can reside in specialist brands, archives, subscriber files, author networks, advertiser relationships, events, and data products. Confidence is limited by the missing LMM denominator, no clean transaction-rate series, owner-editor dependence, licensing structures, and multi-title portfolio deals.

## Demand durability
Professional, scholarly, and niche information remains useful, but aggregate periodical output has contracted and traditional formats face fragmented attention, free content, creator competition, search change, and advertising pressure. Paid digital, institutional use, events, data, and niche authority can offset part of the decline.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are lens sensitivity across heterogeneous subsegments, no defensible n-band, no direct transfer denominator, overstated labor substitution, AI accuracy and rights failures, platform and search disintermediation, advertiser concentration, subscriber churn, owner dependence, and content commoditization.

## Caveats
- a: The occupational evidence combines periodicals with newspapers, books, and directories.
- a: Published occupation shares do not directly measure wage-weighted task exposure or current automation.
- a: The narrowed subsegments differ in peer review, specialist expertise, design intensity, and advertising operations.
- a: The frozen labor-share input is an ancestor-sector estimate and may not represent periodical economics.
- rho: The adoption survey covers international news organizations, not the narrowed US periodical lens.
- rho: Perceived transformation does not measure implemented substitution of exposed labor.
- rho: Scholarly, trade, newsletter, and specialist publishers have different approval and rights constraints.
- e: No direct audit measures the share of LMM 513120 firms in the narrowed recurring-service subsegments.
- e: A recurring subscription to an owned content product is not automatically an outsourced service.
- e: The frozen dataset declares no defensible n-band value, so the eligible-firm count will remain missing.
- e: The estimate is sensitive to how scholarly publishing and paid specialist information are treated under the lens.
- s5: No defensible transaction-rate denominator for eligible LMM periodical publishers was found.
- s5: Title licensing and operating agreements may change publishers without transferring the underlying firm.
- s5: Multi-title portfolio deals can overstate firm-level opportunity, while private single-title deals may be missed.
- q: No direct study measures five-year post-AI benefit retention in LMM periodical publishing.
- q: Advertising, institutional licenses, subscriptions, publication charges, events, and data products have different pass-through dynamics.
- q: Savings may be reinvested in content quality and audience acquisition rather than retained as cash.
- d5: Historical output and employment are not direct measures of constant-quality demand.
- d5: The 1987-2022 periodical series is print-heavy and may not represent the next five years.
- d5: The narrowed trade, scholarly, newsletter, and specialist segment may be more durable than aggregate periodical publishing.
- d5: The range permits modest growth but also material contraction.
- o: No direct study isolates operator substitution in NAICS 513120.
- o: Scholarly and technical journals require more accountable curation than commodity consumer content.
- o: Demand for information can persist while the traditional periodical operator loses distribution and monetization.
