# 712120 — Historical Sites

**Base tier:** STRUCTURAL_OUT · A 5.36 · L 0.29 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → LOW_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** AI can improve research, interpretation production, visitor communications, and administration while authentic place-based demand remains operator-dependent.
**Weakness:** Most historical sites are public or nonprofit and the few commercial sites can be inseparable from restricted real estate, preservation obligations, and founder-specific operations.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US operators that preserve and exhibit historically significant sites, buildings, forts, communities, archaeological sites, battlefields, historical ships, and pioneer villages and repeatedly serve visitors, members, schools, tour groups, renters, or sponsors.
- Excluded: Government-run sites; nonprofit or institutionally captive sites without transferable equity control; passive owners of historic real estate; preservation contractors; ordinary lodging, event, or retail businesses located in historic buildings; museums and natural parks classified outside 712120; shells and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: The coherent transferable-firm population is the small for-profit subset of historical-site operators. Revenue commonly comes from posted-price admissions, tours, memberships or season passes, education programs, special events, venue rental, merchandise, food, sponsorship, and sometimes donations or grants.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed to for-profit operating businesses because the history field is dominated by government-private-nonprofit partnerships whose governance cannot undergo a conventional equity-control transfer; passive historic-property ownership is also excluded because it does not provide the screened visitor service.

## Executive view
Historical sites combine a high labor share with some AI-addressable interpretation and administration, but much of the work is physical, live, or place-specific. More importantly, the field's government-nonprofit structure leaves few transferable commercial operators, and the frozen LMM count may be distorted by contributed revenue, volunteer labor, real estate, and deferred preservation costs.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate source discovery, first drafts of interpretive material, educational adaptation, translation, accessibility content, visitor inquiries, marketing, fundraising, scheduling, reservations, CRM upkeep, and routine finance or HR work. It can also support self-guided audio or visual experiences. Staff remain necessary to verify contested historical claims and provenance, engage visitors, preserve buildings and landscapes, run events, secure sites, maintain facilities, and accept accountability for accurate and sensitive interpretation.

## Value capture
Commercial sites typically use posted admissions, tours, memberships, events, rentals, retail, food, and sponsorship rather than contractual cost pass-through, which permits meaningful initial retention of productivity gains. Retention erodes through affordability constraints, competition from free public sites and other leisure options, employee wage pressure, and the need to reinvest in preservation, maintenance, programming, and accessibility.

## Firm availability
The 2022 history census characterizes the field as a government-private-nonprofit partnership and shows that most nonprofit organizations are very small. A commercial equity-control screen therefore excludes most operators. Even among seemingly eligible sites, title or lease terms, preservation easements, restricted donations, local-government relationships, founder dependence, and the inseparability of land from operations can block transferability. General owner-aging evidence suggests succession events, but no historical-site deal-flow series exists.

## Demand durability
Demand for experiencing authentic places is culturally and educationally durable, but visitation is seasonal, tourism-sensitive, and uneven across institutions. Pandemic recovery data showed strong rebounds without full recovery for mid-sized history organizations, while 2025 NPS traffic eased from a record. Virtual programs broaden access but have behaved mainly as complements. A site that stops refreshing interpretation or funding maintenance can lose demand quickly despite the underlying relevance of history.

## Risks and uncertainty
A bad outcome can arise from an effectively empty transferable target population, hidden preservation capex, property or artifact title defects, government or donor restrictions, seasonal and weather-driven traffic, founder-dependent storytelling, weak repeat visitation, community controversy, or inaccurate AI interpretation that damages trust. The recreation-sector margin bridge may be especially unreliable because volunteer labor, grants, restricted funds, owned property, and deferred maintenance make reported operating economics unlike ordinary attractions.

## Caveats
- a: BLS publishes the occupation mix at NAICS 712100 and includes museums, zoos, gardens, and nature parks as well as historical sites.
- a: AI exposure does not establish safe substitution where contested history, source provenance, cultural sensitivity, accessibility, and public trust require human judgment.
- a: The frozen compensation-to-receipts ratio combines 2024 wages with 2022 receipts and a harmonization divisor, so vintage and basis differences remain material.
- rho: BTOS measures any business-function use, not the share of exposed historical-site labor implemented as substitution or avoided hiring.
- rho: The five-year conversion is judgmental; political controversy, incorrect interpretation, copyright claims, or trust failures could impose tighter review.
- e: History-organization evidence includes museums, historical societies, and other entities outside NAICS 712120 and does not quantify for-profit historical sites directly.
- e: The frozen n=23 is an ESTIMATE using size-class receipts and a recreation-sector EBITDA margin; contributions, restricted funds, deferred maintenance, volunteer labor, and owned real estate can make normalized EBITDA especially misleading.
- e: Some nominally private sites may be burdened by preservation covenants, donated-property restrictions, public leases, or inseparable real estate that prevents a qualifying transfer.
- s5: The owner evidence is economy-wide, older, and not specific to historical sites or the $1-10M EBITDA band.
- s5: Planned sales are not completed transfers and may occur beyond five years.
- s5: A site or property sale may extinguish the operating visitor service and is therefore not automatically a qualifying transfer.
- q: No source directly measures retention of AI-enabled gross benefits at for-profit historical sites.
- q: Site economics vary sharply between destination attractions, seasonal living-history villages, ships, houses, battlefields, and local sites.
- q: Preservation obligations can absorb savings even when customers do not receive an explicit price concession.
- d5: AASLH covers history organizations of many types and governance forms, while NPS is public and includes many park units outside NAICS 712120.
- d5: Attendance is an imperfect proxy for constant-price, constant-quality service quantity because free access, event mix, pricing, and visit duration vary.
- d5: The latest detailed AASLH survey reflects 2022 pandemic recovery rather than the 2026 starting level.
- o: No source directly measures year-five operator-required quantity for commercial historical sites.
- o: Virtual access may substitute more strongly for small interiors, archives, or low-differentiation sites than for immersive destination properties.
- o: AI-driven labor changes are counted in rho and not again as elimination of operator-required demand.
