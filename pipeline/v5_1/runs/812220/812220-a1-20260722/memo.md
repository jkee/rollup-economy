# 812220 — Cemeteries and Crematories

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `812220-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.04 · L 2.08 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Essential, local physical disposition demand makes most of the service basket durable and still operator-required.
**Weakness:** Transferability is uncertain because ownership form, perpetual-care obligations, and funeral-home affiliation are invisible in the available firm counts.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market operators of cemeteries, memorial gardens, mausoleums, pet cemeteries, and stand-alone crematories that provide repeat or recurring disposition, interment, plot, memorial-property, records, and care services to external families or funeral-service customers.
- Excluded: Funeral homes combined with crematories, which Census places outside 812220; public or religious captive units, shells, non-control financing and perpetual-care vehicles, and operations without a transferable external-customer service business.
- Customer and revenue model: Customers are families, estate representatives, funeral homes, and pet owners. Revenue is primarily transaction- and contract-based through interment or cremation charges, cemetery property and memorial rights, and pre-need arrangements, with some recurring care or maintenance fees.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Cemeteries and stand-alone crematories combine a durable death-disposition need with a meaningful, though not dominant, administrative automation opportunity. The core work remains physical, regulated, local, and emotionally sensitive; the more serious screen uncertainty is whether enough LMM firms are transferable rather than public, religious, nonprofit, trust-constrained, or linked to funeral homes.

## How AI changes the work
AI can reduce avoidable hiring in inquiry triage, scheduling, records search, document drafting, billing support, marketing, and routine family communications. Human approval should remain central for legal authorization, identity control, unusual cases, sensitive counseling, equipment operation, grounds work, and final disposition.

## Value capture
Per-service and property-right pricing is more favorable to retention than cost-plus labor billing, but pre-need obligations, local competition, referral relationships, and service expectations can share savings with customers or redirect them into quality and compliance.

## Firm availability
The frozen firm count is only an estimate, and Census's operational definition does not reveal transferable ownership. Cemetery associations, public or religious ownership, perpetual-care restrictions, and funeral-home affiliations can remove firms from the eligible acquisition pool; succession intentions provide only a broad directional proxy for actual control sales.

## Demand durability
The event base is essential and operator-required. Near-term death counts can fluctuate, and the continuing shift toward cremation helps crematories while reducing some cemetery interment and property demand, leaving the combined industry's constant-quality outlook modestly positive but heterogeneous.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest risks are a smaller transferable-firm pool than the margin-bridged count suggests, state-specific regulation and trust obligations, difficult integration of legacy cemetery records, environmental and land liabilities, and mix shift away from traditional burial. A serious identity or authorization error would also create outsized legal and reputational harm.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation source is NAICS 812200, which includes funeral homes as well as 812220.
- a: OEWS excludes self-employed workers and provides occupation employment, not task-level wage exposure.
- a: Cemetery-heavy and crematory-heavy firms have materially different work mixes.
- rho: There is no industry-specific measured five-year AI implementation rate.
- rho: Implementation will vary sharply with record digitization, site count, and state compliance burden.
- e: The frozen n=39 is an ESTIMATE derived through an industry-margin bridge.
- e: Establishment classifications do not reveal control rights, nonprofit restrictions, or whether perpetual-care assets are separable.
- e: Pet cemeteries may differ from human death-care facilities in scale and regulation.
- s5: Gallup is cross-industry, intention-based, and not specific to the LMM band.
- s5: Internal family succession, asset sales without operations, and nonprofit reorganizations are not qualifying transfers.
- q: Billing practices and pre-need regulation vary by state and operator type.
- q: No source directly measures pass-through of automation savings in this industry.
- d5: Deaths are an event-count proxy, not constant-quality service quantity.
- d5: Cremation projections are from an industry association and cover all disposition channels, not only 812220.
- d5: The combined NAICS contains cemetery and crematory businesses that benefit differently from the same mix shift.
- o: The estimate concerns accountable operators, not the amount of human labor inside each case.
- o: Vertical integration into funeral homes can move activity outside 812220 without eliminating physical disposition work.
