## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent lower-middle-market wholesale and mixed-channel growers of nursery stock, ornamental shrubs and trees, fruit and nut plants, Christmas trees, sod, and short-rotation woody crops, emphasizing propagation, contract growing, broad inventories, climate-suitable varieties, delivery coordination, and repeat landscaper, retailer, municipal, and institutional accounts.
- Excluded: Retail garden centers primarily reselling purchased plants; landscaping and installation services; forest nurseries and timber tracts with longer rotations; floriculture without nursery or tree production; captive municipal or institutional nurseries; hobby growers; and businesses outside the LMM earnings band.
- Customer and revenue model: Customers include landscape contractors, garden centers, home-improvement retailers, wholesalers, municipalities, institutions, orchards, forestry programs, and direct consumers. Revenue is earned per plant, tree, pallet, acreage unit, or crop lot; the service-like subset combines forward growing plans, reserved inventory, propagation, specifications, tagging, staged releases, logistics, and repeat seasonal replenishment.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Nursery and tree production is labor-intensive, inventory-heavy, and increasingly suited to irrigation, vision, inventory, and planning automation. The best LMM target is a scaled wholesale nursery with water security, accurate living inventory, broad climate-suitable varieties, and repeat landscaper, retailer, municipal, or orchard programs rather than a small owner-dependent garden center.

## How AI changes the work
Demand forecasting, propagation schedules, irrigation, scouting, spraying, plant counting, grading, labeling, movement, order allocation, routing, and administration are attractive. Irregular living products still require substantial hands-on planting, pruning, spacing, digging, loading, and culling.

## Value capture
Long growing cycles, scarce sizes and varieties, local adaptation, quality, broad assortment, fill rates, and delivery reliability support retention. Standard plants and concentrated large buyers reduce it.

## Firm availability
There is no defensible frozen firm count. Transferability turns on land and water, inventory health and valuation, propagation know-how, pest status, labor, irrigation, equipment, customer concentration, and forward production. Real estate and living inventory can complicate conventional acquisition structures.

## Demand durability
USDA reports broad five-year nominal gains in nursery stock, supported by landscaping, replacement, urban canopy, resilience, orchard, and outdoor-living demand. Housing, construction, water restrictions, interest rates, weather, and retailer destocking remain significant cyclical risks.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key uncertainties are missing labor-share and firm-count data, crop and channel mix, living-inventory valuation, shrink, water, labor, pest and disease exposure, housing sensitivity, land tenure, seasonality, customer concentration, logistics, and the true ROI of automation in field nurseries.

## Caveats
- a: O*NET combines crop, nursery, and greenhouse labor.
- a: Surveyed automation is not synonymous with generative AI.
- a: Container, field, tree, sod, and Christmas-tree operations have different task mixes.
- rho: Current adoption is materially higher in larger and container nurseries.
- rho: Unstructured outdoor work remains harder than fixed greenhouse automation.
- rho: Long crop cycles delay feedback and return measurement.
- e: Wholesale sales are not automatically recurring contracts.
- e: General crop-contract evidence is not nursery-specific.
- e: Customer forecasts may be cancellable and leave the grower with living inventory.
- s5: The dataset intentionally provides no n-band estimate.
- s5: Cross-industry owner intentions are not observed nursery transactions.
- s5: Living inventory and real estate can dominate enterprise value.
- q: Capture is higher for scarce mature specimens and proprietary or local varieties.
- q: Large buyers can shift volume or demand annual concessions.
- q: Weather-driven shortages can temporarily exaggerate pricing power.
- d5: The observed 17% growth is nominal, not real.
- d5: Horticulture census data aggregate multiple business models and sizes.
- d5: Recent category growth may not persist through a housing downturn.
- o: The factor isolates AI-related obsolescence from housing and landscaping cycles.
- o: Some ornamental categories are more discretionary than trees for restoration or food production.
- o: Climate shifts can obsolete specific varieties without eliminating the industry.
