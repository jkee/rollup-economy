# 314999 — All Other Miscellaneous Textile Product Mills

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `314999-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 5.91 · L 1.44 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Repeat custom workflows create unusually useful AI entry points around design, quoting, administration, scheduling, and inspection.
**Weakness:** The catch-all industry's heterogeneity makes eligibility, demand, and transfer incidence hard to measure reliably.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market firms providing repeat contract textile conversion, embellishment, cutting, binding, sewing, and custom or specification-driven industrial textile products to external commercial customers, including embroidery-for-trade and repeat-production technical-textile work.
- Excluded: Consumer-branded sleeping-bag, diaper, dust-cloth, or similar finished-goods manufacturers whose economics are primarily brand and retail product sales; captive units, shells, non-control financing vehicles, one-off artisans, and operations without transferable customer and production systems.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat B2B job, batch, or supply-order revenue, often priced per unit or per production run under quotes, purchase orders, and recurring customer programs; customers outsource specialized labor, equipment, quality control, and fulfillment.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 314999 combines contract or fee textile work with unrelated consumer and industrial product manufacturing. To keep one coherent outsourced-service screen, the lens retains recurring contract converters and specification-driven B2B producers and excludes primarily branded consumer-goods manufacturers.

## Executive view
The coherent opportunity inside this catch-all code is repeat contract and technical-textile conversion, not the full collection of consumer and industrial manufacturers. AI exposure is meaningful in design preparation, quoting, job administration, scheduling, and inspection, while the physical production operator remains essential. Evidence quality is weakened by the code's heterogeneous products and the lack of six-digit transfer or demand series.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate artwork and embroidery-file preparation, estimate jobs, capture orders, plan batches, answer customer questions, forecast materials, inspect output, and document quality. Sewing, material handling, machine setup, maintenance, and exception repair remain physical; legacy systems and small-firm integration capacity limit five-year implementation.

## Value capture
Fixed-price job and unit quotes allow initial retention of efficiency gains. Over time, frequent rebids and price competition share some benefit with customers, while custom designs, setup knowledge, fast turnaround, qualification, and dependable quality create switching costs that support retention.

## Firm availability
Only part of NAICS 314999 fits the narrowed recurring outsourced-service model. Manufacturing ownership is broadly old enough to create succession pressure, but small shops may be inseparable from founders and public transaction data do not show how often qualifying control transfers actually occur.

## Demand durability
The five-year center is modestly below current real volume, reflecting long-run decline in other textile product mill output and import pressure. Advanced medical, protective, specialty, and industrial textile applications and demand for local short-run customization provide upside, whereas simple consumer-linked conversion can move offshore or be insourced.

## Risks and uncertainty
The code mixes products and business models, so broad statistics can mislead. Other risks include customer concentration, fashion and promotion cycles, imported finished goods, raw-material volatility, labor-intensive production, capex needs, founder dependence, and automation that improves throughput without removing labor hours.

## Caveats
- a: The BLS occupation population includes all NAICS 314900, including 314994 and consumer-product plants outside the narrowed lens.
- a: Headcount shares are converted to wage exposure judgmentally; higher-paid commercial and management work raises the wage-weighted share.
- a: Some embroidery digitization and production automation is already deployed, so only not-yet-automated work is included.
- rho: Neither survey isolates US NAICS 314999 or the narrowed contract-service subset.
- rho: Self-reported adoption does not establish sustained labor savings.
- rho: The estimate excludes price, demand, and valuation effects.
- e: No public dataset identifies what share of six-digit firms use the retained contract and B2B model.
- e: Several listed products can be sold through either recurring B2B supply relationships or consumer brands, so website classification may be ambiguous.
- e: The injected LMM firm count is itself an estimate and should not be converted into a claimed observed count.
- s5: The source covers women owners across all manufacturing, not all owners or NAICS 314999.
- s5: Age 55-plus is a broad band and does not measure sale intent or timing.
- s5: Founder dependence can turn succession events into closures rather than transferable operations.
- q: No source directly measures five-year benefit retention for private contract textile firms.
- q: Raw-material and freight movements make labor-benefit pass-through difficult to isolate from invoice prices.
- q: Retention varies materially between standardized bulk work and qualification-heavy custom production.
- d5: The BLS output trend is at NAICS 3149, not 314999 or the narrowed service lens.
- d5: The retained basket spans cyclical consumer decoration and resilient technical or safety applications.
- d5: Domestic output can decline through import substitution even when US end demand is stable.
- o: The value is inferred from the physical nature of the work rather than directly observed.
- o: Import substitution removes demand from US lens operators even though a physical operator remains abroad.
- o: Digital artwork creation can be self-served without eliminating the downstream production operator.
