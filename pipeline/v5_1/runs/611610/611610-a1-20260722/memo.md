# 611610 — Fine Arts Schools

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `611610-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.13 · L 2.32 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Recurring tuition plus automatable administration and preparation can convert workflow adoption into avoided hiring while preserving instructor-led delivery.
**Weakness:** The small LMM target pool and founder- or instructor-dependent customer relationships limit transferable firm availability.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US fine-arts schools in the lower-middle-market band providing recurring or repeat paid instruction in music, dance, drama, visual arts, crafts, or related nondegree arts disciplines to external households and other customers.
- Excluded: Degree-granting colleges, public K-12 schools, commercial and graphic-art career schools, captive programs, solo practices without transferable operations, pure performance or venue businesses, and instrument or art-supply retailers.
- Customer and revenue model: Primarily household-paid recurring tuition or lesson fees, often monthly or term-based, supplemented by camps, recitals, competitions, materials, and private lessons; some schools also receive institutional contracts or grants.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Fine-arts schools combine recurring tuition with a labor-heavy service, but only a minority of the work is plausibly substitutable: the core product is accountable human instruction and performance development. The nearer opportunity is to avoid administrative hiring and compress lesson preparation, marketing, enrollment, billing, scheduling, and routine communications. Firm transfers occur, though founder dependence and the small number of LMM targets constrain availability. Real demand appears broadly stable rather than structurally high-growth.

## How AI changes the work
AI can draft curricula, lesson variants, practice plans, parent emails, marketing content, schedules, recital materials, and first-pass records; multimodal tools can also support asynchronous practice feedback. Instructors still must judge technique and expression, adapt in real time, manage groups and minors, demonstrate physical or musical skills, and remain accountable for educational quality. Implementation will depend on integrated school-management workflows and disciplined human review.

## Value capture
Fixed recurring tuition allows operators to retain initial labor savings, especially when avoided hiring does not visibly reduce service. Retention erodes as local competitors adopt similar tools, instructors bargain for part of productivity gains, or parents expect lower prices, smaller classes, or better programming. Schools that convert savings into greater instructor consistency and customer responsiveness may preserve more value than those that simply reduce staff.

## Firm availability
The LMM population is limited by the supplied estimate of 74 firms. Broad owner surveys show meaningful five-year sale or transfer intentions, and marketplace data confirm completed studio transactions, but many arts schools are bound to the founder's reputation or teaching role. Multi-instructor schools with documented curriculum, recurring billing, assignable leases, stable enrollment, and distributed parent relationships are more transferable than personality-led practices.

## Demand durability
Self-enrichment teaching employment is projected to grow modestly over a decade, consistent with near-flat to slightly positive five-year real demand. Fine-arts instruction benefits from sustained interest in creative development, enrichment, and community performance, but is discretionary and sensitive to household budgets, youth demographics, competing activities, and free digital instruction. Live human-led quantity should remain dominant, with hybrid delivery taking a larger role.

## Risks and uncertainty
The occupation evidence is four-digit rather than 611610-specific, current AI-use evidence comes from public K-12 teachers, and no public dataset directly measures task exposure, automation pass-through, or AI-only substitution in private arts schools. The NAICS code spans disciplines with different facility and instructor needs. Founder dependence can simultaneously reduce eligibility, completed transfers, and post-transfer customer retention; diligence must avoid counting that same weakness as a favorable labor opportunity.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS is available at NAICS 611600 rather than 611610 and excludes self-employed workers.
- a: The teacher AI survey covers US public K-12 teachers, not private fine-arts schools.
- a: Exposure is not the same as full job replacement; most exposed teaching tasks are partial-task assistance.
- rho: Reported time savings are self-reported and conditional on weekly tool use.
- rho: Public K-12 schools differ from private fine-arts schools in training, procurement, class structure, and governance.
- rho: The estimate assumes tools become integrated into workflow software within five years.
- e: The supplied LMM firm count is itself an estimate derived from size classes and a margin bridge.
- e: Dance and fitness studio transactions demonstrate transferability for only part of 611610 and include fitness businesses outside the code.
- e: Firm-level revenue mix and owner dependence are not available from public NAICS data.
- s5: Gallup covers all industries and owner-reported plans, not completed deals.
- s5: BizBuySell data are a selected marketplace sample and combine dance with fitness studios.
- s5: Internal family transfers may or may not meet the qualifying-control definition.
- q: Public sources do not measure contractual pass-through or repricing after automation in this industry.
- q: Private lessons, group classes, camps, and grant-supported programs have different pass-through dynamics.
- q: The estimate excludes implementation difficulty and volume loss, which belong in other primitives.
- d5: Occupational employment is not service demand and includes self-enrichment teachers outside fine-arts schools.
- d5: NEA adult participation measures engagement rather than purchases from private schools.
- d5: Demand is discretionary and locally exposed to income, child population, and public-program availability.
- o: No source directly measures operator displacement in private fine-arts instruction.
- o: Operator requirement is lower for adult hobbyists and beginner theory than for children, ensembles, performance preparation, or movement disciplines.
- o: Rapid improvement in multimodal real-time coaching would push the estimate downward.
