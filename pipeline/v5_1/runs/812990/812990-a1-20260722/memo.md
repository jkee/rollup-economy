# 812990 — All Other Personal Services

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `812990-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 5.43 · L 2.65 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Personal-service firms combine repeat intake and coordination work with growing selected end markets, creating capacity leverage from AI assistance.
**Weakness:** Residual-code heterogeneity and owner-dependent human delivery make eligibility, transferability, and aggregate economics unusually uncertain.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market firms delivering bespoke human personal services to external customers, including personal fitness training, wedding and party planning, personal organizing and shopping, concierge and genealogy services, personal chefs, doulas, house sitting, porter services, and similar appointment- or project-based personal work.
- Excluded: Bail bonding and identity-theft protection because their core economics are financial risk or subscription protection; online dating and other software-led platforms; coin- or card-operated machine and locker concessions; social escort, psychic, and fortune-telling services because their risk and delivery models are not comparable; captive providers; and firms outside the roughly $1-10M normalized EBITDA band.
- Customer and revenue model: Included firms sell hourly sessions, appointments, retainers, packages, or projects directly to individuals and households. Revenue depends on practitioner time, personalized advice or coordination, trust, scheduling, customer acquisition, and reliable delivery; some categories add in-person coaching, safety, cooking, sitting, or event execution.
- Deviation from default lens: The NAICS residual mixes regulated surety, digital subscriptions and marketplaces, unattended concessions, intimate or mystical services, and bespoke human coaching and coordination. A single workflow and billing screen would be incoherent, so the lens retains the broad human-delivered appointment and project service cluster and excludes the structurally different models; the narrowing is for construct coherence, not attractiveness.

## Executive view
The residual code is too heterogeneous for an undifferentiated operating thesis, so the lens retains bespoke human-delivered coaching, planning, coordination, and household personal services. Those firms have substantial information and administrative work, but delivery often rests on practitioner judgment, trust, physical presence, and owner reputation.

## How AI changes the work
AI can automate intake, scheduling, reminders, marketing, research, proposals, program drafts, vendor comparisons, documentation, and routine follow-up. It is less able to replace live technique correction, motivation, safety, sensitive judgment, venue inspection, negotiation, cooking, sitting, birth support, or event-day accountability.

## Value capture
Capacity-constrained practitioners and fixed-fee projects may retain gains initially. Low barriers, client switching, digital substitutes, and scope expansion should push part of those gains into lower prices, richer service, or more responsiveness.

## Firm availability
Census identifies many eligible human-service activities, but it also places financial, digital, unattended-machine, and other incomparable models in the same residual code. The eligible share and supplied LMM firm count are therefore unusually uncertain, while owner dependence reduces transferability.

## Demand durability
BLS expects fitness and event-planning employment to grow, supporting demand for two retained activities. The code-wide outlook is less certain because household discretionary budgets, digital self-service, demographics, and activity mix can pull in different directions.

## Risks and uncertainty
The principal risks are lens-mix uncertainty, absent revenue weights, missing task and deal-flow data, practitioner dependence, fragmented microbusiness systems, sensitive client data, liability in physical or deadline-critical services, and a common-margin firm-count estimate applied to unlike models.

## Caveats
- a: No source measures the wage-weighted task mix of the narrowed six-digit population; fitness and event-planning occupations cover workers and employers outside 812990.
- rho: Implementation economics vary sharply between repeat appointments, one-time events, physical services, and advisory projects.
- e: The eligible share is especially uncertain because 812990 is a residual code and the supplied LMM count uses a common business-services margin across unlike activities.
- s5: No control-sale rate or owner-age series was found for the narrowed cluster; a practitioner retiring without a transferable organization is not a qualifying transfer.
- q: BLS describes work and demand rather than contract pass-through; pricing and renewal behavior likely differs materially by subactivity.
- d5: Occupation projections are national ten-year employment forecasts, not five-year demand ratios for the narrowed NAICS population.
- o: Operator necessity is high for physical and safety-critical services but much lower for research, planning templates, and routine coordination.
