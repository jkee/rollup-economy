# 512290 — Other Sound Recording Industries

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `512290-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 5.39 · L 1.55 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Repeat event and program workflows contain substantial digital post-capture work while complex assignments still value accountable technical delivery.
**Weakness:** The residual industry's composition is poorly observed, and routine meeting recording faces direct substitution from customer-owned and automated tools.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market firms in NAICS 512290 that repeatedly provide external customers with nonstudio sound-recording services, including on-location recording of meetings and conferences and production of recorded radio programs where classified in this industry.
- Excluded: Record labels and distributors, music publishers, sound-recording studios, motion-picture audio postproduction classified elsewhere, mass duplication, event promotion, self-employed creators classified as independent artists, captive internal audiovisual teams, one-off shells, non-control financing vehicles, and firms outside the approximate $1-10 million normalized EBITDA band.
- Customer and revenue model: Revenue is primarily project, event, program, retainer, or managed-service fees paid by conference organizers, institutions, broadcasters, producers, and corporate clients. Work combines capture planning, equipment and crew deployment, live quality assurance, editing, mastering, delivery, archiving, and customer coordination.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Other sound recording is a small residual service industry with credible AI leverage in editing, transcription support, documentation, and administration, but continued dependence on physical capture and live quality control. Repeat institutional customers can support an outsourced-service model, while self-recording technology, uncertain industry composition, and owner-technician dependence create substantial downside.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate speech cleanup, rough cuts, indexing, summaries, transcripts, speaker labeling, file preparation, quality-flag detection, proposals, scheduling, and routine customer communication. Human operators remain important for planning, consent, microphones and cabling, live monitoring, acoustic judgment, equipment failures, secure handling, and accountable final review.

## Value capture
Fixed-deliverable and managed-service contracts can preserve some savings initially, but competitive bidding and customers' awareness of automated alternatives will share gains. Operators may retain more when AI improves turnaround or searchability without lowering the visible field crew, and less when work is billed hourly or clients can self-serve.

## Firm availability
The official category is directly service-oriented, so a meaningful eligible share is plausible, but its residual composition is opaque and likely includes small founder-led shops. Client relationships, crews, equipment, archives, and procedures can transfer; personal technical reputation and concentrated accounts can impair continuity.

## Demand durability
More events, remote participation, podcasts, accessibility, compliance records, and media reuse support recording demand. Built-in conferencing capture, automated transcription and editing, and internal audiovisual teams can eliminate outsourced work, especially for routine meetings, leaving technically complex, confidential, and high-stakes assignments more operator-dependent.

## Risks and uncertainty
Primary risks are self-service substitution, privacy and consent failures, confidential-data exposure, live capture errors, equipment obsolescence, customer concentration, contractor availability, owner dependence, and a poor fit between broad-sector data and this residual code. The absence of a six-digit service-line census makes both demand and operator need unusually uncertain.

## Caveats
- a: BLS occupation evidence covers all sound-recording industries rather than 512290.
- a: The residual NAICS category is poorly described beyond meeting and conference recording, so service-line weights are unknown.
- a: Speech-transcription exposure may not represent music, live-event, or broadcast-program workflows.
- rho: No source reports AI implementation or realized labor savings for 512290.
- rho: Performance on clean recorded speech can materially overstate performance in live, noisy, confidential, or multi-speaker settings.
- rho: Client policies may prohibit cloud processing even where the workflow is technically feasible.
- e: The official definition gives few examples, leaving the composition of the residual category uncertain.
- e: No source measures the repeat-service or transferable-firm share in the supplied LMM population.
- e: The supplied n estimate relies on a broad information-services margin that may not fit field recording and contractor-heavy production.
- s5: Gallup is not specific to the industry, size band, or technical-service business model.
- s5: Intentions do not measure completed qualifying control transfers.
- s5: The employer-versus-nonemployer mix of the estimated LMM population is not observed.
- q: No source measures pricing structure or benefit sharing for 512290.
- q: Retention differs between fixed-deliverable, hourly, crew-day, retainer, and bundled event contracts.
- q: Customer savings from self-service belong in operator-required demand rather than being counted again here.
- d5: BLS covers the entire 512200 group and provides no separate 512290 projection.
- d5: The residual industry's service-line weights and historical revenue series are not available in the cited evidence.
- d5: Demand for more recorded content can coexist with lower spending per recorded hour.
- o: No source measures the future operator-required share for 512290.
- o: The split between simple meeting capture and technically demanding field production is unknown.
- o: Some automation may change the operator's workflow rather than eliminate the accountable service provider.
