# 512120 — Motion Picture and Video Distribution

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `512120-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 7.62 · L 3.17 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Digital catalogs, repeated release workflows, structured platform data, and measurable audience behavior make distribution operations unusually amenable to automation.
**Weakness:** The same digitalization lowers intermediation barriers and lets producers, platforms, and technology vendors absorb the distributor's role.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market distributors that repeatedly acquire, license, represent, market, deliver, monetize, report, and settle rights to motion pictures and video with theaters, broadcasters, networks, streaming services, digital platforms, and other exhibitors or licensees.
- Excluded: Companies primarily producing and distributing their own content classified in 512110; captive distribution units; title-only rights vehicles and passive libraries without an operating team; physical-media wholesalers; exhibitors; streaming infrastructure providers; shells; non-control financing vehicles; and firms outside the lower-middle-market band.
- Customer and revenue model: Distributors earn recurring or repeat distribution commissions, agency fees, platform and media-service fees, fixed license fees, minimum guarantees with overages, and shares of box-office, rental, advertising, subscription, or other title receipts across territories and release windows.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Motion picture and video distribution is a data- and contract-intensive intermediary with substantial exposure in metadata, analytics, localization, delivery, marketing operations, and settlement. AI can improve those workflows, but the industry's independent operator role is vulnerable to platform consolidation, direct licensing, and self-distribution.

## How AI changes the work
AI can classify catalogs, enrich metadata, match titles to audiences and outlets, forecast demand, generate campaign variants, translate captions, validate assets, optimize ads and release timing, abstract contracts, reconcile usage reports, flag royalty exceptions, and support buyer outreach. Rights acquisition, negotiation, relationship management, minimum-guarantee risk, final release decisions, legal accountability, and exception resolution remain human-led.

## Value capture
Commission, percentage, and fixed-fee structures can preserve internal savings until contracts reset. Producers and platforms can demand lower fees, expand scope, audit expenses, or bypass the distributor, so durable capture depends on scarce buyer access, financing capacity, catalog performance, and service quality rather than automation alone.

## Firm availability
The supplied firm population is small and uncertain. Eligible independent operators exist, but integrated producer-distributors, title-holding vehicles, technology platforms, and passive catalogs complicate classification and transferability, while customer, rights, and key-person concentration can impair continuity.

## Demand durability
Video consumption and the number of digital outlets support continued rights and delivery activity, while physical media and linear channels contract. Company results remain title-driven, so a roughly flat five-year service-volume base carries a wide range.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are the true occupation mix, proprietary-system transferability, rights-data quality, platform certification, cybersecurity, hit concentration, minimum-guarantee exposure, customer bargaining power, integrated-studio classification, self-distribution, direct platform licensing, and whether AI platforms complement or replace independent intermediaries.

## Caveats
- a: O*NET occupations span the whole economy and are not the observed occupation mix of 512120.
- a: Integrated studios and technology-led distributors may have different staffing from independent sales agents.
- a: Rights and royalty data are structured in some firms but remain contract-specific and exception-heavy in others.
- rho: Management descriptions of AI strategy are not controlled measures of realized labor removal.
- rho: Cineverse is explicitly technology-first and may not represent typical lower-middle-market distributors.
- rho: Platform delivery and rights errors can create disproportionate financial and reputational losses.
- e: Industry classification can shift integrated producer-distributors into 512110 even when distribution is operationally substantial.
- e: Rights-holding entities and operating distributors may be counted separately or under common ownership.
- e: The supplied firm estimate is margin-bridged despite title volatility, minimum guarantees, and capitalized content costs.
- s5: Two acquisitions by one technology-led company do not measure the industry transfer probability.
- s5: Catalog, rights, platform, and media-service transactions may fall outside 512120 or fail the control test.
- s5: No owner-age or succession distribution is observed for eligible independent distributors.
- q: Integrated-studio agreements may not represent independent third-party distribution contracts.
- q: Revenue share and minimum guarantees describe payment mechanics, not direct pass-through of automation savings.
- q: Rights owners can shift distributors or self-distribute when service becomes standardized.
- d5: Both companies combine production, distribution, streaming, libraries, technology, and other media services.
- d5: Nominal title revenue and audience minutes are not constant-price, constant-quality distributor service quantity.
- d5: A few successful or unsuccessful titles can dominate annual company comparisons.
- o: Operator requirement differs sharply between theatrical and international releases and simple digital catalog delivery.
- o: A platform, producer, or technology vendor can internalize the same accountable functions.
- o: AI-generated or self-served content may expand title volume while reducing the value of traditional intermediation.
