# 325611 — Soap and Other Detergent Manufacturing

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `325611-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** STRUCTURAL_OUT · A 5.67 · L 0.71 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → LOW_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Essential repeat cleaning demand and outsourced private-label production create durable physical throughput with recurring AI-addressable planning and quality work.
**Weakness:** Powerful customers can reprice transparent conversion savings, leaving the manufacturer with implementation and plant risk but only part of the benefit.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market contract, toll, private-label, and white-label manufacturers providing repeat formulation, blending, filling, packaging, labeling, and fulfillment of soaps and detergents for external brands, retailers, chemical companies, and institutional customers.
- Excluded: Manufacturers primarily selling proprietary brands, captive plants, distributors without manufacturing, handcrafted microbusinesses below the economic band, and producers focused on shampoos, cosmetics, specialty cleaners, or sanitation preparations outside NAICS 325611.
- Customer and revenue model: Retailers, consumer brands, industrial and institutional suppliers, and chemical companies purchase recurring finished-product runs or toll conversion under specifications and purchase orders; economics range from customer-owned formula and materials to turnkey formulation, packaging, and logistics.
- Deviation from default lens: The NAICS combines branded product manufacturers with outsourced contract and private-label plants. The lens is narrowed to the latter because only they supply a coherent recurring outsourced manufacturing service to external customers.

## Executive view
The relevant pool is the subset of soap and detergent plants providing external contract, private-label, or toll production. Essential repeat consumption and active buyer interest support the model, but procurement power and visible conversion economics can limit retention of AI-driven labor savings.

## How AI changes the work
Practical uses include formula and specification retrieval, label and document review, demand and inventory forecasting, production scheduling, quote drafting, customer-service automation, predictive quality, changeover planning, and maintenance triage. Mixing, filling, sanitation, QC sampling, wastewater control, warehousing, and shipping remain physical.

## Value capture
Retailer and brand customers often have alternatives and can rebid per-unit economics. The operator retains more value when AI improves fill rate, rapid minimum runs, compliance, formulation speed, scrap, and on-time delivery than when it merely reveals fewer direct labor hours.

## Firm availability
Public transaction evidence shows that contract and private-label cleaning-product manufacturers are being acquired by lower-middle-market sponsors and strategic buyers. The addressable population still excludes branded manufacturers and depends on clean environmental diligence, transferable formulas, customer diversity, and stable plant leadership.

## Demand durability
Soap and detergent usage is tied to routine household, institutional, and industrial hygiene. Quantity should remain stable to modestly growing, though concentrated formulations, packaging changes, retailer inventory decisions, and brand shifts can move individual contract-plant volumes.

## Risks and uncertainty
Eligibility and commercial retention lack direct measurement. Retailer concentration, private-label price competition, raw-material and packaging volatility, environmental liabilities, product quality, formula confidentiality, working capital, customer insourcing, and legacy plant systems can overwhelm the labor opportunity.

## Caveats
- a: No source measures AI-exposed tasks for NAICS 325611 or the narrowed contract-manufacturing subset.
- a: Existing packaging and process automation varies materially by product form and run length.
- rho: Selected AI projects do not establish an industry adoption rate.
- rho: Implementation excludes pricing, market demand, and valuation effects.
- e: The provided firm count is based on a specialty-chemical margin bridge rather than observed soap-manufacturer EBITDA.
- e: Private-label soap may coexist with cosmetics or specialty-cleaner activities classified elsewhere.
- s5: Two announced deals demonstrate flow but cannot measure probability.
- s5: Some target activities may include personal care or specialty cleaners outside strict NAICS 325611.
- q: Retailer private-label, institutional, and customer-supplied toll contracts have different bargaining structures.
- q: This estimate excludes implementation difficulty and demand changes.
- d5: The ACI survey measures attitudes and habits rather than soap or detergent volume.
- d5: Church & Dwight is a branded diversified company, not an LMM contract manufacturer.
- o: Concentrates, refill systems, and dosing innovation can reduce shipped quantity without removing the manufacturing operator.
- o: The estimate concerns operator requirement, not unchanged staffing.
