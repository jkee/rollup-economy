# 561320 — Temporary Help Services

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.19 · L 2.03 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** High-volume recruiting and administration are already adopting AI, creating measurable speed and capacity opportunities across a large operator population.
**Weakness:** The largest labor costs are billable client-controlled assignment hours, so eliminating them often removes revenue rather than creating retainable operator savings.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market temporary-help and staffing firms that repeatedly recruit, employ, administer, and supply workers to external clients for limited assignments across industrial, clerical, professional, technical, education, and health-care categories.
- Excluded: Permanent-placement-only agencies, executive search, professional employer organizations, independent contractor marketplaces without an employer role, farm labor contractors, captive internal staffing units, shells, and firms without transferable client and worker relationships.
- Customer and revenue model: Clients buy contingent labor capacity, usually billed per worker-hour or per unit. The staffing firm pays assigned employees, payroll taxes, benefits where applicable, and workers' compensation, retaining a spread that funds recruiting, branch operations, compliance, and profit; some providers also earn managed-service or outcome-based fees.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Temporary staffing contains a highly automatable recruiting and administrative layer but carries a much larger payroll for workers performing client-controlled physical, professional, and care assignments. AI can improve fill speed and office productivity, yet hourly billing and client control make much of the headline labor exposure difficult for the staffing operator to retain.

## How AI changes the work
AI is already used for sourcing, matching, résumé review, messaging, screening, scheduling, fraud detection, analytics, and workforce administration. It can also substitute for some clerical, support, coding, and analytical assignments, while robotics may affect production and logistics; most physical, bedside, skilled-trade, and site-specific work remains outside current software automation.

## Value capture
Internal recruiting and branch savings can improve speed, capacity, and SG&A. Savings in assigned-worker labor are usually passed through because revenue is earned per hour or unit, and customers can simply buy fewer hours; competitive procurement also presses markups and makes technology-enabled service a baseline expectation.

## Firm availability
There is an active staffing M&A market and a large supplied firm population, but quality varies by vertical, customer concentration, compliance history, claims reserves, working-capital needs, and recruiter or branch dependence. A transferable candidate database and diversified client base matter more than nominal firm count.

## Demand durability
Temporary labor remains useful for flexibility and hard-to-fill roles but is cut quickly in downturns. Official projections imply mildly declining exact-industry employment, recent public-company results show weak demand, and AI threatens several assignment categories even as health care, education, technical, and skilled work remain resilient.

## Risks and uncertainty
The industry combines commodity industrial staffing with professional, health-care, and education niches, so aggregate ranges hide large differences. Wage-law compliance, worker classification, safety, workers' compensation, candidate fraud, client concentration, recession sensitivity, platform disintermediation, and thin gross margins can outweigh office-productivity gains.

## Caveats
- a: Occupation shares are headcount rather than wage shares, although reported wages inform the directional weighting.
- a: The occupational mix aggregates very different staffing verticals and client work environments.
- a: Client-site employees are not directly supervised by the staffing firm, so technical exposure does not imply operator-controlled deployment.
- rho: Bullhorn surveys recruitment professionals globally and includes firms beyond temporary help.
- rho: Purchased or experimental AI is not the same as production implementation or labor avoidance.
- rho: Most on-assignment task implementation is controlled by clients, not the staffing operator.
- e: No source directly measures lens eligibility within the injected firm count.
- e: The code spans low-margin industrial staffing and higher-margin professional or health-care niches.
- e: The injected count uses an industry-agnostic margin bridge that may misclassify staffing firms with large pass-through payroll and low reported margins.
- s5: The owner survey measures intentions across industries rather than completed staffing transactions.
- s5: The M&A report includes North American workforce-solutions businesses outside exact NAICS 561320.
- s5: Asset purchases, contract acquisitions, and team deals may not qualify as transfers of durable operating firms.
- q: Kelly is a large diversified provider rather than a representative lower-middle-market firm.
- q: No source directly measures commercial sharing of AI productivity gains.
- q: Retention differs materially between commodity hourly staffing, scarce professional niches, and outcome-based contracts.
- d5: BLS provides exact-industry employment but output only for broader employment services.
- d5: Employment is an imperfect proxy for constant-quality service quantity.
- d5: Temporary-help demand is highly cyclical and recent company results may reflect a trough rather than a durable trend.
- o: No source directly measures the year-five operator-required share.
- o: The operator role may migrate to platforms, payroll providers, or client-owned talent pools even when human temporary work persists.
- o: AI-enabled candidate fraud and employment regulation may increase the value of accountable screening while also raising cost.
