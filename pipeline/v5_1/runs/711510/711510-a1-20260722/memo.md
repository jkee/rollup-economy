# 711510 — Independent Artists, Writers, and Performers

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `711510-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** PRIORITY · A 6.50 · L 4.44 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** A multi-person creative operator can combine exposed digital workflows with human authorship, taste, rights control, and accountable delivery that customers still need.
**Weakness:** Most of the code is organized around individual creators and performers, making the genuinely transferable firm population much smaller than the supplied firm estimate.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US multi-person creative studios, production teams, ensembles, and technical-creative firms in the $1-10M normalized EBITDA band that repeatedly deliver commissioned or licensed writing, visual art, performance, recording, restoration, design, or production expertise to external customers.
- Excluded: Single-person or celebrity-dependent performance and endorsement vehicles, one-creator royalty or IP holding entities, one-off project companies, individual freelancers without transferable operations, captive units, and financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: External publishers, producers, brands, venues, institutions, platforms, and audiences pay project or performance fees, retainers, royalties, licenses, usage fees, or revenue shares for repeated creative output and production expertise.
- Deviation from default lens: The code explicitly combines freelance actors, writers, visual artists, producers, technicians, restorers, speakers, athletes, and celebrities. To keep one coherent operating-company screen, the lens retains multi-person firms with repeat external work and excludes individual-identity vehicles whose output and customer relationship cannot be separated from one creator or performer.

## Executive view
The code contains a large AI-exposed creative and administrative surface, but it is structurally difficult to consolidate because Census defines it around freelancers and many LMM entities remain personal brands or IP vehicles. The investable population is the narrower set of multi-person studios and production businesses whose customer relationships, delivery capacity, and creative standards persist beyond one named individual.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate research, outlining, drafting, editing, storyboards, concept variants, image and audio preparation, metadata, post-production, promotion, scheduling, and back-office work. It is less able to replace live performance, physical craft, restoration, differentiated taste, customer discovery, factual responsibility, rights clearance, and the human authorship or consent needed for important commercial uses.

## Value capture
Fixed-price and licensed work can retain near-term production savings, while hourly assignments and competitively bid routine content pass savings through more quickly. Customers may convert faster production into more variants and revisions, and operators must reinvest in senior review, provenance, security, and rights management.

## Firm availability
Broad owner exit intent does not translate directly into transferable creative firms. Diligence must distinguish organizations with firm-owned brands, contracts, IP processes, archives, equipment, and second-layer leadership from vehicles whose clients, audience, rights, and earnings leave with a founder, celebrity, writer, or performer.

## Demand durability
BLS projects roughly flat industry employment, while NEA shows independent-artist value added around its 2019 level in 2023 and IAB shows rapid growth in creator advertising. These signals support stable aggregate demand with strong internal rotation toward digital, creator, and self-published work and away from some routine or technically intermediated services.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main uncertainties are extreme business-model heterogeneity, personal-identity concentration, unclear contract and IP transferability, rapid quality improvement in generative media, litigation and licensing rules, platform dependence, and customers' ability to self-serve. A bad outcome is buying a seemingly scaled firm that is actually a single creator's income stream just as routine content prices fall.

## Caveats
- a: The BLS matrix is employment-weighted, while this primitive is wage-weighted; management and high-paid creative roles may receive more weight than their headcount shares.
- a: O*NET writer tasks illuminate only one part of a highly heterogeneous creative workforce.
- a: Creative assistance and faster iteration do not necessarily substitute labor if customers demand more variants or higher quality.
- rho: IAB surveyed creator-ad buyers, not 711510 operators, and combines current use with planned use.
- rho: Census covers all nonfarm industries and any business-function use, not deep workflow implementation.
- rho: Guild restrictions cover important screen and performance work but not all creative firms.
- e: The supplied n is margin-bridged from employer-firm statistics, but the underlying NAICS definition is centered on freelance individuals.
- e: Legal entities may employ substantial support staffs while remaining economically inseparable from one celebrity or creator.
- e: Eligibility varies sharply among writing studios, live ensembles, restoration shops, technical crews, and personal performance vehicles.
- s5: The owner survey is voluntary, cross-industry, and oriented toward exit planning.
- s5: Exit intent is not a completed control transfer.
- s5: Small creative-company transactions and acqui-hires are often undisclosed or ambiguously classified.
- q: The code mixes fixed-fee, hourly, royalty, licensing, ticket, appearance, and endorsement economics.
- q: Union compensation and AI-consent provisions affect covered work but not the whole industry.
- q: This estimate excludes demand and self-service loss, which are reflected in d5 and o.
- d5: Industry employment is not service quantity and omits self-employed people with no payroll staff in some source systems.
- d5: The BLS ten-year projection is converted to five years and covers the full heterogeneous code, not the narrowed lens.
- d5: Creator advertising is only one fast-growing slice, while the NEA value-added observation has a 2023 endpoint.
- o: Copyrightability is not required for every customer use, so human-authorship rules may not preserve all work.
- o: WGA and SAG-AFTRA terms cover union productions and performers, not the entire NAICS code.
- o: The operator requirement is much higher for live, physical, restorative, and high-reputation work than for routine commercial content.

## Sources
- **S1** — [2022 NAICS Definition: 711510 Independent Artists, Writers, and Performers](https://www.census.gov/naics/?details=711510&input=711510&year=2022) (accessed 2026-07-22): Defines the industry as independent freelance individuals performing, creating artistic or cultural works, or providing production expertise, and lists actors, producers, restorers, technicians, artists, speakers, dancers, journalists, writers, athletes, and celebrities.
- **S2** — [BLS National Employment Matrix: Independent artists, writers, and performers](https://data.bls.gov/projections/nationalMatrix?ioType=i&queryParams=711500) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports 62,200 industry jobs in 2024 and 61,200 projected for 2034, a 1.5% decline; provides the industry occupation mix, including 57.8% arts/media and 8.2% office support employment.
- **S3** — [BLS Factors affecting occupational utilization, projected 2024-2034](https://www.bls.gov/emp/tables/factors-affecting-occupational-utilization.htm) (accessed 2026-07-22): Identifies rising self-publishing as support for writers in 711500 and identifies AI or digital technology as increasing productivity or reducing demand for technical writers, audio and video technicians, sound engineers, lighting technicians, and other media work.
- **S4** — [O*NET 27-3043.00 - Writers and Authors](https://www.onetonline.org/link/details/27-3043.00) (accessed 2026-07-22): Lists writing tasks including research, campaign development, varying tone, drafting, editing and rewriting, planning, formatting, client discussion, collaboration, and copyright procedures.
- **S5** — [Large Firms With at Least 20 Employees Biggest AI Users](https://www.census.gov/library/stories/2026/05/ai-use-businesses.html) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports nationally representative BTOS AI use of 17%-20% from December 2025 through May 2026, expected use of 20%-23%, and less than 20% use among firms with four or fewer employees.
- **S6** — [U.S. Copyright Office Releases Part 2 of Artificial Intelligence Report](https://www.copyright.gov/newsnet/2025/1060.html) (accessed 2026-07-22): States that AI outputs are copyrightable only where a human author determines sufficient expressive elements, that prompting alone is insufficient, and that assistive AI does not bar copyrightability.
- **S7** — [Writers Guild of America West: Artificial Intelligence](https://www.wga.org/contracts/know-your-rights/artificial-intelligence) (accessed 2026-07-22): States that AI is not a writer under the 2023 MBA, AI-generated output is not literary material, writers may choose to use AI with company consent, and companies must disclose AI-generated materials.
- **S8** — [SAG-AFTRA 2025 Commercials Contracts](https://www.sagaftra.org/contracts-industry-resources/commercials/2025-commercials-contracts) (accessed 2026-07-22): Requires consent before creating a digital replica and informed consent before use, and provides a 1.5-times session fee plus applicable holding and use fees for replica-generated performance.
- **S9** — [NEA: Arts and Cultural Industries Grew at Twice the Rate of the U.S. Economy](https://www.arts.gov/news/press-releases/2025/arts-and-cultural-industries-grew-twice-rate-us-economy-adding-12-trillion) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports that independent artists, writers, and performers had 2023 value added roughly on par with 2019 but below 2022, and notes that ACPSA payroll employment excludes self-employed arts workers without staff.
- **S10** — [2023 National State of Owner Readiness Report](https://exit-planning-institute.org/hubfs/Member%20Center%20Resources/2023%20National%20State%20of%20Owner%20Readiness%20Report.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-22): Surveyed 1,162 US business owners; 49% wanted to exit within five years, 70% preferred an internal option, and 42% had a written formal business transition plan.
- **S11** — [IAB 2025 Creator Economy Ad Spend & Strategy Report](https://www.iab.com/insights/2025-creator-economy-ad-spend-strategy-report/) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports US creator ad spend of $37 billion in 2025, up 26% year over year, expected to reach $44 billion in 2026, and says three in four surveyed brands use or plan to use AI for creator-marketing tasks.
