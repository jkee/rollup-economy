# 321219 — Reconstituted Wood Product Manufacturing

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `321219-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** STRUCTURAL_OUT · A 4.62 · L 0.34 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → STRUCTURAL_OUT · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Defensible gains in yield, uptime, quality inspection, and scheduling within recurring specification-driven production relationships.
**Weakness:** Most firms in the NAICS code manufacture and sell products rather than provide an outsourced service, sharply limiting lens eligibility.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market manufacturers of reconstituted wood sheets and boards that repeatedly provide custom, toll, specification-controlled, or otherwise service-like production to external customers.
- Excluded: Commodity panel sales with no recurring outsourced-service relationship, captive production, commodity distributors, pure software vendors, non-control financing vehicles, and firms outside the approximately $1-10M normalized EBITDA band.
- Customer and revenue model: Eligible firms earn recurring B2B revenue by manufacturing particleboard, MDF, fiberboard, OSB, or related panels to customer specifications; the broader code predominantly earns point-in-time product revenue through purchase orders.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
The industry offers a real but bounded operating-improvement opportunity: machine vision, process control, maintenance prediction, scheduling, and commercial administration can reduce labor or avoid hiring, while the core flow of wood, resin, heat, pressing, finishing, and shipment remains physical. The larger issue is fit with the fixed lens. Most 321219 businesses sell panels as products, leaving only a small custom, toll, or dedicated-production subset as eligible recurring outsourced services.

## How AI changes the work
AI can improve defect detection, furnish and resin optimization, downtime prediction, production sequencing, quoting, order handling, and compliance documentation. The largest occupations are hands-on production and material-movement roles, so AI usually augments equipment and operators rather than directly replacing whole jobs. Realization depends on sensors, controls integration, reliable training data, and safe escalation to humans.

## Value capture
Benefits hidden in yield, uptime, quality, and faster response are more defensible than transparent labor-rate reductions. Commodity comparison, frequent purchase orders, concentrated customers, and competitive capacity should cause much of a visible cost advantage to be shared through price over five years. Custom specifications and service reliability provide the main protection.

## Firm availability
The supplied LMM population estimate is only 31 firms, and the eligible service-like fraction is probably small. Broad small-business evidence indicates meaningful succession activity, but it does not establish completed control transfers in this niche. A named-firm screen and owner outreach are essential before assuming a viable acquisition pipeline.

## Demand durability
Demand is anchored in housing construction, remodeling, furniture/cabinetry, and industrial uses. Aging housing stock and potential share gains for engineered panels support modest five-year growth, but rates, affordability, construction cycles, imports, new capacity, and material substitution create a wide downside-to-upside band. The physical output continues to require an accountable manufacturer even when more workflow is automated.

## Risks and uncertainty
The principal risks are category mismatch with the service lens, a very small eligible-firm pool, commodity price pressure, customer concentration, capital-heavy integration, plant safety, cyclical end markets, and lack of six-digit evidence on AI task exposure. The opportunity would be unattractive if diligence finds that custom/toll revenue is immaterial, eligible owners are not transferable, or customers rapidly reprice all productivity gains.

## Caveats
- a: The available occupational composition is for NAICS 3212, not the six-digit industry.
- a: Technology exposure is not the same as economically substitutable labor and includes non-AI automation.
- a: The supplied compensation-to-receipts ratio uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and is harmonized to an IPS basis.
- rho: No industry-specific five-year AI implementation cohort was found.
- rho: Small LMM plants may lack clean sensor data, controls engineers, and capital for line integration.
- rho: The industry's 2024 recordable injury rate of 2.5 per 100 full-time workers increases the cost of unsafe automation failures.
- e: Service-like manufacturing is not separately identified in NAICS data.
- e: Repeat purchases alone do not necessarily constitute an outsourced service.
- e: The supplied firm count is a margin-bridged estimate based on 2022 receipts and a January 2026 sector margin.
- s5: Stated plans are not completed transactions.
- s5: The survey spans all small-business industries and sizes.
- s5: The source category includes gifts and rare IPOs that may not qualify under the lens.
- q: The cited public-company revenue model may not represent smaller custom or toll manufacturers.
- q: Customer concentration can both accelerate pass-through and support relationship-based retention.
- q: No direct study tracks five-year retention of AI-created gross benefit in this industry.
- d5: Softwood-lumber elasticity is an adjacent-product proxy.
- d5: Housing starts are cyclical and highly sensitive to rates and affordability.
- d5: Public-company commentary is management judgment and covers markets outside the US and products outside 321219.
- o: The estimate separates operator requirement from overall demand but insourcing and substitution can be difficult to distinguish.
- o: Accountability requirements vary between commodity panels and engineered or tightly specified products.
- o: No survey directly measures customer plans to self-supply reconstituted panels.
