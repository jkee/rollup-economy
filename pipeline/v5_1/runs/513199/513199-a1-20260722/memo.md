**Driver:** The principal driver is AI exposure across creative adaptation, editorial, rights-support, catalog, sales, and administrative workflows in repeat publishing programs.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market all-other publishers that provide recurring or repeat publishing, catalog, licensing, or content programs to external retailers, distributors, institutions, or business customers, including repeat art-print, calendar, reference-artifact, and other nonexcluded print or electronic publishing programs.
- Excluded: Newspaper, magazine, book, directory, mailing-list, music, software, and greeting-card publishers classified elsewhere; one-off direct-to-consumer art or calendar sellers without repeat customer programs; independent artists self-publishing their own work; printers without publishing rights; educational test developers not commonly known as publishers; shells, captive units, non-control financing vehicles, and firms outside the target normalized-EBITDA band.
- Customer and revenue model: Retailers, distributors, institutions, licensors, and business customers buy or license copyrighted art prints, calendars, and other published works through wholesale orders, repeat catalogs, seasonal programs, fixed projects, royalties, subscriptions where applicable, or digital licenses. Publishers acquire or create rights, curate editions, prepare content and metadata, market titles, manage customer programs, and coordinate physical or electronic distribution.
- Deviation from default lens: The residual code is structurally heterogeneous and includes art publishers, calendar publishers, physical and electronic formats, and one-off product sales. For a coherent fixed-lens screen, it is narrowed to firms with repeat external-customer publishing or licensing programs; no content niche was selected for attractiveness.

## Executive view
All Other Publishers is a residual, highly heterogeneous category in which AI can compress creative, editorial, catalog, commercial, and administrative work. The fixed roll-up lens fits only repeat external-customer publishing and licensing programs, and the lack of a defensible LMM firm count prevents confidence in the available pool.

## How AI changes the work
AI can assist concept and image variants, copy, editing, layout, metadata, provenance search, localization, catalogs, sales materials, analysis, and account support. Human curation, rights clearance, creator relationships, licensing negotiation, physical approval, and accountable publication remain important.

## Value capture
Fixed product prices, license fees, and seasonal programs may preserve savings between negotiations, while productivity can increase catalog breadth. Retailer power, royalties, distributors, platforms, generic-content competition, and reinvestment in customization limit retained value.

## Firm availability
The eligible population is a narrow subset with repeat B2B programs, controlled rights, transferable catalogs, operational independence, and normalized LMM earnings. No defensible LMM firm count exists, and many residual publishers are one-off or founder-dependent product businesses.

## Demand durability
Recurring calendar, decorative, institutional, and licensing uses support some demand, but digital substitutes, direct creators, print-on-demand, platforms, and retailer self-service can shrink traditional publishing. Category heterogeneity makes a single demand path weakly identified.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are the missing LMM firm count, residual-code heterogeneity, narrow outsourced-service fit, creator dependence, incomplete rights chains, customer concentration, platform disintermediation, physical-format decline, broad occupation and demand proxies, and asset sales that do not transfer an operating company.

## Caveats
- a: BLS publishes only a broad 513100 occupation mix, not exact 513199 or the narrowed repeat-program population.
- a: The residual code may have a materially different mix of creators, editors, sales staff, digital workers, and outsourced production.
- a: Occupation shares do not measure task-level exposure or current automation.
- rho: No representative source measures AI realization in 513199.
- rho: The residual code's format and product heterogeneity makes one implementation rate especially uncertain.
- rho: Faster content production may expand catalogs and customization rather than reduce labor.
- e: The frozen dataset declares no defensible LMM-band firm count.
- e: The official residual category does not measure repeat-program revenue, normalized EBITDA, or transferability.
- e: Copyright ownership can reside in a founder-dependent catalog that is transferable as an asset but not as an operating service firm.
- s5: Gallup measures intentions among employer-business owners across industries, not completed publisher transfers.
- s5: The proxy does not isolate LMM firms, rights-based businesses, or the repeat-program lens.
- s5: Catalog and copyright asset sales may not be qualifying control transfers.
- q: No representative source measures automation-benefit retention for 513199.
- q: Physical wholesale, commissioned work, licensing, and direct digital sales have materially different pass-through mechanisms.
- q: Productivity gains may fund more catalog breadth and customer-specific versions rather than retained profit.
- d5: BLS projects employment for broad 513100, not constant-price demand for exact 513199.
- d5: The residual category's niches may have opposite demand trajectories.
- d5: Employment can fall through outsourcing or productivity while demand remains stable.
- o: Official scope establishes publishing and copyright control, not the future operator-required share.
- o: Operator need varies materially between rights-intensive licensed catalogs and simple commodity calendars or art prints.
- o: A publisher may retain legal accountability while platforms and software perform most operating work.
