# 621310 — Offices of Chiropractors

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `621310-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.15 · L 2.20 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Centralized AI-assisted administration can improve a repeat-visit, per-service practice without attempting to replace the licensed hands-on treatment at its core.
**Weakness:** Only a small estimated LMM population exists, and many practices' patient goodwill and production remain tightly bound to individual chiropractors.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent group and multisite chiropractic practices in the lower-middle-market band that repeatedly provide diagnostic assessment, spinal or extremity manipulation, related neuromusculoskeletal treatment, rehabilitation guidance, care-plan management, and patient administration to external patients and payers.
- Excluded: Captive hospital or HMO units, shell or non-operating entities, non-control financing positions, solo or owner-dependent practices that cannot support a transferable operating company, practices below the normalized EBITDA band, physical therapy or massage businesses classified elsewhere, and product-only wellness retail.
- Customer and revenue model: Patients are the service recipients and may also be the direct economic customers; commercial insurers, Medicare, workers' compensation programs, and other payers may fund covered visits. Revenue is commonly tied to visits, procedures, treatment episodes, or cash-pay care rather than disclosed labor hours, and repeat treatment creates recurring patient interactions without guaranteeing contractual recurrence.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Scaled chiropractic groups offer repeat patient care with administrative workflows that AI can streamline, but the economic service remains anchored in licensed, hands-on treatment. The opportunity is therefore operational augmentation rather than clinician replacement. A transferable group with associates, standardized protocols, centralized intake and billing, and limited founder dependence is materially more attractive than a personal practice built around one chiropractor's relationships and production.

## How AI changes the work
Near-term AI can draft encounter notes, summarize histories, support coding, automate intake and recall messaging, optimize schedules, and assist marketing and patient communication. Those tools can reduce clerical burden and avoid some administrative hiring. Physical examination, manual adjustment, treatment decisions, patient trust, exception handling, and final clinical responsibility remain human work, limiting the total wage-weighted substitution opportunity.

## Value capture
Visit- and procedure-based reimbursement can allow administrative savings to remain with the practice because price is not explicitly tied to disclosed labor hours. Capture depends on filling freed capacity and controlling denials; fixed payer schedules, cash-pay competition, documentation obligations, and reinvestment in patient service prevent full retention.

## Firm availability
The frozen LMM count is small and estimate-based. Medical-practice transaction evidence confirms a transfer channel that includes chiropractors, but not a chiropractic-specific five-year sale rate. Availability depends on seller timing, licensed successor supply, associate retention, patient goodwill, and whether clinical production and referrals belong to the organization rather than the founder.

## Demand durability
BLS projects chiropractor employment growth and points to aging, pain treatment, and acceptance of nonsurgical care as demand supports. Coverage limits, discretionary cash-pay exposure, competition from other musculoskeletal providers, and variable clinical acceptance create a meaningful downside range around that direction.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are the true employee workflow mix, the margin-bridged firm count, lack of a chiropractic-specific transaction cohort, fragmented technology adoption, payer and cash-pay mix, founder dependence, and whether time savings translate into additional visits. Regulatory privacy, documentation accuracy, coding compliance, and patient consent constrain implementation, while the industry's manual core constrains substitution.

## Caveats
- a: O*NET describes an occupation rather than the employee mix of NAICS 621310 firms.
- a: The ambient-scribe evidence does not include chiropractors and measures selected administrative outcomes rather than total automatable labor.
- a: Existing practice-management automation must be excluded from the not-yet-automated opportunity, and its penetration is not publicly measured for the lens.
- rho: The AMA survey covers physicians, not doctors of chiropractic.
- rho: The ambient-scribe study was voluntary, short-duration, and concentrated in organized health systems.
- rho: Awareness or use of any AI application does not imply deep implementation across all exposed workflows.
- e: No public source measures the share of the frozen LMM firm estimate that satisfies every lens condition.
- e: BLS reports 35% of chiropractor jobs as self-employed, but jobs are not firms and the statistic is not specific to the LMM band.
- e: The dataset's LMM count depends on a broad provider-margin bridge rather than observed practice EBITDA.
- s5: BizBuySell supplies transaction and valuation benchmarks but no eligible-firm denominator or chiropractic-only transfer rate.
- s5: Its typical sold medical practice is far smaller than the target EBITDA band.
- s5: A nominal asset sale may not represent a transferable operating company if patient relationships depend on the departing chiropractor.
- q: Medicare's limited chiropractic benefit is not representative of commercial insurance, workers' compensation, or cash-pay revenue.
- q: Saved clinician time may not be monetized if physical treatment capacity or patient demand is binding.
- q: Public evidence does not quantify the lens's payer or cash-pay mix.
- d5: The BLS projection measures an occupation, not industry service quantity or real revenue.
- d5: Employment can diverge from demand when clinician productivity, hours, ownership, or staffing mix changes.
- d5: Chiropractic utilization is sensitive to coverage design and patient willingness to pay.
- o: Operator need is distinct from the number of support staff or clinicians required after automation.
- o: State scope-of-practice rules vary even though licensure is universal.
- o: Some wellness advice and monitoring can migrate to self-service channels even while manual treatment remains human-delivered.

## Sources
- **S1** — [2022 NAICS Manual: 621310 Offices of Chiropractors](https://www.census.gov/naics/reference_files_tools/2022_NAICS_Manual.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-22): Defines 621310 as independent D.C. practices providing diagnostic and therapeutic neuromusculoskeletal treatment through spinal and extremity manipulation in private or group offices or facilities of others.
- **S2** — [O*NET OnLine: Chiropractors (29-1011.00)](https://www.onetonline.org/link/details/29-1011.00) (accessed 2026-07-22): Provides the chiropractor task and work-context map, including manual adjustments, histories, case records, treatment advice, diagnosis, imaging interpretation, referral, scheduling, and constant contact with others.
- **S3** — [U.S. BLS Occupational Outlook Handbook: Chiropractors](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/healthcare/chiropractors.htm) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports 57,200 chiropractor jobs in 2024, 35% self-employment, universal state licensure, 10% projected employment growth from 2024 to 2034, about 2,800 annual openings, and demand drivers including aging and interest in nonsurgical pain care.
- **S4** — [American Medical Association: 2026 Physician AI Survey](https://www.ama-assn.org/system/files/physician-ai-sentiment-report.pdf/) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports U.S. physician AI use, including 72% incorporating at least one surveyed use case in 2026, and identifies privacy, safety, efficacy, liability, training, and oversight as adoption considerations.
- **S5** — [JAMA Network Open: Use of Ambient AI Scribes to Reduce Administrative Burden and Professional Burnout](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2839542) (accessed 2026-07-22): A 2024 pre/post quality-improvement study of 263 ambulatory clinicians across six U.S. health systems found lower documentation burden and an average-equivalent 10.8 minutes saved per workday after 30 days of ambient-scribe use, with voluntary participation and no chiropractic-specific sample.
- **S6** — [CMS Medicare Coverage Database: Billing and Coding—Chiropractic Services (A56273)](https://www.cms.gov/medicare-coverage-database/view/article.aspx?Cntrctr=151&CntrctrSelected=151%2A1&ContrVer=1&DocType=Active%7CFuture&articleId=56273) (accessed 2026-07-22): States that Medicare's chiropractic benefit is limited to manual manipulation for subluxation, requires detailed records and claims, and pays covered chiropractor services under the Physician Fee Schedule.
- **S7** — [BizBuySell: Medical Practice Business Valuation Benchmarks](https://www.bizbuysell.com/learning-center/valuation-benchmarks/medical-practice/) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports transaction and valuation benchmarks for sold medical practices from 2021 through 2025 and explicitly identifies chiropractors among included specialties, demonstrating a transfer channel while not providing a chiropractic-specific probability or eligible-firm denominator.
