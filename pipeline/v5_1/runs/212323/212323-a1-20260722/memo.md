# 212323 — Kaolin, Clay, and Ceramic and Refractory Minerals Mining

**Driver:** Diversified, recurring physical demand for clay minerals across fillers, ceramics, absorbents, construction materials, drilling, and refractories.
**Weakness:** The required outsourced-service target population and its transferability cannot be established from available evidence.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market firms, if any, providing repeat outsourced mine-site development, extraction, or basic beneficiation of kaolin, ball clay, common clay, bentonite, fuller's earth, fire clay, and ceramic or refractory minerals for external mineral owners or industrial customers.
- Excluded: Mine owners selling their own mineral output as a commodity or proprietary product, captive operations, mineral-rights holding vehicles, post-beneficiation calcining or manufacturing, support activities classified outside 212323, and one-off earthmoving without recurring customer programs.
- Customer and revenue model: Multi-period B2B operating or toll-beneficiation arrangements priced by ton, campaign, capacity, or fixed operating fee, with repeat extraction, washing, screening, grinding, sizing, quality-control, and load-out obligations.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed to repeat outsourced extraction and beneficiation because the NAICS is primarily mineral-owning product production, while the fixed lens requires an outsourced service. Public sources did not establish a defensible LMM population for that subset.

## Executive view
The clay-mining category has stable physical demand and some AI-addressable planning and administrative work, but public evidence does not establish an LMM population of outsourced operators inside 212323. The dominant model is mineral production, not recurring service.

## How AI changes the work
AI can improve geological interpretation, dispatch, maintenance, quality reporting, permitting support, scheduling, and administrative workflows; excavation, hauling, beneficiation, sampling, and compliance still require field operators and equipment.

## Value capture
Contract operators could retain gains during fixed-fee or per-ton pricing periods, although open-book terms, escalation formulas, renewals, and concentrated customers would share much of the benefit.

## Firm availability
The frozen LMM count is explicitly missing, and USGS company counts do not distinguish service operators from mineral owners or establish EBITDA. Eligibility and transfer probability therefore remain missing.

## Demand durability
USGS data show broadly stable total clay volumes and modest kaolin growth, supported by diverse industrial and construction uses. That supports mineral demand but does not prove growth in outsourced mine operation.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are nonexistence of a scalable target pool, misclassification into mining-support NAICS, reserve depletion, permitting and reclamation liabilities, construction cyclicality, customer concentration, freight costs, mineral substitution, and captive customer operation.

## Caveats
- a: The BLS occupation mix covers NAICS 212, not 212323 or a verified contract-operator subset.
- a: ILO exposure measures technical task potential rather than current unautomated work.
- a: The frozen labor ratio is measured at ancestor 21232 and may not match the narrowed lens.
- rho: No 212323-specific AI implementation study was found.
- rho: Implementation varies materially with mine scale, connectivity, fleet age, and plant instrumentation.
- e: Industry counts in USGS cover clay and shale companies broadly, not the screened service model or LMM band.
- e: Contract support operations may be classified in NAICS 213115 rather than 212323.
- s5: Mine asset sales are not necessarily transfers of eligible operating firms.
- s5: Large mineral-company divestitures do not proxy owner succession in an unidentified LMM population.
- q: No representative public contract-term dataset for 212323 outsourced operators was found.
- q: Mineral-product sale pricing should not be confused with service billing under the narrowed lens.
- d5: USGS clay totals include mineral categories and operators outside the exact service lens.
- d5: Product tonnage is not a direct measure of outsourced operating-service demand.
- d5: Quality differences make simple tonnage an imperfect constant-quality measure.
- o: The estimate separates AI displacement from conventional autonomous equipment.
- o: Loss through customer insourcing is more plausible than software-only replacement.
