# 923110 — Administration of Education Programs

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `923110-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** no base (evidence-first) · A — · L — · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness STRESS_TEST_ONLY · action EVIDENCE_FIRST

**Driver:** Information-intensive grant, policy, reporting, and data workflows create substantial AI-assistance potential inside public agencies.
**Weakness:** The industry is government-only, so no privately owned LMM operator can satisfy the fixed acquisition lens.

## Business-model lens
- Included: No operating establishment in the defined NAICS population qualifies. The fixed lens would require a privately transferable U.S. lower-middle-market firm providing recurring outsourced education-program administration to external customers, while NAICS 923110 is expressly limited to government establishments.
- Excluded: Federal, state, and local education departments and agencies; school districts and instructional institutions; private education consultants, software vendors, grant administrators classified elsewhere; captive units, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Establishments administer public education funds, policy, intergovernmental activity, statistics, data collection, grants, monitoring, and centralized programs under statutory authority and public appropriations, not commercial recurring-service revenue from external customers.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
AI can materially assist the document, data, grant, reporting, and service workflows of education-program administration, but this NAICS code is structurally outside the roll-up lens. Census defines it as government establishments, the dataset supplies neither a receipts-based labor share nor an LMM firm count, and there is no privately transferable operator population.

## How AI changes the work
Language and analytic systems can draft and summarize materials, classify submissions, check completeness, answer routine questions, extract evidence, reconcile reports, and prioritize reviews. Accountable officials must still interpret law and policy, allocate funds, supervise programs, resolve exceptions, protect sensitive data, and make consequential decisions.

## Value capture
Operational benefits can improve service, reduce backlogs, or free public resources, but they do not become commercial margin retained by an in-industry owner. Appropriations, oversight, procurement, and public-budget processes determine where savings go.

## Firm availability
There are no eligible firms under the published industry definition. Private software, consulting, data, and administrative-service vendors may be acquisition targets in their own NAICS industries, but importing them here would violate the frozen population.

## Demand durability
Education administration remains necessary despite a modest projected enrollment decline because grants, policy, intergovernmental coordination, compliance, statistics, and public accountability persist. The volume and organizational location of the work remain exposed to legislative change, program consolidation, and shifts between federal and state authority.

## Risks and uncertainty
Task exposure is inferred from functions rather than measured time use, and implementation evidence is broader federal-government evidence. Privacy, security, legacy systems, procurement, records law, accessibility, and rights-impacting decisions can slow adoption. The decisive issue is not technology uncertainty but the absence of a transferable commercial firm population.

## Caveats
- a: No six-digit occupation-by-task dataset was found for this government-only industry.
- a: The estimate concerns current not-yet-automated tasks, but agencies differ substantially in legacy-system maturity.
- a: Exposure does not imply authority to automate final eligibility, funding, or enforcement decisions.
- rho: GAO's adoption evidence covers selected federal agencies, not state and local education agencies specifically.
- rho: State procurement, civil-service, records, and student-data rules can differ from federal requirements.
- rho: Implementation may augment staff or absorb backlogs rather than reduce payroll.
- e: Private contractors may perform related work but are classified in other industries and are outside this record.
- e: The injected LMM firm-count input is missing because government establishments are outside the relevant business datasets; it must not be replaced with a private-contractor estimate.
- s5: Zero was not imputed because there is no eligible-firm population on which to define a transfer probability.
- s5: Government restructurings and outsourcing contracts are not qualifying control transfers.
- q: Budget savings may be redeployed, returned, or reduce future appropriations, but that is not commercial benefit retention by a transferable operator.
- q: Grant recipients and vendors are counterparties to agencies, not the lens operators in this NAICS code.
- d5: Enrollment is not a direct measure of administration output and the NCES projection vintage includes pandemic-era changes.
- d5: Federal, state, and local workloads may move in opposite directions when authority is devolved or programs are consolidated.
- d5: The range describes constant-quality administrative demand, not appropriations or employment.
- o: Agency consolidation can change which public operator performs the work without eliminating the operator requirement.
- o: Outsourcing support tasks does not remove the government's accountable decision authority.
