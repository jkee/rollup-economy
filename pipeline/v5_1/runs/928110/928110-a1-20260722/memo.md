**Driver:** Defense has strong strategic pressure and dedicated infrastructure to deploy AI across information-intensive and enterprise workflows.
**Weakness:** Government Armed Forces establishments cannot supply the private control-transfer and commercial-retention opportunity required by the fixed lens.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. government establishments of the Armed Forces and National Guard primarily engaged in national security, including military operations, military police, military courts, military air-traffic control, and military training schools other than service academies, assessed under the fixed external-service lower-middle-market lens.
- Excluded: Private defense contractors, weapons and equipment manufacturers, private research and development firms, civilian security services, international affairs, civilian air-traffic control, military service academies, passive financing vehicles, captive private units, and shells.
- Customer and revenue model: The in-code establishments execute sovereign national-security missions funded through congressional appropriations rather than recurring commercial fees paid by external customers. Contractors sell goods and services to defense agencies but remain classified in their private supplier industries, not NAICS 928110.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
National security contains major AI opportunities across intelligence, planning, logistics, sustainment, investigations, readiness, policy, and enterprise administration. It is nevertheless structurally outside the roll-up lens: NAICS 928110 consists of government Armed Forces establishments, with no commercial revenue denominator, private-firm population, or qualifying control-transfer market.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate surveillance and intelligence analysis, fuse operational data, support commanders, optimize logistics and maintenance, generate and compare plans, personalize training, streamline investigations, and automate enterprise document workflows. Combat, physical operations, command judgment, lawful use of force, maintenance in contested settings, and final accountability remain human-led.

## Value capture
Benefits appear as readiness, speed, decision quality, resilience, reduced administrative burden, lower public cost, or greater mission capacity. They accrue through sovereign outcomes and appropriations rather than through an in-code operator retaining margin after customer repricing.

## Firm availability
No eligible in-code firms exist under the official government-only definition. Private defense contractors may be attractive or transferable businesses, but they sit in other supplier industries; importing them here would violate the frozen industry lens. Thus missing n reflects structural scope, not merely sparse data.

## Demand durability
National-security demand is durable and planned real defense costs rise modestly over the relevant period, but budget is an imperfect proxy for constant-quality output. Force structure, compensation, acquisition costs, geopolitical threats, allied burden sharing, automation, and classified mission changes can move spending and service quantity differently.

## Risks and uncertainty
AI implementation faces classified and fragmented data, adversarial attack, model manipulation, cyber compromise, reliability, testing, interoperability, procurement delay, talent gaps, autonomous-weapons governance, legal constraints, and high consequences of error. For the screen, the decisive limitation is the absence of any private in-code acquisition population.

## Caveats
- a: The frozen labor-share input l is missing because the government code has no comparable receipts denominator.
- a: Public descriptions emphasize priority use cases and do not provide representative time allocation across the Armed Forces.
- a: AI may increase decision quality, tempo, and mission capacity without reducing personnel hours proportionally.
- rho: Public sources cannot reveal the full classified deployment portfolio or performance evidence.
- rho: Pilot, strategy, and investment announcements do not establish scaled use or net labor savings.
- rho: Adversarial environments impose security and assurance requirements beyond ordinary enterprise software.
- e: The frozen n input is missing, consistent with the absence of SUSB private-firm coverage.
- e: A large private defense industrial base exists, but its firms are classified under manufacturing, professional services, facilities support, technology, or other supplier codes.
- e: Government-owned or contractor-operated facilities require entity-level review but do not create an ordinary private-firm population inside the stated industry boundary.
- s5: A numeric zero would imply an observed probability for an eligible denominator that does not exist.
- s5: Mergers among defense contractors occur outside this government industry and cannot be imported into s5.
- q: Mission effectiveness, readiness, deterrence, and avoided loss are valuable but do not map to the commercial-retention primitive.
- q: Defense contractors may retain innovation benefits under procurement contracts, but they belong to other NAICS industries.
- d5: Budget cost is not a direct measure of constant-quality national-security service quantity.
- d5: The cited FYDP predates later budgets, geopolitical changes, reconciliation funding, and supplemental appropriations.
- d5: Classified missions and readiness quality are not observable in public spending aggregates.
- o: This is a structural judgment rather than an observed operator-displacement rate.
- o: Force-structure consolidation may reduce establishments without eliminating the accountable national-security operator.
- o: Contracting and autonomy change task allocation but do not eliminate sovereign responsibility.
