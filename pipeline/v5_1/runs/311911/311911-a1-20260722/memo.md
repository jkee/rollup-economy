# 311911 — Roasted Nuts and Peanut Butter Manufacturing

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `311911-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** STRUCTURAL_OUT · A 5.51 · L 0.44 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → STRUCTURAL_OUT · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Durable physical outsourced production combined with commercially proven AI inspection and quality-monitoring tools.
**Weakness:** A material-heavy, competitively bid business gives large customers multiple paths to capture productivity gains at renewal.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market U.S. manufacturers in NAICS 311911 that repeatedly roast, season, process, blend, or package nuts and nut butters for external customers under private-label, contract-manufacturing, foodservice, institutional, or commercial-ingredient arrangements.
- Excluded: Branded-only consumer-product manufacturers without recurring outsourced customer work; captive plants, shells, and non-control financing vehicles; candy-coated nut manufacturing classified elsewhere; and oilseed-butter manufacturing classified outside NAICS 311911.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat B2B production runs and supply programs for retailers, food brands, distributors, restaurants, institutions, and ingredient users, commonly priced through bids, product specifications, purchase orders, and fixed-price commitments rather than time-and-materials billing.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed to private-label, contract-manufacturing, foodservice, institutional, and commercial-ingredient suppliers because NAICS 311911 also contains branded-only consumer manufacturers whose economics are driven by brand ownership rather than an outsourced service relationship.

## Executive view
The coherent opportunity is in repeat private-label, contract-manufacturing, foodservice, and ingredient programs, not branded-only nut products. AI can improve inspection, line visibility, planning, inventory, documentation, and administration, but the industry remains physical, material-heavy, regulated, and commercially exposed to powerful customers. The opportunity is therefore operationally real but bounded: modest labor exposure, partial five-year implementation, meaningful repricing leakage, and durable operator-required demand.

## How AI changes the work
Computer vision and deep learning can detect defects and contaminants, monitor quality continuously, and reduce manual inspection; planning, inventory, customer service, compliance documentation, and other administrative tasks add a second opportunity pool. Direct roasting, grinding, cleaning, material movement, sanitation, maintenance, and packaging-line intervention remain largely physical, while FDA preventive controls and validation limit rapid unattended deployment.

## Value capture
Benefits can accrue through labor avoidance, more consistent quality, lower rejects, better yield, faster release, and improved traceability. Private-label bidding, contract renewals, concentrated buyers, and resistance to price increases mean customers and competitors are likely to absorb a substantial share over five years; specialized, validated, small-batch, or high-service programs should retain more than commodity tenders.

## Firm availability
Private-label and contract models are visibly present, but the code also includes branded manufacturers, so firm eligibility requires channel-level verification. Broad ownership data point to an aging manufacturing base and prospective transfers, yet stated intentions, family succession, plant specialization, working capital, and buyer capacity make completed qualifying control deals materially less frequent.

## Demand durability
Nut roasting and nut-butter production remain physical services with strong operator need. Peanut butter has long-run consumption support, but recent USDA forecasts show near-term softness in peanut food use and snacks; the five-year base is consequently near flat in real quantity, with product mix and customer wins likely more important than category growth.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are the eligible-firm share, actual installed automation baseline, brownfield implementation economics, and commercial retention by channel. Food-safety failures, allergen incidents, aflatoxin exposure, commodity-price volatility, customer concentration, competitive bids, retailer consolidation, private-label program losses, and volume shifts can overwhelm labor savings. Public-company and broad-sector proxies may not reflect LMM specialists.

## Caveats
- a: BLS data are for NAICS 311900 and include other food manufacturing activities.
- a: Vendor capabilities demonstrate feasibility, not current penetration or net labor removal.
- a: The range excludes tasks already automated at the current baseline.
- rho: The aflatoxin study addresses one sorting application and one peanut type rather than the full task basket.
- rho: Vendor materials may emphasize successful use cases.
- rho: Implementation depends on plant layout, throughput, product mix, and existing controls.
- e: Public examples establish that eligible models exist but do not measure their prevalence.
- e: Many firms combine branded and outsourced revenue, making eligibility sensitive to the revenue-share threshold.
- e: The frozen LMM firm-count estimate may include establishments or firms with economics unlike the screened population.
- s5: No source directly measures five-year control-transfer incidence for NAICS 311911.
- s5: Survey intentions are not completed transactions.
- s5: Manufacturing-sector ownership statistics include much larger and structurally different businesses.
- q: One public company's commercial structure may not represent smaller specialist processors.
- q: Retention differs between private-label tenders, contract manufacturing, foodservice, and customized ingredient programs.
- q: This estimate does not assume demand growth or implementation success, which belong in other primitives.
- d5: Peanut utilization is not the entire roasted-nut and nut-butter service basket.
- d5: USDA data include branded and vertically integrated production outside the lens.
- d5: Constant-price demand can diverge from reported shipment value because nut commodity prices are volatile.
- o: Operator requirement does not ensure that the same LMM suppliers retain the volume.
- o: Customer insourcing and supplier consolidation could shift demand away from the lens without eliminating consumption.
- o: The estimate assumes no discontinuous substitute for physical nut processing.
