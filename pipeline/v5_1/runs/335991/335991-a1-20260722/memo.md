# 335991 — Carbon and Graphite Product Manufacturing

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `335991-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** STRUCTURAL_OUT · A 5.57 · L 0.71 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → LOW_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Qualified, repeat engineered production serving diversified electrical, steel, energy-storage, and industrial applications.
**Weakness:** Most of the NAICS category is product manufacturing rather than outsourced service, sharply limiting eligible acquisition supply.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent U.S. firms performing repeat outsourced production of engineered carbon, graphite, and metal-graphite products, including brushes, electrodes, fibers, and machined or formed components for external customers.
- Excluded: Captive plants, raw graphite mining, graphite gasket manufacturing, commodity resale, one-off development shops without repeat production, and branded product businesses that do not provide an outsourced production relationship.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat business-to-business programs based on drawings, specifications, qualification, machining or forming capability, and recurring purchase orders; customers include steelmakers, battery and energy users, motor and electrical OEMs, refractory users, and industrial equipment manufacturers.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed to repeat outsourced engineered production because 335991 mixes electrodes, fibers, brushes, and other products, while much of the category is proprietary or commodity product manufacturing rather than the default outsourced-service model.

## Executive view
The attractive part of 335991 is a narrow engineered-component and recurring-production subset, not the full product industry. Qualification and specialized process know-how can create durable relationships, but only a small share of the estimated firm universe likely fits the outsourced-service lens.

## How AI changes the work
AI should improve quoting, planning, documentation, purchasing, quality analysis, and customer service; the physical and safety-critical production core remains labor- and equipment-dependent.

## Value capture
Operators can retain value through faster quotes, lower administrative load, better yield analysis, and procurement scale, especially under fixed-price programs with slow repricing; competitive rebids and material-index clauses reduce retention.

## Firm availability
The frozen dataset estimates 57 LMM-band firms, but product manufacturers, captive operations, and integrated suppliers substantially reduce the eligible pool. Succession is plausible but supported only by economy-wide owner-age evidence.

## Demand durability
Energy storage, EAF steelmaking, electrical equipment, refractory, and advanced-material uses support demand, while recent natural-graphite consumption weakness and mature brush or electrode segments justify a wide range.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are an overly small eligible universe, product-family incompatibility, customer qualification barriers, energy and feedstock volatility, cyclicality in steel and industrial markets, and growth accruing to large integrated battery suppliers.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation mix is for NAICS 335, not 335991.
- a: ILO exposure measures technical potential rather than current unautomated opportunity.
- rho: No five-year adoption study specific to carbon and graphite plants was found.
- rho: Implementation varies sharply with production digitization and product criticality.
- e: The frozen 57-firm estimate is not a verified company count.
- e: NAICS assignment does not reveal whether revenue is outsourced, captive, proprietary, or recurring.
- s5: The Census statistic covers responding owners of employer businesses economy-wide.
- s5: Multiple owners can represent one firm and older ownership need not result in an external control transfer.
- q: No public billing-structure distribution exists for the screened lens.
- q: Material-price changes can obscure retention of labor-driven gains.
- d5: Raw natural-graphite consumption is not equivalent to finished 335991 output.
- d5: Synthetic graphite and carbon-fiber dynamics differ from natural graphite.
- d5: End-market growth may be captured by large integrated producers outside the lens.
- o: The estimate separates generative-AI displacement from conventional industrial automation.
- o: Some low-complexity design and procurement work could become more self-served.
