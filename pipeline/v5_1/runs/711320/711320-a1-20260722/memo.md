# 711320 — Promoters of Performing Arts, Sports, and Similar Events without Facilities

**Driver:** Booking, marketing, ticketing, sponsorship, contracting, coordination, and settlement create a concentrated set of AI-addressable information workflows around durable demand for live events.
**Weakness:** Promoter economics are volatile and relationship-driven, with artists and venues capturing much of the value and single-event structures weakening eligibility and transferability.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market firms that repeatedly organize, promote, book, produce, or manage live performing-arts, sports, concert, festival, fair, and similar events in third-party facilities for artists, rights holders, sponsors, venue partners, and event audiences.
- Excluded: Single-event shells, passive financing vehicles, captive internal promotion units, operators of the facilities themselves, performing-arts producers and sports teams classified elsewhere, and firms whose activity is non-live media or convention and trade-show organization.
- Customer and revenue model: Promoters contract with artists, booking agents, rights holders, venue operators, sponsors, and production vendors. Revenue is commonly ticket-led, with possible service charges, sponsorship, concessions or merchandise participation, camping and other event fees; costs include artist guarantees or profit shares, venue rental, marketing, and production services.
- Deviation from default lens: The code includes both repeat outsourced promotion/management businesses and event-specific entities taking proprietary ticket risk. The lens retains repeat operators with external artist, rights-holder, sponsor, venue, or audience relationships and excludes single-event shells because they are not durable recurring service firms.

## Executive view
The eligible core is a repeat promoter or booking organization that coordinates live events in third-party venues and can survive beyond a single event or founder. Its work is more information-intensive than facility operation, creating meaningful AI exposure, but ticket-risk economics, artist bargaining power, and relationship dependence constrain implementation, retention, and transferability.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate artist and market research, campaign and creative variants, sponsor proposals, fan segmentation, customer-service triage, schedule coordination, contract extraction, production briefs, reconciliation, and post-event reporting. Human judgment remains central in talent relationships, event selection, negotiations, brand control, live production, crisis response, and final financial approvals.

## Value capture
Savings in promoter overhead can improve event contribution margin without an immediate ticket-price change. Over time, however, competition for artists and rights, profit-sharing structures, stronger guarantees, venue negotiations, sponsor pricing, and consumer scrutiny can pass part of the benefit away. The ticket-led model is not a simple fixed-fee service model.

## Firm availability
Most scaled firms likely operate repeat calendars or tours and therefore fit the recurring-service lens, but single-event shells and founder-centric portfolios must be excluded. Broad employer-owner surveys indicate meaningful five-year transfer intent, while promoter contracts, guarantees, key relationships, and event liabilities make completed control transfers less certain.

## Demand durability
Official projections imply modest real growth for the broader promoter-and-agent market, and current large-company advance sales support continued appetite for live events. Demand is still discretionary, seasonal, and volatile; local venue capacity, artist supply, cancellations, economic slowdowns, and rising guarantees can reduce promoter quantity even when headline ticket revenue grows.

## Risks and uncertainty
Six-digit evidence is weak for occupation mix, recurring-service eligibility, independent-promoter contracts, and completed transactions. A bad target would be a collection of event-specific entities with founder-owned relationships, concentrated artist or festival exposure, large guarantees, weak data rights, and no repeat operational team. AI may raise campaign volume or service quality rather than reduce labor, and self-service may erode simpler promotion assignments.

## Caveats
- a: The available occupation mix combines promoters with and without facilities.
- a: Economy-wide AI task use identifies deployed use cases, not the full technically exposed share.
- a: Freelancers and outsourced production labor may not appear consistently in employer payroll data.
- rho: BTOS is not industry-specific and its AI question was broadened in late 2025.
- rho: Use of AI is not equivalent to reliable end-to-end workflow implementation.
- rho: Seasonal and project-based staffing may turn productivity into greater event capacity rather than lower headcount.
- e: No source measures eligibility under the exact recurring-service and EBITDA lens.
- e: Ticket-risk promotion can look like a service relationship while economically resembling principal investing in events.
- e: Classification and financial statements may be distorted by event-specific subsidiaries and gross ticket accounting.
- s5: Gallup measures intentions rather than completed deals and is cross-industry.
- s5: Live Nation is a global strategic buyer and is not representative of LMM transaction frequency.
- s5: Acquisitions of event brands or assets without operating control should be excluded.
- q: No source directly observes pass-through of AI-enabled labor savings.
- q: Retention differs between fixed guarantees, profit shares, fee-for-service promotion, and proprietary event brands.
- q: The public-company evidence combines promotion with venue, ticketing, sponsorship, and international operations.
- d5: BLS output is broader than 711320 and the projection horizon is ten years.
- d5: Live Nation is global, vertically integrated, and much larger than the screened firms.
- d5: Gross ticket revenue can move differently from constant-quality promoter service quantity.
- o: The share of events that can be self-promoted is not directly measured.
- o: Artist and venue sophistication varies widely, as does access to ticketing and production platforms.
- o: Accountability may migrate to venues, managers, or software-enabled agencies rather than disappear.
