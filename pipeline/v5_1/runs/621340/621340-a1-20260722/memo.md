# 621340 — Offices of Physical, Occupational and Speech Therapists, and Audiologists

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `621340-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** PRIORITY · A 7.87 · L 4.10 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Repeated reimbursed visits create scalable clinic operations with addressable documentation and administration around a service that remains clinically necessary.
**Weakness:** Hands-on clinician capacity and payer-controlled reimbursement limit both the size and retention of AI-enabled productivity gains.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market independent and group outpatient practices providing recurring or repeat physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech-language pathology, audiology, and closely related rehabilitation services to external patients and payors.
- Excluded: Hospital departments, skilled nursing and home-health providers classified elsewhere, captive employer clinics, software-only exercise products, pure hearing-aid retail, shell entities, and solo practices without transferable operations.
- Customer and revenue model: Patients receive evaluated plans and repeated treatment visits, commonly following referral or medical need; practices earn per-visit or per-service reimbursement from commercial insurers, Medicare, workers' compensation, and patients, with some audiology device revenue and contract services.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Outpatient rehabilitation practices have valuable administrative and documentation workflows for AI, but their principal service remains embodied, licensed, and locally delivered. Strong demographic demand and demonstrated acquisitions support the roll-up logic, while payer control, therapist scarcity, referral concentration, and seller retention constrain benefit capture and transferability.

## How AI changes the work
AI can draft structured notes, summarize charts, prepare home programs, code claims, check eligibility, support authorization, schedule visits, route messages, and analyze routine progress data. Clinicians and assistants remain essential for evaluation, physical handling, guarded exercise, swallowing and communication assessment, device fitting, patient motivation, progression, and safety decisions.

## Value capture
Per-visit reimbursement allows practices to retain some savings from faster documentation and centralized administration. Government fee changes, commercial negotiation, utilization review, audit requirements, clinician compensation, and the hands-on capacity bottleneck transfer or consume part of the implemented benefit.

## Firm availability
Independent clinics and multi-site groups are common within the code, and exact-sector acquisitions demonstrate buyer demand. Referral relationships, payer credentials, local brands, clinician teams, and seller rollover require careful diligence because many transactions depend on continuing founder involvement.

## Demand durability
All principal occupations have favorable national projections, supported by aging, chronic disease, surgery recovery, developmental needs, and hearing loss. Workforce shortages, reimbursement pressure, authorization, home programs, and migration to other care settings constrain realized practice growth.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main risks are clinical-documentation errors, protected-data exposure, payer denials and fee cuts, therapist shortages, concentrated referrals, seller dependence, mixed discipline economics, weak adoption evidence outside physical therapy, and self-service substitution for low-acuity care. Exact workflow hours and closed-deal denominators are unavailable.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS occupation shares do not reveal task hours or current automation penetration.
- a: The code combines professions with different hands-on intensity, documentation, devices, and reimbursement.
- a: Self-employed practitioners are excluded from OEWS, while the screened population is limited to transferable firms.
- rho: APTA guidance shows technology availability and governance needs, not representative adoption or labor removal.
- rho: AMA evidence covers physicians and health systems rather than independent rehabilitation practices.
- rho: Evidence is strongest for physical-therapy documentation and weaker for the other professions in the code.
- e: No source measures eligibility inside the frozen lower-middle-market firm count.
- e: The supplied count is margin-bridged using a broad healthcare-facilities margin that may misclassify practices.
- e: A qualifying revenue base may still depend on one clinician-owner or a concentrated referral source.
- s5: Gallup measures intentions across industries rather than completed rehabilitation-practice transfers.
- s5: One public consolidator's strategy may not represent the full buyer universe or screened practice sizes.
- s5: Transactions with substantial seller rollover or management-service structures may not qualify as straightforward control transfers.
- q: The evidence is concentrated in physical therapy and one large public operator.
- q: No source directly measures payer or clinician sharing of AI-enabled productivity.
- q: Audiology device margins and speech or occupational therapy reimbursement may respond differently from physical-therapy visits.
- d5: Occupational employment projections are not direct measures of constant-quality service demand.
- d5: National occupation growth includes hospitals, schools, home health, and other settings outside this code.
- d5: The code's discipline mix and payer exposure can shift materially over the horizon.
- o: No source directly measures the year-five operator-required quantity share.
- o: Routine exercise progression and low-acuity speech or hearing support may migrate to self-service faster than expected.
- o: Operator necessity does not ensure that today's independent-practice model retains the service rather than hospitals, home health, or integrated networks.
