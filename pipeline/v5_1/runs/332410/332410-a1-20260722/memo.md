# 332410 — Power Boiler and Heat Exchanger Manufacturing

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `332410-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 6.45 · L 1.24 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Durable need for accountable physical fabrication combined with AI-assisted engineering and operating workflows.
**Weakness:** AI addresses only a minority of labor while safety, certification, legacy-data, and project-cycle constraints slow implementation.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market independent manufacturers supplying custom or repeat power boilers, heat exchangers, condensers, related components, contract fabrication, installation, repair, and service to external industrial customers.
- Excluded: Captive internal shops, shells, non-control financing vehicles, firms outside the EBITDA band, pure distributors, and installers that do not manufacture the equipment.
- Customer and revenue model: Primarily engineered-to-order or configured equipment and components sold under project or purchase-order contracts, with repeat parts, retrofit, maintenance, repair, and installation revenue for industrial, energy, defense, data-center, and process customers.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
This is a physical, certification- and know-how-intensive manufacturing niche with real but bounded AI opportunity. The most credible gains are in engineering support, estimating, planning, procurement, documentation, and inspection triage, while physical fabrication and accountable quality release remain operator-led.

## How AI changes the work
AI should shorten quote preparation, retrieve prior designs and procedures, assist scheduling and purchasing, draft quality documentation, and flag inspection anomalies. Heterogeneous legacy data, safety-critical reliability, customer approvals, and craft execution limit substitution in welding, forming, assembly, testing, and field work.

## Value capture
Project and purchase-order pricing can preserve savings through a contract cycle, and qualifications plus lead-time performance create differentiation. Repeated competitive bidding and sophisticated industrial buyers should capture part of the gain at renewal or rebid.

## Firm availability
The frozen estimate implies a meaningful LMM population, but verified eligibility is lower because some operations are captive, strategically large, owner-dependent, or lack transferable technical depth. National employer-owner succession data suggest regular transfer opportunities, not assured completed sales.

## Demand durability
Physical heat-transfer and steam equipment, replacement parts, retrofit, and service remain necessary. Data-center, LNG, nuclear, carbon-capture, and infrastructure activity can offset softer traditional-energy markets, although project cycles and customer capital spending create volatility.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main evidence gaps are six-digit occupation exposure, LMM AI implementation outcomes, verified target ownership, and constant-dollar segment demand. Product liability, qualification failures, customer concentration, long-cycle estimates, input inflation, and skilled-trade constraints can overwhelm administrative savings.

## Caveats
- a: The BLS occupation evidence is for NAICS 332 rather than 332410.
- a: The estimate separates AI exposure from conventional robotics, although the boundary is imperfect in vision-assisted inspection and robotic welding.
- rho: The NIST roadmap and NAM survey cover manufacturing generally and are not 332410 adoption measurements.
- rho: Large manufacturers likely have better data and integration capacity than the target LMM firms.
- e: The frozen firm count is margin-bridged and not a verified list of acquisition targets.
- e: The Census questionnaire confirms product and service breadth but does not measure lens eligibility.
- s5: Gallup is cross-industry and measures plans rather than completed qualifying transfers.
- s5: Internal family transfers may not result in an externally acquirable control transaction.
- q: Revenue models vary between new equipment, contract manufacturing, parts, installation, and service.
- q: The public-company filing reflects a much larger and more diversified supplier than the lens firms.
- d5: Backlog is nominal and can reflect price, mix, acquisitions, and project timing rather than constant-quality quantity.
- d5: The proxy company operates globally and across adjacent equipment categories.
- o: Standardized components may shift to larger automated suppliers or imports.
- o: The estimate concerns operator requirement, not whether the same incumbent firm retains the work.
