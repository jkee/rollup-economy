## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market merchant wholesalers with repeat B2B accounts that source, authenticate or quality-control, finance, merchandise, and distribute finished jewelry, watches, clocks, precious or semiprecious stones, precious metals, costume jewelry, silverware, or jewelers' findings to independent retailers and other business buyers.
- Excluded: Retail jewelers, manufacturers, auction houses, pure brokers and exchanges that do not take title, investment funds and financing vehicles, captive brand distribution, shells, and precious-metal traders whose activity is primarily speculative balance-sheet exposure rather than repeat outsourced distribution service.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat product sales to independent jewelers, chains, designers, repair shops, online sellers, and specialty retailers; revenue is product resale spread, supported in stronger firms by sourcing access, credit, memo or consignment programs, authentication, assortment curation, compliance, merchandising, and fulfillment.
- Deviation from default lens: The code is narrowed to repeat-account merchant distributors with transferable sourcing, credit, quality-control, and retailer-service capabilities. Speculative bullion traders and pure intermediaries are excluded because their economics are not coherent with recurring outsourced distribution.

## Executive view
Jewelry and precious-goods wholesalers offer software-exposed sales, catalog, inventory, and compliance workflows, but the frozen labor ratio is low and the code is exceptionally heterogeneous. A coherent target needs repeat retailer accounts, disciplined title-taking and inventory controls, transferable sourcing, credible authentication, and management beyond the principal.

## How AI changes the work
AI can enrich catalogs, match inventory to retailer demand, prepare quotes and campaigns, triage inquiries, assist screening and document review, and automate reports. Human accountability remains central for grading, provenance, high-value claims, AML judgment, secure handling, relationship selling, and bespoke assortment decisions.

## Value capture
Savings are not normally contractually passed through, but online transparency and standardized certifications intensify price competition. Durable retention rests on trusted supply, exclusivity, credit and memo terms, curation, speed, authenticity, and low error rates rather than generic back-office efficiency.

## Firm availability
The supplied count implies breadth, yet many candidates may be owner-personal, thinly controlled, speculative, concentrated, or really brokers or retailers. Succession can generate opportunities, but trusted relationships, working capital, inventory valuation, and personal guarantees complicate transfers.

## Demand durability
The category should persist as adornment, gifting, collecting, and value storage, but real volume is sensitive to precious-material prices and channel change. Recent U.S. gold-jewelry tonnage declined even as nominal value held up, and lab-grown diamonds simultaneously stimulate accessible demand and destabilize established economics.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are inventory authenticity and valuation, theft, provenance, AML, sanctions, supplier and retailer concentration, commodity prices, lab-grown substitution, direct sourcing, and reputational damage from inaccurate claims. The largest evidence gaps are firm-level task mix, normalized transferable EBITDA, and control-transfer frequency.

## Caveats
- a: No six-digit occupation-by-task dataset was located, and the mix differs sharply among bullion, diamonds, fashion jewelry, watches, and findings.
- a: The frozen compensation ratio uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and is harmonized to an IPS basis.
- rho: Digitization at the broad wholesale-sector level does not establish AI readiness for unique, high-value inventory.
- rho: Incorrect grading, treatment, origin, or laboratory-grown disclosures carry outsized reputational and legal costs.
- e: The NAICS definition combines fine jewelry, precious metals, loose stones, costume jewelry, watches, clocks, flatware, and findings.
- e: The frozen n of 1,339 is an ESTIMATE using size-class receipts and a general distributor margin bridge, not verified normalized EBITDA.
- s5: Owner age is only a succession-pressure proxy, not an observed sale probability.
- s5: High working-capital needs and personal guarantees can impede otherwise intended transfers.
- q: Metal and stone price movements can obscure labor-benefit retention.
- q: Pricing power differs radically between exclusive branded goods and transparent commodities.
- d5: Gold tonnage covers only part of this heterogeneous industry and may understate demand for lighter pieces or substitute materials.
- d5: Constant-quality measurement is difficult when carat, stone origin, branding, design, and lab-grown substitution change rapidly.
- o: The accountable operator may be a brand, marketplace, or vertically integrated retailer rather than an independent LMM wholesaler.
- o: Bullion and standardized certified stones are more digitally disintermediable than unique jewelry assortments or trust-intensive sourcing.
