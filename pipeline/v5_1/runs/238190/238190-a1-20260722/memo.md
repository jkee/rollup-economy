# 238190 — Other Foundation, Structure, and Building Exterior Contractors

**Base tier:** STRUCTURAL_OUT · A 6.06 · L 0.98 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → LOW_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Specification-heavy preconstruction and project administration offer focused AI leverage while safety-critical physical installation preserves operator-required demand.
**Weakness:** The residual NAICS is highly heterogeneous, so eligible-firm count, task mix, transaction supply, and demand cannot be observed cleanly for the narrowed business model.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market contractors installing and repairing exterior architectural-metal and structural-access systems for external customers, including metal curtain walls, fire escapes, metal balconies and stairways, and ornamental or decorative metalwork, with associated on-site welding.
- Excluded: Concrete-form erection and dismantling, undifferentiated mobile welding, activities classified to poured concrete, structural-steel/precast, framing, masonry, glazing, roofing, or siding codes, captive crews, shell firms, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Project-based specialty subcontracting for general contractors and building owners, usually against drawings, specifications, bids, and progress-payment schedules; repair, rehabilitation, and code-driven replacement provide repeat work across a customer and property base.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed for coherence to exterior architectural-metal and structural-access installation and repair. NAICS 238190 is a residual category that also includes concrete-form erection and broad on-site welding, whose equipment, customers, work cycles, and economics differ materially from curtain wall, fire escape, stair, balcony, and ornamental-metal contractors.

## Executive view
The coherent portion of this residual NAICS is a safety-critical, project-based architectural-metal installation business. AI can improve document-heavy preconstruction and project control, but physical field work remains dominant; demand is durable enough to persist yet exposed to a weak and uneven nonresidential construction cycle.

## How AI changes the work
AI is most useful in drawing and specification review, takeoff assistance, bid comparison, submittal and RFI drafting, scheduling, procurement, progress-photo organization, compliance records, and invoice matching. Human estimators, project managers, supervisors, welders, and installers remain responsible for scope gaps, dimensions, engineering interfaces, field conditions, joins, access, safety, and final quality.

## Value capture
Fixed-scope packages and awarded backlog can preserve savings initially. Durable retention is lower because work is repeatedly bid, general contractors exert purchasing power, quantities and schedules are scrutinized, and rivals can adopt similar tools; improved speed, risk control, and execution reliability matter alongside overhead reduction.

## Firm availability
Construction owners report meaningful exit intent, and firms with trained crews, backlog, certifications, bonding, and durable GC relationships can transfer. The supplied firm count covers a heterogeneous residual code, however, and owner-held estimating knowledge, project liabilities, customer concentration, and working-capital needs can make apparent firms ineligible or unsaleable.

## Demand durability
Buildings continue to require exterior assemblies, stairs, balconies, fire escapes, ornamental metal, maintenance, rehabilitation, and code compliance. The current nonresidential outlook is uneven and real growth is not assured; a few sectors may expand while offices, manufacturing, or financed development weaken.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are the activity mix hidden inside 238190, the estimated LMM count, nonresidential cyclicality, metal and tariff volatility, labor and union constraints, bonding and retainage, safety incidents, rework and warranty claims, and whether small contractors can integrate AI reliably into BIM and project systems. Most numeric evidence is broader than the narrowed lens.

## Caveats
- a: No six-digit occupation mix exists for the narrowed lens.
- a: Structural ironworkers span industries and structures beyond the selected architectural-metal activities.
- a: Observed Claude use is not the same as technical task substitutability.
- a: The frozen compensation ratio is LOW quality and uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts at ancestor 23819.
- rho: RICS respondents were global and only 10% came from the Americas.
- rho: The survey measures organizational adoption, not the share of an exposed opportunity successfully implemented.
- rho: Integration with BIM, estimating, ERP, and field systems may be harder for smaller firms than for survey respondents.
- e: The 71-firm LMM count is a margin-bridged ESTIMATE rather than observed firm-level EBITDA data.
- e: Public NAICS data do not split receipts or firm counts among the illustrative activities.
- e: Narrowing makes eligibility especially sensitive to classification error and multi-trade firms.
- s5: FMI surveyed nearly 300 broader engineering and construction leaders, not 238190 firms.
- s5: Gallup combines sales and other ownership transfers and reports intentions rather than completions.
- s5: Internal transfers can be qualifying control events, but gifts and minority or management changes without control are excluded.
- q: Public, commercial, and owner-direct jobs differ in transparency, retainage, and bargaining power.
- q: Material and fabrication costs may dominate the package and obscure administrative savings.
- q: The sources describe contracting structures rather than an observed five-year pass-through rate.
- d5: Neither source isolates the narrowed 238190 service basket.
- d5: AIA forecasts nominal spending only through 2027, shorter than the five-year horizon.
- d5: Demand differs sharply among data centers, offices, healthcare, hospitality, multifamily, and rehabilitation projects.
- o: This measures whether an accountable operator remains necessary, not whether individual steps become automated.
- o: Factory preassembly may reduce on-site hours without eliminating the specialty contractor.
- o: No five-year technology-substitution study specific to these contractor types was located.

## Sources
- **S1** — [2022 NAICS Definition: 238190 Other Foundation, Structure, and Building Exterior Contractors](https://www.census.gov/naics/?details=238190&input=238190&year=2022) (accessed 2026-07-22): Census defines the residual industry and identifies metal curtain-wall installation, concrete-form erection and dismantling, on-site welding, ornamental and decorative metalwork, and fire-escape installation as illustrative activities; it states that work may include new work, alterations, maintenance, and repairs.
- **S2** — [Ironworkers](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/construction-and-extraction/structural-iron-and-steel-workers.htm) (accessed 2026-07-22): BLS lists physical ironworker tasks and reports that foundation, structure, and building exterior contractors employ 50% of structural ironworkers; it projects ironworker employment to grow 4% from 2024 to 2034 and attributes demand to new structures and rehabilitation.
- **S3** — [Anthropic Economic Index report: Cadences](https://www.anthropic.com/research/economic-index-june-2026-report) (accessed 2026-07-22): Anthropic reports that physical occupation categories, including Construction & Extraction, are under-represented in both its survey and Claude sessions and that respondents frequently identify judgment and management as capabilities AI lacks.
- **S4** — [RICS Artificial Intelligence in Construction Report 2025](https://www.rics.org/news-insights/artificial-intelligence-in-construction-report) (accessed 2026-07-22): RICS reports more than 2,200 global responses, current AI adoption of 15%, planned investment of 31%, 78% of organizations at no implementation or pilot stage, and skills shortages named as a barrier by 46%.
- **S5** — [FMI Releases 2024 Study on Ownership Transfer and Management Succession](https://fmicorp.com/about/news/fmi-releases-2024-study-on-ownership-transfer-and-management-succession) (accessed 2026-07-22): FMI says its nearly 300-leader engineering and construction survey found 38% of owners planned to exit in three to five years and half of those lacked an ownership transition plan; it also reports employee and family transfer routes.
- **S6** — [Most Small-Business Owners Lack a Succession Plan](https://news.gallup.com/poll/657362/small-business-owners-lack-succession-plan.aspx) (accessed 2026-07-22): Gallup reports that 22% of employer-business owners planned to sell or transfer ownership within five years; for all working owners, 14% planned to sell, go public, or transfer and 8% planned to close.
- **S7** — [Governing rules and responsibilities](https://www.sba.gov/federal-contracting/contracting-guide/governing-rules-responsibilities) (accessed 2026-07-22): SBA guidance describes construction specifications, fixed-price contracts, lump-sum payment for smaller contracts, progress payments for larger fixed-price contracts and subcontracts, and a 25% self-performance requirement for specialty construction set-asides.
- **S8** — [AIA Consensus Construction Forecast — July 2026](https://www.aia.org/resource-center/july-2026-consensus-construction-forecast) (accessed 2026-07-22): AIA reports 2025 nonresidential building spending fell 2% to $846 billion, forecasts a 0.3% decline in 2026 and 3.0% growth in 2027, and shows that commercial strength is concentrated in data centers while other segments remain weak.
