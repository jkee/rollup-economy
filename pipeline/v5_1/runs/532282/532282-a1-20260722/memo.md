## Business-model lens
- Included: US businesses whose recurring external-customer service is renting physical prerecorded video media through staffed locations, kiosks, or delivery channels and whose normalized EBITDA falls within the specified lower-middle-market band.
- Excluded: Streaming subscriptions, transactional digital video, physical-media retail sales without a rental service, libraries, captive internal media operations, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Consumer rental fees, memberships, late or extension fees, and ancillary physical-media revenue tied to repeat use of an owned disc inventory.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
The surviving business is a shrinking physical-distribution niche. AI can improve customer contact and administration, but it cannot reverse format substitution, create a supply of screened firms where the injected estimate finds none, or remove the inventory and fulfillment core.

## How AI changes the work
Useful applications are catalog search, recommendation support, membership service, marketing copy, purchasing assistance, reconciliation, and exception triage. Physical stocking, disc handling, delivery, and kiosk maintenance remain operator tasks.

## Value capture
Consumer posted pricing allows initial retention of implemented savings, but low switching costs and streaming's convenience constrain durable capture. Cost savings may chiefly cushion volume erosion.

## Firm availability
The injected estimate identifies zero firms in the screened EBITDA band. Even if a surviving operator qualifies, forced closures and asset disposals are more likely than clean control transfers of durable operating businesses.

## Demand durability
FCC spending evidence and Netflix's exit show long-running substitution away from physical media. A residual niche can persist around unavailable titles, collectors, and connectivity gaps, but it is unlikely to preserve most current quantity.

## Risks and uncertainty
The decisive uncertainties are the number and economics of surviving operators, separation of rentals from physical sales in public data, survivor-specific transaction trends, and whether any ownership event transfers a going concern rather than liquidated assets.

- a: The occupation source covers NAICS 532 broadly rather than NAICS 532282.
- a: Surviving niche operators may use a different labor mix from the historical industry and from equipment-rental businesses.
- rho: No industry survey measures five-year implementation of the defined AI-exposed opportunity.
- rho: Business contraction can reduce both investment capacity and the payoff period for implementation.
- e: The injected firm count is zero, so this eligibility share does not imply any presently available firms in the screened band.
- e: The frozen firm-count estimate uses a margin bridge of 30 percent that may not fit distressed or mixed retail-rental operators.
- s5: Distress events are not qualifying transfers unless an operating business changes control.
- s5: The injected firm count is zero, making any conditional transfer probability practically secondary.
- q: The FCC evidence concerns aggregate video spending and does not measure benefit pass-through by physical-rental operators.
- q: Retention may be illusory if savings merely offset declining utilization rather than expand cash flow.
- d5: The FCC series combines physical-media purchases and rentals rather than isolating NAICS 532282 output.
- d5: Netflix's former mail-disc business differs operationally from stores and kiosks.
- d5: The estimate extrapolates beyond observed history and is highly sensitive to survivor selection.
- o: The estimate separates operator necessity from total demand decline imperfectly because streaming drives both.
- o: Rare-title licensing changes could materially alter the residual physical basket.
