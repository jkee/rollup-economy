# 561611 — Investigation and Personal Background Check Services

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `561611-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 5.95 · L 2.43 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Structured records work and repeated report workflows are amenable to AI while regulated accuracy and exception resolution preserve an accountable service role.
**Weakness:** The code mixes scalable recurring screening with episodic owner-dependent investigation, leaving the eligible firm share and transferable workflow mix unobserved.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market firms providing recurring or repeat outsourced employment, tenant, volunteer, vendor, or other personal background screening; continuous workforce monitoring; and repeat corporate, insurance, fraud, due-diligence, or legal investigation services to external organizations.
- Excluded: One-off domestic, infidelity, missing-person, or other personal assignments; predominantly episodic individual-client detective work; captive employer loss-prevention or internal investigation units; credit bureaus classified in NAICS 561450; shells; and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Employers, property managers, insurers, law firms, financial institutions, nonprofits, and other organizations buy reports, verifications, monitoring, or investigations under per-check, subscription, retainer, hourly, or project pricing, with recurring screening volumes and repeat enterprise relationships.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 561611 combines scalable records-based personal background screening with field-heavy private detective and investigative assignments. The lens retains recurring screening and repeat organizational investigation workflows and excludes episodic personal-case work so that data retrieval, verification, exception handling, field investigation, and accountable reporting form a coherent external-service model; the narrowing is for business-model coherence rather than attractiveness.

## Executive view
The coherent recurring opportunity within 561611 is enterprise background screening, workforce monitoring, and repeat corporate investigation. AI can accelerate records search, matching assistance, exception triage, case summarization, report production, and customer service, but fragmented source data, false-match risk, disputes, privacy law, licensing, and evidentiary standards preserve a substantial accountable human layer.

## How AI changes the work
AI can normalize names and records, prioritize likely matches, compare sources, identify missing fields, summarize case files, draft reports, route disputes, and support customer communication. Investigators and screening specialists still verify identity and record disposition, resolve conflicting data, conduct interviews or surveillance, assess legal and customer rules, document sources, and approve consequential findings.

## Value capture
Per-check, subscription, retainer, and fixed-fee work can retain productivity gains until enterprise rebids and competitive APIs transmit savings to customers. Hourly investigations pass time savings through more quickly, while data access, model validation, cybersecurity, insurance, compliance, and dispute handling consume part of the benefit.

## Firm availability
The code contains both scaled screening organizations and owner-dependent detective practices. LMM-sized firms are plausibly more weighted toward repeat organizational customers than the establishment population, but no firm-level activity mix exists; licensing, customer trust, data contracts, and key-person knowledge can make otherwise willing sellers difficult to transfer.

## Demand durability
Hiring, tenant turnover, fraud, litigation, vendor diligence, and ongoing worker assessment support repeat demand. BLS expects investigator employment to grow and background checks to remain a source of work, but hiring cycles, customer insourcing, cheaper software, and automated low-risk checks create a meaningful downside.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are the screening-versus-detective revenue mix, current automation baseline, false-match and dispute rates, state privacy and licensing variation, data-vendor dependency, cyber risk, customer concentration, hourly billing exposure, and the margin-bridged injected firm count. Algorithmic outputs can expand compliance obligations rather than remove them.

## Caveats
- a: The cited occupation is broader than the industry lens and includes investigators employed in retail, finance, government, and other industries.
- a: No source quantifies the current automation baseline or wage-weighted task mix of lower-middle-market screening and investigation firms.
- a: Exposure differs sharply between high-volume background screening and surveillance- or interview-heavy investigations.
- rho: Regulatory evidence establishes constraints and existing algorithmic products, not representative adoption or realized labor savings.
- rho: Large technology-enabled screening providers may implement faster than lower-middle-market firms with legacy systems.
- rho: Field investigation and manual source verification have lower implementation potential than standardized database screening.
- e: The Census definition lists activities but provides no establishment, revenue, customer, or recurrence shares.
- e: CFPB states that hundreds of companies provide employment background checks but does not map them to NAICS 561611 or the EBITDA band.
- e: The injected count of 46 depends on a broad Business and Consumer Services margin assumption that may not represent either screening platforms or field-investigation firms.
- s5: Gallup covers all US employer businesses and measures intentions rather than completed transactions.
- s5: Licensing, data-provider contracts, customer approvals, and owner reputation may limit transferability.
- s5: No industry-specific owner-age or completed-deal series was found.
- q: No source directly measures five-year retention of AI-derived benefits in independent background-screening or investigation firms.
- q: Pricing and pass-through differ materially between standardized checks and hourly bespoke investigations.
- q: Data-source fees, cyber controls, accuracy review, and dispute handling may absorb savings that a labor-only view would overstate.
- d5: BLS projects an occupation across multiple employing industries rather than outsourced 561611 service quantity.
- d5: The demand outlook mixes hiring-sensitive screening with less cyclical fraud, legal, tenant, and monitoring work.
- d5: Reported revenue growth could reflect data costs, inflation, or product mix rather than constant-quality service quantity.
- o: FCRA obligations do not require every customer to use an independent screening firm.
- o: Private-investigator licensing varies by state and applies unevenly to database-based screening.
- o: Routine checks may migrate to self-service software even while regulated accountability remains with a consumer reporting agency.
