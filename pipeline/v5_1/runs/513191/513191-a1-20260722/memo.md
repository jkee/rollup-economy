**Driver:** The principal driver is repeat seasonal and occasion-based publishing work with AI-exposed creative, catalog, commercial, and administrative workflows.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market greeting-card publishers that provide recurring or repeat publishing programs to external retailers, distributors, licensors, or business customers, including seasonal line refreshes, licensed or custom collections, digital catalog production, merchandising content, and repeat replenishment support.
- Excluded: One-off direct-to-consumer product sellers without repeat customer programs; independent artists who self-publish; commercial printers that do not publish; retailers and distributors that do not own or control publishing rights; shells, captive units, non-control financing vehicles, and firms outside the target normalized-EBITDA band.
- Customer and revenue model: Retailers, distributors, licensors, and business customers buy finished card lines, seasonal assortments, private-label or licensed collections, and related digital assets through wholesale orders, repeat programs, royalties, or fixed project and replenishment arrangements. The publisher controls or licenses creative rights and coordinates design, editorial review, production, cataloging, sales, and distribution.
- Deviation from default lens: The code spans physical and electronic products and includes one-off consumer publishing that is not an outsourced service. For coherence with the fixed screen, the lens retains only publishers with recurring or repeat external-customer programs; the narrowing is based on business-model fit, not attractiveness.

## Executive view
Greeting-card publishing combines repeat seasonal demand and copyright-controlled creative catalogs with substantial design, editorial, sales, and administrative workflows. AI can accelerate content and catalog production, but the fixed outsourced-service lens fits only a subset of publishers and the LMM firm pool is unknown.

## How AI changes the work
AI can generate and adapt concepts, copy, imagery, metadata, proofs, catalogs, sales materials, forecasts, and routine account responses. Human creative direction, rights clearance, licensing, retailer negotiation, physical approval, brand judgment, and accountable release remain central.

## Value capture
Publishers may retain savings inside wholesale prices, royalty structures, and seasonal programs between negotiations, or use them to produce broader assortments. Retailer power, licensors, returns, markdown support, and rapid imitation can transfer benefits away from the operator.

## Firm availability
Eligible firms are the subset with repeat external-customer programs, transferable catalogs and rights, operational independence, and normalized LMM earnings. The frozen dataset provides no defensible LMM firm count, and many small publishers are creator-dependent or primarily sell one-off products.

## Demand durability
Life events and holidays sustain greeting behavior, but the channel can shift from physical cards and traditional publishers toward digital messaging, direct creators, personalization platforms, and retailer-controlled programs. Available demand evidence is broad or stale, so the five-year range is wide.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are an unknown LMM firm pool, narrow lens fit, founder-dependent creative value, retailer concentration, licensed-rights constraints, returns and markdowns, digital substitution, direct creator competition, broad occupation and demand proxies, and stale greeting-card market evidence.

## Caveats
- a: BLS publishes the occupation mix for broad NAICS 513100, not exact 513191 or the narrowed repeat-program lens.
- a: The occupation table measures jobs and wages, not task-level AI exposure or current automation.
- a: The exact mix of in-house design, licensed content, outsourced printing, and digital publishing can materially change exposed hours.
- rho: No representative source measures AI implementation or labor realization for greeting-card publishers.
- rho: Generative output may increase assortment breadth and testing rather than reduce labor.
- rho: Copyright, licensing, brand-safety, and retailer-acceptance controls may keep human review intensive.
- e: The frozen dataset declares no defensible LMM-band firm count for this industry.
- e: Possession of copyrights and a repeat seasonal catalog does not by itself establish an outsourced service or normalized EBITDA.
- e: Independent artists and small digital publishers may be inseparable from the founder and lack transferable operations.
- s5: Gallup covers employer-business owners across industries and measures intentions, not completed control transfers.
- s5: The survey is not specific to publishers, the LMM band, or firms with transferable intellectual property.
- s5: Asset or catalog sales may not constitute control transfers of eligible operating firms.
- q: No representative evidence measures how greeting-card publishers share automation benefits with retailers, licensors, or consumers.
- q: The 2019 trade-association facts are stale and describe retail prices and consumer demand, not publisher contract pass-through.
- q: Savings may be absorbed by greater assortment breadth, higher royalties, returns, or retailer demands rather than retained.
- d5: BLS projects broad 513100 employment, not exact-code constant-price greeting-card demand.
- d5: The Greeting Card Association figures are from 2019 and may not describe current post-pandemic purchasing or the narrowed B2B lens.
- d5: Employment can decline through productivity or outsourcing even when card demand is stable.
- o: Official definitions establish publishing and rights ownership, not the future operator share.
- o: The narrow lens may be more defensible for retailer programs than for electronic consumer cards.
- o: A publisher may remain legally accountable while software and outside platforms perform most workflow tasks.
