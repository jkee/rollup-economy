# 561990 — All Other Support Services

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `561990-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.41 · L 2.61 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Recurring routes and field contracts create repeat revenue while administrative coordination, exception triage, and reporting offer bounded AI leverage.
**Weakness:** The residual code is structurally heterogeneous, and sensors, advanced meters, RFID, and customer self-service can remove routine outsourced work rather than merely make it cheaper.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market firms providing recurring or repeat outsourced route-, crew-, or field-data support, including inventory taking, contract meter reading, traffic flagging, utility locating, bottle exchange, and comparable day-to-day operational support delivered under customer contracts.
- Excluded: Office administration, facilities support, staffing, call centers, business service centers, security, building services, packaging, trade-show organizing, and other activities assigned to separate NAICS codes; also excluded are shells, captive units, non-control financing vehicles, solo operators below the LMM band, and idiosyncratic or episodic 561990 activities such as independent auctioneering, float decorating, bartering, one-off lumber grading, and contract diving.
- Customer and revenue model: Utilities, retailers, manufacturers, contractors, municipalities, and other organizations outsource recurring site visits, counts, readings, route work, or staffed operating tasks. Revenue is commonly per visit, meter, location, unit, crew-hour, route, or fixed contract, with renewals, service-level requirements, scheduling, field supervision, exception handling, and reporting.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 561990 is a residual code spanning unrelated physical, field, creative, marketplace, and technical services. The lens is narrowed for coherence to recurring route-, crew-, and field-data support operations; the narrowing is based on shared delivery mechanics, not attractiveness.

## Executive view
The coherent opportunity inside this residual code is recurring field-data and operational support, not the entire collection of unrelated activities. AI can improve scheduling, reporting, customer communication, exception handling, and billing, but much of delivery remains physical and technology can eliminate some routes entirely. Attractive firms would have dense routes, recurring contracts, trained supervisors, digital records, and low customer concentration.

## How AI changes the work
AI can draft bids and work orders, optimize schedules and routes, extract customer instructions, classify photos and readings, reconcile counts, flag anomalies, prepare compliance records, answer routine customer questions, and generate invoices and reports. Field access, physical verification, traffic safety, equipment handling, judgment at unusual sites, and accountable sign-off remain human-heavy.

## Value capture
Per-site, per-unit, route, and fixed-price contracts can retain some productivity through higher density, lower overtime, and more supervisor span. Crew-hour contracts and competitive rebids pass savings through more quickly, and customers may demand machine-readable outputs without paying more. Durable capture depends on proprietary operating data, route density, service reliability, and bundling exception resolution with data delivery.

## Firm availability
The whole NAICS code is not a coherent acquisition population. Within the narrowed subset, recurring routes, customer contracts, trained crews, permits, and operating history can be transferable, but residual-code classification, owner dependence, customer concentration, and the estimate-based firm count create substantial uncertainty.

## Demand durability
Inventory assurance, infrastructure work, temporary traffic control, and field verification remain useful as commerce and physical networks expand. Smart meters, RFID, sensors, and customer self-service can eliminate routine visits, so durable demand increasingly consists of exceptions, audits, complex sites, and safety-critical work rather than simple data collection.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest gaps are six-digit activity shares, occupation and wage mix for the narrowed subset, actual contract economics, workflow-level AI savings, completed transaction rates, and client substitution after sensing technologies. The all-services margin used for the injected firm count may be especially misleading across labor-heavy, equipment-heavy, and specialist operations. A buyer could also mistake a shrinking manual route book for recurring revenue if customer automation plans are not diligenced.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation table is for NAICS 561900 and includes packaging and convention or trade-show operations outside the narrowed lens.
- a: O*NET office-clerk tasks describe one component of delivery and do not measure wage weights or time shares in 561990.
- a: Existing route software, handheld devices, barcode scanners, and automated meters mean some technically automatable work is already absent from the current opportunity.
- rho: BTOS is economy-wide and measures use of AI in any business function rather than dependable labor avoidance.
- rho: The narrowed activities vary sharply in data quality, workforce digital literacy, and safety exposure.
- rho: Customer procurement and integration timelines may dominate tool capability.
- e: No source measures the share of 561990 LMM firms in the narrowed recurring operational subset.
- e: The injected firm count applies an all-services margin to business models with very different labor, equipment, insurance, and subcontractor costs.
- e: NAICS residual-code classification and mixed-service firms create substantial boundary error.
- s5: Gallup covers all employer businesses rather than this industry, size band, or narrowed lens.
- s5: Plans to sell or transfer are not completed arm's-length control transactions.
- s5: Customer concentration, municipal procurement, insurance history, and key-person dependence may impair transferability.
- q: No source measures contract mix or retained AI benefit for the narrowed population.
- q: Public-sector and utility procurement may pass savings through differently from private negotiated contracts.
- q: Retention varies between data-heavy inventory work and labor-heavy traffic control or field services.
- d5: BLS output is for all NAICS 561900, including packaging and trade-show services.
- d5: The selected activities respond to different end markets and may not share the parent industry's growth rate.
- d5: A recession or municipal and utility budget pressure could reduce project volumes below the range.
- o: Office- and material-clerk projections span the economy and reflect automation beyond AI.
- o: EIA smart-meter data cover electricity, not water or natural gas, and contract meter reading is only one activity in the lens.
- o: Autonomous sensing and computer vision could displace routine physical collection faster than assumed, while regulation and safety rules could preserve more operator work.

- **S5** — [Gallup: Most Small-Business Owners Lack a Succession Plan](https://news.gallup.com/poll/657362/small-business-owners-lack-succession-plan.aspx) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports that a meaningful minority of US employer-business owners planned a sale or ownership transfer within five years, used only as a broad succession-intent proxy.
