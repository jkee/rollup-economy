# 712110 — Museums

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `712110-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** STRUCTURAL_OUT · A 5.36 · L 0.22 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → LOW_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** AI can assist a meaningful share of research, content, visitor-communications, development, and administrative work while the physical experience remains operator-led.
**Weakness:** Almost all museums are nonprofit, public, or institutionally governed, so the conventional transferable-control target population is exceptionally small.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US museum operators preserving and exhibiting historical, cultural, or educational objects, including art, science, technology, children's, specialty, wax, and similar museums, where visitors, members, schools, sponsors, or renters repeatedly purchase or support museum experiences.
- Excluded: Government-run and university-captive museums; nonprofit entities without transferable equity control; retail art galleries; independent exhibit designers; historical sites, zoos, botanical gardens, and nature parks classified outside 712110; shell entities and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: The coherent transferable-firm population is the very small for-profit subset of museum operators. Customers are mainly visitors, members, school groups, event renters, and sponsors; revenue is a mix of fixed-price admissions, memberships, programs, retail/food, rentals, sponsorships, and sometimes contributions or grants.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed to for-profit museum operators because nonprofit, government, and university governance cannot undergo a conventional qualifying equity-control transfer, while the underlying visitor-service model remains the same.

## Executive view
Museums contain real AI-addressable knowledge and administrative work, while the physical venue, collection, and visitor experience remain operator-dependent. The binding issue is ownership form: the museum field is overwhelmingly nonprofit, public, or university-governed, leaving very few conventional control-acquisition targets even before diligence on collections and real estate.

## How AI changes the work
Near-term tools can accelerate cataloging and metadata cleanup, research and interpretation drafts, educational materials, translation, donor and visitor communications, marketing production, segmentation, scheduling, and routine finance or HR administration. Human review remains essential for provenance, attribution, cultural sensitivity, copyrights, factual accuracy, conservation decisions, safeguarding, and live visitor experiences; physical handling, security, facilities, and exhibit installation remain largely outside the opportunity.

## Value capture
Eligible commercial museums mostly sell fixed-price admissions, memberships, programs, events, rentals, retail, food, and sponsorships, so savings are not automatically passed through contract formulas. Retention is nevertheless moderated by admission affordability, abundant leisure substitutes, the need to refresh exhibits, staff wage pressure, and the possibility that quality or access improvements are required to preserve attendance.

## Firm availability
The frozen count is especially fragile here because private-sector datasets can include tax-exempt employers and a recreation-sector margin is a poor bridge for contribution-supported museums. IMLS and AAM governance evidence indicates that conventional for-profit operators are only a sliver of the field. Among that sliver, general owner aging supports possible succession, but museum-specific completed-transfer evidence is absent and transferability may be defeated by collection ownership, restricted gifts, founder dependence, leases, or real estate.

## Demand durability
Museum-going incidence has recovered, but repeat frequency and institution-level traffic are uneven, suggesting a mature service with persistent cultural, educational, tourism, and event demand rather than rapid structural growth. Digital discovery can complement physical visits, yet online leisure, weak tourism, economic stress, and failure to refresh exhibits can depress particular operators. Physical collections and venues keep most surviving quantity operator-required.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest risks are a near-empty transferable target set, nonprofit contamination of the frozen firm count, weak museum-specific transaction data, rights or title defects in collections, dependence on a founder or singular attraction, attendance volatility, high fixed facility costs, and reputational damage from inaccurate or insensitive AI output. A commercial museum can also resemble a one-asset attraction more than a recurring service business, making normalized EBITDA and repeat demand unusually sensitive to exhibit freshness and tourism.

## Caveats
- a: The available BLS occupation table is NAICS 712100 rather than six-digit 712110 and covers all ownership types and employer sizes.
- a: Exposure is task-level and does not imply safe substitution; provenance, copyright, factual accuracy, conservation ethics, and interpretive judgment constrain use.
- a: The frozen compensation-to-receipts input uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and a harmonization divisor, so its vintage and basis caveats remain material.
- rho: BTOS is economy-wide and measures any business-function use, not the implemented share of exposed museum labor opportunity.
- rho: The five-year conversion from present adoption to operational substitution is judgmental and could be lower if rights, provenance, or public-trust failures trigger stricter review.
- e: The 0.2% for-profit estimate is old and covers a broader museum universe than employer establishments in NAICS 712110.
- e: AAM accreditation statistics are self-reported and not representative of all museums.
- e: The frozen n=172 is an ESTIMATE based on size-class firms and a sector margin bridge; museum nonprofits and irregular contributed revenue can make EBITDA-band classification misleading.
- s5: Owner-age and planned-sale evidence is economy-wide, older, and not specific to museums or the $1-10M EBITDA band.
- s5: Planned sales can occur outside five years, fail to close, transfer internally, or liquidate assets without preserving a transferable operating business.
- q: No source measures five-year retention of implemented AI benefits for commercial museums.
- q: AAM revenue evidence is dominated by nonprofit institutions and may understate the earned-income share and pricing discretion of eligible for-profit operators.
- q: Mission or reputation pressures can cause even commercial operators to convert savings into access discounts or improved programming rather than margin.
- d5: AAM survey respondents and museum-goer panels are not a probability sample of for-profit 712110 operators.
- d5: Attendance is an imperfect proxy for constant-quality service quantity because free visits, memberships, pricing, exhibit quality, and ancillary purchases vary.
- d5: The 2025 recovery comparison uses 2019 as a benchmark rather than the run-date level.
- o: There is no direct measure of operator-required year-five museum quantity.
- o: Immersive digital experiences could substitute more strongly for small or low-differentiation attractions than for collection-rich destination museums.
- o: The estimate separates service elimination from labor implementation; AI-driven staffing changes are counted in rho, not again here.
