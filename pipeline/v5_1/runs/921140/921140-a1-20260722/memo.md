# 921140 — Executive and Legislative Offices, Combined

**Driver:** Document-intensive administrative and analytical workflows offer tangible public-sector AI efficiency opportunities.
**Weakness:** The industry contains government offices, not transferable LMM service firms, making eligibility, control transfer, and commercial retention inapplicable.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Government establishments serving as combined executive and legislative councils, boards of commissioners, or boards of supervisors are retained for operational analysis; no commercial lower-middle-market firm population is included because the NAICS definition is exclusively governmental.
- Excluded: Separate executive offices, separate legislative bodies, private government contractors classified in other industries, public agencies outside this code, financing vehicles, shells, and any activity without combined executive-legislative authority.
- Customer and revenue model: These offices exercise public authority for residents and constituents and are funded through appropriated government budgets and public revenues rather than customer contracts, service fees, or normalized EBITDA. Their output includes deliberation, ordinances, policy decisions, hearings, constituent communication, agendas, records, and executive oversight.
- Deviation from default lens: The default outsourced-service LMM lens cannot be populated: Census defines the industry as government establishments, and the frozen data provide neither a receipts concept nor an LMM firm count. The full industry is retained only to make that structural mismatch explicit, not narrowed to a favorable private analogue.

## Executive view
The code has meaningful administrative AI exposure but no population that fits a private LMM roll-up lens. It consists exclusively of combined local-government executive and legislative bodies, lacks commercial receipts and EBITDA, and cannot undergo private control transfers. Operational efficiency is plausible; investable firm availability and commercial value capture are not measurable constructs here.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate agendas, minutes, records retrieval, correspondence, policy comparison, budget analysis, translation, meeting preparation, and constituent triage. Human officials must still deliberate, negotiate, hold hearings, make lawful decisions, vote, and bear political and legal accountability.

## Value capture
Efficiency may reduce administrative workload or improve public service, but savings flow through appropriations, staffing decisions, or service capacity rather than an investor-owned operator's price and margin. The commercial-retention primitive is therefore missing rather than assigned a synthetic value.

## Firm availability
There are no eligible LMM firms in this government-only NAICS code. Officeholder changes, elections, municipal reorganizations, and jurisdiction consolidations are not transferable control transactions, and private vendors serving the offices reside in other industries.

## Demand durability
The number of general-purpose governments has been very stable, supporting approximately flat demand for combined governance functions. Workload can shift with population, regulation, fiscal conditions, emergencies, and public expectations, but an accountable council or board remains institutionally required.

## Risks and uncertainty
The decisive issue is structural lens failure, not technology risk. Operational estimates also face a non-specific occupation proxy, substantial variation across municipalities and counties, public-records and privacy obligations, fragmented systems, procurement constraints, and political resistance to automated work products.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation source spans all local government functions and is not specific to combined executive-legislative offices.
- a: Federal AI adoption is only directional evidence for state and local offices.
- a: The frozen labor-share input is missing because government activity has no compatible receipts denominator.
- rho: No implementation-rate evidence specific to NAICS 921140 was found.
- rho: Federal agency experience may overstate technical resources and understate the speed of small-jurisdiction deployment through commodity software.
- e: Private technology, consulting, staffing, and records vendors serving these offices are classified outside NAICS 921140.
- e: The frozen dataset explicitly provides no defensible LMM firm count.
- s5: Political succession is not ownership succession.
- s5: Government consolidation or dissolution does not create an acquirable firm.
- q: Public budget retention is not equivalent to EBITDA benefit retained after customer pass-through.
- q: Assigning a neutral value would conceal the construct's inapplicability.
- d5: Government-unit counts are not service quantity and include governments whose executive and legislative offices are separately classified.
- d5: The range excludes changes in service quality, meeting complexity, and constituent expectations that counts cannot reveal.
- o: This is a bounded institutional judgment, not an observed operator-share statistic.
- o: A high operator requirement does not imply a private acquisition opportunity.
