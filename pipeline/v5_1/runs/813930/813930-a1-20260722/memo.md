# 813930 — Labor Unions and Similar Labor Organizations

**Base tier:** STRUCTURAL_OUT · A 5.50 · L 0.23 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → LOW_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Large recurring contract, grievance, membership, and compliance information flows offer AI-assisted capacity gains around an enduring representation function.
**Weakness:** Member governance, nonprofit economics, and the near absence of conventional ownership transfers undermine the roll-up mechanism.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market-scale labor unions, locals, councils, and similar employee organizations providing recurring collective bargaining, contract administration, grievance handling, organizing, member communication, training, and representation to members.
- Excluded: Employer human-resources departments, pension and benefit funds, political action committees, professional or business associations, outside law and consulting firms, shells, captive internal units, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Organizations are generally member-governed and funded primarily through recurring dues, assessments, and related member receipts; they spend those funds on representation, bargaining, grievance administration, organizing, communications, legal support, training, and operations rather than distributing profits to owners.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Labor unions combine highly document-intensive case, contract, membership, and compliance work with bargaining and representation that remain fundamentally relational and accountable. AI can improve staff capacity, but member governance and the absence of conventional transferable ownership sharply constrain the roll-up premise.

## How AI changes the work
AI can summarize contracts and case files, search precedents, draft routine correspondence and proposals, triage grievances, translate member communications, prepare reports, and support scheduling and outreach. Human representatives remain responsible for organizing, negotiation, mediation, testimony, strategic judgment, and member trust.

## Value capture
Dues-funded organizations can retain much of an implemented administrative benefit as service capacity or reserves because fees are not normally cost-plus, but member governance may redirect it to lower dues, more representation, organizing, or improved response rather than distributable savings.

## Firm availability
Some staffed locals and councils have recurring receipts and durable operations, but most are member-governed nonprofits or affiliated bodies rather than owner-controlled businesses. Commercial EBITDA classification and conventional control transfer are therefore poor fits.

## Demand durability
A large represented-worker base and recurring contracts and grievances sustain the service, while long-run membership-density decline, policy shifts, employer resistance, consolidation, and uneven public-private trends limit growth visibility.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are actual wage-weighted task time, current software maturity, data confidentiality, local autonomy, legal accuracy, member acceptance, the commercial margin bridge behind n, and whether any organizational merger qualifies as a transferable control event.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS employment shares are not wage-weighted task shares and exclude self-employed or unpaid officers.
- a: O*NET describes an occupation across employer and union settings rather than work performed only inside NAICS 813930.
- a: Existing case-management, membership, document, and communications systems may already automate part of the nominally exposed work.
- rho: BTOS does not identify labor unions or measure implementation of exposed task opportunity.
- rho: Use of AI in any function is not evidence of reliable deployment in bargaining, grievance, or legal workflows.
- rho: Local autonomy, legacy systems, and sensitive member information can slow standardized implementation.
- e: The frozen n estimate applies a commercial EBITDA margin bridge to member organizations, where surplus is not equivalent to transferable normalized EBITDA.
- e: A local, council, international body, benefit fund, and related political committee may be separate legal entities with interdependent operations.
- e: Recurring member representation resembles an outsourced service, but members are also the organization's governors rather than ordinary external customers.
- s5: No source measures qualifying control transfers among LMM-scale labor organizations.
- s5: Leadership elections and routine affiliation changes are not equivalent to acquisition of a transferable operating firm.
- s5: The legal and economic treatment of a union merger may differ from a control transaction in a commercial company.
- q: No public study measures retained AI benefit in labor organizations.
- q: Member governance may direct savings into lower dues or expanded service rather than financial surplus.
- q: Redeployment can be valuable retained capacity but does not necessarily create removable headcount.
- d5: Membership and representation counts are proxies for constant-price service demand.
- d5: The 2025 annual estimate uses an 11-month average because October CPS data were not collected.
- d5: Public- and private-sector membership trajectories and service intensity can diverge sharply.
- o: The sources establish organizational purpose and human tasks, not a five-year operator-required quantity share.
- o: Affiliates or outside specialists may replace a local operator while union representation continues.
- o: Digital self-service can reduce local administrative presence without eliminating formal representation.
