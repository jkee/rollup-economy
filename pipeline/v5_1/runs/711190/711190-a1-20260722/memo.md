# 711190 — Other Performing Arts Companies

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `711190-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 6.00 · L 1.60 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Repeat contracted live-show delivery offers automatable administrative and production-preparation work while preserving a human-operated core service.
**Weakness:** The already-small estimated firm pool shrinks materially after excluding ticket-risk, nonprofit, and owner-dependent acts, and code-specific evidence is sparse.

## Business-model lens
- Included: For-profit circus, magic, ice-skating, traveling-carnival, and similar live-performance companies in the lower-middle-market band that repeatedly provide produced shows to venues, promoters, corporate buyers, fairs, or other external customers under booking or performance contracts.
- Excluded: Primarily self-promoted ticket-risk productions, grant-dependent nonprofit organizations, owner-only or non-transferable individual acts, promoters that do not produce their own shows, captive entertainment units, and companies whose economics depend mainly on venue ownership, intellectual-property licensing, or merchandise.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat business-to-business bookings and fixed or negotiated performance fees from venues, promoters, fairs, casinos, resorts, and corporate-event buyers; some companies may also receive admissions participation or ancillary revenue.
- Deviation from default lens: The code combines contract-performance businesses with consumer-facing ticket-risk productions, nonprofits, and personality-dependent acts. The lens is narrowed to recurring contracted show providers because those models have coherent external-service revenue and transferable operating organizations.

## Executive view
The coherent target is a contracted touring-show operator rather than the entire residual performing-arts code. Such a company combines repeat venue or event-buyer relationships with a transferable production organization, but the eligible population is likely a minority because many firms are ticket-risk, nonprofit, seasonal, or inseparable from a featured owner-performer.

## How AI changes the work
AI can reduce labor in proposals, marketing content, customer communication, tour and crew scheduling, budgeting, bookkeeping, procurement support, design ideation, and production documentation. It is much less able to substitute for physical performance, live technical execution, safety management, artistic direction, and accountable delivery at the venue, so the implementable opportunity is concentrated in overhead and preparation rather than the show itself.

## Value capture
Negotiated or fixed performance fees should preserve some cost savings until renewal, while competitive rebids, buyer leverage, open-book budgets, and admissions-sharing can pass benefits back to customers. Brand differentiation, proprietary show concepts, and demonstrated reliability improve retention; undifferentiated event acts face faster repricing.

## Firm availability
The injected LMM count is small and estimated rather than observed. After excluding nonprofits, ticket-risk productions, owner-only acts, and companies without repeat contract customers or transferable management, only a limited set is likely eligible. Generic small-business exit intent suggests some turnover, but actual qualifying transactions should be lower because closures and internal succession do not count.

## Demand durability
BLS expects real output growth for the broader performing-arts-company industry, and NEA data show meaningful attendance in the heterogeneous other-live-performance category. Demand is nevertheless discretionary, cyclical, exposed to travel and venue economics, and vulnerable to audience-fashion shifts. For demand that persists, the physical and accountable nature of live production makes complete software displacement unlikely.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main evidence weakness is classification: the best workforce and forecast data cover all performing arts companies, while the target code itself mixes circuses, magic, ice shows, and traveling carnivals. Key-person creative dependence, performer retention, injury and insurance risk, seasonality, venue concentration, recession sensitivity, and weak disclosure of private transactions could all invalidate a firm-level thesis.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS is for NAICS 711100 and includes theater, dance, and music companies outside this code.
- a: OEWS excludes self-employed workers and does not identify whether tasks are already automated.
- a: Observed Claude usage is not the same quantity as technical exposure or labor substitution.
- rho: No implementation study was found for NAICS 711190.
- rho: Lean organizations may adopt general-purpose tools quickly but lack clean data and dedicated change-management capacity.
- rho: Implementation must preserve safety, artistic quality, and contract compliance.
- e: The supplied n is margin-bridged from firm counts and average receipts rather than observed EBITDA.
- e: The most specific published revenue-model evidence found is from 2007 and does not isolate the current contracted subset.
- e: Eligibility can change sharply with whether a show brand and performer relationships survive an owner exit.
- s5: The source survey is old, cross-industry, and measures expected exit rather than completed qualifying control transfers.
- s5: Private small-company transfers and closures are poorly disclosed.
- s5: Internal family succession is not counted as a qualifying transfer under this primitive.
- q: No current contract sample for the narrowed lens was available.
- q: The source confirms admissions and contract fees as operating revenue categories but does not report current 711190 contract terms.
- q: Customer concentration and show differentiation can move retention materially in either direction.
- d5: BLS output is for NAICS 711100 and not the narrowed contracted subset.
- d5: The NEA attendance category includes several music genres outside 711190 and measures people attending at least once, not quantity purchased.
- d5: Pandemic recovery distorts recent comparisons, and live entertainment is cyclical and discretionary.
- o: No source directly measures operator displacement for 711190.
- o: Rapid advances in synthetic media could increase substitution for some corporate or promotional events.
- o: The estimate separates service elimination from AI assistance inside a continuing operator.

## Sources
- **S1** — [2022 NAICS Definition: 711190 Other Performing Arts Companies](https://www.census.gov/naics/?details=7111&input=7111&year=2022) (accessed 2026-07-22): Defines 711190 as companies or groups producing live theatrical presentations other than theater, dance, and musical companies, with circuses, magic shows, ice-skating companies, and traveling carnival shows as examples.
- **S2** — [May 2023 National Industry-Specific Occupational Employment and Wage Estimates: NAICS 711100 Performing Arts Companies](https://www.bls.gov/oes/2023/May/naics4_711100.htm) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports 135,030 total jobs and occupation shares and wages, including 41.79% arts/design/entertainment/sports/media, 8.62% management, 7.58% business and financial operations, and 7.48% office and administrative support.
- **S3** — [Anthropic Economic Index report: Economic primitives](https://www.anthropic.com/research/anthropic-economic-index-january-2026-report) (accessed 2026-07-22): Documents observed AI usage by O*NET and SOC task groups, increased arts and media writing use, and growing enterprise automation of office workflows such as email, document processing, CRM, and scheduling.
- **S4** — [5 Reasons to Hire Your Child](https://www.sba.gov/blog/5-reasons-hire-your-child) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports a cited survey in which 29% of business owners expected to exit within five years, 54% within ten years, and 37% wanted to transfer the business to family.
- **S5** — [Time and Money: Using Federal Data to Measure the Value of Performing Arts Activities](https://www.arts.gov/sites/default/files/102.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-22): Uses Economic Census data to show other performing arts companies and explains that for-profit performing-arts revenue mainly came from admissions and contract fees; also documents material differences between taxable and tax-exempt organizations.
- **S6** — [Employment and output by industry, 2024-2034](https://www.bls.gov/emp/tables/industry-employment-and-output.htm) (accessed 2026-07-22): Projects NAICS 711100 real output from $29.8 billion in 2024 to $36.9 billion in 2034, a 2.1% compound annual increase, and employment from 142,100 to 147,200.
- **S7** — [Measuring the Arts: Indicator B.1, Who Attends Arts Events in Person?](https://www.arts.gov/sites/default/files/b1-report-202403.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports that 38% of U.S. adults attended a live arts performance in 2022 and that attendance at the survey's other-live-performance category, which may include magic and circus acts, rose from 15% in 2017 to 21% in 2022.
