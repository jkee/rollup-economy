# 326130 — Laminated Plastics Plate, Sheet (except Packaging), and Shape Manufacturing

**Driver:** Specification-heavy repeat production creates AI opportunities in quality, process control, quoting, and documentation.
**Weakness:** Six-digit demand and occupation evidence is thin, and much of the high labor base remains tied to physical production.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market manufacturers repeatedly supplying laminated plastic plate, nonpackaging sheet, rod, and other shapes made by bonding or resin-impregnating profiles and compressing them under heat.
- Excluded: Captive lamination operations, flexible packaging laminators, unlaminated sheet and profile makers, downstream fabricators with no lamination manufacturing, shell entities, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat B2B sales of standard and customer-specified laminated stock under purchase orders, blanket agreements, distributor programs, and qualified supply relationships, priced by sheet, unit, area, weight, or production run.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Laminated plastic plate, sheet, and shapes combine repeat physical supply with more specification and quality complexity than many commodity plastics, supporting useful AI applications and some commercial defensibility. The opportunity remains bounded because the core transformation, handling, testing, and finishing work is physical and six-digit evidence is scarce.

## How AI changes the work
DOE identifies automated visual quality control, manufacturing characterization, process optimization, and predictive maintenance as practical AI applications. Laminators can also automate specification extraction, certificate and batch-record review, quoting, scheduling, and customer documentation. Deployment depends on labeled defect data, sensor coverage, legacy-control integration, product variability, and customer acceptance of AI-supported quality workflows.

## Value capture
Engineered formulations, certifications, qualification histories, tooling, and dependable supply can slow customer switching and preserve a meaningful portion of savings. Standard stock sold through distributors is more transparent, and resin-index mechanisms, annual cost-down demands, and competitive rebids return value to customers over time.

## Firm availability
Most independent laminate manufacturers with repeat external customers should qualify, but public classifications can mix actual lamination with distribution and fabrication. The frozen candidate count requires diligence on process ownership and normalized economics. Succession and strategic interest create transfer opportunities, but internal succession, indefinite holds, minority deals, and closures are excluded.

## Demand durability
BLS projects modest broad plastics-product output growth, while the plastics-industry report shows diverse final uses and resilient long-term demand. Laminated stock benefits from insulation, corrosion resistance, strength-to-weight, and specification-driven applications, yet is exposed to durable-goods cycles and substitution by metals, glass, wood composites, unlaminated plastics, imports, and alternative manufacturing methods.

## Risks and uncertainty
Evidence is mostly four-digit or whole-industry rather than 326130. Plant processes range from standardized sheet to labor-intensive specialty laminates, so automation baselines and margins vary widely. Environmental or worker-exposure restrictions on resin systems, customer concentration, qualification costs, scrap, energy use, and imported competition could dominate AI economics.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS is four-digit NAICS 326100, not six-digit 326130.
- a: Task shares are inferred, not directly measured.
- a: Processes range from standardized continuous sheet to labor-intensive batch laminates, creating large plant-level variation.
- rho: No five-year adoption study specific to laminated plastics was found.
- rho: The same vision system may perform differently across textures, colors, reinforcements, and low-volume custom runs.
- e: Eligibility is not directly measured and the frozen count is margin-bridged.
- e: Public descriptions often blur lamination, stock-shape distribution, machining, and downstream fabrication.
- s5: No six-digit deal-rate or owner-age series was found.
- s5: Strategic acquisitions reported publicly skew toward larger engineered-material companies and may not represent LMM firms.
- q: Contract terms and product differentiation were not observed.
- q: Retention likely differs markedly between commodity sheet and engineered electrical, aerospace, defense, or industrial laminates.
- d5: Both demand sources are broader than 326130.
- d5: Value-based end-use shares do not directly measure constant-quality laminate volume.
- d5: Environmental restrictions on resin systems and shifts in imported stock could change domestic demand.
- o: No direct operator-requirement study exists.
- o: The estimate separates elimination of the current service basket from labor reduction within the manufacturing operator.
