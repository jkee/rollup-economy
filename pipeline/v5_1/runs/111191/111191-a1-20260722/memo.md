# 111191 — Oilseed and Grain Combination Farming

**Driver:** Structured row-crop workflows and geospatial data support further autonomy and AI, while steady food, feed, crush, and export demand preserves the physical operating need.
**Weakness:** Both frozen opportunity denominators are missing, and family and land succession rarely maps cleanly to an actionable control transfer of an intact farm business.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market independent commercial farms that grow a combination of oilseeds and grains with no single crop family representing at least half of production value and repeatedly sell harvested crops or seed to external elevators, processors, merchandisers, cooperatives, or other customers.
- Excluded: Hobby and residential farms, passive farmland landlords, custom-farming contractors without crop ownership, captive production units, farms dominated by one oilseed or grain family and classified elsewhere, shells, non-control financing vehicles, and operations outside the roughly $1-10M normalized EBITDA band.
- Customer and revenue model: Recurring annual commodity and seed sales, usually priced against transparent regional and global markets and delivered under spot, forward, hedged, cooperative, or production contracts; revenue varies with acres, yields, crop mix, quality, basis, and commodity prices.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Oilseed and grain combination farming has substantial AI exposure in field operations, agronomy, and administration, but much precision automation is already installed and commodity economics limit retained value. Physical demand is mature and durable, with yields offsetting softer acreage. The decisive screen weakness is acquisition availability: the frozen labor share and firm count are both missing, and aging, family-owned farms often transition land or management internally rather than sell an intact control-ready company.

## How AI changes the work
Auto-steer already covers more than half of major row-crop acreage, so the remaining opportunity is supervised-to-higher autonomy, robotic scouting, targeted spraying, sensor and imagery interpretation, predictive maintenance, crop and input planning, compliance, recordkeeping, and marketing support. Scaled farms adopt faster, but weather, agronomic edge cases, short planting and harvest windows, rural connectivity, interoperability, liability, and equipment cycles keep humans accountable.

## Value capture
Early adopters can retain lower labor and input costs, better field timing, and yield gains for several seasons. Transparent commodity prices, global competition, higher output, cash-rent resets, and equipment, seed, input, and software pricing transfer much of the benefit over five years. Operators with owned land, distinctive execution, disciplined hedging, and less competitive rental markets should retain more than heavily rented price-taking farms.

## Firm availability
No defensible n is available and it must remain missing. The broader farm sector is 95% family-owned and operated, with an average producer age of 58.1, but those facts do not establish a count of LMM combination farms or actionable sales. USDA expects only about 5% of farmland acres to move by sale or gift within five years, and family trusts, wills, leases, and gradual succession often separate land ownership from business control.

## Demand durability
USDA's baseline projects slightly lower major-crop acreage but rising yields and production. Corn use grows modestly and soybean production reaches records, supported by feed, exports, crush, food, and industrial demand. Weather, trade, biofuel rules, global output, and crop prices create annual volatility, while the combination-farm classification itself can change with revenue mix even when the underlying operation persists.

## Risks and uncertainty
Material risks include missing frozen l and n, commodity and basis volatility, drought and extreme weather, trade policy, crop insurance and farm-program changes, rising land and equipment costs, tenant concentration, data rights, equipment lock-in, input resistance, and succession complexity. Automation failures during narrow seasonal windows are costly. The evidence is mostly crop- or whole-farm-sector data rather than this exact six-digit LMM population.

## Caveats
- a: USDA technology data are crop- and acreage-based, not specific to NAICS 111191 or the LMM EBITDA lens.
- a: The latest crop-specific guidance adoption observations range from 2016 to 2019 and therefore do not measure the 2026 installed base.
- rho: Precision-tool adoption does not establish implementation of generative AI or driver-out autonomy.
- rho: Farm size strongly predicts adoption, but the frozen LMM population and equipment age are not observed.
- e: The frozen n is missing and must not be inferred from the much broader Census of Agriculture farm counts.
- e: USDA reports 95% of all US farms are family-owned and operated, but that is not a direct eligibility rate for this six-digit LMM population.
- s5: TOTAL covers all farmland and reports acres rather than operating-firm control events.
- s5: Family succession, trusts, gifts, land sales, and operator retirement often do not create a qualifying transferable company.
- q: No source directly measures AI-benefit sharing for mixed oilseed and grain farms.
- q: Owned-versus-rented acres, crop marketing skill, and local land competition cause large retention differences.
- d5: The USDA baseline assumes normal weather and current law and is not a forecast of cyclical shocks.
- d5: NAICS classification can change when one crop family exceeds half of a farm's production value, even if the farm remains operating.
- o: The estimate distinguishes labor substitution from elimination of the accountable farm operator.
- o: A change in legal or contractual operator form may remove a lens firm even though physical crop production continues.

## Sources
- **S1** — [NAICS Sector 11 Definitions: 111191 Oilseed and Grain Combination Farming](https://www.census.gov/naics/resources/archives/sect11.html) (accessed 2026-07-23): Official scope: farms growing a combination of oilseeds and grains with no one oilseed or grain family accounting for half of agricultural production value.
- **S2** — [Precision Agriculture in the Digital Era: Recent Adoption on U.S. Farms](https://www.ers.usda.gov/publications/105893) (accessed 2026-07-23): Digital-agriculture task and adoption evidence: substantial mapping and variable-rate use on corn and soybean acreage and automated guidance on well over half of major row-crop acreage.
- **S3** — [Largest farms most likely to adopt precision agriculture guidance systems](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/charts-of-note/105914) (accessed 2026-07-23): Size-based adoption proxy: guidance use reached 73% of the largest corn farms and 68% of the largest soybean farms, versus 10% and 11% among the smallest groups in the cited crop surveys.
- **S4** — [USDA releases 2022 Census of Agriculture data](https://data.nass.usda.gov/Newsroom/2024/02-13-2024.php) (accessed 2026-07-23): Farm-structure proxy: 95% of US farms were family-owned and operated, average producer age was 58.1, 32% of farmland was in oilseed and grain specialization, and only 6% of farms had at least $1 million in sales.
- **S5** — [Most of the U.S. Rented Farmland is Owned by Non-Farmers](https://www.nass.usda.gov/Newsroom/2026/03-12-2026.php) (accessed 2026-07-23): Transfer proxy from the 2024 TOTAL survey: about 43 million acres, roughly 5% of US farmland, are slated for ownership transfer by sale or gift within five years; only 23 million acres are expected to be sold to nonrelatives.
- **S6** — [USDA Agricultural Projections to 2035](https://ers.usda.gov/sites/default/files/_laserfiche/outlooks/113817/OCE-2026-1.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-23): Long-run demand and pricing proxy: lower major-crop acreage is offset by yield growth; corn production rises from 15.8 billion bushels in 2026/27 to nearly 16.6 billion in 2035/36, total corn use grows less than 4%, and soybean production ends at record levels.
- **S7** — [U.S. Soybean Production Expands Since 2002 as Farmers Adopt New Practices, Technologies](https://ers.usda.gov/amber-waves/2023/july/u-s-soybean-production-expands-since-2002-as-farmers-adopt-new-practices-technologies) (accessed 2026-07-23): Productivity and commercial-retention proxy: from 2002 to 2022 soybean acreage rose 18% and yields 30%, while inflation-adjusted cost per bushel fell as technology and practices changed.
