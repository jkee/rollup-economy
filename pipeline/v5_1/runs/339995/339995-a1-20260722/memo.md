# 339995 — Burial Casket Manufacturing

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `339995-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** STRUCTURAL_OUT · A 5.13 · L 0.82 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → LOW_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Time-critical repeat orders and personalization create information and logistics workflows where AI can improve accuracy and speed around an indispensable physical product.
**Weakness:** Cremation's sustained share gains create a material five-year decline in the current burial-casket demand basket.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market manufacturers of metal, wood, cloth-covered, oversize, veteran, personalized, and related burial caskets supplied repeatedly to independent funeral homes, distributors, cemeteries, and direct purchasers.
- Excluded: Concrete burial vault makers, funeral homes without casket manufacturing, pure distributors, urn-only or memorial-product firms, captive plants, and platform-scale casket companies outside the lower middle market.
- Customer and revenue model: Revenue is primarily repeat business-to-business product supply to funeral professionals through catalogs, scheduled or rapid delivery, inventory programs, and personalized configurations, with some direct-to-consumer orders delivered to funeral homes under the Funeral Rule.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Burial casket manufacturing has repeat funeral-home workflows, time-sensitive distribution, and personalization tasks that AI can improve, but its addressable market is in structural decline as cremation gains share. The physical product remains operator-required; the more serious issue is whether enough independent LMM manufacturers exist and can offset falling units through share, adjacency, or consolidation.

## How AI changes the work
AI can streamline product selection, order capture, personalized designs, inventory planning, purchasing, route scheduling, customer communications, and documentation. Manufacturing, finishing, upholstery, inspection, warehousing, and deadline-critical delivery remain physical, and emotional sensitivity raises the quality bar.

## Value capture
Reliable rapid delivery and personalization can defend price, especially for funeral-home relationships. FTC price transparency and outside-casket rights, direct sellers, large suppliers, and declining burial volume give buyers alternatives and limit retention of productivity gains.

## Firm availability
Census reports 72 employer establishments, while the frozen model estimates only five LMM firms. Large national suppliers dominate public visibility, so the actionable independent universe may be smaller still; closures driven by cremation are not automatically qualifying transfers.

## Demand durability
Total deaths remain around three million annually, but NFDA projects cremation at 63.4% and burial at 31.6% in 2025, with cremation continuing higher. That creates a clear negative volume bridge for burial caskets even if population aging supports overall deaths.

## Risks and uncertainty
Principal risks are cremation and green-disposition substitution, very small target supply, large-supplier concentration, funeral-home consolidation, direct-to-consumer competition, FTC transparency, steel and lumber costs, bulky inventory and delivery, personalization errors, and the possibility that an owner exit becomes liquidation rather than a transferable business.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation source is broader than the six-digit industry.
- a: Vertically integrated distribution can materially change the wage-weighted task mix.
- rho: Failure costs are high because products are time-critical and emotionally sensitive.
- rho: Some large suppliers already offer digital selection and ordering tools, reducing not-yet-automated opportunity.
- e: Only five LMM firms are estimated in the frozen input, so a single classification error is material.
- e: Large national manufacturers dominate visible public information.
- s5: Establishments are not independent firms.
- s5: Broad manufacturing transaction evidence does not distinguish going-concern sales from distressed asset exits.
- q: FTC rules govern funeral providers, not manufacturer transfer prices directly.
- q: Large-supplier capabilities may not reflect LMM pricing power.
- d5: Disposition forecasts are not product-shipment data.
- d5: A manufacturer that successfully shifts to cremation containers or adjacent memorial goods may outperform the current burial-casket basket.
- o: Cremation substitution belongs principally in d5 rather than operator share.
- o: Digital sales can change channel ownership without eliminating physical manufacture.
