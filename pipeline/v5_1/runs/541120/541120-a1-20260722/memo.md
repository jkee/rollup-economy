## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market offices, including Puerto Rico practices, whose recurring or repeat outsourced work is drafting, approving, executing, receiving, indexing, or storing legal instruments such as real-estate instruments, wills, powers, trusts, and contracts, with the legally empowered notary retaining professional accountability.
- Excluded: Law firms primarily practicing law (541110); ordinary notaries limited to oaths, affidavits, depositions, or signature witnessing without power to draw and approve legal documents (541199); title abstract and settlement offices (541191); captive government or corporate notarial units; shells and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Individuals, property participants, financial institutions, and recurring professional-referral channels pay statutory or negotiated per-instrument fees plus permissible preparation, research, consultation, certification, and related legal-service fees; repeat business is relationship- and channel-driven rather than subscription-based.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
This is a narrow, regulated legal-document industry, not the familiar mass of side-gig signature witnesses. AI can compress drafting, review, intake, indexing, reporting, and retrieval, while tariff-style pricing can preserve a meaningful share of the gain. The central constraint is that professional authority, judgment, public faith, and protocols remain tied to licensed people, and the absolute LMM firm population is unknown.

## How AI changes the work
The practical AI stack is document-centric: structured intake, precedent retrieval, first drafts, clause comparison, consistency and missing-field checks, legal-research assistance, index preparation, and workflow reminders. Licensed notaries must validate facts and intent, give required warnings, correct defects, protect confidentiality, and own the final instrument, so AI is an assistive production layer rather than an autonomous notary.

## Value capture
Statutory minimums and value-based fees for many Puerto Rico instruments separate revenue from hours worked, creating room to retain labor savings. Capture is less secure for negotiated preparation and advisory fees, where customers and competitors can demand lower prices, and BLS explicitly notes that legal AI productivity can reduce billable hours and support more competitive pricing.

## Firm availability
The industry should contain repeat, channel-driven practices, but transferability is unusually personal: admission to law practice and a notarial examination are required in Puerto Rico, and a departing notary's protocols move into a regulated archive. The broad owner population is old, yet many cessations may be closures or archive handoffs rather than control sales. The frozen n input is missing, and CBP excludes 541120, so absolute opportunity cannot be inferred from this packet.

## Demand durability
Legally consequential real-estate, estate, power, trust, contract, and non-contentious matters continue to require formal instruments. Puerto Rico's 0.6% population decline in 2024-2025 is a volume headwind, but aging may support estate-related matters and law keeps the accountable notary central. Property and credit cycles remain the largest near-term swing factors.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest evidence gaps are the true geographic and occupational mix of 541120, an industry-specific task-and-wage study, observed automation realization, LMM firm counts, and qualifying practice sales. The frozen labor ratio is an ancestor 5411 proxy with a vintage mismatch, while the strongest operating evidence is Puerto Rico-specific. Misclassification with law firms, title offices, and ordinary notary-public services could materially change every conclusion.

## Caveats
- a: The 44% benchmark is broader legal work, not a 541120 occupation mix or wage-weighted measure.
- a: The frozen compensation ratio is LOW quality and measured at ancestor 5411 using 2024 wages over 2022 receipts before IPS harmonization; broad legal services may have a materially different labor mix from licensed notarial offices.
- a: Puerto Rico rules are a concrete fit for empowered civil-law notarial practice but may not describe every U.S. establishment classified in 541120.
- rho: No industry-specific adoption or realized-hours study was located.
- rho: The Integrated Notarial System already digitizes some reporting, so estimates must exclude work already automated at the current baseline.
- rho: Implementation varies with office size, document standardization, legacy records, Spanish-language tooling, and local professional rules.
- e: There is no defensible n-band value, and this percentage must not be used to infer an absolute eligible-firm count.
- e: Census explicitly excludes 541120 from County Business Patterns, limiting establishment-level size and geography evidence.
- e: The general employer-firm sole-ownership evidence is not industry-specific and only informs the breadth of owner dependence.
- s5: Owner age is from 2018 responding employer businesses across industries, not 541120 firms or the LMM band.
- s5: A protocol's transfer to an archive is not a qualifying business control transfer.
- s5: No observed five-year deal cohort for this NAICS code was located.
- q: The statutory fee text is presented by Justia and carries its warning that official sources may be more current.
- q: Fee rules are jurisdiction-specific; the national revenue mix and share subject to Puerto Rico's tariff are not observed.
- q: This estimate addresses commercial pass-through only and does not treat implementation difficulty or demand loss as leakage.
- d5: A single-year population change is a weak proxy for constant-price, constant-quality notarial demand.
- d5: The geographic mix of all U.S. 541120 establishments is not observed; Puerto Rico evidence may dominate the conceptual fit without representing every establishment.
- d5: The estimate holds service quality constant and excludes AI-driven price changes.
- o: The operator requirement is strongest in Puerto Rico's civil-law notarial regime and may be weaker elsewhere.
- o: Legal reform could narrow mandatory notarization or authorize new digital public-service models within five years.
- o: The estimate allows software to remove ancillary document work even when the final act still requires a notary.
