## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market 111998 growers that repeatedly provide crop-production services under production contracts in which an external contractor owns the crop or key inputs, directs production, and pays the grower a fee; the most plausible included cases are contract seed growers and similar specialty-crop operators.
- Excluded: Spot-market growers; growers using only marketing contracts while retaining ownership and production control; direct-to-consumer farms; maple, hop, mint, hemp, spice, agave, and combination-crop farms that simply sell their own output; custom farming or management-only operations classified as agricultural support activities; shells, captive units, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Recurring production fees paid by seed companies, processors, or other crop owners under agreements specifying production practices, quantity or acreage, quality, and delivery; ordinary crop-sale revenue is excluded.
- Deviation from default lens: The code is heterogeneous and overwhelmingly product farming, which is incoherent with the fixed outsourced-service lens. It is therefore narrowed to genuine fee-paid production-contract growing, the crop arrangement USDA distinguishes from product-focused marketing contracts; the narrowing is based on business-model coherence, not attractiveness.

## Executive view
NAICS 111998 is principally a product-farming category, so only a small exception—fee-paid production-contract growers—fits the recurring outsourced-service lens. Within that exception, AI can improve the information layer of farm operations, but physical production remains dominant, firm availability is poorly measured, and buyer-controlled contract economics limit confidence in retained gains.

## How AI changes the work
AI is most applicable to crop monitoring interpretation, input and irrigation recommendations, scheduling, records, compliance documentation, forecasting, procurement, and contractor communication. Harvesting, material handling, equipment operation, repair, irrigation setup, and field execution remain physical; robotics and precision equipment matter, but deployment is crop-specific and capital intensive.

## Value capture
A grower paid a production fee can initially retain savings achieved inside the contract term. At renewal, a sophisticated contractor can observe performance, compare growers, reset formulas, or demand lower fees, so durable retention depends on contract duration, bargaining power, differentiated agronomy, and whether the grower owns the enabling data and equipment.

## Firm availability
USDA evidence indicates genuine production contracts are rare across farms and, among crops, concentrated in seed and a few other categories. The aging farm population creates succession pressure, but planned farmland transfers remain limited and do not map cleanly to transferable operating-company control. The missing LMM firm-count input is therefore a consequential recorded gap.

## Demand durability
Contractors will still need physical crops and accountable growers, but demand for the narrow outsourced service can move independently of end consumption. Buyers may integrate production, switch crops or regions, reduce contracted acreage, or use marketing contracts instead. Recent hop contraction and stable-to-lower maple output illustrate mixed commodity conditions rather than a common growth trend.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest risk is category fit: ordinary 111998 farms sell products and are not service providers. Other material uncertainties are the unobserved eligible-firm count, absent six-digit labor economics, crop-mix volatility, weather and biological risk, regulation of hemp or cannabis, buyer concentration, old evidence on seed production contracts, and the difference between land transfers and company-control transfers.

## Caveats
- a: O*NET covers crop, nursery, and greenhouse farmworkers nationally, not NAICS 111998 or the fee-paid contract-growing subset.
- a: No six-digit occupational employment mix was found, so the estimate is not directly wage weighted from observed 111998 employment.
- a: The frozen labor-share input is null, so this task share cannot be translated into an industry labor-cost opportunity from this packet.
- rho: Precision agriculture is not synonymous with AI, and much of the cited equipment is already adopted rather than part of the remaining opportunity.
- rho: USDA adoption evidence emphasizes major row crops and farm-size categories, not the 111998 contract-growing lens.
- rho: Weather, field connectivity, seasonal labor, biological variation, and contractor-mandated practices may slow implementation.
- e: The production-contract incidence anchor is for all farms, and the seed-contract evidence is dated.
- e: A repeat marketing contract does not qualify because it remains a sale of farmer-owned output rather than outsourced production service.
- e: The frozen LMM firm-count input is missing, so even a bounded eligibility share cannot yield a defensible eligible-firm count.
- s5: Land ownership transfer is not the same as operating-company control transfer.
- s5: The USDA evidence covers all farms and farmland rather than NAICS 111998 or LMM production contractors.
- s5: Aging alone does not establish sale intent, transferability, or an external control transaction.
- q: No source directly measures five-year retention of AI-enabled gross benefit in crop production contracts.
- q: Contract terms can differ sharply by seed, crop, buyer concentration, supplied inputs, acreage, and performance incentives.
- q: This estimate excludes ordinary marketing-contract and spot-market crop sellers under the lens.
- d5: One-year acreage and production changes are supply outcomes affected by weather and inventories, not direct measures of constant-price demand.
- d5: The 111998 crop mix is unusually heterogeneous and the eligible production-contract subset is not separately measured.
- d5: Cannabis and hemp demand and regulation can be volatile and geographically fragmented.
- o: Operator requirement is inferred from production tasks rather than observed as a five-year quantity.
- o: Vertical integration by buyers would reduce demand for the lens firm even though physical crop production continues.
- o: Autonomous machinery may reduce human labor more rapidly than it removes the need for an accountable operating company.

## Sources
- **S1** — [2022 North American Industry Classification System United States Manual](https://www.census.gov/naics/reference_files_tools/2022_NAICS_Manual.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-22): Defines 111998 as growing otherwise-unclassified crops, mixed crops, or gathering tea or maple sap; examples include agave, mint, hay and grass seed, hops, and spices.
- **S2** — [O*NET: Farmworkers and Laborers, Crop, Nursery, and Greenhouse](https://www.onetonline.org/link/details/45-2092.00) (accessed 2026-07-22): Shows a task profile dominated by physical activity, handling, machinery, irrigation, inspection, harvesting, and repair, alongside AI-relevant planning, analysis, records, monitoring, and communication tasks.
- **S3** — [Precision Agriculture in the Digital Era: Recent Adoption on U.S. Farms](https://www.ers.usda.gov/publications/105893) (accessed 2026-07-22): Documents broad crop-farm digital and automation adoption, including 5-25% adoption of several mapping or variable-rate tools on selected crops and automated guidance on more than half of acreage for several major crops.
- **S4** — [Precision agriculture use increases with farm size and varies widely by technology](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/charts-of-note/110550) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports 2023 guidance-system use by 52% of midsize and 70% of large-scale crop farms, 68% use of yield or soil mapping among large-scale crop farms, and lower adoption among small farms.
- **S5** — [Farmers' Use of Contracts Has Declined Over Last 25 Years](https://ers.usda.gov/amber-waves/2022/june/farmers-use-of-contracts-has-declined-over-last-25-years) (accessed 2026-07-22): Defines marketing versus production contracts, reports 5% and 2% of U.S. farms using them respectively in 2020, and states most contracted crop production uses marketing contracts.
- **S6** — [Agricultural Contracting: Trading Autonomy for Risk Reduction](https://www.ers.usda.gov/amber-waves/2006/february/agricultural-contracting-trading-autonomy-for-risk-reduction) (accessed 2026-07-22): States that 96% of contract crop production used marketing contracts in 2003, production contracts were common only for seed and some vegetable or flower crops, and production-contract farmers provide services for fee-like payment.
- **S7** — [USDA releases 2022 Census of Agriculture data](https://data.nass.usda.gov/Newsroom/2024/02-13-2024.php) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports 1.9 million farms, 95% family ownership and operation, average producer age of 58.1, and a 61% Census response rate.
- **S8** — [Most of the U.S. Rented Farmland is Owned by Non-Farmers](https://www.nass.usda.gov/Newsroom/2026/03-12-2026.php) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports that about 43 million acres, roughly 5% of U.S. farmland, were planned to transfer to others within five years, including 23 million acres to nonrelatives.
- **S9** — [Crop Production, June 2025](https://www.nass.usda.gov/Publications/Todays_Reports/reports/crop0625.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports 2025 U.S. hop acreage strung for harvest down 6% from 2024 and maple syrup production of 5.77 million gallons, down 2% from the prior season.
