# 541519 — Other Computer Related Services

**Base tier:** HIGHEST_PRIORITY · A 8.20 · L 6.83 · interval CONDITIONAL → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** A large technical work share, contracted recurring pricing, and already-deployed automation in ticketing, patching, and monitoring create credible labor leverage.
**Weakness:** Residual-category heterogeneity and fast vendor-led automation make it difficult to know which firms retain differentiated, operator-required demand rather than commoditized support or pass-through revenue.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market firms in NAICS 541519 that deliver recurring or repeat outsourced computer-related services to external customers, including computer disaster recovery, software or hardware installation, technical support, monitoring, backup, cybersecurity, domain-related services, and residual computer consulting that does not belong in programming, systems design, facilities management, hosting, publishing, training, repair, or distribution codes.
- Excluded: Captive internal IT units, shells, non-control financing vehicles, one-person practices without transferable operations, and firms whose primary activity belongs in custom programming, systems integration or design, computer facilities management, data processing or hosting, software publishing, training, repair, telecommunications, or hardware and software wholesaling. Product-resale-only operations and nonrecurring installation practices without repeat outsourced service are outside the lens.
- Customer and revenue model: Customers include small and midsize businesses, larger enterprises, and public-sector organizations. Revenue can combine monthly managed-service subscriptions, per-user or per-device charges, fixed-fee bundles, disaster-recovery and backup retainers, incident-response fees, hourly consulting, installation projects, and software or hardware pass-throughs.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Other Computer Related Services combines a technical workforce, recurring support economics, and visible AI adoption with an unusually loose residual-industry boundary. Routine ticketing, monitoring, documentation, and scripting offer substantial labor leverage, but cyber risk, physical installation, complex escalation, and client accountability preserve human-led service delivery.

## How AI changes the work
AI changes the operating model through automated triage, knowledge retrieval, response drafting, patch and monitoring workflows, code and script assistance, reporting, and increasingly autonomous remediation. Technicians shift toward exception handling, architecture context, security judgment, user communication, physical work, and approval of consequential changes.

## Value capture
Per-user, per-device, subscription, and bundled contracts can retain productivity gains until renewal and support more customers per technician. Competition, shrinking deal sizes, service-level commitments, hourly work, license pass-through, and customer repricing pressure share part of the gain over the full horizon.

## Firm availability
General succession intentions and heavy MSP acquisition activity support a real transfer pipeline. The exact pool is less certain because 541519 includes project firms, resellers, owner-dependent consultants, and neighboring-service businesses whose classification, recurring revenue, and transferability differ materially.

## Demand durability
Cybersecurity, backup and recovery, compliance, cloud complexity, AI deployment, and small-business outsourcing support expanding demand. Basic help-desk work, routine installation, and monitoring face self-service and software substitution, so durability depends on moving toward accountable security, recovery, and complex managed outcomes.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main uncertainty is classification: exact-industry occupation, contract, adoption, and transaction data are scarce, forcing reliance on broader computer-services and global MSP proxies. Cyber liability, customer concentration, vendor dependence, commoditization, rapid tool change, and an uncertain mix of recurring service versus projects and resale can move outcomes materially.

## Caveats
- a: BLS publishes the occupational mix at broader NAICS 541500, not exact NAICS 541519, and employment shares are not payroll shares.
- a: The software-developer experiment and Claude usage data cover task populations that overlap only part of this residual service category.
- a: The estimate explicitly discounts work already automated, but no exact-industry baseline separates conventional automation from remaining tasks.
- a: The injected labor-intensity input combines 2024 QCEW wages with 2022 SUSB receipts and the specified IPS harmonization; that vintage mismatch is not direct evidence about task exposure.
- rho: Both implementation sources survey MSPs globally and may not represent disaster-recovery, installation, or residual consulting firms classified in 541519.
- rho: Self-reported use and workload automation do not independently verify quality, durability, or labor-hour savings.
- rho: The respondents are reached through industry and vendor channels that may select for more technology-active firms.
- e: The official definition and survey form identify included and neighboring activities but do not measure the eligible share of firms.
- e: The residual category is unusually heterogeneous and classification practices may vary among similar providers.
- e: The injected count of 705 firms is margin-bridged from SUSB size classes using a 15.65% EBITDA margin, so its band assignment is uncertain even though it is not re-estimated here.
- s5: The succession survey is cross-industry and measures plans rather than completed qualifying control transfers.
- s5: The transaction report covers the global MSP market, a narrower business model that only partially overlaps NAICS 541519.
- s5: Deal-count definitions and small private transaction disclosure are not standardized.
- s5: The estimated 705-firm denominator inherits uncertainty from size-class receipts and the 15.65% margin bridge.
- q: The billing survey is vendor-sponsored, global, and specific to MSPs rather than exact NAICS 541519.
- q: Predominant billing model does not reveal revenue weights, contract duration, or post-automation repricing behavior.
- q: Recent MSP evidence reports shrinking deal sizes and strong competition, which may erode retention faster than historical pricing data imply.
- d5: BLS output is projected only at broader NAICS 541500 and includes custom programming, systems design, and facilities management excluded from 541519.
- d5: Vendor-survey revenue growth is self-reported, global, and reflects MSP service lines rather than constant-quality demand for the exact current basket.
- d5: Cybersecurity and AI can create new services while simultaneously automating legacy work, making category-level net demand unusually uncertain.
- o: No source directly measures the year-five operator-required share for NAICS 541519.
- o: The category mixes physically grounded installation and recovery with remote services that may be more readily self-served or software-supplied.
- o: A major security failure by an autonomous tool could raise operator demand, while reliable vendor-native agents could lower it sharply.
