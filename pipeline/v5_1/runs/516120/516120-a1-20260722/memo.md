## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market television-station operators selling recurring local advertising and digital campaigns, retransmission or affiliation distribution, and locally produced programming to external audiences, advertisers, and distributors.
- Excluded: National television networks, captive or internal-only operations, shell license holders without operating activity, non-control financing vehicles, and stations whose licenses or operations cannot be transferred with regulatory approval.
- Customer and revenue model: Recurring and repeat revenue from local and regional advertising, negotiated retransmission consent or affiliation economics, sponsored content, and related digital media services; the accountable operator controls programming, sales, distribution, and compliance.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Local television combines a labor-intensive newsroom and production base with recurring advertising, retransmission, and digital workflows. AI can reduce routine production and support work, but the opportunity remains constrained by secular linear-TV pressure, regulatory transfer requirements, group ownership, and a missing defensible count of LMM firms.

## How AI changes the work
The most plausible five-year changes are assisted research and scripting, transcription, clipping, captioning, graphics, versioning, metadata, content repurposing, ad trafficking, analytics, and administrative support. Live reporting, editorial judgment, relationship sales, engineering, and accountable compliance remain human-led, with review especially important for news accuracy.

## Value capture
Negotiated advertising and retransmission prices are not direct labor-cost pass-throughs, enabling initial margin retention. Competitive ad markets, renewal repricing, network and content payments, commissions, and reinvestment in digital products share away a meaningful portion over five years.

## Firm availability
Station transactions and general employer-owner succession intent indicate potential supply, but neither provides a denominator for independently transferable LMM station firms. The declared dataset gap for firm count is material; FCC licenses, ownership groups, shared-services contracts, and network relationships must be resolved asset by asset.

## Demand durability
Traditional television demand is declining, yet local news, sports, political advertising, emergency information, retransmission, and station digital extensions support continued use. The surviving service basket should still need an accountable operator, though audience and advertiser migration to direct digital platforms creates downside.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main risks are faster cord-cutting and audience fragmentation, ad-price pressure, network affiliation changes, retransmission disputes, content and sports-rights costs, FCC transfer limits, labor or newsroom quality failures, and overestimating remaining tasks where automation already exists. Proxy evidence is not a substitute for station-level payroll, transaction, and contract diligence.

## Caveats
- a: The May 2023 occupation mix predates much of the industry's generative-AI adoption and measures employees rather than tasks or wages by workflow.
- a: The estimate concerns current not-yet-automated work; stations that already automate traffic, transcription, clipping, or master control have less remaining exposure.
- rho: The Reuters sample is publishers rather than a probability sample of US local television stations.
- rho: Exploration and perceived importance are not equivalent to operational implementation or labor substitution.
- e: The frozen dataset provides no defensible LMM firm-count band, so the population size cannot be checked and this estimates only the conditional eligible share.
- e: Licensee, operating company, station, and broadcast group are not interchangeable units; shared-services and affiliation contracts can impair transferability.
- s5: Owner plans are not completed deals and Gallup is not industry-specific.
- s5: Prominent broadcast transactions are value-weighted examples and may overrepresent large groups relative to LMM owners.
- q: Retention varies sharply between advertising, retransmission, affiliation, and digital-service revenue.
- q: This range excludes volume loss, which is represented in demand and operator-required primitives.
- d5: Revenue forecasts confound price, mix, currency, and quantity and therefore require a proxy bridge.
- d5: Political advertising creates a four-year cycle, making endpoints unusually sensitive to election timing.
- o: The boundary between an operator-required station service and self-served platform distribution may shift rapidly.
- o: This is conditional on surviving demand and does not repeat the volume decline captured in d5.
