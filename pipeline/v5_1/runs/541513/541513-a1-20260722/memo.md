# 541513 — Computer Facilities Management Services

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `541513-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** PRIORITY · A 7.94 · L 4.95 · interval CONDITIONAL → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Recurring unit-based contracts and information-heavy support workflows create a credible path to AI-enabled technician leverage and avoidable hiring.
**Weakness:** The modern MSP evidence base is broader than NAICS 541513, while cloud migration and existing automation can erode the specific client-facility service basket being screened.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market firms primarily providing recurring outsourced management, operation, monitoring, administration, maintenance, and user or network support for external customers' computer systems or data-processing facilities, including on-site and hybrid delivery consistent with NAICS 541513.
- Excluded: Captive internal IT departments, temporary staffing, custom software development, systems-integration design, pure consulting, operation of the provider's own hosting or data-processing infrastructure, hardware resale without managed operations, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Customers are businesses and institutions that outsource some or all day-to-day IT operations. Revenue commonly recurs under managed-services agreements priced per user, per device, as tiered bundles, or as fixed monthly subscriptions, with project, incident, and resale revenue as supplements. Service levels, security duties, supported assets, user counts, and response obligations shape price and delivery cost.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Computer facilities management is a recurring, digitally delivered service in which AI can materially change technician throughput without removing the need for a trusted operator. Ticket triage, knowledge retrieval, documentation, routine diagnostics, scripting, and monitoring interpretation are exposed, but privileged access, cyber risk, heterogeneous customer estates, physical work, and SLA accountability keep the likely outcome human-supervised. The firm universe is sizable but modeled, and modern MSP activity often crosses adjacent NAICS codes.

## How AI changes the work
AI can classify and summarize tickets, retrieve customer-specific documentation, suggest resolutions and scripts, correlate alerts, draft change plans, automate routine user administration, update knowledge bases, and guide junior technicians. Complex incidents, security-sensitive changes, physical installation, architecture, vendor escalation, customer communication, and final approval remain operator work. Existing RMM, scripting, and chatbots reduce the incremental opportunity relative to a greenfield workflow.

## Value capture
Per-user, per-device, bundled, and fixed-subscription pricing allows a provider to serve more endpoints or avoid hiring without immediately reducing revenue. Benefits can appear as lower ticket cost, faster response, wider coverage, fewer escalations, and better technician leverage. Renewal negotiation, transparent competition, SLA reinvestment, customer churn, and rising software or security costs will share part of the gain.

## Firm availability
The official NAICS definition fits recurring outsourced operations, and managed-services M&A has active strategic and sponsor-backed buyers. The main diligence problem is classification: many firms combine consulting, projects, resale, hosting, security, and facilities support. A verified target must have durable recurring contracts, assignable customer relationships, professionalized operations, and limited owner or single-client dependence.

## Demand durability
Customer reliance on networks, cloud applications, cybersecurity, and continuous support sustains demand, especially where smaller organizations cannot staff expertise internally. External providers also benefit from scale across customers. The weakness is code-specific: cloud migration, direct vendor services, chatbots, and routine automation may reduce on-site computer-facility work even while the broader MSP market expands.

## Risks and uncertainty
Privileged-access compromise can affect many customers, so weak controls, hallucinated remediation, or autonomous changes can create losses larger than labor savings. Other risks include tool fragmentation, vendor dependence, price competition, customer concentration, misclassified targets, cloud disintermediation, and acquisition integration failures. Direct evidence is missing for the NAICS occupation mix, current automation baseline, autonomous-resolution rates, real demand, and completed control-transfer frequency.

## Caveats
- a: The occupational sources include in-house IT workers and adjacent computer-services industries.
- a: O*NET importance scores are not hours or wage weights.
- a: The frozen compensation ratio mixes 2024 wages with 2022 receipts and then applies an IPS harmonization factor.
- a: The boundary between conventional automation and incremental AI is especially difficult in this software-intensive industry.
- rho: The field experiment studied customer-service chats at one software company, not managed IT operations.
- rho: The MSP survey is global, vendor-sponsored, and does not identify respondents by NAICS.
- rho: Existing users may report time savings without reducing staffing or avoiding future hiring.
- e: The supplied LMM count is margin-bridged from firm-size data using a broad services margin and is not a verified acquisition universe.
- e: Modern MSP activity crosses hosting, systems design, consulting, security, and support classifications, making firm-level NAICS accuracy uncertain.
- e: The UK MSP study confirms broad service mixing but is not evidence about U.S. 541513 eligibility.
- s5: Gallup is cross-industry and includes transfers that may not qualify as outside control events.
- s5: The MSP deal count is global, uses a broader sector definition, and has no directly comparable denominator.
- s5: Platform recapitalizations and add-on purchases may cause deal counts to overstate unique eligible sellers.
- q: The pricing survey is from 2023, global, vendor-sponsored, and broader than the NAICS lens.
- q: Security and cloud vendor costs can rise with supported users or endpoints even when technician labor falls.
- q: Customers remain responsible for their own cyber risk, which can intensify contract scrutiny and benefit sharing.
- d5: Employment is not service quantity and automation can reduce labor while demand rises.
- d5: The strongest market-growth evidence covers global MSPs rather than U.S. computer facilities management.
- d5: Recurring security, cloud, and consulting revenue may be classified outside 541513.
- d5: A shift from client facilities to provider-owned cloud infrastructure can lower this NAICS service basket even if total outsourced IT spending grows.
- o: Accountability requirements differ by customer sector, contract, and system criticality.
- o: Provider responsibility and customer legal liability are distinct, so formal contracts do not prove every task requires a human.
- o: The evolution of trusted agent permissions is unusually uncertain over the five-year horizon.
