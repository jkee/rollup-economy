## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market manufacturers of industrial separation, screening, sifting, centrifuge, and filtration machinery that provide repeat aftermarket parts, replacement elements or screens, rebuilds, or ongoing service to external industrial and municipal customers.
- Excluded: Automatic fire-sprinkler systems, balers, bridge and gate lifting machinery, cremating ovens, hydraulic or pneumatic jacks, scales and balances, makers without recurring external-customer revenue, captive units, and platform-scale manufacturers outside the lower middle market.
- Customer and revenue model: The coherent sub-lens sells engineered capital equipment through quotes and applications work, followed by recurring replacement screens, filter elements, wear parts, maintenance, field service, rebuilds, and emergency support over a long installed-base life.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 333998 is a residual code spanning unrelated products including fire-sprinkler systems, balers, bridge lifts, centrifuges, cremation ovens, sieves, jacks, industrial filters, and scales. One commercial and demand screen would be incoherent, so the lens is narrowed to industrial separation, screening, centrifuge, and filtration equipment with common process-industry customers and recurring aftermarket workflows; the narrowing is for business-model coherence, not attractiveness.

## Executive view
The residual NAICS code is too heterogeneous for one useful operating thesis, so this packet evaluates a coherent separation, screening, centrifuge, and industrial-filtration sub-lens. That sub-lens combines long-lived physical equipment with repeat parts and service, creating an information-rich aftermarket where AI can improve speed and consistency. The decisive limitation is that the LMM firm-count input is missing, leaving absolute roll-up availability unresolved.

## How AI changes the work
AI can retrieve configurations and prior applications, draft quotes and manuals, cross-reference replacement parts, assist troubleshooting, summarize inspection findings, schedule field work, and improve procurement and installed-base marketing. Engineers and technicians remain accountable for material compatibility, sanitation, process performance, rotating-equipment balance, and field execution.

## Value capture
Proprietary installed-base parts, urgent repairs, rebuilds, and engineered replacements should retain more benefit than transparent cross-reference filters or screens. Uptime and process risk support value-based pricing, but procurement competition and renewal repricing prevent full retention.

## Firm availability
Eligibility is plausible for independent manufacturers with repeat aftermarket revenue, but the residual code does not identify the narrowed product families cleanly. Because the supplied LMM firm count is explicitly missing, no absolute acquisition pool is inferred; firm-by-firm product and ownership mapping is required.

## Demand durability
Food, pharmaceutical, chemical, mineral, plastics, wastewater, industrial, and municipal processes continue to require physical separation and filtration. Replacement media and wear parts add durability, while current industrial output and water-infrastructure finance provide modest support. Capital cycles, imports, long equipment lives, and customer-specific project timing keep the range wide.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest risks are classification heterogeneity and the missing firm count. Other uncertainties include installed-base data quality, proprietary-versus-compatible aftermarket mix, end-market concentration, export exposure, tariffs, product liability, process safety, technician scarcity, long sales cycles, and whether a target is truly a manufacturer rather than a distributor or service shop.
