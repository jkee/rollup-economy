## Business-model lens
- Included: United States lower-middle-market organizations that repeatedly raise, pool, administer, and distribute charitable funds for educational, scientific, cultural, health, public-benefit, and other social-welfare purposes outside voluntary health organizations.
- Excluded: Voluntary health organizations, grantmaking foundations classified elsewhere, religious and political organizations, shells, captive corporate programs, inactive charities, non-control financing vehicles, and organizations whose donor relationships, governance, or key-person identity cannot transfer.
- Customer and revenue model: Revenue commonly comes from recurring donations, workplace and federated campaigns, grants, sponsorships, bequests, events, and administrative or program awards. Donors and grantors provide funds that the organization solicits, stewards, and directs to external beneficiaries or charitable programs, so payor and beneficiary are often different parties.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Other grantmaking and giving services have an unusually information-intensive fundraising and administration mix, but only part of the industry fits the outsourced-service lens and nonprofit control transfers remain rare.

## How AI changes the work
AI can assist prospect research, segmentation, appeals, proposals, acknowledgments, campaign testing, document intake, compliance preparation, grant screening, reporting, and routine donor service. Major relationships, allocation decisions, fiduciary review, community engagement, and governance remain human-accountable.

## Value capture
Fixed awards and unrestricted funding can turn efficiency into broader fundraising reach, grant throughput, mission capacity, or reserves. Donor restrictions, competitive fundraising, platform costs, governance controls, and renewal rebudgeting limit retention.

## Firm availability
Scaled organizations may have durable donor files, brands, staff, systems, campaign relationships, and administrative processes. Many others are affiliates, donor-controlled, campaign-specific, mission-bound, or key-person dependent, and charitable assets cannot support a conventional equity sale.

## Demand durability
Charitable giving remains large and recently grew in real terms, supporting continued fundraising and stewardship demand. Asset markets, tax policy, cause rotation, direct digital giving, and large-donor concentration can produce abrupt changes in intermediary workload.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are weak fit to a purchased-service lens, nonprofit scale-band measurement, scarce qualifying transfers, donor concentration, restricted funds, privacy and cyber exposure, biased automated allocation, digital disintermediation, affiliate governance, and reputational damage from low-quality outreach.
