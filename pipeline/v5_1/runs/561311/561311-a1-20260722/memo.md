# 561311 — Employment Placement Agencies

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `561311-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** HIGHEST_PRIORITY · A 7.64 · L 6.07 · interval CONDITIONAL → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** A large digitally mediated workflow, combined with repeat employer demand and fee structures that can reward successful placement rather than hours worked, creates a credible path from AI assistance to higher recruiter capacity and retained margin.
**Weakness:** Value is vulnerable to hiring cyclicality and to the possibility that client and candidate relationships reside with individual recruiters, while self-service platforms and internal teams can compete away productivity gains.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Recurring outsourced vacancy intake, candidate sourcing, resume review, matching, screening, interviewing, referral, placement coordination, and client/candidate communication for external employers. The lens emphasizes established lower-middle-market agencies with repeat employer relationships and approximately $1 million to $10 million of normalized EBITDA.
- Excluded: Executive search services classified in 561312, temporary help services in 561320, professional employer organizations in 561330, captive internal talent-acquisition teams, pure recruiting-software or job-board businesses, shell or non-operating entities, non-control situations, and registries or owner-dependent practices that cannot support a transferable operating company.
- Customer and revenue model: Employers are the principal economic customers. Revenue is generally earned through successful-placement, contingent, fixed, retained-project, or other recruiting-service fees; repeat hiring clients may generate multiple assignments even when each placement is episodic. Candidates are service participants rather than employees of the placement agency under the NAICS definition.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Employment placement agencies combine an unusually digital recruiting workflow with a relationship- and judgment-heavy client service. AI can assist a substantial portion of search, matching, drafting, outreach, scheduling, recordkeeping, and first-pass screening, but reliable placement still depends on human calibration, persuasion, exceptions, and accountability. The attractive operating form is a repeat-client agency whose relationships, recruiter team, process, and data survive the founder; a personal recruiter practice is materially less transferable.

## How AI changes the work
Near-term impact is more likely to be recruiter augmentation than fully autonomous placement. Current recruiting-professional evidence shows active experimentation and meaningful reported time savings, while the task map shows both machine-suitable digital work and frequent high-contact decisions. Over five years, integrated tools can increase desk capacity and response speed, but privacy, bias, adverse-impact testing, data quality, candidate experience, and client-specific judgment should keep humans in the loop for consequential selection steps.

## Value capture
Outcome- or project-linked fees can let an agency retain some productivity through more searches per recruiter, faster shortlists, or better service without repricing every labor hour. Capture will vary with demand and competition: a constrained hiring market may leave capacity unused, while sophisticated clients, internal recruiting teams, job platforms, and rival agencies may claim savings through lower fees or higher service expectations.

## Firm availability
The frozen firm-count estimate indicates a finite but meaningful lower-middle-market population. Actual five-year acquisition availability is narrower because founder timing, customer concentration, recruiter dependence, cyclical earnings, and relationship portability determine whether a nominal agency is a transferable control opportunity. Broad service-business transaction data demonstrate a functioning market but do not directly measure qualifying 561311 deals.

## Demand durability
BLS projects real output growth for the broader Employment Services industry and faster-than-average employment growth for Human Resources Specialists, supporting continued demand for hiring expertise. Permanent-placement agencies remain exposed to hiring cycles, client insourcing, self-service sourcing, and fee pressure, so specialty diversity, repeat-client behavior, and placement outcomes matter more than the broad projection alone.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are the absence of 561311-specific workflow and transaction datasets, the broad scope of adoption surveys and industry projections, heterogeneous fee models, cyclicality, and the pace at which employers accept autonomous screening or matching. Employment-law accountability and candidate privacy can slow realization, while platform advances could accelerate substitution in standardized roles. Founder and top-recruiter concentration can also erase otherwise attractive technology economics.

## Caveats
- a: The task map is occupational rather than industry-specific and includes broader HR work.
- a: Reported time savings among current AI users are not the same quantity as fully automatable wage-weighted labor share.
- a: Specialty, candidate seniority, client process, and compliance requirements cause substantial agency-level variation.
- rho: Survey experimentation can be shallow and need not become sustained production use.
- rho: International and corporate respondents may not represent U.S. lower-middle-market agencies.
- rho: Employment-law and disability-accommodation controls may slow autonomous screening more than assistive drafting or scheduling.
- e: No public source directly measures the share of establishments satisfying every default-lens criterion.
- e: Establishment counts and enterprise counts differ, particularly for multi-office agencies.
- e: A successful-placement model can produce repeat customers without contractually recurring revenue.
- s5: The market source is not NAICS-specific and is concentrated below the target EBITDA range.
- s5: Listing and closing data may omit private processes and sponsor-to-sponsor transactions.
- s5: Observed transferability depends heavily on customer and recruiter retention after the founder exits.
- q: Public evidence does not quantify the 561311 revenue mix by contingency, fixed, retained-project, or time-based fees.
- q: Saved recruiter time does not create value if client demand or candidate supply is the binding constraint.
- q: Competitive pass-through may vary sharply by specialty and local labor market.
- d5: The principal projection is for 561300 rather than 561311.
- d5: Real industry output is not a direct measure of constant-quality demand for a typical target agency.
- d5: Recruiting demand is highly cyclical and sensitive to hiring volumes and vacancy duration.
- o: The estimate separates continued need for an accountable operator from the number of staff required.
- o: Human involvement may persist because of regulation or client preference even if technical capability advances faster.
- o: Standardized roles and proprietary candidate networks can produce very different substitution risk.
