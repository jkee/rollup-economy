**Driver:** High recurring scouting, spraying, irrigation, forecasting and harvest-coordination needs create a credible precision-agriculture efficiency opportunity where acreage is contiguous and management is disciplined.
**Weakness:** Biological and weather-driven decline, especially in Florida, plus missing labor and firm-count data and the tendency for grove sales to become land conversions make the going-concern acquisition thesis fragile.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. private lower-middle-market owner-operators of commercial orange groves producing fruit for fresh sale or processing through recurring annual crop cycles, including grove scouting, irrigation, nutrition, pest and disease management, pruning, mowing, harvest coordination, and farm administration.
- Excluded: Packing houses, juice processors, nurseries, grove-care contractors, brokers, and equipment vendors unless orange growing is the primary business; hobby, residential, or noncommercial groves; land held mainly for development or extraction; growers primarily producing other citrus or crops; and operations outside the target EBITDA band.
- Customer and revenue model: Growers sell fresh or processing oranges by box, weight, grade, or contracted crop to packing houses, marketers, cooperatives, and juice processors. Revenue recurs with each harvest but varies sharply with yield, fruit quality, disease, storms, water, labor, market channel, imports, and realized grower prices.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Orange groves offer recurring crop revenue and clear precision-agriculture opportunities, but the investment case is dominated by biology, weather, water, land and market channel rather than AI. Autonomous scouting, precision spraying, irrigation control, forecasting and administrative tools can lower inputs and improve decisions; broad robotic harvesting remains constrained by grove design and fruit requirements. With both labor share and firm count declared missing, and with severe Florida contraction offset only partly by stronger California fresh-market production, this record carries unusually high underwriting uncertainty.

## How AI changes the work
The near-term stack is sensing and decision support: canopy and disease mapping, prescription spraying, irrigation and nutrition optimization, yield and harvest forecasts, labor and route planning, traceability and paperwork. Autonomous mowing or spraying can follow where terrain and row geometry permit. Full picking automation is a higher case because irregular canopies, occlusion, fruit damage, equipment uptime and legacy spacing remain difficult; processing fruit is more mechanically tolerant than fresh fruit.

## Value capture
A grower retains value when technology reduces chemicals, catches disease earlier, improves yield or quality, avoids crop loss or stretches scarce seasonal labor. Packing, processor and commodity pricing prevent full pass-through retention, while equipment, service and financing costs absorb part of the gain. The best candidates will have stable offtake, sufficient contiguous acreage, high equipment utilization and management systems that measure per-acre results.

## Firm availability
Grove acreage is demonstrably liquid, particularly where alternative development, mining or leasing value is high, but this does not equal saleability of a going-concern orange business. The target pool cannot be bounded because n is missing. A buyer must separate land value from normalized crop earnings and verify water, tree age and health, HLB exposure, contracts, seasonal workforce, insurance, debt, environmental obligations and owner dependence.

## Demand durability
End demand for oranges and juice persists, but domestic growers face import substitution and severe regional production risk. Florida's processing-oriented sector has suffered HLB, hurricanes and exits, while California leads orange production and primarily serves fresh markets but faces water, labor and quarantine risk. Five-year demand for domestic grove output is therefore likely to contract moderately in the base case with a broad range.

## Risks and uncertainty
The principal risks are HLB and other pests, hurricanes, freezes, heat and water, multi-year tree replacement, labor availability, commodity and processor concentration, imports, crop insurance, land conversion, and region-specific fresh versus processing economics. Research-stage robotics may not commercialize economically. Missing labor and firm-count inputs prevent a grounded estimate of both wage exposure and target availability.
