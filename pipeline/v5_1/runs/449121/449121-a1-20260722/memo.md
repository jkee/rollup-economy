## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market floor-covering retailers that repeatedly serve external residential, builder, property-manager, or commercial customers through product selection, estimating, ordering, delivery, installation coordination, installation, repair, and after-sale accountability.
- Excluded: Manufacturers and wholesalers without retail service, installation-only contractors outside the code, ceramic-tile-only or hardwood-only retailers classified elsewhere, captive chain units that cannot transfer independently, passive inventory or real-estate vehicles, and pure software marketplaces.
- Customer and revenue model: Project-based retail sales of flooring materials, frequently combined with measurement, estimating, delivery, installation, and repair; residential remodel is prominent, and operators generally quote a fixed project price with deposits and final payment tied to installation.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Floor-covering retail combines a fragmented independent channel, meaningful software and AI opportunity, and a durable physical project-delivery role. AI can streamline selling and coordination but cannot remove site and installation accountability. The missing LMM firm count prevents a target-number conclusion, while housing and remodeling cyclicality temper otherwise durable replacement demand.

## How AI changes the work
The practical use cases are room visualization, lead response, estimate and proposal drafting, waste and margin checks, product matching, digital marketing, purchasing, scheduling, invoicing, collections, and management reporting. Vertical business-management systems provide an implementation path. Physical samples, field measurement, substrate assessment, delivery, cutting, installation, repair, and warranty judgment remain human-heavy.

## Value capture
Bundled product-and-installation quotes are less directly comparable than shelf prices, which supports retention of reduced waste, faster cycle time, and lower administrative cost. Operators still compete with home centers, online sellers, buying groups, and local bids. Benefits will be shared through price, service, financing, and faster completion, but differentiated local execution can preserve a majority at the base case.

## Firm availability
The industry survey depicts a channel dominated by independent, single-location businesses, and trade sources document sustained acquisition activity. Broad owner-aging evidence supports further supply, though small-business exits often become closures. Transferability depends on installer relationships, estimating discipline, vendor terms, reputation, customer mix, and whether key know-how resides only with the owner.

## Demand durability
Aging floors and housing stock create replacement need, while renovation, turnover, construction, and commercial refurbishment create discretionary cycles. Real predecessor-industry output improved through 2025, but the near-term remodeling forecast slows below inflation and big-box stores are expanding. Five-year specialist demand is therefore centered near flat with a wide cyclical range.

## Risks and uncertainty
The compensation ratio is a broad retail ancestor estimate with vintage and basis adjustments, the firm-count input is missing, and occupation data combine furniture and furnishings. The strongest industry survey is vendor-sponsored and not probability sampled. Installer scarcity, rework, tariffs, material costs, big-box competition, weak housing activity, and owner dependence could overwhelm administrative savings.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS combines furniture and other home-furnishings retailers and excludes self-employed owners.
- a: Many retailers subcontract installation, so installer labor may sit outside payroll and the provided compensation ratio even though it remains operationally essential.
- a: Generative-AI-affected hours include augmentation and better selling, not only substitution or avoided hiring.
- rho: Technology interest does not prove implementation, continued use, or labor savings.
- rho: The survey sponsor sells flooring business software and does not establish a probability sample.
- rho: Errors in takeoffs, waste factors, product compatibility, or installation scope can erase savings.
- e: The frozen LMM firm-count input is missing.
- e: Survey respondents skew toward small independent software prospects and may not represent all firms.
- e: A business dependent on the owner's estimating, customer relationships, or installer network may not be transferable despite being independent.
- s5: Gallup and McKinsey are cross-industry sources.
- s5: Trade-publication acquisition counts include commercial dealers and do not measure eligible firms at risk.
- s5: Closure or sale of inventory and leases is not a qualifying transfer of continuing operations.
- q: No source directly measures post-AI benefit retention.
- q: Commercial and builder contracts may be more competitively bid and less retentive than residential remodel work.
- q: Measurement or specification errors can turn apparent efficiency into warranty and rework cost.
- d5: Legacy-code output is the closest direct time series but 2022 NAICS added portions of electronic and direct selling.
- d5: Remodeling spending covers many project types and is nominal, while this primitive requires constant price and quality.
- d5: Specialist-store quantity can diverge from total flooring demand as home centers and online channels gain share.
- o: Only 41% of surveyed stores reported full-time employee installers, so accountable operation does not imply installation payroll.
- o: Simple click-lock flooring and rugs are more self-serviceable than carpet, sheet goods, tile, or complex commercial work.
- o: Big-box retailers can remain accountable operators while displacing LMM specialists.
