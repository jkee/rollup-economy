# 621991 — Blood and Organ Banks

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `621991-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 6.07 · L 1.32 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** AI can compress standardized donor, quality, inventory, and logistics work around a physical service that hospitals and manufacturers still need performed by a regulated operator.
**Weakness:** The coherent independent target pool is hard to observe and likely constrained by nonprofit systems, large chains, existing automation, regulatory validation, and donor or customer concentration.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market independent operators providing recurring blood or source-plasma donor recruitment, collection, processing, testing coordination, storage, inventory management, and distribution services to hospitals or plasma-product manufacturers.
- Excluded: Organ procurement organizations, transplant programs, sperm, egg, embryo, placenta, and eye or tissue banks; captive hospital transfusion services; facilities owned inside national blood-center or plasma chains; shells and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Operators receive per-unit product or processing revenue, contracted hospital or manufacturer payments, collection and testing fees, and sometimes donor-related service revenue; customers require validated collection, traceability, quality, storage, and delivery rather than software alone.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 621991 combines blood and plasma operations with organ procurement and reproductive or tissue banking, whose regulation, ownership, workflows, and revenue models are too different for one coherent screen. The lens is narrowed to recurring independent blood and source-plasma collection, processing, storage, and distribution operations, without selecting among them for attractiveness.

## Executive view
Independent blood and plasma operations combine automatable information, quality, and logistics workflows with irreducibly physical collection and strict regulatory accountability. The opportunity is operationally real but narrower than generic healthcare-AI claims because donor-management, laboratory-interface, barcode, inventory, and apheresis systems already automate much of the obvious workflow.

## How AI changes the work
AI can improve donor recruitment and scheduling, questionnaire and document review, inventory forecasting, component allocation, exception routing, quality analytics, predictive maintenance, and shipping coordination. Venipuncture, donor observation, equipment handling, specimen processing, cold-chain execution, product release, and clinical interpretation remain human- and facility-dependent.

## Value capture
Per-unit and contract pricing can retain productivity, lower discard, and better inventory utilization until renewal. Hospital and manufacturer purchasing power, donor compensation, compliance expense, software and equipment cost, contract rebids, and constrained donor or customer demand reduce the retained share.

## Firm availability
The six-digit code overstates the coherent target pool because it includes organ procurement and reproductive or tissue banks, nonprofit systems, captive hospital operations, and sites inside national chains. Eligible firms need independent ownership, transferable registrations and contracts, a clean quality record, durable donor access, diversified customers, and management depth.

## Demand durability
National blood data indicate a stabilized baseline with declining RBC and plasma measures but growing platelet activity, while broader ambulatory output is projected to grow. Physical blood and plasma demand should remain essential, though component mix, patient blood management, donor availability, and vertical integration create uncertainty.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main gaps are a six-digit task mix, the already-automated baseline, independent-center implementation results, contract economics, eligible ownership, and completed transfers. A poor outcome would combine donor scarcity, adverse quality findings, cyber or operational downtime, high validation cost, customer concentration, low equipment utilization, and hospital or manufacturer consolidation.

## Caveats
- a: No six-digit occupation mix or task-time study was found for the narrowed blood and plasma lens.
- a: The BLS occupation table includes ambulance services and other miscellaneous ambulatory care, while O*NET spans all phlebotomy employers.
- a: FDA device clearances establish existing technical capabilities but not adoption or labor hours saved at lower-middle-market centers.
- rho: Device authorization and documented software functions do not measure realized labor conversion.
- rho: Existing automation is substantial, so gross technology capability can overstate incremental opportunity.
- rho: FDA validation, traceability, quality, and cybersecurity requirements slow deployment and preserve accountable human review.
- e: No source measures eligibility in the specified earnings band or narrowed blood and plasma lens.
- e: NAICS 621991 includes nonprofit and public-interest entities as well as commercial plasma operations and reproductive tissue banks.
- e: The frozen firm count may treat legally distinct facilities or entities differently from economically independent control targets.
- s5: Gallup measures intentions rather than completed transfers and covers all employer businesses.
- s5: Broad health-services M&A data do not identify blood or plasma operators or provide an eligible-firm denominator.
- s5: Nonprofit governance, chain ownership, licensing, and quality history can prevent an apparent establishment from being a transferable control target.
- q: No public source was found for billing mix or retained automation benefit in eligible independent blood and plasma operations.
- q: Product revenue, service fees, donor economics, and testing or logistics pass-through can coexist in the same contract.
- q: Capacity gains have little value when donor recruitment, product mix, shelf life, or contracted demand is the binding constraint.
- d5: NBCUS blood-component measures do not fully cover source plasma for further manufacture or all services in the narrowed lens.
- d5: The BLS projection is four-digit and includes ambulance and miscellaneous ambulatory services.
- d5: Component trends diverge, and changes in clinical practice can reduce units while raising processing complexity.
- o: No source directly measures year-five operator-required share for independent lower-middle-market firms.
- o: Hospital systems and plasma manufacturers can vertically integrate or consolidate collection and processing.
- o: Software substitution is limited, but organizational substitution through larger networks may be material.
