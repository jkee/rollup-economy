**Driver:** Recurring fixed-scope contracts and repeatable floor-care workflows create a credible path to retain selective labor and administrative productivity gains.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market firms in NAICS 561720 that repeatedly clean building interiors, transportation-equipment interiors, or windows for external commercial, institutional, government, multifamily, or residential customers, including custodial, housekeeping, washroom-sanitation, and recurring specialty-cleaning programs.
- Excluded: Captive in-house custodial departments, shells, non-control financing vehicles, one-person practices without transferable operations, building-exterior cleaning other than windows, carpet-only specialists classified in 561740, facilities-management businesses classified elsewhere, and firms whose work is only an isolated cleanup with no repeat outsourced-service model.
- Customer and revenue model: Customers include offices, schools, hospitals, factories, distribution centers, retailers, airports, transportation operators, government facilities, multifamily properties, and households. Revenue is commonly generated by monthly fixed-price, square-foot, cost-plus, transaction-price, or hourly contracts, with work orders for deep cleans, move-outs, construction cleanup, disinfecting, and other supplemental services.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Janitorial services combine recurring contracts, a large frontline labor base, and durable physical demand. The credible technology opportunity is selective: autonomous floor care and AI-enabled administration can reduce avoidable hiring and improve consistency, but most cleaning remains dexterous, variable, local, and accountable.

## How AI changes the work
AI can improve bidding, scheduling, inspection review, supply planning, training, customer communication, and exception triage, while autonomous scrubbers and vacuums take over repeatable open-floor routes. Frontline teams shift toward setup, monitoring, detail cleaning, restrooms, hazards, touchpoints, and service recovery.

## Value capture
Monthly fixed-price and square-foot contracts can retain productivity gains between bids and renewals. Cost-plus and hourly work pass through more of the benefit, and intense local competition, short cancellation rights, rebidding, robot costs, and customer demands for expanded service limit five-year retention.

## Firm availability
The code contains many recurring outsourced operators and supports route-density tuck-ins, but qualifying transfers are constrained by owner dependence, customer concentration, short-duration contracts, labor compliance, and inconsistent service systems. General succession intentions exceed the likely closed external-sale rate.

## Demand durability
Clean and healthy occupied spaces remain essential across institutional, healthcare, industrial, logistics, transport, residential, and office settings. Technology can limit labor growth and office-space changes can reduce volume, but sanitation, hazards, regulation, and physical building use preserve the service basket.

## Risks and uncertainty
Exact-industry occupation, automation adoption, contract mix, and transaction data are sparse. Outcomes depend on customer mix, open-floor density, contract duration, wage regulation, turnover, immigration, union exposure, robot utilization, equipment service, quality failures, customer concentration, and whether technology savings fund margin or simply higher service levels.

## Caveats
- a: BLS occupational shares are for broader NAICS 561700 and include landscaping, pest control, and other building services.
- a: The 2020 custodial-automation paper contains forecasts and supplier economics rather than a representative measurement of labor hours eliminated.
- a: The KBS case is provider-authored and concerns a large national grocery chain, not a typical lower-middle-market contractor or customer portfolio.
- a: The estimate discounts conventional mechanization already embedded in current workflows, but no source cleanly separates that baseline from remaining AI-enabled opportunity.
- rho: No source measures five-year implementation for U.S. LMM janitorial firms.
- rho: The most concrete successful case involves hundreds of large retail sites after specialist intervention and may not transfer to fragmented small-building portfolios.
- rho: Robot deployment can reallocate labor to detail cleaning or service quality rather than eliminate paid hours.
- rho: Customer consent, safety, data connectivity, equipment service, and contract duration can constrain realization independently of technical capability.
- e: The Census definition establishes activities but does not measure recurring revenue or transferability.
- e: ABM demonstrates contract forms used by a large integrated provider and is not representative of LMM firms.
- e: The injected count of 1,034 firms is margin-bridged from size classes, so band membership is uncertain even though it is not re-estimated here.
- e: Residential, commercial, institutional, aircraft, rail-car, ship, and window-cleaning models can have materially different contract quality and operating complexity.
- s5: Gallup measures intentions across industries, not completed janitorial control transfers.
- s5: The facility-services report aggregates seven subsectors and includes announced as well as closed transactions.
- s5: Only 3% of the reported 2025 facility-services transaction mix was janitorial and cleaning, and undisclosed small deals are not captured consistently.
- s5: The injected firm denominator inherits uncertainty from the margin bridge used to place firms in the LMM band.
- q: ABM is a public integrated-facilities company rather than a representative sample of LMM pure-play janitorial firms.
- q: The filing lists contract types but does not disclose janitorial revenue weights by type or post-automation price behavior.
- q: Low barriers to entry and price-based competitive bidding can transfer savings to customers faster than contract form alone suggests.
- q: Robotics leasing, maintenance, consumables, supervision, and higher service levels reduce the gross benefit available for retention.
- d5: BLS real-output projections are available only for broader NAICS 561700, not exact 561720.
- d5: Occupational employment growth is not the same as constant-price demand and can be reduced by productivity gains.
- d5: Post-pandemic hygiene standards, office occupancy, and building-use shifts may move different customer segments in opposite directions.
- d5: The range does not assume that higher nominal wages or supply prices constitute real demand growth.
- o: No source directly measures the year-five operator-required share for NAICS 561720.
- o: Large open floors are much more substitutable than restrooms, furnished offices, healthcare rooms, windows, and transport interiors.
- o: Equipment vendors may increasingly sell directly to facility owners, shifting floor care away from outsourced contractors.
- o: Accountability and quality requirements may preserve the operator while reducing its labor content, so o should not be interpreted as a staffing share.
