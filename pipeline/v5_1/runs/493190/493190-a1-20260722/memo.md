## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent U.S. LMM operators providing recurring outsourced nonrefrigerated specialized physical-goods storage and handling, including bulk petroleum, chemicals, automobiles, lumber, whiskey, and other goods requiring dedicated tanks, yards, buildings, security, equipment, or regulatory controls.
- Excluded: Paper-document and records storage; household-goods storage and moving; general merchandise, refrigerated, and farm-product warehousing; self-storage real estate; hazardous-waste treatment; merchant petroleum bulk stations; captive or private facilities; non-U.S. operations; shells; and businesses not realistically transferable as control investments in the roughly $1-10M normalized EBITDA band.
- Customer and revenue model: Industrial, commercial, and institutional customers outsource custody and handling of specialized goods. Revenue generally combines recurring time- or capacity-based storage charges with receiving, movement, loadout, transaction, compliance, and other value-added activity fees; dedicated arrangements may use negotiated all-inclusive or cost-plus contracts.
- Deviation from default lens: The Census code explicitly combines household-goods storage, paper-document storage, and specialized physical-goods storage. I narrow to specialized physical goods because records and household-goods businesses have materially different demand, labor, technology-substitution, asset, and contracting economics. The narrowing is for analytical coherence, not attractiveness, and uncertainty is widened because public data generally aggregate the full code or broader warehousing sector.

## Executive view
Specialized physical-goods storage combines durable custody needs, recurring capacity or activity fees, and a moderate AI opportunity in planning, records, allocation, billing, and exception management. It is not a clean single industry: the full code mixes household, records, and specialized storage, and the selected specialty lens still spans markedly different assets and regulatory regimes. The frozen 246-firm estimate is attractive only before substantial eligibility dilution.

## How AI changes the work
AI can improve inventory reconciliation, document extraction, scheduling, workforce and equipment dispatch, space allocation, incoming-flow forecasts, customer reporting, compliance-document preparation, anomaly detection, and predictive-maintenance triage around existing WMS and sensor systems. It cannot remove the core need to receive, inspect, secure, preserve, move, maintain, and release physical goods, and incremental benefits must exclude conventional automation already deployed.

## Value capture
Capacity-, time-, transaction-, and activity-based charges are generally not mechanically linked to labor hours, allowing initial retention of efficiency and error-reduction gains. Cost-plus dedicated contracts, renewal negotiations, competitive bids, volume tiers, customer consolidation, and insourcing should share a meaningful part of the benefit over five years; stronger reliability, accuracy, and compliance can defend the remainder.

## Firm availability
The frozen model estimates 246 firms in the target EBITDA band for the entire code, but current Census categories show that household-goods and paper-records operators are mixed with the included specialty-storage businesses. Captive terminals, strategic networks, merchant operations, and asset-only real estate further shrink the acquirable pool. An adjacent 2024 warehouse acquisition confirms transaction feasibility but not the eligible count or transfer rate.

## Demand durability
Physical goods will continue to require custody, security, and handling by accountable operators, while outsourcing lets customers access specialized facilities without building their own. Broad warehousing output is projected to grow, but the included lens should be underwritten closer to modest growth because it excludes e-commerce-heavy general warehousing and carries industrial-cycle, product-transition, capacity, and customer-insourcing risk.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest research risk is classification: almost every quantitative source is broader than the narrowed lens. Other risks include environmental liabilities, spills and worker injury, obsolete or poorly integrated systems, cybersecurity, capital-intensive retrofits, customer and site concentration, regulatory noncompliance, product-specific demand shocks, asset ownership complications, and overstating AI opportunity by counting existing WMS or controls as incremental AI.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation mix covers all warehousing and storage, not 493190 or the selected subsegment.
- a: The fixed labor ratio uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and a cross-code IPS harmonization, so its downstream economic weight has a vintage and mapping mismatch.
- a: The specialized-goods lens itself spans tank, yard, building, and high-security operating models with different task mixes.
- rho: The evidence establishes capability and direction, not a representative adoption rate.
- rho: Some productivity gains attributed to AI are inseparable from conventional WMS, sensors, robotics, and process redesign.
- rho: Hazardous-material and oil-storage obligations can delay or constrain autonomous operation.
- e: No public source provides the selected subsegment's share of current LMM firms.
- e: The 246-firm frozen estimate uses a transportation-sector EBITDA-to-sales margin bridge that may misclassify asset-heavy specialty-storage operators.
- e: NAICS assignment may follow an establishment's primary activity even when the parent combines storage, transport, wholesaling, or real estate.
- s5: One adjacent transaction cannot establish an industry transfer rate.
- s5: The cited target included transportation and temperature-controlled activities outside the selected lens.
- s5: Private asset sales and undisclosed family transfers may be absent from public sources.
- q: The source is a service provider's guide and may present favorable marketing claims.
- q: Chemical, petroleum, automobile, lumber, and beverage-storage contracts may price and allocate savings differently.
- q: The estimate concerns retained unit-economics benefit, not demand growth or throughput gains.
- d5: BLS output projections cover all warehousing and storage and are not quantity forecasts for the selected lens.
- d5: Nominal revenue, real output, capacity, throughput, and storage-days can diverge materially.
- d5: The petroleum component faces transition and regulatory risks that may differ sharply by product and geography.
- o: Operator-required service does not imply full-time labor at every site; one team may supervise multiple facilities remotely.
- o: EPA SPCC applicability depends on capacity, location, and discharge risk and does not cover every facility in the lens.
- o: Customer insourcing can remove outsourced quantity without eliminating the underlying physical-storage task.
