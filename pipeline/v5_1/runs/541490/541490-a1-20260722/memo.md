# 541490 — Other Specialized Design Services

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `541490-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.40 · L 3.73 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** A predominantly digital design-and-support workforce exposes research, ideation, variation, finishing, documentation, and administration to rapidly improving creative AI.
**Weakness:** The unlicensed, heterogeneous service basket faces customer self-service, rapid repricing, and founder-dependent transferability precisely where AI makes generic creative output abundant.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market studios whose primary business is repeat outsourced design work for external business or institutional customers across the NAICS 541490 specialties, including fashion, clothing, footwear, textile, jewelry, lighting, costume, and other residual professional design services. Included firms manage briefs, research, concepts, design development, technical specifications or production packages, revisions, and client delivery.
- Excluded: Captive in-house design teams, shells, non-control financing vehicles, firms outside the approximate $1-10M normalized EBITDA band, independent theatrical artists classified in NAICS 711510, design fields assigned to other NAICS codes, merchandise- or manufacturing-led businesses, and predominantly one-off bespoke consumer practices without repeat outsourced service relationships.
- Customer and revenue model: Project, collection, season, campaign, or product-line design services sold mainly to brands, retailers, manufacturers, entertainment producers, developers, and institutions. Revenue may be fixed per project or deliverable, hourly, retainer-based, productized or subscription-based, and sometimes supplemented by change orders, licensing, royalties, or merchandise resale.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 541490 is a residual category spanning fashion, textile, footwear, jewelry, lighting, costume, float, and other design businesses whose customers, production processes, and economics differ materially. To preserve a coherent screen without selecting a favored specialty, the lens keeps repeat B2B outsourced design studios across all listed specialties and excludes goods-led, captive, and one-off consumer models.

## Executive view
Other specialized design is a labor-heavy but unusually heterogeneous catch-all. Digital research, ideation, variation, finishing, documentation, and support work offer meaningful AI exposure, and creative professionals already report strong productivity. The counterweight is weak structural protection: customers can self-serve low-stakes concepts, AI-enabled entrants can compete faster, and founder taste or relationships can make firms difficult to transfer. Production-ready, materially informed, differentiated work remains more defensible than generic visual output.

## How AI changes the work
AI is moving creative labor away from first-pass generation and post-production toward direction, selection, refinement, client negotiation, rights review, and accountability. In fashion-like workflows it can compress trend research, mood boards, concepting, and technical-package drafting; support functions can also automate proposals and administration. Physical sampling, material choice, fit, supplier coordination, lighting conditions, prototype review, and final production oversight remain substantially human and specialty-specific.

## Value capture
Fixed projects, retainers, and productized packages can preserve early productivity gains, while hourly work passes savings through as fewer billed hours. Capture decays as clients demand compressed schedules, renew contracts, compare AI-enabled competitors, or generate acceptable low-stakes work internally. Studios retain more value where taste, speed, provenance, supplier-ready detail, and trusted accountability matter, but generic concepts are likely to reprice quickly.

## Firm availability
The supplied dataset estimates 82 firms in the target EBITDA band, but the count is margin-bridged rather than a verified company list. Eligibility is reduced by the residual code's mixed specialties, neighboring-service misclassification, merchandise or manufacturing activity, one-off consumer work, and founder dependence. Broad employer-business succession evidence supports a plausible transfer pipeline, but there is no direct observed control-transfer series for this industry.

## Demand durability
The base case is flat to modestly growing real demand, supported by continuing collection and product refresh cycles, social and direct-to-consumer commerce, sustainability requirements, entertainment production, and the need for differentiated commercial design. Downside comes from discretionary retail cycles, offshoring, in-house design, customer self-service, and commoditization of generic visual work. The outlook varies far more by specialty than the single NAICS label suggests.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest gaps are clean firm classification, true wage-weighted task time, production-grade hours avoided, revenue by pricing model, repeat-client incidence, and completed firm transfers. Vendor-sponsored creative surveys may overstate adoption and savings. Intellectual-property and brand-continuity concerns can slow implementation, while insufficient human authorship can weaken copyright protection. A roll-up could also acquire founder-dependent boutiques whose client relationships and creative identity do not survive control transfer.

## Caveats
- a: The commercial occupation compilation says it uses US government data but displays graphic and interior designer shares even though those services are formally excluded from NAICS 541490, indicating classification or reporting noise.
- a: Occupation shares are headcount rather than wages or task time, so wage weighting is judgmental.
- a: The supplied compensation ratio uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and is harmonized by a cross-industry median; specialty mix and merchandise contamination could make it unrepresentative of the narrowed service lens.
- a: Creative-professional surveys include disciplines and firm sizes outside this industry.
- rho: Adobe is a creative-software vendor, and its respondents are likely more digitally engaged than the full target population.
- rho: Reported time savings are self-assessed and do not establish net labor savings after prompt development, comparison, revision, provenance checks, and client review.
- rho: Fashion workflow evidence comes from a large retailer's proprietary system and may not transfer economically or operationally to LMM outsourced studios.
- rho: Implementation will vary widely across digital concept work, technical product design, lighting, jewelry, costume, and physical prototype activities.
- e: The supplied firm count is an ESTIMATE produced from size-class receipts and an EBITDA-margin bridge, not an observed list of firms.
- e: No public source measures repeat-client or B2B revenue share for NAICS 541490 firms in the target EBITDA band.
- e: The broad residual code and displayed neighboring-service revenue create material classification risk.
- s5: Gallup covers all US employer businesses and measures intentions rather than closed transactions.
- s5: The survey does not isolate professional design firms or the target EBITDA band.
- s5: Internal minority buy-ins, talent acquisitions without durable operations, and owner deaths without transfer are excluded from the primitive.
- q: The pricing evidence pools several agency types and reports the primary model per firm, not revenue share by contract type.
- q: Client self-service belongs principally in operator-required demand, but it also increases competitive price pressure on retained projects; the estimate avoids counting eliminated volume as retained-benefit erosion.
- q: Licensing, royalty, and merchandise economics are not quantified and vary sharply by specialty.
- d5: Fashion designers are only one occupation and many work in-house outside NAICS 541490.
- d5: The revenue series is nominal, taxable-employer only, ends in 2022, and does not isolate the narrowed repeat-B2B lens.
- d5: Creative content demand is adjacent to, but not the same as, fashion, textile, jewelry, footwear, lighting, costume, and other product-oriented design demand.
- d5: End-market cyclicality and specialty mix make one industry-wide quantity ratio unusually uncertain.
- o: No source directly measures revenue-weighted software substitution for the narrowed NAICS 541490 lens.
- o: Operator need differs sharply between a simple concept image and a manufacturable shoe, textile repeat, jewelry line, lighting plan, or costume package.
- o: Copyrightability is only one form of commercial protection and does not by itself require an outside design firm.
