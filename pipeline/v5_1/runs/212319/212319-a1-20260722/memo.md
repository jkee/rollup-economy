# 212319 — Other Crushed and Broken Stone Mining and Quarrying

**Driver:** Permitted deposits close to recurring customers can form durable local assets, with AI offering credible incremental productivity upside.
**Weakness:** The industry primarily sells mined material rather than outsourced services, and its residual-category heterogeneity reduces confidence in broad underwriting assumptions.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market quarry and preparation-plant operators mining or beneficiating crushed stone other than limestone and granite, including traprock, sandstone, quartzite, marble, slate, volcanic material, and bituminous limestone or sandstone; genuine recurring contract or toll processing is included where present.
- Excluded: Dimension-stone operations, granite and ordinary limestone quarries, sand-and-gravel producers, captive sites serving only an integrated parent, greenfield mine development, and businesses without meaningful third-party recurring revenue.
- Customer and revenue model: Revenue is principally per-ton stone sales and delivery charges to local or regional construction, infrastructure, landscaping, industrial, and specialty-material customers, with repeat purchase orders but only a small outsourced-service component from contract processing or managed material handling.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Other crushed-stone quarrying offers durable physical demand and some location-based scarcity, but it is a weak recurring outsourced-service rollup candidate because product sales dominate revenue. The broad residual NAICS also hides material variation across stone types and end markets.

## How AI changes the work
AI can improve dispatch, bid preparation, production planning, maintenance triage, quality records, compliance, and customer communication. It has far less leverage over the physical extraction, comminution, materials handling, equipment service, and safety work that defines the operating base.

## Value capture
Operational gains can appear as higher uptime, tighter energy and yield control, faster order handling, better routing, and lower administrative effort. Commodity competition and customer bidding can pass part of those gains through to price.

## Firm availability
Succession may bring independent assets to market, but reserves, permits, mineral rights, environmental obligations, equipment condition, and local demand all narrow the transactible set. Applying the service-revenue lens makes the eligible pool smaller still.

## Demand durability
Road and infrastructure maintenance create repeat consumption, supplemented by broader construction and select specialty uses. The outlook varies by stone type and geography and remains exposed to project timing, weather, construction cycles, substitutes, and freight economics.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key uncertainties are the absence of stone-type task data, unknown contract-service revenue, category heterogeneity, local competitive structure, reserve quality and life, permitting constraints, and actual five-year seller conversion.

## Caveats
- a: The operating mix varies by stone type and end use.
- a: Addressability depends on sensor coverage, standardized controls, and equipment age.
- rho: No representative quarry-specific autonomous completion study was located.
- rho: The wide product mix makes workflow standardization uneven.
- e: Published industry statistics do not disclose contract-processing share.
- e: Some specialty stones may have different service attachment than construction aggregate.
- s5: Gallup is cross-industry rather than mining-specific.
- s5: The industry contains materially different stone types and asset profiles.
- q: Competitive advantage is site- and end-use-specific rather than national.
- q: Specialty stone may be more defensible than ordinary road aggregate.
- d5: Aggregate-wide data are dominated by limestone and granite, which this NAICS excludes.
- d5: Construction spending includes price effects and does not directly measure stone volume.
- o: Individual functions can be outsourced without eliminating the firm-level operator requirement.
