**Driver:** Document-heavy policy coordination and public communication create broad assistive AI opportunity across executive-office staffs.
**Weakness:** The code contains no private LMM firms, transferable ownership, receipts denominator, or commercial benefit-retention mechanism.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. federal, state, county, municipal, and town government establishments serving as offices of chief executives, including presidents, governors, mayors, city and town managers, county executives or supervisors, and their executive advisory committees and commissions.
- Excluded: Private management consulting and administrative-services firms, campaign organizations, legislative bodies, combined executive-legislative bodies, courts, public-finance agencies, operating departments, captive units classified elsewhere, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: These offices exercise governmental executive authority and provide policy direction, coordination, representation, appointments, budgeting, communications, and constituent-facing administration funded primarily by appropriations and taxes rather than recurring fees from external customers.
- Deviation from default lens: The default LMM outsourced-service firm lens is structurally inapplicable: Census defines this code exclusively as government establishments, the frozen dataset has no receipts concept, and no defensible firm count exists. The packet preserves the full government-office population to assess work exposure and service durability, while marking firm-transfer and commercial-retention constructs missing rather than inventing a private-company subset.

## Executive view
Executive offices contain substantial document, research, scheduling, communications, and analytical work that AI can assist, but high-stakes judgment and lawful authority remain human. The industry is entirely governmental, so private-firm eligibility, control transfer, and commercial retention are undefined rather than merely unattractive.

## How AI changes the work
AI can summarize briefings, search records, draft correspondence, translate public communications, manage schedules, monitor commitments, and support policy and budget analysis. Elected or appointed judgment, negotiation, appointments, representation, crisis leadership, and final approvals require accountable people.

## Value capture
There is no customer-price or EBITDA retention mechanism. Benefits appear as staff capacity, faster response, fewer errors, better analysis, or budget savings that may be reappropriated rather than retained as profit.

## Firm availability
Census defines only government establishments, and the frozen dataset has neither an LMM firm count nor a receipts concept. Government contractors belong in other NAICS codes; elections and appointments are not company control transfers.

## Demand durability
Every continuing jurisdiction requires executive leadership, while population, emergencies, intergovernmental programs, and policy complexity sustain support work. Staffing may shift with budgets, consolidation, shared services, and digitization, but the accountable office itself is highly durable.

## Risks and uncertainty
Major uncertainties include federal-to-local heterogeneity, missing six-digit occupational data, cybersecurity, privacy, records law, bias, procurement, legacy systems, political transitions, public trust, model error, and the structural mismatch between a government industry and a private-company screen.

## Caveats
- a: No six-digit occupational mix or compensation-weighted task study was found; federal agency evidence is not representative of governors' or local executive offices.
- a: The frozen dataset provides no receipts denominator, so labor opportunity cannot be linked to a conventional private-firm labor share.
- rho: Federal AI governance and capacity differ materially from state and local governments.
- rho: Time saved may improve briefing quality or responsiveness without reducing payroll or avoiding hires.
- e: Government contractors serving executive offices belong to other NAICS codes and cannot be imported into this record.
- e: Treating public offices as firms would create a category error rather than a conservative estimate.
- s5: A change in elected or appointed officeholder does not transfer ownership of the government establishment.
- s5: Privatization of selected support functions would create contractors in other industry codes, not a sale of the executive office.
- q: Budget savings may be reappropriated, absorbed by new mandates, or converted into service quality rather than retained as operating profit.
- q: Procurement savings accruing to an office are different from commercial pricing power.
- d5: Overall public employment is not a measure of demand for executive-office services and is dominated by functions outside this code.
- d5: Government demand is mandate- and budget-driven rather than a market quantity, so constant-quality volume is inherently difficult to observe.
- o: Supporting work can move to shared-service agencies or contractors even when executive authority remains internal.
- o: The primitive's operator concept was designed for private firms and maps imperfectly to a public institution.
