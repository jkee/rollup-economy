# 611692 — Automobile Driving Schools

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `611692-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 5.91 · L 2.01 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** A repeatable digital administration and theory layer sits beside a regulated, capacity-constrained in-car service that still needs an accountable local operator.
**Weakness:** The LMM firm pool is small, and much of the industry's payroll remains tied to live behind-the-wheel instruction that AI cannot safely remove.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Private automobile driving schools delivering classroom or online theory instruction, behind-the-wheel lessons, refresher training, and related noncommercial road-test preparation to external customers.
- Excluded: Public-school captive programs, truck and bus driving schools, stand-alone licensing agencies, software-only course publishers without an accountable school operator, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Primarily consumer-paid fixed-price course and lesson packages, supplemented by hourly lessons and occasional institutional or publicly supported cohorts; each customer engagement is finite, while the school repeatedly delivers a standardized service to successive novice and refresher students.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Automobile driving schools combine a standardized, digitizable theory and administration layer with a safety-critical, locally delivered in-car core. The opportunity is therefore operational rather than fully virtual: improve scheduling, communications, curriculum support, recordkeeping, and instructor utilization while preserving accountable human supervision.

## How AI changes the work
AI can draft and localize learning material, answer routine student questions, personalize theory practice, summarize instructor notes, schedule cars and instructors, flag incomplete records, and support sales follow-up. It is much less suited to taking responsibility for a novice in live traffic, judging situational awareness, or certifying practical competence without an instructor and approved process.

## Value capture
Posted package and hourly pricing allow savings to be retained before local competitors reprice. Transparent consumer comparison, one-time customers, and substitutable online theory courses create meaningful medium-term pass-through, while scarce instructor and vehicle capacity can let a well-run operator retain savings through faster throughput and availability.

## Firm availability
The industry definition is coherent, but the supplied LMM population is only 16 firms and some apparent candidates may be inseparable from a working owner or state-specific license. A real pipeline depends more on transferable instructor rosters, compliant records, vehicles, and local reputation than on a corporate shell.

## Demand durability
Teen licensure remains a durable replenishing need, augmented by adult beginners, immigrants, refresher students, and test preparation. Online theory can displace classroom hours, but current state systems continue to attach value to supervised practice, completion records, and professional certification. Five-year quantity is more likely stable than structurally high-growth.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main risks are state-by-state regulation, instructor scarcity, insurance and vehicle costs, safety liability, customer acquisition dependence, and the possibility that AI-enabled online courses commoditize the easiest work. Evidence is weakest on six-digit occupational mix, owner succession, actual control-transfer rates, and national lesson volumes.

## Caveats
- a: O*NET 25-3021 covers many self-enrichment teachers, not only driving instructors.
- a: No national six-digit wage-weighted occupation mix or task-time study was located.
- a: Exposure includes avoidable hiring as well as direct substitution and does not assume safe unsupervised vehicle instruction.
- rho: State rules vary materially and can slow or prohibit automated curriculum changes.
- rho: Observed availability of online coursework is not evidence that generative-AI workflows meet each state's approval, privacy, or record-retention requirements.
- rho: The estimate excludes pricing and demand effects.
- e: The supplied firm count is an estimate based on margin-bridged size classes rather than observed EBITDA.
- e: Licenses, instructor certifications, and school approvals are state-specific and may not transfer automatically.
- e: Eligibility is inferred from the industry definition, not observed across the 16 supplied firms.
- s5: No national 611692 transaction-rate series was found.
- s5: Broad educational-services owner demographics do not reveal whether a school has a transferable management layer.
- s5: Deaths, internal reorganizations, and non-going-concern asset sales are excluded.
- q: One AAA regional offer is not a national price sample.
- q: Many customers buy only once, so retention occurs through posted-price discipline and new-customer competition rather than contract renewal.
- q: The estimate excludes demand loss and implementation difficulty.
- d5: Licensed-driver stocks include people who never used a commercial school and omit lesson intensity.
- d5: The 2019-to-2024 comparison spans pandemic disruption.
- d5: Self-enrichment teachers include many activities unrelated to driving instruction.
- o: California is a large but not representative national rule set.
- o: NHTSA's common-hours statement does not establish the revenue share of classroom versus in-car delivery.
- o: Advanced simulators or future regulatory changes could substitute for more practical instruction than assumed.
