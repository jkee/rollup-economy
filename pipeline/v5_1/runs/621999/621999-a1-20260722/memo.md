# 621999 — All Other Miscellaneous Ambulatory Health Care Services

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `621999-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.34 · L 2.44 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Structured records, repeat outreach, and continuous device data create automation leverage inside recurring health-service contracts that still require accountable review and escalation.
**Weakness:** The residual code is highly heterogeneous, and the most digital workflows also face the strongest risk of payer repricing, software bundling, and customer insourcing.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market firms repeatedly providing outsourced health screening, employee testing, remote physiologic or cardiac monitoring support, and medical case or care-management services to employers, payers, health systems, and clinician practices.
- Excluded: One-off consumer testing, head-lice removal, standalone smoking-cessation or fitness programs without recurring contracts, physician and other practitioner offices, outpatient centers, laboratories, home health, ambulances, blood and organ banks, captive payer or health-system departments, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Customers pay per screen or test, per monitored patient or device period, per case, per member per month, time-based service fees, fixed program fees, or payer fee-schedule amounts; providers combine technology, clinical or technical staff, records, escalation, and customer reporting.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 621999 mixes employee drug testing, health and hearing screening, medical case management, pacemaker monitoring, fitness evaluation, smoking cessation, and head-lice services. These differ too much in workflow, payer, regulation, and recurrence for one coherent screen, so the lens is narrowed to recurring business-to-business screening, testing, monitoring-support, and care-management providers without selecting a subtype for attractiveness.

## Executive view
Recurring screening, monitoring-support, and care-management providers have meaningful exposure in intake, records, routing, billing, outreach, and routine data review, while physical testing, patient communication, clinical escalation, and accountability constrain substitution. The opportunity varies sharply by service line and depends on payer and customer contracts.

## How AI changes the work
AI can automate scheduling, questionnaire review, record abstraction, coding, report drafting, device-data summarization, risk prioritization, routine reminders, and case routing. Staff remain necessary for specimen collection, vital signs, device setup, counseling, sensitive outreach, judgment, escalation, and coordination with clinicians and social services.

## Value capture
Fixed program, per-case, per-test, and per-member contracts can retain efficiency gains, while time-based billing, payer fee schedules, rebids, service minimums, software and device expense, compliance, and denials limit capture. Released capacity creates value only when the provider can add covered patients or contract volume.

## Firm availability
The residual NAICS category contains both recurring scalable vendors and one-off or owner-dependent programs. Eligible firms need diversified recurring contracts, assignable payer and customer relationships, documented protocols, compliance, interoperable systems, management depth, and limited dependence on a single clinician or founder.

## Demand durability
Broader other-ambulatory output is projected to grow, remote monitoring participation has increased, and chronic-care programs support recurring need. Demand can still be reduced by reimbursement changes, employer spending pressure, customer insourcing, self-service devices, software bundling, and enforcement against weak monitoring models.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest gaps are six-digit service and occupation mix, current automation, realized vendor implementation, contract economics, eligible ownership, and direct transfer data. A poor outcome would combine payer cuts, concentrated customers, nonassignable contracts, privacy or clinical failures, integration costs, low enrollment, denials, and rapid insourcing by health systems or platforms.

## Caveats
- a: No six-digit staffing or task-time evidence was found for the narrowed service lines.
- a: The four-digit BLS table is heavily affected by ambulance and blood-bank employment that is outside this lens.
- a: O*NET tasks are cross-industry and do not reveal the current automation baseline or wage weights within eligible firms.
- rho: Hospital predictive-AI adoption is not realized implementation at independent 621999 service firms.
- rho: CMS coverage establishes digital workflows and payment, not vendor productivity or clinical acceptance.
- rho: Privacy, device and EHR interoperability, payer documentation, liability, and human-review requirements constrain deployment.
- e: No source measures eligibility under the narrowed recurring-service lens or earnings band.
- e: NAICS 621999 is a residual category spanning very different service and ownership models.
- e: The frozen firm count may be particularly sensitive to the broad healthcare margin used to bridge heterogeneous firms into the earnings band.
- s5: Gallup measures intentions rather than completed qualifying transfers and covers all employer businesses.
- s5: Broad health-services M&A value and commentary do not provide a 621999 transaction count or denominator.
- s5: Healthcare transaction scrutiny, payer dependence, and customer-contract assignability can prevent completion.
- q: No source directly measures retained automation benefit in eligible 621999 firms.
- q: CMS payment rules illustrate monitoring and management structures but do not represent employer or commercial-payer contracts.
- q: Time documentation, service minimums, and fee-schedule changes can cause labor savings to reduce billable revenue or flow to the payer.
- d5: The BLS output series is four-digit and combines materially different ambulance, blood-bank, and miscellaneous services.
- d5: CMS reports growth in remote-monitoring participation without publishing a comparable five-year quantity forecast on the cited page.
- d5: Reimbursement policy, fraud enforcement, employer budgets, and service-line mix can cause demand to diverge sharply from broad ambulatory output.
- o: No source directly measures year-five demand that still requires an independent 621999 operator.
- o: CMS describes reimbursable provider functions, not the share outsourced to independent vendors classified in this code.
- o: Simple screening and monitoring are more exposed to self-service and platform bundling than complex case management or clinical escalation.
