## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. federal, state and local government establishments judicially administering probation and parole offices and boards and pardon boards, including investigation, risk and needs assessment, supervision planning, case contacts, monitoring, court reporting, violation response, referrals, records and administration. These government operations are analyzed only to characterize AI exposure and service durability.
- Excluded: Private monitoring, software, treatment, halfway-house and case-management vendors; correctional institutions whose probation or parole function is embedded in a central office; courts, police, prosecutors and public defenders; pretrial-only agencies outside the classification; prisons and jails; and any purported private acquisition target under this government-only NAICS code.
- Customer and revenue model: Courts, correctional systems and the public authorize and fund these offices through government budgets rather than commercial revenue. Officers supervise people subject to court or parole conditions and provide investigations and recommendations to judicial or executive decision makers. Workload recurs with active caseloads, entries, exits, contacts, monitoring events and statutory reporting, but there is no private revenue model.
- Deviation from default lens: The default private LMM acquisition lens cannot apply: the official NAICS definition expressly consists of government establishments and the frozen inputs confirm there is no receipts concept or defensible firm count. The government operating lens is retained only to estimate technological exposure and public-service durability; eligibility and saleability are zero.

## Executive view
This record is categorically outside a private LMM acquisition universe: NAICS 922150 is composed of government establishments, with no commercial receipts or defensible firm count. Operationally, probation and parole are information-heavy and already use risk tools, monitoring and case systems, so AI can materially assist documentation, triage, scheduling, communications and resource allocation. Human judgment, relationship work, field safety, due process and public authority sharply constrain replacement. The result is a public-sector modernization opportunity, not an acquisition thesis.

## How AI changes the work
Generative systems can summarize records and contacts, draft routine reports, schedule work, translate communications and surface exceptions. Predictive tools can update risk and prioritize attention; monitoring devices and smartphone workflows can reduce some in-person checks. Officers must still investigate, validate, counsel, respond, testify and make accountable recommendations. Because model improvements may be modest relative to interpretable statistical tools, implementation quality matters more than novelty.

## Value capture
Benefits appear as caseload capacity, timeliness, consistency, safety and potentially better outcomes rather than EBITDA. Agencies may reinvest saved time in higher service intensity, while appropriators, courts, taxpayers, supervised people and vendors share the benefit. Monitoring can add alerts and compliance burdens, so gross labor exposure should not be confused with net budget savings.

## Firm availability
There are no eligible firms or saleable offices under this code. Private suppliers of software, monitoring, treatment, testing and managed services may be investable under other NAICS codes, but including them here would violate the official government-establishment scope and the frozen dataset basis.

## Demand durability
Community supervision remains a large and legally embedded component of the justice system, though the adult caseload has declined materially over a decade. Five-year demand is likely to contract modestly in the base case, with workload affected by supervision intensity, risk mix, policy and violations as much as total headcount.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key operational risks are due process, bias, explainability, privacy, cybersecurity, model drift, inaccurate data, alert overload, unequal technology access, procurement fragmentation and automation deference in liberty-sensitive decisions. For investment scoring, the decisive issue is not uncertainty but categorical ineligibility: l and n are missing because this is government public administration without a receipts concept.

## Caveats
- a: Pretrial services overlap operationally but are not always classified in 922150.
- a: Risk tools supplement rather than replace professional judgment.
- a: Automation of a task does not authorize automated liberty-depriving decisions.
- rho: New technology can increase rather than reduce workload by generating more alerts.
- rho: Local agencies may lack data engineering, procurement capacity and cybersecurity resources.
- rho: Legal and ethical constraints vary by jurisdiction.
- e: Governments may outsource components, but vendors are not 922150 government establishments.
- e: A private-public partnership opportunity would require analysis under the vendor's own industry code.
- s5: This is saleability of the establishment, not procurement opportunity around it.
- s5: Government reorganizations or interagency transfers are not M&A exits.
- q: This is public operational retention, not investor value capture.
- q: Efficiency may be reinvested in service intensity rather than reduce spending.
- q: Poorly targeted monitoring can shift costs to officers and supervised people.
- d5: National caseload trends conceal large jurisdiction differences.
- d5: Demand for staff time is not proportional to the number supervised.
- d5: Legislative and court policy can change quickly.
- o: Technology can change delivery without replacing the public authority.
- o: Incarceration is a policy substitute but not necessarily a lower-cost or desirable one.
- o: Some functions are embedded in central corrections offices and classified elsewhere.
