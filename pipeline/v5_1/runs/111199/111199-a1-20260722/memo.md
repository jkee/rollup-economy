**Driver:** Recurring physical demand for feed, brewing, food, seed, and export grains supported by scalable mechanized field operations.
**Weakness:** No defensible outsourced-service firm population, contract economics, or LMM denominator can be established inside 111199.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market firms, if any, providing repeat outsourced operation and management of barley, oat, rye, sorghum, wild-rice, or other 111199 grain production for external landowners or program customers while materially operating the farm.
- Excluded: Owner-operated farms selling their own crops, passive land or financing vehicles, manage-only agricultural services classified in NAICS 115, wheat, corn, cultivated rice, sweet corn, oilseed-grain combination farms, and one-off custom fieldwork without recurring operating responsibility.
- Customer and revenue model: The qualifying model would use recurring farm-management, crop-share, acreage, or production agreements with accountability for a full growing program. Public sources describe commodity production but do not establish a defensible population or contract model for outsourced operators inside 111199.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed to repeat outsourced farm operation because the NAICS is primarily grain production for commodity sale, while the fixed lens requires an outsourced service. The crop subsector also distinguishes operate-and-manage farms from manage-only support establishments, making the eligible boundary uncertain.

## Executive view
111199 has durable physical grain demand and meaningful digital decision-support potential, but it is fundamentally commodity crop production. Public data do not establish a lower-middle-market population providing repeat outsourced farm operation, and both frozen dataset inputs are missing.

## How AI changes the work
AI can improve planning, agronomy, procurement, marketing, compliance, records, and maintenance support. Field operations remain physical and substantial mechanization, including autosteer, is already embedded, limiting the unautomated opportunity.

## Value capture
Commercial retention cannot be bounded without actual farm-operation agreements. Commodity margins, crop prices, and precision-agriculture savings do not specify how a landowner and operator share productivity benefits.

## Firm availability
No defensible LMM denominator or eligible outsourced-operator numerator is available. Farm counts, acreage, and farm succession cannot substitute for a verified transferable operating-company population.

## Demand durability
Barley, oats, sorghum, rye, wild rice, and seed uses provide recurring demand, but crop substitution, weather, exports, and feed economics create variability. Commodity demand does not prove outsourced-service demand.

## Risks and uncertainty
The key risks are absence of a scalable service target pool, missing labor and firm-count inputs, commodity and weather volatility, land and operator entanglement, thin margins, existing mechanization, classification leakage to agricultural support services, and nontransferable family operations.

## Caveats
- a: USDA labor shares cover all agriculture rather than other-grain farms.
- a: Precision-adoption data include larger field crops and measure existing digitalization, not generative-AI exposure.
- a: The frozen labor-share input is missing, so this primitive cannot be combined with a measured industry labor intensity.
- rho: No five-year implementation study specific to 111199 was found.
- rho: USDA shows precision-technology adoption rises sharply with farm size, so LMM applicability may differ from the farm average.
- e: Farm counts are not equivalent to LMM firms and may include sole proprietors, family operations, and multiple establishments.
- e: Manage-only firms may be classified in agricultural support activities rather than 111199.
- s5: Farm or land sales do not necessarily transfer a recurring outsourced operating business.
- s5: Owner age alone would not establish transferability of customer contracts and operations.
- q: Commodity-price exposure is distinct from commercial retention of productivity gains.
- q: Terms from custom fieldwork or manage-only firms outside the lens cannot be assumed.
- d5: Commodity production outlook is not a direct measure of outsourced farm-operation demand.
- d5: Global outlook changes may not translate to U.S. acreage.
- d5: Weather and annual crop switching can dominate short-period trends.
- o: The estimate separates generative AI from mechanization and autonomous equipment.
- o: The accountable outsourced operator population itself remains unverified.
