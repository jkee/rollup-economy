**Driver:** Custom formulations and qualification-heavy physical production create repeat customer programs that software alone cannot supply.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market manufacturers that repeatedly develop, blend, quality-control, pack, and supply custom or private-label flavoring syrups, beverage concentrates, fountain bases, and related drink compounds for external beverage brands, foodservice operators, retailers, distributors, and bottlers.
- Excluded: Global brand-owner concentrate systems, proprietary syrup brands without material outsourced-production revenue, captive bottler plants, finished ready-to-drink beverage filling classified elsewhere, coffee extracts classified elsewhere, shells, non-control financing vehicles, and firms outside the approximately $1-10M normalized EBITDA band.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat B2B formulation and production programs priced per gallon, pound, bag-in-box, case, batch, or contract run, often combining product development, ingredient sourcing, blending, regulatory and label support, quality assurance, packaging, and palletized delivery.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 311930 includes both proprietary concentrate owners and independent outsourced manufacturers. The lens is narrowed to custom, contract-packed, co-manufactured, or private-label syrup and concentrate programs because these are the recurring external services specified by the default screen.

## Executive view
The coherent opportunity is the independent custom and private-label syrup manufacturer, not proprietary global concentrate systems. Customer-specific formulas and regulated physical production support repeat relationships and switching friction, but only 41 LMM firms are injected and the eligible share is uncertain.

## How AI changes the work
AI can assist formulation search, specification and label drafting, production scheduling, ingredient procurement, quality-record review, maintenance triage, customer service, and administration. Batch operation, sanitation, filling, warehousing, laboratory verification, and food-safety release remain physical or accountable human work.

## Value capture
Recipe complexity, sensory approval, packaging compatibility, certifications, and reliable execution can preserve savings under fixed pricing. Large customers, open-book arrangements, input-cost indexation, bids, and customer-owned formulas can return much of the benefit at renewal.

## Firm availability
Independent contract and private-label operators clearly exist, but no source sizes them within the LMM population. Manufacturing-owner aging supports succession-driven supply, while the small population and low general broker close rate make completed transfers episodic.

## Demand durability
Conventional North American concentrate demand is mature and sugary-drink health pressure is real, yet foodservice growth and continued innovation in low-sugar, energy, functional, flavored-water, and private-label formats support a broadly stable service basket. Physical blending, verification, packaging, and lot release remain necessary.

## Risks and uncertainty
Customer concentration, recipe ownership, sugar and ingredient volatility, labeling error, contamination, allergen and sanitation failures, product recalls, minimum-run economics, and health regulation can dominate labor gains. The true eligible share and contract-level capture require direct diligence.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation mix covers all NAICS 3119 rather than 311930.
- a: AI opportunity excludes conventional batching and filling automation already deployed.
- a: The injected compensation ratio is low and based on mismatched 2024 wage and 2022 receipt vintages, so small errors in task share can matter less than revenue volatility.
- rho: No source directly measures five-year AI implementation for custom syrup manufacturers.
- rho: Small firms may lack clean recipe and batch histories, while sophisticated contract manufacturers may adopt faster.
- e: Public operator examples establish existence, not market share.
- e: Several firms mix branded formulas, standard catalog syrups, and eligible custom production.
- e: The small injected firm count means classification of a few firms materially changes eligibility.
- s5: Owner-age data are for all manufacturing and data year 2020.
- s5: Sale-intention evidence is Washington-specific and not industry-specific.
- s5: One transaction can move observed rates materially in this small population.
- q: No public source quantifies pass-through or renewal repricing for LMM custom syrup manufacturers.
- q: Customer concentration can make nominal switching costs ineffective in negotiation.
- q: Proprietary formula ownership by the customer reduces supplier leverage.
- d5: Coca-Cola is a global proprietary system, not the LMM contract-manufacturing lens.
- d5: Foodservice employment is an indirect channel proxy and does not map one-for-one to syrup volume.
- d5: CDC health evidence creates policy and preference downside but does not provide a current volume forecast.
- o: Regulatory accountability does not guarantee that the same independent supplier retains the work.
- o: Consolidation into large concentrate systems or direct brand-owner manufacturing can reduce the lens population while an operator remains necessary.
