# 813110 — Religious Organizations

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `813110-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** STRUCTURAL_OUT · A 5.19 · L 0.29 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → STRUCTURAL_OUT · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** AI can compress the administrative, communications, research, fundraising, and content workload surrounding recurring ministry.
**Weakness:** Nonprofit asset dedication and the absence of saleable ownership make qualifying firm eligibility and control transfer exceptionally scarce.

## Business-model lens
- Included: United States lower-middle-market religious organizations that repeatedly provide worship, religious education, pastoral, membership, and related community services to congregants or other external participants and have institutional operations separable from a single leader.
- Excluded: Schools, hospitals, social-service agencies, media businesses, cemeteries, and other activities classified elsewhere; informal congregations without enterprise operations; donor-controlled shells; captive denominational administrative units; and organizations whose assets, governance, or relationships cannot support a qualifying control transfer.
- Customer and revenue model: Participants generally receive recurring religious and community services without a commercial service contract. Revenue is primarily donations, offerings, grants, dues, facility use, and program fees, with spending governed by mission and nonprofit restrictions rather than distributable owner profit.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Religious organizations contain repeatable administrative, communications, fundraising, education, and content workflows where AI can raise staff capacity. The economic lens fit is weak because service is mission-funded rather than commercially outsourced, ownership is generally absent, and exempt-purpose restrictions sharply constrain qualifying control transfers.

## How AI changes the work
AI can assist research, translation, scheduling, donor and member communications, bookkeeping support, volunteer coordination, lesson materials, visual media, and document preparation. Worship leadership, pastoral care, rites, doctrinal judgment, safeguarding, and community trust remain human-led.

## Value capture
Savings are less exposed to contractual pass-through than in fee-for-service industries and may remain as institutional capacity. They are nevertheless mission assets rather than owner cash flow and may be redirected to programs, oversight, or lower fundraising need.

## Firm availability
Some large, professionally staffed religious organizations have durable systems, facilities, brands, and recurring participation. Most lack saleable equity, and the supplied EBITDA-band firm count may misclassify donation receipts and exempt operating surpluses as commercial enterprise economics.

## Demand durability
Short-term U.S. religious service participation has been broadly stable, but long-run secularization and weaker participation among younger cohorts create downside. Hybrid participation can preserve reach without creating more transferable organizations.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are nonprofit and denominational control restrictions, leader and congregation dependence, donation volatility, privacy of pastoral data, doctrinal errors, synthetic-content backlash, safeguarding, copyright, and a mismatch between public-data receipts and normalized EBITDA.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS reports employees rather than unpaid volunteers or self-employed clergy and does not measure tasks.
- a: The occupation mix aggregates very different faith traditions, organization sizes, and service models.
- a: Pastor AI use demonstrates assistance, not avoidable hiring or wage substitution.
- rho: The survey covers Protestant pastors rather than the full NAICS population or firm-level systems.
- rho: Use frequency does not establish productivity, quality, or labor savings.
- rho: Volunteer-led and small organizations may lack data, integrations, and change capacity.
- e: Religious participation is not normally an outsourced commercial customer relationship.
- e: The margin-bridged n input treats nonprofit receipts as if they were conventional sales and EBITDA.
- e: Facilities and mission assets may be transferable only to another exempt organization, not to an acquirer for private benefit.
- s5: Leadership turnover and denominational reassignment are not qualifying control transfers.
- s5: No representative closed-transaction denominator was found.
- s5: State religious-corporation law and denominational property rules vary.
- q: Mission retention is not distributable owner cash flow.
- q: Donations may respond to perceived need, offsetting apparent savings.
- q: No direct study measures realized AI benefit retention in religious organizations.
- d5: Attendance is not constant-quality service quantity or real organizational revenue.
- d5: Survey mode changes limit long-run comparisons.
- d5: National averages conceal large denominational and regional differences.
- o: Operator requirements are theological and institutional, not solely technical.
- o: Remote participation can change delivery without eliminating the religious organization.
- o: No source directly measures the operator-required share of future service quantity.
