# 813940 — Political Organizations

**Driver:** High-volume digital communications, fundraising, research, data, and compliance workflows create substantial AI-assisted operating leverage.
**Weakness:** Temporary campaign lives, regulated political control, contribution-funded economics, and almost nonexistent salable ownership break the conventional transfer mechanism.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market-scale political parties, political action committees, and multi-cycle political organizations with recurring fundraising, compliance, voter contact, communications, field, and administrative operations serving a party, candidates, donors, or supporters.
- Excluded: One-cycle candidate committees without a recurring operating organization, dormant or winding-down committees, social-advocacy nonprofits, lobbying and public-relations agencies, contract fundraising and telemarketing vendors, government offices, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Organizations raise regulated contributions and transfers and spend them on candidate or party support, voter contact, media, fundraising, field operations, compliance, staff, and administration; supporters and political principals fund mission activity rather than purchasing a conventional fee-for-service product.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 813940 mixes durable party and multi-cycle committee operations with temporary single-candidate campaign entities. The lens retains recurring organizations and excludes one-cycle or winding-down committees because combining both operating lives would make five-year implementation, demand, and transfer assumptions incoherent.

## Executive view
Political organizations have unusually high exposure in content, research, fundraising, data, compliance preparation, and administration, but the industry mixes durable parties and PACs with temporary campaigns. AI can raise campaign capacity, while regulated governance, cyclical demand, contribution-funded economics, and the absence of salable ownership weaken a roll-up thesis.

## How AI changes the work
AI can draft and adapt messages, summarize issues and opposition research, segment donors and voters, translate content, prepare compliance work, monitor media, support fundraising, and automate scheduling and administration. Human leaders remain responsible for strategy, candidate voice, legal approval, coalition relationships, field leadership, and reputational judgment.

## Value capture
Because funding arrives as contributions rather than cost-plus customer prices, productivity can remain as additional political capacity. Competitive pressure will usually redeploy gains into more outreach, fundraising, media, or field activity, while vendors and platforms capture part of the benefit.

## Firm availability
A small set of durable parties, PACs, and multi-cycle committees has recurring staff and systems, but most entities are temporary, principal-controlled, volunteer-heavy, or legally constrained rather than transferable owner-controlled firms. Commercial EBITDA and acquisition concepts fit poorly.

## Demand durability
Recurring elections and large regulated financial flows sustain demand, but the calendar, race competitiveness, donor sentiment, law, platform rules, and media prices create large cycle-to-cycle variation that obscures constant-quality volume.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are the true 813940 occupation mix, existing digital automation, synthetic-content backlash, privacy and cybersecurity, election law, temporary-entity prevalence, state activity, real versus nominal demand, and the commercial margin bridge behind the frozen firm count.

## Caveats
- a: The available OEWS mix covers the broader 813900 group rather than 813940 and is not a wage-weighted task-time measure.
- a: Public-relations tasks omit campaign-specific field, compliance, fundraising, data, creative, and political-strategy roles.
- a: Political organizations already use extensive digital tools and vendors, so nominal task exposure may include work that is already automated.
- rho: BTOS does not identify political organizations or election-cycle deployment.
- rho: Using AI in any business function is not equivalent to operationally implementing the exposed opportunity.
- rho: High staff turnover and decentralized campaigns can both accelerate experimentation and prevent durable implementation.
- e: The frozen n estimate applies a commercial margin bridge to contribution-funded entities for which normalized EBITDA is not an economic ownership measure.
- e: Federal filings omit state and local organizations, while Census establishments may include entities outside FEC jurisdiction.
- e: Narrowing to recurring organizations excludes a large but operationally temporary part of the NAICS definition.
- s5: No source measures qualifying control transfers among political organizations.
- s5: A new candidate, chair, treasurer, or election result does not itself transfer a salable operating business.
- s5: Committee assets and donor relationships are legally and politically constrained and may not be transferable like commercial goodwill.
- q: No public source measures retained AI benefit in political organizations.
- q: Savings are likely redeployed into competitive activity rather than realized as distributable profit.
- q: Vendor and platform pricing may capture a meaningful share of productivity gains.
- d5: FEC receipts and disbursements are nominal financial flows, not constant-price, constant-quality service quantity.
- d5: Federal totals include temporary candidate committees and transfers that fall outside the narrowed lens.
- d5: The five-year endpoint can fall in a different phase of the two- and four-year election cycles.
- o: The sources establish legal organizational roles but do not measure a five-year operator-required quantity share.
- o: A party, candidate, or platform can internalize functions while political activity continues without a separate LMM-scale organization.
- o: AI may sharply reduce staffing and vendors even where a formal committee remains legally necessary.
