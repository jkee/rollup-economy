# 561920 — Convention and Trade Show Organizers

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `561920-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 7.30 · L 3.73 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Repeatable planning-office workflows can be automated while valuable in-person events still need coordinated, accountable execution.
**Weakness:** Benefits and transferability vary sharply by event ownership and contract model, with demand exposed to travel, budgets, and disruption shocks.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US convention, conference, and trade-show organizers in the lower middle market that repeatedly plan, promote, manage, or own business events for external customers, exhibitors, sponsors, or attendees.
- Excluded: Captive in-house event departments, venue-only operators, social-event planners outside NAICS 561920, shell entities, non-control financing vehicles, and firms without recurring or repeat outsourced-service economics.
- Customer and revenue model: Customers include associations, corporations, exhibitors, sponsors, and attendees. Revenue can combine management or production fees with exhibit-space, sponsorship, registration, ancillary-event, media, and digital-service revenue; contracts and advance bookings commonly precede the event.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Convention and trade-show organizers combine a sizable information-work automation surface with physical, relationship-heavy delivery that should preserve the need for an accountable operator. The opportunity is strongest in repeat programs or owned-event portfolios with standardized workflows and diversified revenue, but transfer volume and benefit retention remain estimated rather than directly measured.

## How AI changes the work
AI can compress research, proposal and RFP drafting, marketing copy, agenda development, attendee communications, registration support, scheduling, vendor comparison, invoice review, and post-event reporting. Human work remains central in commercial relationships, creative judgment, negotiation, venue and supplier management, safety, live exception handling, and final accountability.

## Value capture
Fixed fees and owned-event revenue can preserve labor savings, especially between contract renewals, and revenue streams such as exhibit space, sponsorship, and registrations are not direct labor pass-throughs. Capture erodes where work is cost-plus, clients demand shared savings, competitors reprice, or automation makes basic planning easier to self-source.

## Firm availability
The code is largely made up of external-service businesses, but not every firm has recurring clients, diversified event rights, transferable relationships, or LMM earnings. Broad owner succession intentions and active events M&A suggest a real acquisition pool, although no US NAICS-specific close-rate denominator supports a precise estimate.

## Demand durability
Business events continue to support networking, product discovery, sponsorship, education, and relationship formation that digital channels do not fully replace. Near-term exhibition forecasts and planner expectations are positive overall, while funding cuts, travel costs, trade policy, geopolitics, and venue inflation create meaningful downside.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main risks are event cyclicality, cancellations, customer and flagship-show concentration, dependence on venue and travel ecosystems, owner-held relationships, working-capital timing, and sharp variation between owned-event and fee-for-service models. The largest evidence gaps are firm-level task weights, realized AI labor savings, contract repricing, and NAICS-specific completed control transfers.

## Caveats
- a: Occupation task lists do not supply task hours or wage weights for NAICS 561920.
- a: PCMA's 2025 survey had 80 respondents and was dominated by associations rather than independent organizers.
- rho: Adoption is not equivalent to production deployment or labor substitution.
- rho: Survey respondents include internal planners and suppliers outside the exact lens.
- e: No source directly measures the share of firms satisfying both repeat-service and normalized-EBITDA criteria.
- e: Owned-event businesses and third-party management firms can have materially different transferability.
- s5: The owner-intention survey is cross-industry and intentions are not completed control transactions.
- s5: The reported event deal count is global and includes adjacent event, media, and technology businesses.
- q: No direct evidence measures benefit pass-through or renewal repricing in LMM organizer contracts.
- q: Retention likely varies sharply between owned-event portfolios and agency-style event management.
- d5: Forecasts are not observations of year-five constant-price, constant-quality demand.
- d5: PCMA's small association-heavy sample is not representative of all NAICS 561920 firms.
- o: The operator-required share is inferred from workflows and attendance, not directly observed.
- o: Small meetings are more self-serviceable than large trade shows, creating substantial within-code variation.
