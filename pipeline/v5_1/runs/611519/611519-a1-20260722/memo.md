# 611519 — Other Technical and Trade Schools

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `611519-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** PRIORITY · A 6.47 · L 4.69 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Standardized digital course, assessment-support, learner-service, and administrative workflows create a broad implementable AI surface.
**Weakness:** Online commoditization and mixed-to-declining enrollment evidence can force efficiency gains into lower prices rather than durable retained benefit.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Privately controlled schools repeatedly selling primarily classroom, webinar, correspondence, or online job-specific instruction and certification preparation to individuals or external employers, including real-estate, insurance, banking, security, broadcasting, graphic-arts, bartending, and other asset-light vocational subjects.
- Excluded: Truck, bus, heavy-equipment, mechanical, culinary, health-clinical, and other schools whose core delivery requires fleets, shops, kitchens, clinical labs, or supervised physical practice; captive employer schools; public institutions; degree programs; and one-off grant vehicles without recurring customers.
- Customer and revenue model: Mostly posted tuition or fixed per-enrollment and per-cohort fees paid by individuals or employers, with repeat demand from new entrants, continuing-education requirements, employer upskilling, and course updates.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 611519 combines asset-light online or classroom credential schools with fleet-, shop-, kitchen-, laboratory-, and clinical-practice schools whose labor substitution, capital intensity, and operator requirements are too different for one coherent screen. The lens keeps the classroom/online credential model and excludes equipment- and supervised-practice-intensive delivery solely to make the operating model coherent.

## Executive view
The coherent asset-light portion of other technical and trade schools has high exposure in course creation, learner support, assessment preparation, marketing, and administration. AI implementation is practical, but online price transparency and low marginal-cost competition limit retention, while broad enrollment evidence suggests flat-to-soft demand rather than automatic growth.

## How AI changes the work
AI can draft and localize lessons, update materials when rules change, generate exercises and formative tests, provide round-the-clock explanations, summarize learner progress, automate records and scheduling, and support marketing and admissions. Humans remain important for final assessment integrity, difficult coaching, regulated sign-off, course approval, customer trust, escalation, and accountable operation.

## Value capture
Fixed tuition allows near-term retention, particularly for strong brands, recurring continuing education, and employer cohorts. Over five years, comparable online offerings, discounting, public or employer substitutes, and frequent renewal choices are likely to pass a meaningful share of savings to customers or reinvest it in service and acquisition.

## Firm availability
The six-digit code is unusually diverse, so only an estimated 42% of the frozen LMM firm universe fits the asset-light classroom or online lens. Among eligible firms, founder ownership and broad owner-age evidence create succession opportunity, but state approvals, accreditation, Title IV obligations, instructor relationships, and brand continuity can constrain transferability.

## Demand durability
Licensing and job-specific certification support recurring demand and keep accountable providers relevant. However, Title IV for-profit short-program enrollment is below its 2010 level, career-technical teacher employment is projected roughly flat, and generic or nonregulated instruction can shift to employer self-service, free content, or software. The central case is therefore slightly contracting demand with a wide subject-dependent range.

## Risks and uncertainty
The key gaps are the current product-line composition of LMM 611519 firms, occupation mix for the narrowed lens, non-Title-IV enrollment trends, transfer history, realized tuition and discounting, and regulator treatment of AI-delivered courses. A bad outcome combines falling enrollment, rising customer acquisition cost, price compression, and regulatory or exam-pass deterioration that erodes school approval or reputation.

## Caveats
- a: The BLS source is four-digit NAICS 611500 and includes delivery models excluded from the lens.
- a: The estimate treats assistive use as exposure only when it can substitute labor or avoid hiring, not merely improve course quality.
- a: The frozen compensation ratio uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and a harmonization divisor, with the stated vintage and basis limitations.
- rho: Census data are all-industry and the survey question broadened in November 2025 from production to any business function.
- rho: Guidance shows permissibility and intended uses, not productivity or avoided hiring.
- rho: State-by-state approval and recordkeeping rules can slow deployment even when the instructional technology is ready.
- e: No source measures the share of $1-10M EBITDA 611519 firms that matches the narrowed lens.
- e: Schools often teach multiple subjects or combine online theory with physical practice, making binary classification difficult.
- e: The frozen firm count is a margin-bridged estimate using size-class data and an education-sector margin rather than observed firm EBITDA.
- s5: The source is economy-wide, based on 2018 owner data, and represents responding owners rather than firms.
- s5: Control transfers that lose required approvals, students, instructors, or operating continuity do not qualify.
- s5: Deaths, closures, minority investments, and internal reorganizations are excluded.
- q: The regulatory sources cover real-estate education in two states, not every subject in the lens.
- q: Approval barriers can support price in some states, while online delivery and public or employer substitutes can compress it elsewhere.
- q: The estimate excludes volume loss and implementation difficulty, which are represented in other primitives.
- d5: IPEDS covers Title IV institutions and excludes many short, noncredit, non-Title-IV providers in the lens.
- d5: Teacher employment is not student demand and includes public secondary and postsecondary institutions outside 611519.
- d5: The mixed subject portfolio can diverge sharply with local licensing, housing, technology, hospitality, and security labor markets.
- o: Real-estate rules illustrate regulated credential training but do not apply to every subject in the narrowed lens.
- o: An approved operator can become much thinner and more software-centric while still counting as operator-required demand.
- o: Rules can change to permit direct assessment or self-service, especially in subjects without safety-sensitive practice.
