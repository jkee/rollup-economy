# 711410 — Agents and Managers for Artists, Athletes, Entertainers, and Other Public Figures

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `711410-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** PRIORITY · A 7.04 · L 4.62 · interval CONDITIONAL → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Commission-based revenue can retain AI-enabled labor savings while human agents continue to own access, negotiation, and accountability.
**Weakness:** Client and rainmaker relationships may walk at acquisition, making apparent agency earnings far less transferable than the firm count suggests.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US agencies and management firms in the $1-10M normalized EBITDA band that repeatedly represent and manage artists, performers, athletes, creators, and other public figures for commissions or recurring management fees.
- Excluded: Solo practitioners without transferable client relationships or operating infrastructure, captive representation units, one-off event promoters, law firms acting only as legal counsel, and financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: The represented person is the client; revenue is predominantly a contractual percentage of client earnings, bookings, endorsements, or other commissionable income, with some recurring management or business-management fees.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
The industry combines a meaningful administrative automation surface with unusually durable human roles in access, negotiation, judgment, and trust. Commission revenue can preserve early cost savings, but transferable value is fragile when clients and relationships belong to individual rainmakers rather than the firm.

## How AI changes the work
AI can draft and personalize outreach, research prospects and market trends, summarize contracts, maintain CRM records, coordinate calendars and travel, monitor payments, prepare client reports, and surface negotiation comparables. Human agents remain central for selecting talent, winning trust, resolving conflicts, setting career strategy, and taking responsibility for high-stakes negotiations.

## Value capture
Percentage-of-client-income contracts do not mechanically pass lower labor cost to clients, which supports retention of implemented savings. Capture still leaks through commission competition, broader service expectations, producer incentives, technology spending, and client churn; union or regulatory caps also limit pricing flexibility.

## Firm availability
Broad owner exit evidence suggests a real transfer pool, but completed outside control sales should be much lower than stated exit intent. A buyer must screen aggressively for assignable client agreements, low star-client concentration, firm-owned data and relationships, and a management layer that survives the founder.

## Demand durability
BLS projects modest growth in the core occupation, consistent with continued need to represent a growing and fragmenting universe of talent and monetization channels. Self-service tools will take routine work and some smaller clients, while regulated procurement, network access, negotiation, and accountability preserve most operator-mediated demand.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are the absence of direct industry task-time data, variation across talent verticals, client portability at change of control, winner-take-most revenue concentration, and whether software platforms disintermediate entry-level representation. Confidential data, hallucinated advice, conflicts, unauthorized legal work, and labor or union restrictions can also slow implementation.

## Caveats
- a: O*NET describes occupation tasks and importance, not industry payroll shares or time spent.
- a: The industry also employs assistants, accountants, marketers, and executives whose mix is not separately weighted here.
- a: Exposure means technical opportunity, not that clients or counterparties will accept autonomous execution.
- rho: The Census adoption figures cover all nonfarm sectors and any business function, not this industry or labor substitution.
- rho: Small agencies may adopt general-purpose tools quickly while still failing to redesign workflows enough to realize labor savings.
- rho: Client consent, privacy, union rules, and unauthorized-practice concerns can constrain deployment in sensitive advice and negotiation.
- e: The supplied n is an estimated EBITDA-band count and may include firms with materially different agency, management, and business-management models.
- e: Occupation self-employment is not the same population as LMM employer firms.
- e: Eligibility can change abruptly when a star client or rainmaker departs.
- s5: The survey is voluntary, cross-industry, and oriented toward owners engaged with exit planning.
- s5: Exit intent is not a completed qualifying control transfer.
- s5: Public deal databases can undercount small private transactions.
- q: SAG-AFTRA rules apply to covered performers and franchised agents, not all sports, literary, music, influencer, or public-figure representation.
- q: Commission economics can retain cost savings but also make revenue volatile with client earnings.
- q: The estimate excludes demand or volume loss, which is reflected in d5 and o.
- d5: Employment is not service quantity and includes self-employed workers.
- d5: The ten-year national occupation projection is converted to a five-year industry demand ratio.
- d5: The source does not isolate the LMM agency population or constant-quality service basket.
- o: California licensing and SAG-AFTRA franchise rules cover important but incomplete slices of the national industry.
- o: Sports, literary, music, influencer, and business-management segments have different operator requirements.
- o: Technology may unbundle low-value clients from full-service representation faster than it replaces high-stakes representation.
