# 551114 — Corporate, Subsidiary, and Regional Managing Offices

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `551114-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 6.75 · L 1.41 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Centralized corporate functions contain a large volume of digitized, repeatable analysis and administration that AI can assist while executives retain final authority.
**Weakness:** The official industry definition is fundamentally captive and intra-enterprise, so the fixed external-customer lens leaves almost no demonstrated eligible or independently transferable firms.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Only the rare edge case in which an establishment recorded in 551114 provides recurring, paid management or administrative services to unaffiliated external customers with meaningful operational accountability. Because the official definition requires management of affiliated establishments within the same enterprise, this included population is expected to consist mainly of classification leakage or short-lived transition arrangements rather than the normal industry population.
- Excluded: Corporate, subsidiary, and regional offices that manage affiliated establishments; parent and holding-company offices; captive shared-services centers; nonoperating central administrative offices; shells and non-control financing vehicles; and consulting, accounting, payroll, legal, advertising, or other specialist providers that belong in their respective service industries.
- Customer and revenue model: The standard 551114 establishment is funded by its parent or affiliates through budgets and intercompany cost allocations, not by arm's-length external customers. A qualifying edge case would need recurring third-party management agreements and externally paid fees, with the provider accountable for delivery rather than merely owning or financing the client.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
NAICS 551114 is a poor structural match to the fixed acquisition lens. Its defining activity is managing affiliated establishments within the same enterprise, and Census separately treats central administrative offices as nonoperating establishments. The normal population therefore lacks both unaffiliated customers and standalone service revenue; any qualifying target is more likely a coding anomaly or temporary transition structure than a repeatable market segment.

## How AI changes the work
The underlying work is highly exposed to AI in research, document analysis, reporting, reconciliation, planning support, HR administration, and routine communications. Deployment should be feasible in centralized digital workflows, but confidentiality, governance, integration, and required executive review keep realized adoption below technical capability.

## Value capture
Most normal establishments generate parent-level savings rather than third-party provider margin. Cost allocation and pass-through pricing can transmit gains to affiliates, while an arm's-length fixed-fee edge case could retain more; direct evidence on commercial capture inside this code is absent.

## Firm availability
Availability is constrained first by near-zero eligibility and then by ownership structure. A corporate office ordinarily transfers with its parent or through a reorganization, not as an independent provider with portable external contracts. The frozen count of 6,988 is a margin-based estimate, not a verified target list, and can be materially distorted by internal receipts and the placeholder margin.

## Demand durability
The need for governance, budgeting, personnel management, planning, and organizational coordination is durable, with broad BLS projections indicating modest employment growth. That supports persistence of the work itself, not the existence of an external customer market under this NAICS code.

## Risks and uncertainty
The principal research risk is category error: treating internal headquarters activity and intercompany allocations as outsourced services and customer revenue. Additional uncertainty comes from broader-industry labor proxies, economy-wide AI adoption data, one-off contract evidence, and the inability of the frozen compensation-to-receipts ratio to distinguish internal funding from market revenue.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation mix is for NAICS 551100, not 551114.
- a: Technical task capability does not establish deployment, acceptance, or retained economic value.
- a: The frozen compensation-to-receipts input is economically difficult to interpret for internal or nonoperating establishments whose receipts are not arm's-length service revenue.
- rho: The Census adoption evidence is not specific to NAICS 551114 and measures reported use, not the fraction of addressable labor realized.
- rho: Large-enterprise adoption rates are not representative of every office in the industry.
- e: The frozen firm count is margin-bridged rather than a count of named targets and uses a 15% placeholder margin.
- e: Central administrative offices may not be independent firms, and internal allocations can make the frozen revenue and EBITDA-band bridge misleading.
- e: The nonzero range represents possible anomalies and transition structures, not a demonstrated external-service segment.
- s5: Gallup measures owner plans, not completed transactions.
- s5: The Gallup population is broader than the industry and the fixed EBITDA band.
- s5: Availability applies only after the near-zero eligibility filter.
- q: The agreement is one issuer's post-separation transition contract, not a 551114 industry sample.
- q: It evidences pricing mechanics, not observed AI-driven margin retention.
- q: The normal industry population does not sell services to external customers.
- d5: The BLS projection covers NAICS 551000 and national employment rather than 551114 external-service demand.
- d5: Internal administrative demand does not establish an arm's-length customer market.
- d5: Corporate reorganizations can cause establishment counts to move independently of workload.
- o: Operator control is inferred from duties, not observed after AI deployment.
- o: The industry consists largely of captive establishments rather than owner-operated external providers.
