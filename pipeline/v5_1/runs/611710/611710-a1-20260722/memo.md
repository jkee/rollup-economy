# 611710 — Educational Support Services

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `611710-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** PRIORITY · A 7.56 · L 5.09 · interval CONDITIONAL → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** A large concentration of document, analysis, administrative and content workflows that can be AI-assisted while firms retain accountable client-facing delivery.
**Weakness:** Heterogeneous business models and scarce direct evidence on recurring-revenue eligibility, transfer probability and five-year price retention.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US educational consulting, curriculum and assessment support, testing and evaluation, guidance and counseling, student-support administration, and related non-instructional services sold repeatedly or under recurring contracts to schools, institutions, families, or other external customers by firms in the lower-middle-market EBITDA band.
- Excluded: Captive school-district or university departments, pure software vendors, instructional schools and tutoring businesses classified elsewhere, shells and financing vehicles, one-person practices whose client relationships cannot transfer, and purely one-off project firms without repeat demand.
- Customer and revenue model: Customers include public and private school systems, colleges, education agencies, foundations, employers, and families. Revenue commonly combines school-year service contracts, retained or shared specialist capacity, fixed-scope packages, testing volumes, and hourly or daily consulting fees.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Educational support services combine a sizable labor base with knowledge-heavy workflows that AI can compress, but the industry is not a uniform software-substitution story. Testing, counseling, curriculum, student support and institutional consulting differ materially in recurrence, regulation and owner dependence. The most durable operator value lies in accountable interpretation, validation, implementation and client trust around AI-assisted production.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate research, curriculum and test-item drafting, report and proposal writing, record summarization, customer communications, scheduling, intake and first-pass analytics. It is less able to replace observation, counseling, live training, stakeholder alignment, exception handling, high-stakes validation and responsibility for educational outcomes. Human review, FERPA controls, integration and academic-integrity concerns limit the rate at which technical exposure becomes realized labor savings.

## Value capture
School-year contracts and fixed-scope packages can preserve savings during a term, while hourly billing passes benefits through faster by reducing billable time. At renewals, public procurement and informed customers can demand lower fees or broader scope. Firms retain more value when they bundle AI-enabled speed with trusted expertise, auditability, responsiveness and measurable outcomes rather than selling undifferentiated labor hours.

## Firm availability
The NAICS category contains repeat-service firms that can transfer, but also project consultancies and owner-branded practices whose goodwill is fragile. Adjacent education-business marketplace data confirms that transactions occur, yet it does not supply a clean denominator or represent the target EBITDA band. Transfer diligence must center on customer contracts, credential portability, concentration, key-person dependence, data rights and whether normalized earnings survive owner exit.

## Demand durability
BLS expects private educational-support employment to rise over 2024-2034, supporting modest five-year demand growth, while NCES projects fewer K-12 teachers and weaker enrollment-related fundamentals in part of the customer base. Compliance, testing, special-needs services, institutional change and outsourcing support demand, but simple content, navigation and guidance can migrate to self-service AI. Most remaining volume should still require an accountable operator even when fewer labor hours are needed.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main uncertainties are the category's business-model heterogeneity, lack of wage-weighted task data, lack of direct LMM transfer rates, unmeasured billing mix and rapid software substitution in commodity services. Procurement pressure could give savings to customers; privacy failures or unreliable outputs could erase savings; and falling school-age populations may offset outsourcing growth. The provided labor ratio mixes 2024 wages with 2022 receipts, and the 785-firm count is itself an EBITDA-margin bridge rather than an observed count.

## Caveats
- a: BLS publishes this industry as 611700, the five-digit presentation for the single six-digit 611710 industry, and the matrix is employment-weighted rather than wage-weighted.
- a: O*NET describes instructional coordinators, one important occupation, not every occupation or business model in the industry.
- a: The dataset labor ratio uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and is harmonized to the IPS basis, so its vintage and normalization limitations remain material.
- rho: OECD teacher data are not US outsourced educational-support firms and measure current use rather than durable implementation.
- rho: FERPA applicability and contracting controls vary by customer, record type and whether the provider acts for an educational institution.
- rho: Small firms may adopt commodity tools quickly but lack clean data, integrations, formal quality assurance and change capacity.
- e: No source directly measures the lens-eligible share of firms in the $1-10M normalized EBITDA band.
- e: The provided firm count is a margin-bridged ESTIMATE based on size-class receipts and a sector margin rather than observed firm EBITDA.
- e: NAICS 611710 is heterogeneous, and classification errors between consulting, tutoring, software, staffing and social services can be consequential.
- s5: BizBuySell is voluntary broker-reported marketplace data and is neither NAICS-pure nor representative of lower-middle-market EBITDA firms.
- s5: The source confirms sale activity and pricing, not an industry transaction probability.
- s5: Succession intent, owner age and strategic-acquirer activity for 611710 are not directly observed.
- q: One cooperative's pricing menu is not representative of all US commercial, family-pay or higher-education contracts.
- q: The mix of fixed fee, retainer, per-test, daily and hourly billing is not measured for the industry.
- q: The estimate excludes demand volume effects and implementation difficulty, which belong in other primitives.
- d5: BLS provides employment rather than output for the detailed 611700 line and the projection starts in 2024, not 2026.
- d5: NCES K-12 teacher projections were prepared in 2022 and cover customers rather than outsourced support-service demand.
- d5: Demand varies sharply across testing, counseling, curriculum, exchange and institutional-consulting subsegments.
- o: No source directly measures software substitution or operator elimination for NAICS 611710.
- o: Commodity guidance and content services may disappear faster than regulated testing, counseling or institution-specific implementation.
- o: The estimate separates operator requirement from labor implementation and pricing retention to avoid double counting.
