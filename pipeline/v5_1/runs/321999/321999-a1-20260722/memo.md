# 321999 — All Other Miscellaneous Wood Product Manufacturing

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `321999-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** STRUCTURAL_OUT · A 6.15 · L 0.47 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → LOW_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Repeat B2B component and processing relationships create transferable operations and standardized information workflows that AI can improve.
**Weakness:** The residual code is highly heterogeneous and physically intensive, making both the eligible population and wage-weighted AI opportunity difficult to observe.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent US manufacturers of miscellaneous wood and cork products with repeat external B2B supply relationships, including standardized dowels, handles, ladders, kiln-drying output, wooden housings, and other recurring industrial or wholesale products, provided the operation is transferable and in the target band.
- Excluded: One-off craft and consumer-artifact businesses, captive plants, hobby operations, project shells, pure retailers, and wood-product categories assigned to other NAICS codes such as millwork, pallets, manufactured homes, and prefabricated buildings.
- Customer and revenue model: Revenue comes from per-unit, batch, or processing sales, often under repeat purchase orders or distributor relationships; it is product revenue rather than a recurring service fee, except for limited repeat processing such as kiln drying.
- Deviation from default lens: The catch-all code mixes materially different product markets and is not primarily an outsourced-service industry. It is narrowed to repeat B2B supply and processing relationships to keep one coherent transferable-firm population.

## Executive view
The catch-all industry has a real but limited AI opportunity in commercial and production-support workflows. Its biggest analytical problem is heterogeneity: repeat OEM components, processing services, branded household goods, and craft-like products should not be treated as one business model, so only repeat B2B supply is retained.

## How AI changes the work
AI can improve quoting, order entry, purchasing, inventory, scheduling, quality documentation, product content, customer service, and management reporting. The core cutting, shaping, drying, finishing, assembly, packing, and material-handling work remains physical.

## Value capture
Savings can be retained where specifications, quality, short runs, or service differentiate supply, but commodity products face purchasing pressure and material alternatives. Pass-through is likely meaningful over a five-year customer repricing cycle.

## Firm availability
The injected firm count suggests a broad pool, but only a subset has repeat external relationships, standalone management, target-band economics, and low owner dependence. General US succession evidence supports moderate transfer flow, while industry-specific ownership and closure data are absent.

## Demand durability
A diversified set of mature physical products makes collapse unlikely, but the residual category lacks a clear growth engine and faces imports and material substitution. Flat real demand is the center, with product-line data needed to narrow the range.

## Risks and uncertainty
Risks include product-mix opacity, weak recurring-service fit, commodity pricing, imports, alternative materials, customer concentration, owner dependence, poor digital systems, and already-automated machine tasks. Broad four- and three-digit proxies carry substantial composition error.

## Caveats
- a: No six-digit occupation mix is publicly available.
- a: The product mix is unusually heterogeneous, so an average can conceal large firm-level differences.
- rho: No industry-specific AI adoption or implementation study was located.
- rho: Very small firms may rely on owner knowledge and spreadsheets that are difficult to integrate but easy to augment manually.
- e: The Census definition lists illustrative products but does not report their revenue weights.
- e: The injected n is an estimated margin bridge and may include firms that do not fit the recurring-service lens.
- s5: Cross-industry intentions are not realized transaction probabilities.
- s5: Family gifts, internal reorganizations, and asset liquidations may not qualify.
- q: No public study measures productivity pass-through for this catch-all code.
- q: Capture varies materially across commodity dowels, branded kitchenware, custom housings, and processing services.
- d5: The residual NAICS category can change composition over time.
- d5: Broad wood-product projections cannot isolate the products retained in the lens.
- o: This is an operator-requirement measure, not a forecast of employment.
- o: Different products face very different insourcing and substitution risks.
