**Driver:** Specification-sensitive physical products and replacement or project demand preserve an accountable manufacturing role.
**Weakness:** A production-heavy labor mix and recent broader-subsector output contraction limit confidence in near-term AI labor leverage and demand.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent US lower-middle-market manufacturers that repeatedly supply external commercial, construction, industrial, utility, or distribution customers with ceramic plumbing fixtures, electrical ceramic supplies and insulators, magnets, chemical stoneware, or repeat-production ceramic ware.
- Excluded: Captive plants, non-control vehicles, artist studios, bespoke decorative pottery, direct-to-consumer craft businesses, and firms without recurring or repeat outsourced customer relationships.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat purchase orders, distributor programs, project and specification sales, and approved-supplier relationships for formed, glazed, and fired ceramic products, usually billed per unit, lot, or project release.
- Deviation from default lens: The NAICS code spans studio-like pottery and repeat industrial or construction manufacturing with incompatible customer economics. It is narrowed to recurring external-customer manufacturing rather than one-off artistic or craft production.

## Executive view
The coherent opportunity lies in repeat commercial, construction, and industrial ceramics rather than one-off craft pottery. These are physical, often specification-sensitive products, but AI reaches only the information-heavy edge of a labor- and capital-intensive manufacturing process.

## How AI changes the work
AI can assist quoting, specification retrieval, planning, defect classification, corrective actions, maintenance, order processing, and purchasing. Human operators remain necessary for forming, casting, glazing, kiln operation, material handling, finishing, inspection accountability, and production exceptions.

## Value capture
Qualified technical products can retain savings, while plumbing fixtures and repeat ware face distributor pressure, project bids, imports, and periodic price resets. Contract mix matters more than nominal product category.

## Firm availability
The supplied dataset estimates 80 firms in the EBITDA band, but code heterogeneity and craft or founder dependence reduce eligible share. Private control-transfer evidence is not directly matched to this population.

## Demand durability
Renovation, replacement, utilities, construction, and industrial needs preserve a physical product basket, but recent broader-subsector output weakness, imports, and construction cyclicality warrant a wide downside range.

## Risks and uncertainty
Product heterogeneity, domestic demand weakness, energy-intensive kilns, environmental liabilities, imports, and founder-dependent craft knowledge are major risks. Six-digit task exposure, implementation results, contracts, and transfers need direct diligence.

## Caveats
- a: BLS occupation evidence covers all NAICS 327 rather than 327110 or the narrowed lens.
- a: Exposure may differ materially between plumbing fixtures, technical ceramics, magnets, and tableware.
- rho: No 327110-specific AI implementation cohort was found.
- rho: Conventional automation or capital replacement is excluded unless AI is the enabling constraint.
- e: The supplied firm count is an ESTIMATE based on size classes and an assumed margin.
- e: The establishment-based industry definition does not reveal firm revenue model or transferability.
- s5: No directly observed eligible-firm control-transfer series was found.
- s5: Small private deals and failed processes are underreported.
- q: The Census definition supports product segmentation but not billing terms.
- q: Value capture is likely much higher in qualified technical niches than in imported or distributor-compared fixtures and ware.
- d5: BLS output data cover broader NAICS 327 and are historical annual changes, not a five-year forecast.
- d5: The lens spans products with very different construction, consumer, and industrial cycles.
- o: Imported products can displace a domestic operator even though a producer remains necessary somewhere.
- o: Continued operator requirement does not imply unchanged domestic staffing or manufacturing process.
