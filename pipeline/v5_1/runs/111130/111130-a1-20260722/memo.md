# 111130 — Dry Pea and Bean Farming

**Driver:** Durable physical food production keeps an accountable operator necessary while digital crop monitoring and decision support offer a bounded efficiency opportunity.
**Weakness:** Commodity price formation and an unobserved pool of transferable LMM farms make both benefit retention and acquisition availability highly uncertain.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independently managed US commercial farms primarily growing dry peas, dry beans, lentils, or closely classified pulse crops and repeatedly selling harvested crops to external elevators, processors, packers, exporters, food manufacturers, or other buyers.
- Excluded: Fresh green bean and pea farming; captive internal growing units; passive landholding and lease-only interests; hobby and noncommercial farms; shell entities; and operations without a transferable crop-production business.
- Customer and revenue model: Revenue is predominantly seasonal crop sales priced per hundredweight, bushel, pound, or contracted acreage/output, with economics driven by planted acres, yield, grade and quality, commodity or contract pricing, and crop rotation. Customers recur, but the product is an agricultural commodity rather than an outsourced service.
- Deviation from default lens: The NAICS activity is crop production, not a recurring outsourced service. The closest coherent population retained is independently managed commercial farms with repeat external crop sales; passive land interests and noncommercial operations are excluded because they are not transferable operating businesses.

## Executive view
This is a physical crop-production business with recurring external sales, not an outsourced-service industry. AI can improve decisions and administration, but the addressable wage base is limited and much fieldwork is already mechanized. Commodity pricing restricts retention of efficiency gains, while demand is durable but crop-specific economics are volatile. The missing compensation and LMM-firm-count inputs are central gaps and prevent a grounded view of absolute labor or acquisition opportunity.

## How AI changes the work
The most credible uses are crop and soil monitoring, remote-sensing interpretation, agronomic decision support, yield and input forecasting, recordkeeping, procurement, marketing, and equipment scheduling. Larger farms are better positioned to implement these tools. Seasonal feedback cycles, connectivity, mixed equipment fleets, and the cost of agronomic mistakes slow adoption; already deployed guidance and precision systems are not counted again.

## Value capture
Savings are difficult to hold in transparent crop markets. Buyers, crop substitution, and competing producers can push improved economics into lower prices, while equipment and technology vendors can claim part of the benefit. Specialty contracts, reliable quality, and short-lived execution advantages offer some retention, but recent pulse price declines underscore that farm-gate pricing dominates unit economics.

## Firm availability
The eligible share is uncertain because the assigned LMM establishment count is missing and public production statistics do not identify normalized EBITDA, operating-company boundaries, or transferability. Many operations are owner-dependent, mixed-crop, family-held, or inseparable from land. General employer-business succession intentions indicate potential supply, but planned transfers do not reliably become qualifying control transactions.

## Demand durability
Dry pulses remain established food and ingredient crops, and broader crop-production output is projected to grow modestly. Pulse demand and production can nevertheless swing with exports, competing-crop acreage, yields, and prices: USDA's latest outlook showed rising dry-pea output alongside declining dry-bean output and lower grower prices. An accountable farm operator remains necessary even as field and office workflows automate.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are the missing compensation and firm-count inputs, the absence of occupation data specific to dry pulse farms, self-employed labor omitted from BLS employment, volatile export and commodity conditions, classification changes caused by crop rotation, and poor public evidence on LMM farm transfers or retained automation benefits. Land ownership, water access, weather, disease, and policy exposure can overwhelm operating improvements.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation source covers all NAICS 11 industries rather than NAICS 111130.
- a: BLS occupational employment excludes proprietors and other self-employed farmers, an important share of farm work.
- a: Precision-agriculture evidence covers major field crops and farm-size groups rather than dry pulses specifically.
- rho: The estimate applies only to the exposed opportunity in a, not all labor.
- rho: Reported precision-technology adoption is not itself evidence that newer AI tools will achieve equal adoption or labor savings.
- rho: Farm size strongly affects adoption, so an LMM-specific mix is important but unavailable.
- e: The assigned establishment-count input is missing, so this share cannot imply a firm count.
- e: USDA crop statistics describe production rather than the legal entities or EBITDA needed to test eligibility.
- e: Many farms rotate crops, making primary-industry classification unstable across years.
- s5: The proxy covers employer businesses across industries, not dry pulse farms.
- s5: Stated intention is not a completed qualifying control transfer.
- s5: Intrafamily transfers may not create an externally acquirable opportunity.
- q: No source directly measures the retained share of AI-enabled benefits.
- q: Retention varies between commodity spot sales and specialty or identity-preserved contracts.
- q: Recent grower-price declines illustrate exposure to market pricing but do not isolate pass-through of automation savings.
- d5: The BLS series is for all crop production, not NAICS 111130.
- d5: Recent USDA figures are physical production and price observations, not a five-year constant-price demand forecast.
- d5: Weather, export access, competing crops, and substitution among pulse varieties create substantial volatility.
- o: The boundary between farm operator, custom field-service provider, and vertically integrated buyer could change.
- o: A high operator-required share does not mean the incumbent staffing model remains unchanged.
