## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market organizations classified in 813319 that repeatedly provide advocacy, public education, member information, community mobilization, campaign administration, or related program services to unaffiliated members, funders, contracting agencies, or constituencies, supported by recurring memberships, contributions, grants, sponsorships, or service contracts.
- Excluded: Human-rights organizations in 813311; environmental, conservation, and wildlife organizations in 813312; civic and social membership organizations in 813410; legal-service providers; direct community-service agencies classified elsewhere; political organizations; captive advocacy units; volunteer-only entities; shells; and organizations without a repeat external-funder or member service model.
- Customer and revenue model: Economic supporters and service counterparties include members, donors, foundations, sponsors, government agencies, and institutional partners, while the public or a defined constituency often receives the mission output. Revenue is commonly recurring or episodic contributions, grants, memberships, sponsorships, events, and some service contracts rather than conventional customer fees.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Other social advocacy organizations are labor-intensive networks for research, fundraising, communications, organizing, and administration. AI can assist much of the information workflow, but the fixed acquisition lens fits poorly because most organizations are mission-led nonprofits without transferable equity ownership or distributable economics.

## How AI changes the work
Useful applications include issue and policy monitoring, first-draft briefs and grant materials, donor and member research, CRM cleanup, audience segmentation, content adaptation, meeting preparation, reporting, and routine correspondence. Humans remain central to trusted persuasion, coalition building, sensitive public statements, field organizing, donor relationships, governance, strategy, and final accountability.

## Value capture
Fixed grants, unrestricted contributions, dues, and sponsorships can preserve savings as added mission capacity, while restricted grants, cost reimbursement, donor expectations, and annual budgeting pass benefits into more output or lower future funding. Retained mission capacity should not be confused with owner cash flow.

## Firm availability
The injected firm stock is large but margin-bridged using for-profit economics. Only a small subset is likely to combine recurring external payer relationships, professional systems, transferable contracts or memberships, and a legal form that permits a qualifying control transaction.

## Demand durability
Social causes, policy conflict, public education, and community mobilization create durable needs, with broad BLS projections showing continued activity. Individual causes and funding streams remain volatile, and direct digital mobilization can substitute for some formal-organization work.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are nonprofit legal form, asset and donor restrictions, absence of saleable control, cause and political concentration, grant dependence, donor churn, reputational harm, misinformation and bias, privacy of donor or constituency data, volunteer dependence, governance constraints, and a dataset firm count whose margin bridge may not fit nonprofit economics.

- a: The broad industry includes human-rights and environmental organizations whose legal, scientific, and field work may not resemble other advocacy organizations.
- a: O*NET fundraiser and public-relations occupations represent important workflows but not the full workforce.
- a: The injected compensation ratio uses 2024 wages and 2022 receipts and may be distorted by nonprofit accounting and contributed resources.
- rho: No source measures five-year AI implementation in 813319.
- rho: Capabilities and constraints differ sharply between scaled national organizations and small local advocacy groups.
- rho: Reputational or political failures can outweigh labor savings even when a workflow is technically automatable.
- e: The NAICS definition establishes activities and funding mechanisms but not the share fitting a transferable outsourced-service lens.
- e: For nonprofits, EBITDA and ownership-transfer concepts are often inapplicable even when operations are economically substantial.
- e: The injected n of 702 is margin-bridged from firm counts using a for-profit business-and-consumer-services margin and may materially overstate the relevant stock.
- s5: No cited source measures qualifying control transfers in 813319.

- s5: Legal form and donor restrictions can prevent an acquirer from capturing residual economics or redirecting assets.
- q: No source measures retention of implemented AI benefits in this industry.
- q: Mission capacity retained by an organization is not equivalent to cash distributable to an owner.
- q: Funding structures vary widely across memberships, donations, grants, sponsorships, and contracts.
