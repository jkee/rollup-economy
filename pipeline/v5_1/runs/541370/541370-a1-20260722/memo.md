# 541370 — Surveying and Mapping (except Geophysical) Services

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `541370-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.71 · L 3.74 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** A large digital production layer sits beside durable field verification and licensed accountability, creating room to expand capacity while preserving an operator role.
**Weakness:** No industry-specific evidence directly measures five-year control transfers, AI implementation, or retained productivity benefits for lower-middle-market firms.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market providers of cadastral, boundary, topographic, geodetic, construction, hydrographic, aerial, photogrammetric, LiDAR, GIS, and cartographic surveying or mapping services supplied to external customers on repeat project, task-order, or continuing-service relationships.
- Excluded: Geophysical surveying, map and atlas publishing, captive government or corporate survey units, software-only products, shells, non-control financing vehicles, and firms without repeat outsourced-service relationships.
- Customer and revenue model: Professional project and task-order services for public agencies, engineering and construction firms, utilities, real-estate owners, and resource clients. Engagements are commonly billed hourly or per diem, as cost plus a fixed fee, as a lump sum for defined scope, or by a fee related to construction cost; repeat business comes from continuing capital programs, land transactions, compliance, asset records, and map updates.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
This is a field-and-office professional service in which AI can compress digital production without removing the need for physical evidence, licensed judgment, and accountable delivery. Repeat public infrastructure, construction, utility, land, and map-update work supports a durable service base, while project billing and competitive rebids limit how much productivity becomes lasting operator benefit. Evidence is strongest on tasks and demand direction and weakest on the exact transaction rate and retained-benefit share for firms in the frozen size band.

## How AI changes the work
AI is most relevant to imagery and point-cloud classification, feature extraction, GIS and CAD drafting assistance, deed and document extraction, report preparation, scheduling, and anomaly detection. Mature GPS, robotic instruments, scanning, and drones already removed part of the opportunity and should not be counted again. Field setup, monument recovery, resolving conflicting evidence, accuracy control, client coordination, and professional sign-off remain human-intensive.

## Value capture
Lump-sum and defined-scope work can retain labor savings until repricing, and qualifications-based selection can reward expertise rather than the lowest initial price. Hourly, per-diem, and cost-based billing exposes productivity through fewer billable hours, while renewals, fee negotiation, and rival adoption share gains with customers. Capacity released into a healthy backlog is more monetizable than headcount reduction in a weak market.

## Firm availability
The establishment definition is naturally aligned with external service providers, and many eligible firms should have repeat client relationships. Broader AEC evidence points to active acquisitions, founder-led sellers, and retirement pressure, but it does not establish a direct control-transfer rate for this industry. Licensed-principal dependence, local relationships, and weak succession planning can make an apparent firm less transferable even when revenue is repeat.

## Demand durability
Boundary certification, construction verification, infrastructure work, planning, utility records, and disaster mapping remain necessary, while national elevation programs call for repeated higher-quality collection. Positive core-occupation outlooks support modest growth rather than contraction. Construction cycles, delayed public appropriations, baseline map maturity, customer self-service, and software productivity create a meaningful downside case.

## Risks and uncertainty
The task share is inferred from occupations rather than measured inside the industry; implementation is bridged from drones, GPS, and other adjacent tools; and contract mix is unobserved. AI errors in geometry or legal records can create costly liability, while fragmented legacy data and software integration slow deployment. A roll-up could also lose licensed principals or local client trust after a control change. Conversely, scarce staff and growing data volumes could make validated automation more valuable than estimated.

## Caveats
- a: BLS occupation pages describe workers across industries, not the wage mix inside NAICS 541370.
- a: RICS evidence is qualitative, UK-based, and spans geomatics activities beyond the frozen US firm population.
- a: The boundary between mature automation and new AI is especially uncertain for photogrammetry, point clouds, and feature extraction.
- rho: UAS and GPS are adjacent mature technologies, not direct measures of generative or predictive AI implementation.
- rho: State transportation agencies have procurement scale and technical resources unlike many privately held firms.
- rho: POB respondent selection and firm-size composition are not reported well enough to establish representativeness.
- e: No source measures the eligible share of the frozen lower-middle-market firm count.
- e: Project-based revenue can be repeat without being contractually recurring.
- e: The supplied firm count is margin-bridged from older Census size data, so the firms represented may not align perfectly with current normalized EBITDA.
- s5: The best available transaction evidence covers broader AEC firms and is not a probability sample of NAICS 541370.
- s5: Surveyed AEC firms skew larger than the full small-firm population, although the frozen EBITDA band overlaps part of the sample.
- s5: Retirement triggers and buyer intentions may not result in completed qualifying control transfers.
- q: The billing source is guidance from a Massachusetts trade association, not a national revenue-share survey.
- q: Federal highway procurement rules apply only to covered public contracts.
- q: Retention may be lower where firms sell staff hours and higher where scarce licensure, capacity, or trusted local records drive selection.
- d5: Occupational employment is not service quantity and includes workers outside NAICS 541370.
- d5: USGS program benefits are estimates and next-generation collection depends on funding.
- d5: The supplied labor ratio combines more recent payroll with older receipts and therefore may not reflect the same demand vintage.
- o: Licensure applies most directly to land surveying and legal certification, not every cartographic or GIS deliverable.
- o: Public LiDAR and imagery can complement firms but can also let customers self-serve some mapping work.
- o: Future software liability regimes and customer acceptance are uncertain.

## Sources
- **S1** — [NAICS 541370 Surveying and Mapping (except Geophysical) Services](https://www.census.gov/naics/?details=541370&input=541370&year=2017) (accessed 2026-07-22): Defines the industry as surveying and mapping of the earth's surface, including the sea floor, and lists cadastral, cartographic, topographic, and geodetic services while excluding geophysical surveying and map publishing.
- **S2** — [Surveyors](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/architecture-and-engineering/surveyors.htm) (accessed 2026-07-22): Describes legal-record research, field measurement, verification, mapping and reporting duties; reports 56,100 US jobs in 2024, projected employment growth of 4 percent through 2034, and licensure requirements in every state and the District of Columbia.
- **S3** — [Surveying and Mapping Technicians](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/architecture-and-engineering/surveying-and-mapping-technicians.htm) (accessed 2026-07-22): Describes field measurement, data entry, imagery processing, GIS and CAD work, calculations, and technology-output review; reports 59,400 US jobs in 2024 and projected employment growth of 5 percent through 2034.
- **S4** — [Cartographers and Photogrammetrists](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/architecture-and-engineering/cartographers-and-photogrammetrists.htm) (accessed 2026-07-22): Describes collecting and integrating surveys, LiDAR, imagery, GIS, and CAD data and updating maps; reports 13,400 US jobs in 2024 and projected employment growth of 6 percent through 2034.
- **S5** — [AI will support rather than replace surveyors](https://ww3.rics.org/uk/en/journals/land-journal/ai-will-not-replace-surveyors.html) (accessed 2026-07-22): Identifies data analysis, feature extraction, digitization, report generation, quality assurance, and document conversion as AI applications in geomatics while stressing field operation, human judgment, relationships, and accountability.
- **S6** — [States Expand UAS Use to Save Time and Money](https://www.fhwa.dot.gov/innovation/innovator/issue77/page_02.html) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports broad state transportation agency use of unmanned aircraft and cited surveying time reductions, including a Utah example in which surveying time fell by as much as half.
- **S7** — [The Economic Benefits of the Global Positioning System](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/gcr/2025/NIST.GCR.25-062.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports survey evidence that licensed-surveyor GPS adoption rose from about one fifth in 1990 to about seven tenths in 2000 and that almost all surveyed surveyors now use GPS in some capacity.
- **S8** — [Equipment Report Reveals Newer Tech Taking Hold for Surveyors](https://www.pobonline.com/articles/102091-equipment-report-reveals-newer-tech-taking-hold-for-surveyors) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports that 42 percent of respondent firms used laser scanners and about one third used drones in 2020, with productivity and efficiency the leading purchase trigger.
- **S9** — [The Next Generation 3D Elevation Program](https://pubs.usgs.gov/publication/cir1553/full) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports that 98.3 percent of US baseline elevation coverage was available or in progress in fiscal 2024 and proposes repeated, higher-quality recollection to meet documented user needs.
- **S10** — [AEC M&A Outlook 2025](https://online.flippingbook.com/view/175163864/1/) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports more than 2,700 AEC transactions since 2021, describes many sellers as small or mid-sized and founder-led, and identifies steady surveying deal activity tied to geospatial, resilience, and infrastructure demand.
- **S11** — [AEC M&A Outlook 2025: Buyer Intentions and Retirement](https://online.flippingbook.com/view/175163864/44/) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports that roughly two thirds of surveyed buyers expected acquisitions during the next five years and more than 55 percent of surveyed firms expected senior-principal retirement to trigger a transaction.
- **S12** — [ACEC-FMI Ownership Transition Study Finds Gap Between Knowledge and Intent](https://engineeringinc.acec.org/blog/acec-fmi-ownership-transition-study-finds-gap-between-knowledge-and-intent-2/) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports an ownership-transition survey of 186 engineering firm owners, including that 62 percent had an ownership plan and only 51 percent of firms with fewer than 50 employees had a formal plan.
- **S13** — [How an Engineering Firm or Land Surveying Firm Charges for Its Services](https://www.acecma.org/news/how-an-engineering-firm-or-land-surveying-firm-charges-for-its-services/) (accessed 2026-07-22): Describes hourly or per-diem, salary-cost multiplier, cost-plus-fixed-fee, lump-sum, and construction-cost-linked billing methods used by engineering and land-surveying firms.
- **S14** — [23 CFR 172.7 Procurement Methods and Procedures](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/23/172.7) (accessed 2026-07-22): Requires qualifications-based selection for covered federal-aid highway engineering and design-related consulting, bars price from initial ranking, and provides for fee negotiation after selection.
