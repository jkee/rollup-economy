**Driver:** Recurring physical care requirements and diversified potential rabbit uses that cannot be supplied by software alone.
**Weakness:** The measured mink segment is contracting sharply, while no defensible outsourced-service firm population or rabbit demand series is available.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market firms, if any, providing repeat outsourced breeding, raising, husbandry, or production of rabbits or other fur-bearing animals for external animal owners, research programs, processors, or brands.
- Excluded: Owner-operated farms selling their own animals or pelts, hobby and backyard operations, passive animal or financing vehicles, wild trapping and hunting, unrelated laboratory-animal species, boarding or veterinary services, and one-off animal transactions without recurring operating responsibility.
- Customer and revenue model: The qualifying model would use recurring per-animal, capacity, husbandry, breeding, or production agreements with accountability for animal care, biosecurity, records, and delivery. Public sources document animal and pelt production but do not establish an outsourced-service population or representative contracts inside 112930.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed to repeat outsourced animal production because 112930 combines rabbit markets with mink and other fur-animal production and is primarily product farming, while the fixed lens requires recurring outsourced service. The included end markets have materially different demand and regulatory profiles.

## Executive view
112930 is a small, heterogeneous product-farming category with a sharply contracting mink segment and opaque rabbit end markets. Public evidence does not establish an LMM outsourced husbandry population, while both frozen dataset inputs are missing.

## How AI changes the work
AI can improve breeding and health records, feed planning, monitoring triage, compliance, inventory, and scheduling. Continuous physical animal care, cleaning, handling, biosecurity, and welfare accountability remain operator-dependent.

## Value capture
Commercial retention cannot be bounded without actual contract-production agreements. Pelt and live-animal prices measure product economics, not contractual sharing of labor productivity.

## Firm availability
Agriculture data identify animals and operations but not a transferable LMM service-firm denominator. Industry contraction may create closures and asset sales rather than qualifying control transfers.

## Demand durability
Mink-pelt production has fallen steeply over the past decade. Rabbit demand may be more diversified across food, breeding, pet, and research uses, but public data are insufficient to establish that it offsets fur-market decline or creates outsourced service demand.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are absence of a scalable service target pool, missing labor and firm counts, fur-demand decline, animal-welfare and biosecurity obligations, disease, customer and auction concentration, opaque rabbit markets, small operation scale, product substitution, and nontransferable family farms.

- rho: No 112930-specific AI implementation study was found.

- q: Commodity pelt or animal prices are not service billing structures.
