# 611420 — Computer Training

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `611420-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** PRIORITY · A 7.13 · L 4.50 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Recurring technology change supports reskilling demand while digital workflows expose a large share of provider labor to AI assistance.
**Weakness:** Generic computer instruction can migrate rapidly to vendor content, self-paced platforms, and AI tutors, limiting retention and operator necessity.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Privately owned computer-training firms that repeatedly provide external employers with programming, software application, business-system, computer operations, cybersecurity, cloud, data, and network training, including instructor-led, virtual, blended, lab, and managed-learning delivery.
- Excluded: Consumer-only one-off bootcamps, degree programs, computer repair training, captive internal academies, pure courseware or software vendors without an accountable training service, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Recurring employer contracts, annual training programs, subscriptions with services, managed cohorts, or repeat fixed-fee and per-seat engagements; revenue combines instruction, labs, curriculum adaptation, assessment, and program administration.
- Deviation from default lens: The code mixes consumer career training and repeat employer-funded upskilling with materially different acquisition, retention, and pricing. The lens is narrowed to recurring external employer training to preserve a coherent outsourced-service population.

## Executive view
Recurring enterprise computer training has a credible target population and benefits from continued technology-driven reskilling, while its highly digital workflow offers substantial AI-enabled labor leverage. The same technology also expands self-service learning and price transparency, so durable value depends on enterprise integration, trusted facilitation, secure labs, assessment, and accountability rather than generic content alone.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate curriculum refreshes, code examples, exercises, assessment generation, translation, personalization, first-line tutoring, sales support, scheduling, and reporting. Human experts remain necessary to verify technical accuracy, diagnose enterprise needs, facilitate difficult cohorts, manage secure labs, protect credential integrity, and own outcomes.

## Value capture
Providers can retain savings under fixed-fee cohorts and annual managed programs, especially where the service is customized or authorized. Generic course delivery is contestable, and renewals are likely to pass part of the benefit to buyers through price, extra scope, or improved support.

## Firm availability
The supplied estimate of 159 target-band firms indicates a nontrivial potential universe, but eligibility depends on separating recurring employer providers from consumer bootcamps, courseware businesses, and project-only trainers. No industry-specific control-transfer rate was found.

## Demand durability
Computer and IT employment and the broader training industry are projected to grow, and frequent changes in AI, cloud, cybersecurity, data, and software create repeated skill gaps. The service basket must move toward advanced, customized, and accountable learning because introductory content is increasingly free or AI-supplied.

## Risks and uncertainty
Evidence is mostly at combined NAICS 611400 or end-occupation level. Key risks are self-service substitution, course commoditization, vendor disintermediation, rapid content obsolescence, technical errors, certification constraints, client security restrictions, and acquisition targets whose value resides in founders or nontransferable authorizations.

## Caveats
- a: BLS combines business, secretarial, computer, and management training at NAICS 611400.
- a: Claude usage is a selected product sample and not a capability or implementation measure.
- a: Freelance instructors and self-employed workers may be missing from payroll-based occupation data.
- rho: No representative 611420 AI implementation survey was found.
- rho: High staff familiarity with technology can speed adoption but sophisticated subject matter raises error costs.
- e: The supplied count of 159 target-band firms is margin-bridged and marked ESTIMATE.
- e: NAICS descriptions establish activities and delivery modes but do not measure repeat revenue or customer mix.
- s5: No observed industry-specific transfer rate or owner-age distribution was found.
- s5: Deal databases may overrepresent larger and technology-branded providers.
- q: No representative contract dataset was available.
- q: Vendor-authorized and bespoke enterprise training may retain more benefit than generic skills libraries.
- d5: The industry forecast is for combined NAICS 611400, not 611420.
- d5: Employment, job openings, and training-service quantity are different constructs.
- d5: Rapid course obsolescence creates both replacement demand and production cost.
- o: Remote delivery is not the same as operator elimination.
- o: The relevant substitution rate varies sharply between generic software skills and regulated or secure enterprise environments.
- o: NCES distance-learning figures cover a broader and older postsecondary population.
