## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market merchant wholesalers of plastic resins, compounds, unsupported film and sheet, rod, tube, and other basic shapes that repeatedly serve external processors, fabricators, OEMs, contractors, and institutional customers through sourcing, inventory, technical selection, cutting or other light value-added preparation, and logistics.
- Excluded: Resin and plastic-product manufacturers classified outside 424610; wholesale agents and brokers that do not take title; captive internal distribution; pure financial commodity trading; nontransferable owner-only books; shells; and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Distributors buy materials on their own account and earn a gross spread while providing repeat availability, supplier access, credit, material and regulatory guidance, inventory buffering, cut-to-size or light fabrication, and delivery coordination. Revenue is predominantly product sales rather than separately billed service fees.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Plastics materials distribution is a coherent repeat-purchase business with technical sales, inventory, logistics, and transaction workflows that can absorb AI. Its labor opportunity is meaningful but bounded by physical handling and engineering accountability, while stable material demand and fragmented customer needs preserve the operator's role.

## How AI changes the work
AI can improve product and specification search, quote preparation, CRM and order capture, demand planning, purchasing, document extraction, routine customer service, and exception triage. Material approvals, application engineering, credit, final pricing, cutting and warehouse work, traceability, and complex claims remain human-controlled.

## Value capture
Savings are initially retained inside the product spread, but commodity transparency and competitive bidding should pass a substantial share to customers. Specialty materials, small lots, technical support, cut-to-size work, reliable inventory, and fast delivery provide better pricing defense.

## Firm availability
The 2025 industry report identifies 2,575 establishments and 40,400 employees in the six-digit wholesale code, and an observed 2026 strategic acquisition shows team and relationship transferability. The frozen LMM firm count remains uncertain because commodity price cycles distort receipt-based EBITDA bridging.

## Demand durability
Resin sales and captive use grew modestly in 2025, while long-run real plastics shipments have been nearly flat. Diversified end uses and a shift toward recycled or redesigned materials support continued distribution demand, though producer-direct channels and single-use restrictions constrain growth.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are resin price and inventory volatility, supplier concentration and authorization changes, customer cyclicality, trade policy, direct producer sales, environmental regulation, product-liability errors, cyber or ERP failures, and overestimating LMM firms from revenue in a high-throughput sector.

## Caveats
- a: The available BLS occupation mix combines NAICS 4242 and 4246 and is not specific to plastics distribution.
- a: The frozen compensation-to-receipts ratio uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and is harmonized to an IPS basis; resin price swings can materially distort labor intensity.
- rho: The evidence is occupational and operational rather than a measured five-year adoption rate for the industry.
- rho: Smaller distributors may lack normalized product, application, and transaction data needed for reliable implementation.
- e: The industry report provides establishments, not firms or LMM eligibility.
- e: The frozen LMM count is an ESTIMATE using a broad distributor EBITDA margin; resin price cycles, inventory gains, and the mix of commodity versus specialty products can materially shift firms across the band.
- s5: One strategic acquisition demonstrates transferability but not incidence.
- s5: BizBuySell covers a broader and generally smaller wholesale population, and workforce age is not owner age or sale intent.
- q: No source directly measures pass-through of AI-enabled savings in plastics distribution.
- q: Resin price and inventory movements can swamp operating savings and make gross-margin attribution difficult.
- d5: The industry association is an advocacy organization and its long-run measure is broader than merchant distribution.
- d5: Resin volume includes captive use and direct producer sales; wholesaler service demand can diverge.
- d5: Tariffs, feedstock prices, construction and automotive cycles, recycled-content rules, and single-use restrictions widen the range.
- o: Operator need is higher for specialty and regulated applications than for truckload commodity resin.
- o: A digital marketplace can preserve an accountable operator in a thinner form; this primitive is not a labor-share estimate.
