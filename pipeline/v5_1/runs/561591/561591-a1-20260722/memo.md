# 561591 — Convention and Visitors Bureaus

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `561591-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 5.77 · L 1.52 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Marketing, information, translation, research, and reporting workflows are digitally intensive and already show practical AI adoption.
**Weakness:** Publicly funded nonprofit and board-governed structures sharply limit the pool of independently transferable firms.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market operators repeatedly providing destination marketing, convention and meeting sales support, visitor information, itinerary content, local-partner promotion, and related destination-management services to external municipalities, tourism districts, lodging stakeholders, event organizers, businesses, and travelers.
- Excluded: Government tourism-development offices, captive municipal departments, entities without independently transferable control, shells, convention and trade-show organizers, venue operators, travel agencies, tour operators, and software-only travel-information products.
- Customer and revenue model: The economic customers and funders are commonly municipalities, tourism improvement districts, lodging and hospitality members, and other destination stakeholders, while travelers and meeting planners receive many services without a direct charge. Revenue commonly comes from dedicated occupancy or sales taxes, district assessments, government service contracts, memberships, sponsorships, advertising, and grants rather than transaction fees from visitors.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Convention and visitors bureaus have substantial AI-assisted marketing, research, translation, reporting, and visitor-service workflows. The central constraint is not technical adoption but ownership: public funding and nonprofit or board governance make ordinary transferable equity control uncommon.

## How AI changes the work
AI can draft and localize destination content, synthesize research, prepare campaign variants and sales materials, answer routine visitor questions, summarize CRM activity, and support reporting and personalization. Human teams remain necessary for convention selling, local fact validation, public accountability, community relations, funding advocacy, brand judgment, and multi-party event coordination.

## Value capture
Dedicated taxes, assessments, memberships, and approved contracts are not priced per labor hour, so automation can be retained as greater service capacity, faster response, broader language coverage, or margin. Public-budget reviews, nonprofit reinvestment, procurement, and stakeholder scrutiny can redirect much of that benefit rather than leave it as distributable earnings.

## Firm availability
The frozen population is not a clean acquisition universe. Destination organizations receive most funding through public-sector channels, and prominent operators are private nonprofits under boards and municipal contracts. Only a small subset is likely to have privately transferable control and separable normalized earnings.

## Demand durability
Real domestic leisure, business, and group travel are forecast to grow, preserving the need to attract visitors and meetings. Basic information discovery is moving toward AI and platforms, but official destination representation, convention coordination, local partnerships, advocacy, and stewardship remain durable operator functions. Funding politics can break the link between travel growth and bureau budgets.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest risks are treating nonprofit budgets as commercial EBITDA, counting governance changes or contract rebids as control transfers, public-funding diversion, factual or brand failures from AI content, international and local travel shocks, and accelerated disintermediation of destination websites. A population dominated by nontransferable entities would offer little conventional roll-up inventory despite useful automation.

## Caveats
- a: BLS publishes an occupation mix for NAICS 561500, not Convention and Visitors Bureaus alone, and that parent is dominated by travel agencies and reservation services.
- a: Destinations International describes functions and examples but does not report a wage-weighted task inventory or current automation baseline.
- a: Public-facing marketing workflows may be more exposed than convention sales, stakeholder governance, and destination-development work, so organization mix matters materially.
- rho: The 2025 Futures Study has 537 participants across 36 countries rather than a representative U.S. six-digit sample.
- rho: The industry blog emphasizes a handful of named adopters and does not publish the survey response count or realized payroll savings.
- rho: Public records, accessibility, procurement, brand safety, and community-trust obligations can slow implementation even when tools are technically available.
- e: No source measures the legal-form and transferability distribution of the 101 frozen LMM-band firms.
- e: The frozen firm count is margin-bridged using a commercial-services margin that may be misleading for nonprofit and budget-consuming destination organizations.
- e: Public funding does not by itself prove public ownership, and some nonprofit organizations contract with governments while remaining operationally independent, but neither structure creates ordinary equity control.
- s5: Gallup covers employer businesses across industries and measures plans rather than completed qualifying transfers.
- s5: Destination-organization governance changes, executive succession, mergers of nonprofits, and public contract rebids usually are not purchases of equity control.
- s5: The tiny eligible subset makes deal-frequency estimates especially unstable.
- q: No source measures the five-year share of implemented gross benefit retained by U.S. lower-middle-market CVBs.
- q: Retention as distributable margin differs sharply from retention as additional mission capacity in nonprofit organizations.
- q: Funding structures vary among dedicated taxes, improvement-district assessments, municipal contracts, memberships, sponsorships, advertising, and grants.
- d5: Travel spending is a demand driver, not a direct measure of CVB service quantity or constant-quality output.
- d5: The forecast extends through 2030, one year short of a full five-year horizon from 2026, and travel forecasts can change materially.
- d5: Funding risk is global survey evidence, while travel forecasts are national U.S. aggregates that mask destination-specific winners and losers.
- o: No source quantifies the operator-required share for CVBs over five years.
- o: AI-powered search is already reducing traditional website traffic, but its effect on convention wins, partner value, and public mandates is not measured.
- o: Large convention destinations and small visitor-information bureaus have different exposure to self-service substitution.
