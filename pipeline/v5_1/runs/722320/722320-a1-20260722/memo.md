# 722320 — Caterers

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `722320-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 6.65 · L 1.60 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Repeat-event operators can use AI to compress high-friction quoting, planning, scheduling, purchasing, and back-office work while retaining a locally delivered physical service.
**Weakness:** Most labor is physical and public data do not isolate LMM caterers, leaving both the implementable task share and qualifying transfer rate dependent on broad proxies.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US catering firms in the lower-middle-market band that prepare, deliver, and often serve food for customer events, with repeat demand from corporate clients, institutions, venues, event planners, or recurring social-event referral channels.
- Excluded: Food-service contractors operating cafeterias or concessions under NAICS 722310, mobile food services under NAICS 722330, restaurants whose catering is incidental, captive in-house event teams, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: External corporate, nonprofit, public-sector, venue, planner, and household customers usually buy a defined event package or per-guest service. Revenue is project-like at the event level; repeatability comes from recurring customer events, venue and planner referrals, and framework or preferred-vendor relationships.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Catering combines a real but bounded administrative automation opportunity with a service core that remains physical, local, and safety-accountable. The most credible operating gains are in proposals, customer follow-up, event specifications, staff scheduling, purchasing, inventory, payroll, marketing, and post-event records. The investment case weakens where a firm's value is primarily the founder's taste, sales relationships, or on-site judgment, or where demand is concentrated in discretionary one-off social events.

## How AI changes the work
AI can turn inquiries and prior-event data into draft proposals, menus, staffing plans, production sheets, purchasing lists, schedules, and customer messages; it can also assist forecasting, bookkeeping, compliance documentation, and marketing. Human review remains necessary for allergies, quantities, menu feasibility, venue constraints, staffing, timing, and customer promises. Cooking, packing, loading, transport, setup, service, sanitation, and live exception handling dominate the headcount and are not near-term language-model substitutions.

## Value capture
Fixed package and per-guest pricing can preserve savings on work already contracted, especially when better forecasting reduces overtime, waste, missed follow-up, and avoidable administrative hiring. Retention fades when each event is newly quoted, preferred-vendor arrangements are rebid, customers compare standardized packages, or competitors adopt the same tools. Benefits that improve responsiveness or reduce errors may be more defensible than generic content-generation savings.

## Firm availability
Older-owner evidence and observed restaurant transactions indicate a succession and transaction channel, but neither measures LMM caterers directly. Transferability depends on repeat accounts, venue and planner relationships, documented recipes and production systems, permits, a second management layer, and the ability to sell without the founder. Businesses below the screened band dominate public small-business marketplace data, so direct broker evidence is still needed.

## Demand durability
Official projections for Special Food Services point to modest employment growth, while the catering basket remains tied to human gatherings and outsourced physical execution. Corporate events and weddings can contract sharply in a downturn, and simple drop-off meals can migrate to restaurants or platforms. Full-service and operationally complex events are more durable because customers continue to need coordinated food production, safety accountability, delivery, setup, and service.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest evidence weakness is that public occupation and demand data aggregate caterers with food-service contractors and mobile food services. Restaurant technology surveys may overstate what independent event caterers can implement. Revenue can be seasonal and customer acquisition can depend on reputation, venues, and planners. Food inflation, labor availability, event cancellations, local permitting, allergens, customer concentration, and founder dependence can overwhelm administrative savings. A poor target would have weak repeat channels, informal processes, thin management, and no clean separation between owner relationships and enterprise value.

## Caveats
- a: The closest public occupation mix is four-digit NAICS 722300 and includes food-service contractors and mobile food services.
- a: Task exposure is researcher-mapped rather than directly measured, and no source reports wage-weighted AI exposure for caterers.
- a: The BLS occupation profile is May 2023, while O*NET task descriptions were accessed in 2026.
- rho: The technology survey covers restaurants rather than caterers and reports plans, not completed labor substitution.
- rho: Planned spending can exceed realized implementation, while five-year product improvements are uncertain.
- rho: Implementation varies sharply with firm scale, software stack, menu complexity, and event customization.
- e: No public dataset separates recurring-channel caterers from one-off consumer-event firms within 722320.
- e: Eligibility is estimated after the frozen LMM firm-count bridge and does not revise that dataset input.
- e: A firm may have many events but still depend on a founder's personal reputation and relationships.
- s5: Owner-age evidence is broad across US employer businesses and is not industry-specific.
- s5: BizBuySell restaurant transactions are platform-listed small businesses, not a complete caterer or LMM control-transfer census.
- s5: Retirement intention, timing, transferability, and successful closing are distinct and are not observed jointly.
- q: The GAO case is federal emergency catering and may not represent corporate or social-event contracting.
- q: No source directly measures five-year pass-through of AI-enabled catering cost savings.
- q: Customer mix, local competition, food inflation, and venue exclusivity can materially change retention.
- d5: Employment is a proxy for constant-price, constant-quality service quantity and embeds productivity assumptions.
- d5: NAICS 722300 includes food-service contractors and mobile food services in addition to caterers.
- d5: Catering demand is exposed to recessions, event cancellations, remote-work patterns, and changes in wedding and corporate-event budgets.
- o: The FDA Food Code is a model adopted and modified by jurisdictions, not a single uniform federal operating mandate.
- o: O*NET's institutional-cook occupation is not specific to event caterers.
- o: Drop-off formats and standardized corporate meals face more substitution than full-service, customized events.
