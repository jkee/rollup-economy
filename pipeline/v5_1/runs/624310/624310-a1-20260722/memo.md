# 624310 — Vocational Rehabilitation Services

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.20 · L 3.31 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Case documentation, planning support, monitoring, and job-development workflows offer implementable AI assistance inside recurring payer-authorized services.
**Weakness:** The code's nonprofit, public, and sheltered-workshop composition makes the eligible transferable-firm population unusually uncertain and limits conventional control transactions.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market providers of recurring or repeat vocational assessment, rehabilitation counseling, case management, job development, placement, coaching, supported-employment, and related training services delivered for external state agencies, federal programs, workers' compensation payers, employers, or other institutional customers.
- Excluded: State agencies performing services internally, captive programs, shells, non-control financing vehicles, schools classified elsewhere, general career counseling that is not rehabilitative, and sheltered-workshop or social-enterprise operations whose primary business is employing participants to produce or sell goods rather than supplying vocational rehabilitation services to an external payer.
- Customer and revenue model: Providers are commonly authorized or contracted by government vocational-rehabilitation programs and paid through fixed fee schedules, time-based service units, documented deliverables, or placement and successful-closure milestones. Other revenue can come from workers' compensation, employer, nonprofit, grant, and participant-program sources.
- Deviation from default lens: The code combines outsourced counseling and supported-employment providers with sheltered workshops and enterprises that employ participants in production, retail, custodial, or logistics work. The lens is narrowed to externally paid vocational-rehabilitation service providers because workshop operating labor, revenue, automation, and transfer economics would make a combined screen incoherent.

## Executive view
Vocational rehabilitation is highly labor intensive and contains real opportunities to streamline documentation, case workflow, labor-market research, and job matching. The core service remains individualized, regulated, and relationship dependent, while the eligible acquisition population is narrowed by nonprofit governance, public delivery, mixed disability-service portfolios, and sheltered-workshop activities inside the code.

## How AI changes the work
AI can draft case notes and reports, summarize records, surface job leads, support structured assessments and plans, monitor milestones, and help counselors tailor communications. It should remain decision support rather than autonomous placement or eligibility machinery because disability-sensitive hiring and assessment can create discrimination and accessibility risks. Human counseling, advocacy, employer negotiation, accommodation planning, coaching, and judgment remain central.

## Value capture
Fixed fees and milestone payments may preserve workflow gains between rate reviews, but time-unit billing rewards recorded activity rather than speed. Public payers can update schedules, rebid contracts, demand more capacity, or disallow work that does not meet documentation and human-service requirements. Capture therefore depends on the contract mix and whether AI frees genuinely avoidable labor rather than scarce staff capacity.

## Firm availability
External community rehabilitation vendors clearly exist, and provider mergers and program acquisitions occur. The investable subset is uncertain because many organizations are nonprofits, diversified human-services groups, public programs, or workshop operators, and the frozen firm count relies on a placeholder margin that may not translate well to grant-funded and nonprofit entities.

## Demand durability
Persistent disability employment gaps and continuing state-federal vocational-rehabilitation funding support durable need. The occupational forecast is subdued, and federal reallotment evidence reveals uneven agency funding and execution. Vendor demand is therefore likely to be stable but exposed to public budgets, referrals, policy priorities, and the outsourced share of delivery.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main risks are a small conventional control-transfer population, reimbursement that does not reward productivity, privacy and accessibility failures, algorithmic disability bias, fragmented state procurement, counselor licensure and oversight, and client harm from poor matching. The largest evidence gaps are lens-specific wage task shares, provider ownership and EBITDA, actual AI deployments, payer contract mix, and qualifying transaction rates.

## Caveats
- a: The BLS occupation table covers the full NAICS category rather than the narrowed lens.
- a: No source supplies wage-weighted task shares or a measured not-yet-automated fraction.
- a: The AI review discusses potential and reported applications but does not measure displacement in US lower-middle-market providers.
- rho: The sources do not report implementation rates for the screened firm population.
- rho: AI used to improve service quality or expand caseload capacity may not reduce current payroll.
- rho: State-by-state authorization, privacy, accessibility, and procurement differences widen the range.
- e: The frozen firm count is margin-bridged using an estimated industry margin and may be materially distorted by grants, donations, participant wages, and nonprofit accounting.
- e: Public fee schedules establish vendor use but do not identify how many vendors fall inside the earnings band.
- e: The narrowed service-provider lens cannot be isolated in published six-digit establishment data.
- s5: The examples are not limited to NAICS 624310 or the lower-middle-market band.
- s5: Neither source supplies a population denominator or a systematic transfer rate.
- s5: A nonprofit governance change may not create the economics or control rights expected in a conventional acquisition.
- q: The cited fee rules cover selected public programs and not workers' compensation, employer, or philanthropic contracts.
- q: No source measures the discounted retained share of implemented benefit.
- q: Provider savings may be absorbed by uncompensated documentation, client complexity, vacancies, or required service expansion.
- d5: The BLS forecast is occupational and cross-industry rather than demand for NAICS 624310 services.
- d5: Government appropriations, reallotment requests, and disability employment gaps are not direct measures of purchased service quantity.
- d5: Policy, agency capacity, and state budget variation can move vendor demand independently of underlying client need.
- o: No source directly measures substitution of an accountable vocational-rehabilitation provider.
- o: Digital access and usefulness vary materially by disability and accommodation requirement.
- o: The estimate applies to the narrowed outsourced-service basket, not excluded sheltered-workshop production.
