# 541618 — Other Management Consulting Services

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `541618-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** PRIORITY · A 8.41 · L 5.43 · interval PRIORITY → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Research, synthesis, analysis, reporting, presentation, and administrative work are digitally native and demonstrably accelerated by generative AI.
**Weakness:** The supplied labor ratio is economically implausible at 1.3991, while generic consulting output also faces direct client self-service and fee pressure.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US other-management-consulting firms in the $1-10M normalized EBITDA band that repeatedly provide outsourced advice, analysis, recommendations, facilitation, and implementation support to external organizations, including telecommunications and utilities management consulting.
- Excluded: Captive internal strategy units, shell entities, non-control financing vehicles, administrative and general management consulting, human-resources consulting, marketing consulting, process and logistics consulting, and practices whose client relationships, expertise, or delivery capability cannot transfer beyond an individual founder.
- Customer and revenue model: Business, nonprofit, and government clients buy scoped advisory projects, fixed-fee deliverables, time-and-materials work, value-priced engagements, and recurring retainers; repeat revenue arises from continuing client relationships, successive initiatives, and implementation support.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Other management consulting has a broad AI-addressable knowledge workflow but a materially unreliable frozen labor input: the supplied compensation-to-receipts ratio exceeds one because of vintage and denominator problems. AI can accelerate research, synthesis, analysis, deliverables, and administration, yet consulting value still depends on problem framing, specialist context, stakeholder trust, facilitation, and implementation. Commercial capture should be better on project and value-based fees than on hourly work, but client bargaining will share gains.

## How AI changes the work
AI can draft proposals, interview guides, reports, presentations, summaries, benchmarks, and preliminary analyses; search internal knowledge; and automate project administration. Controlled evidence shows large speed and quality gains on tasks inside the capability frontier. The same evidence shows confident failure outside that frontier, so firms need expert review, task triage, client-data controls, and redesigned quality assurance rather than indiscriminate automation.

## Value capture
Project and value-based engagements can retain an efficiency gain when the promised outcome and fee remain fixed. Hourly work passes through fewer hours, while renewals and competitive procurement should reset some fixed fees. Firms that combine proprietary expertise, implementation responsibility, and measurable client outcomes should retain more than firms selling generic research and presentation production.

## Firm availability
The frozen dataset estimates 256 firms in the EBITDA band. Most staffed consulting firms should serve external customers with repeat potential, but founder-dependent sales, concentrated relationships, tacit expertise, and miscoding reduce transferability. Broad small-business succession data and active professional-services M&A support a nontrivial transfer probability, though planned and announced events exceed qualifying completed control transfers.

## Demand durability
Organizations should continue buying help with efficiency, cost control, telecom and utility management, organizational change, and AI adoption. Demand is cyclical and exposed to government and corporate budget cuts, while clients can increasingly self-serve generic analysis. Complex diagnosis, stakeholder alignment, implementation, and responsibility for recommendations support a continuing operator role.

## Risks and uncertainty
The principal empirical risk is the frozen labor ratio above one, which can overstate the economic opportunity regardless of primitive quality. Operating risks include hallucinated analysis, confidentiality breaches, loss of junior skill formation, fee compression, scope expansion, public-sector cuts, founder dependence, and clients replacing generic consulting with internal AI. The largest evidence gaps are six-digit occupation mix, task time, net realized AI savings, and completed LMM transfers.

## Caveats
- a: No source directly reports a wage-weighted task-exposure share for this six-digit residual industry.
- a: The OEWS mix covers management, scientific, and technical consulting broadly and likely differs materially from 541618.
- a: The frozen compensation-to-receipts ratio is 1.3991, which is economically implausible as a true compensation share and reflects a 2024-wage/2022-receipts mismatch plus harmonization; this materially weakens the labor-opportunity foundation but is not re-estimated here.
- rho: Tool access and individual speed gains do not prove firm-level operational implementation or avoided hiring.
- rho: BCG is far larger and better resourced than the firms in the frozen lens.
- rho: The frozen labor ratio's value above one means even a well-researched rho cannot cure uncertainty in the downstream labor base.
- e: The frozen n of 256 is already a margin-bridged estimate and is not re-estimated here.
- e: NAICS 541618 is a residual code dominated by telecommunications and utilities management consulting but may include miscoded general consultancies.
- e: Existing-client revenue evidence covers consultants broadly rather than the exact industry and EBITDA band.
- s5: Gallup covers all employer businesses, not consulting firms or the LMM EBITDA band.
- s5: A stated plan does not necessarily close, and some transfers are gifts or internal succession rather than qualifying control sales.
- s5: Professional-services deal counts include accounting, staffing, human-capital services, and consulting specialties outside 541618.
- q: The survey population is consultants across niches and likely includes many solo operators below the frozen EBITDA band.
- q: Pricing method prevalence is not revenue-weighted and does not show contract-specific pass-through.
- q: Demand loss belongs in d5 and o, and implementation friction belongs in rho, not in this estimate.
- d5: Management analysts are an occupation across industries and do not measure output of NAICS 541618.
- d5: The residual industry has material telecom and utilities exposure not isolated in the occupational projection.
- d5: Government procurement, enterprise investment cycles, and AI-driven new-service demand create wide uncertainty.
- o: Management consulting has no universal licensure requirement or statutory barrier to client self-service.
- o: Operator need varies materially between high-context telecom or utility engagements and generic research or benchmarking.
- o: AI capability boundaries may move rapidly, but persuasive errors can preserve the need for accountable expert review.

## Sources
- **S1** — [2022 NAICS Definition: 541618 Other Management Consulting Services](https://www.census.gov/naics/?details=541618&input=541618&year=2022) (accessed 2026-07-22): Defines the residual industry as management consulting other than the separately classified administrative/general, HR, marketing, and process/logistics categories, and specifically includes telecommunications and utilities management consulting.
- **S2** — [May 2023 OEWS: Management, Scientific, and Technical Consulting Services](https://www.bls.gov/oes/2023/may/naics4_541600.htm) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports the broader consulting-sector occupation mix, including management occupations at 16.21%, management analysts at 11.91%, project-management specialists at 3.55%, computer occupations at 9.56%, and office and administrative support at 14.98% of employment.
- **S3** — [Occupational Outlook Handbook: Management Analysts](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/business-and-financial/management-analysts.htm) (accessed 2026-07-22): Describes consultant duties and proposal-based work, projects 9% employment growth from 2024 to 2034, and states demand for consulting services should increase as organizations seek efficiency and cost control.
- **S4** — [Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier: Field Experimental Evidence of AI on Knowledge Worker Productivity and Quality](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/orsc.2025.21838) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports a randomized study of BCG consultants: on 18 inside-frontier tasks AI users completed 12.2% more tasks and worked 25.1% faster with higher performance, while on an outside-frontier task they were 19 percentage points less likely to produce a correct solution.
- **S5** — [McKinsey Global Survey: The state of AI in 2025](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports nearly nine in ten respondents' organizations regularly use AI, while about 6% qualify as high performers with significant value and scaled workflow redesign, illustrating wide adoption but limited mature impact.
- **S6** — [Consulting Success Fees Study 2023](https://www.consultingsuccess.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/What-Consultants-Really-Charge-In-2023-Consulting-Success.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports consultant pricing methods: 37% project rate, 26% value-based, 21% hourly, 13% monthly retainer, and 3% performance-based; also reports more than 80% had lowered fees to win a client.
- **S7** — [Gallup: Most Small-Business Owners Lack a Succession Plan](https://news.gallup.com/poll/657362/small-business-owners-lack-succession-plan.aspx) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports from 1,264 US owner-workers that within five years 13% of employer owners plan to sell and 11% plan to transfer ownership, with a combined sell/transfer/public share of 22%; 52.3% of employer businesses are owned by people 55 or older.
- **S8** — [FOCUS Professional Services Q3 2025 Report](https://focusbankers.com/professional-services-services-q3-2025-report/) (accessed 2026-07-22): Reports 466 announced US professional-services M&A transactions in the first nine months of 2025 and says consulting and accounting represented more than half of sector deal volume.
