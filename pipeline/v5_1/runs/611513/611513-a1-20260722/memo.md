# 611513 — Apprenticeship Training

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `611513-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.98 · L 3.75 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Growing apprenticeship participation combined with document-heavy administration and curriculum workflows creates repeatable AI-enabled labor leverage.
**Weakness:** The addressable ownership pool and commercial retention are obscured by a mixed ecosystem of captive, public, union, nonprofit, and grant-funded delivery models.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Privately controlled firms that repeatedly provide apprenticeship-program design, related technical instruction, curriculum, apprentice administration, compliance, and multi-employer coordination to external employers, sponsors, or apprentices.
- Excluded: Captive employer training units, government agencies, public colleges, labor unions and joint labor-management trusts that are not transferable control businesses, grant-only vehicles without recurring customers, and organizations whose primary activity falls outside NAICS 611513.
- Customer and revenue model: Recurring employer, sponsor, association, or workforce-system contracts and per-apprentice or per-cohort instruction fees, sometimes supplemented or reimbursed by federal or state workforce funds and tuition support.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Apprenticeship training has a credible labor-efficiency opportunity in administration, reporting, curriculum production, assessment support, and learner communications, while its applied and mentored core remains human-heavy. Demand has strong participation and policy tailwinds, but the investable population is narrowed by captive employer programs, unions, public colleges, nonprofits, and grant-dependent entities.

## How AI changes the work
AI can draft and update curricula, generate practice and formative assessments, personalize explanations, answer routine learner questions, summarize mentor notes, reconcile records, prepare compliance submissions, and coordinate cohorts. It is less able to replace live trade demonstration, safety judgment, workplace mentorship, final competency validation, employer relationship management, and accountable program operation.

## Value capture
Retention should be best in fixed-price employer or association contracts with measurable outcomes and weak cost disclosure. It is weaker in cost-reimbursement grants, publicly subsidized tuition, competitive renewals, and markets where community colleges or no-cost technical assistance anchor prices. Savings are therefore likely shared over five years rather than fully retained.

## Firm availability
The frozen count suggests a nontrivial LMM universe, but only an estimated 45% fits a transferable external-service lens. Succession pressure is plausible from broad owner-age evidence, yet program approvals, concentrated sponsor relationships, nonprofit governance, and institutional affiliations can prevent or complicate a qualifying control transfer.

## Demand durability
Registered apprenticeship participation has expanded materially and federal policy targets further growth. Current programs still require applied learning, instruction, mentorship, and accountability, supporting durable operator need; nevertheless, growth can accrue to captive employers, public colleges, unions, or software-supported self-administration rather than outsourced firms.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest evidence gaps are 611513-specific occupation data, the share of firms that are truly private and transferable, actual control-transfer history, provider-level contract economics, and externally purchased demand. Grant and policy cycles, public substitutes, customer concentration, privacy, training quality, and liability can all reduce implementation or capture. A poor-quality AI rollout could damage completion outcomes or sponsor standing rather than save labor.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation source is NAICS 611500 rather than 611513 and includes cosmetology, flight, and other technical schools.
- a: The estimate concerns current not-yet-automated tasks; it does not assume that all generative-AI-assisted work becomes labor substitution.
- a: The frozen compensation ratio uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and a harmonization divisor, so it has the stated vintage and basis limitations.
- rho: The Census adoption rates cover all industries and changed question wording in November 2025 to include any business function.
- rho: Federal guidance and portals demonstrate enablement, not measured productivity or headcount avoidance.
- rho: Small LMM providers may adopt later than larger institutions because integrations and data governance have fixed costs.
- e: No source measures lens eligibility among $1-10M EBITDA firms in NAICS 611513.
- e: SUSB private-firm counts can include organizational forms whose economics or governance are not practically transferable.
- e: The frozen firm count is a margin-bridged estimate using broader size-class data and an education-sector margin.
- s5: Owner age is an economy-wide and dated proxy, not observed deal flow for this industry.
- s5: A transfer may require customer, grant, credential, lease, or sponsor approvals and therefore may not qualify even when ownership changes.
- s5: Deaths, closures, internal reorganizations, and transfers without operating continuity are excluded.
- q: The sponsor payment survey is old and covers registered apprenticeship broadly, not LMM NAICS 611513 providers.
- q: Public funding rules vary substantially by state and program, and some savings may be passed through as lower reimbursable cost.
- q: The estimate excludes demand loss and implementation difficulty, which belong in other primitives.
- d5: Active apprentices, total people served, programs, and outsourced training demand are different quantities.
- d5: Policy goals are not forecasts and may change with appropriations or administration priorities.
- d5: The estimate holds service quality and price constant and does not count new AI-training content as price inflation.
- o: No observed source measures the share of apprenticeship-training quantity that will still require an external accountable operator in five years.
- o: Regulatory requirements establish accountability but do not guarantee that a lens firm, rather than an employer, union, or college, supplies it.
- o: Software may complement human mentors more than it replaces them, while administrative intermediation may be more substitutable.
