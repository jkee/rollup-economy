## Business-model lens
- Included: Privately owned US commercial operations primarily raising finfish or hatching fish and repeatedly selling food fish, fingerlings, eggs, baitfish, sport fish, ornamental fish, or stocking fish to external processors, distributors, retailers, restaurants, other farms, pond owners, or agencies.
- Excluded: Federal, state, tribal, municipal, university, and nonprofit hatcheries operated principally for conservation, restoration, research, or public stocking; captive hatcheries serving only a parent organization; wild-catch fishing; shellfish and crustacean farms; processors; shells; passive water or real-estate interests; and noncommercial hobby operations.
- Customer and revenue model: Revenue comes from repeated sales of live or harvested fish and eggs, generally priced per pound, head, thousand fish, lot, or supply contract. Food-fish economics depend on biomass growth, survival, feed conversion, harvest size, commodity prices, and processor demand, while hatchery, sport, bait, and ornamental products can earn species, genetics, size, health, timing, or service premiums.
- Deviation from default lens: The NAICS activity is animal production rather than an outsourced service and includes both commercial farms and public-purpose hatcheries. The lens retains privately owned commercial operators with repeat external sales and excludes government, conservation, research, and captive hatcheries because those are not transferable operating companies with commercial customer revenue.

## Executive view
Finfish farming and hatcheries combine commercial animal production with public-purpose hatcheries, so the lens must exclude government, conservation, research, and captive facilities. In private operations, AI can improve continuous monitoring, biomass and mortality estimation, feeding support, health surveillance, records, and administration, but core work remains physical and biological. Commodity food-fish pricing and imports constrain value retention, while differentiated hatchery and ornamental products offer more room. Missing compensation and LMM-firm-count inputs prevent a grounded absolute opportunity estimate.

## How AI changes the work
USDA research demonstrates computer vision for noninvasive fish count, size, condition, and mortality monitoring, particularly in recirculating systems. Additional uses include water-quality anomaly detection, feed and growth optimization, health and biosecurity alerts, inventory, compliance records, scheduling, purchasing, and sales. Wet environments, sensor fouling, species variation, sparse labels, legacy controls, and severe consequences of missed events limit implementation; existing automatic feeders and conventional controls are not counted again.

## Value capture
Commodity food-fish growers face processors, competing proteins, imported seafood, and observable market prices, so a meaningful part of cost improvement can be passed through or captured by feed and technology vendors. Operators selling healthy fingerlings, scarce genetics, reliable stocking schedules, sport, bait, or ornamental species can retain more through differentiation. Product and customer mix is therefore decisive.

## Firm availability
USDA counted 3,453 aquaculture farms with sales in 2023 at an average well below the target earnings band, but that census spans all aquatic species and low-sales farms. The classification also contains government and public-purpose hatcheries, consistent with the assigned wage data lacking a revenue denominator. With the LMM count missing, public data cannot identify how many private, finfish-focused, profitable, permit-transferable operating companies exist.

## Demand durability
Aquaculture sales grew between the 2018 and 2023 censuses, including food fish and faster-growing ornamental, sport, and bait categories, while broader animal-production real output is projected to rise. Domestic finfish demand remains exposed to imports, processor economics, feed and energy costs, disease, regulation, and public stocking budgets. Any fish produced still requires an accountable biological operator even if monitoring and feeding become more autonomous.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main uncertainties are missing compensation and firm-count inputs, public versus private establishment mix, sparse occupation and commercial AI-adoption data, species and production-system heterogeneity, permits and water rights, disease and mass-mortality risk, feed and energy costs, imported seafood competition, customer concentration, and the difference between nominal aquaculture-wide sales and constant-quality finfish demand.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation source covers all NAICS 11 industries and excludes self-employed workers.
- a: USDA's computer-vision result is a technical demonstration in recirculating systems, not representative commercial adoption evidence.
- a: Production methods differ materially in their sensor density and automation readiness.
- rho: The estimate applies only to the exposed opportunity in a.
- rho: High technical precision in a research setting does not establish reliable autonomous operation.
- rho: Regulatory and animal-health obligations can require human review even when monitoring is automated.
- e: The assigned LMM establishment-count input is missing, so this share cannot imply a firm count.
- e: The Census of Aquaculture covers all aquatic species and includes products distributed for conservation or recreation, not only private finfish firms.
- e: Aquaculture farms in USDA data begin at a sales threshold far below the target earnings band.
- e: Government hatcheries are economically important within the code but excluded from the lens.
- s5: The succession source is cross-industry and measures intentions rather than completed deals.
- s5: No public source separates qualifying private finfish control transfers from hatchery asset transfers or public reorganizations.
- s5: Permits and water rights may not transfer cleanly with the operating company.
- q: No source directly measures retention of AI-enabled gross benefits.
- q: The included product markets have very different buyer concentration and pricing structures.
- q: Broad seafood trade includes wild-caught and shellfish products outside this industry.
- d5: The BLS projection is dominated by animal-production activities outside aquaculture.
- d5: USDA's aquaculture totals include mollusks, crustaceans, and other products outside NAICS 112511 and are nominal sales.
- d5: Seafood import statistics include wild-caught and non-finfish products.
- d5: Public stocking demand may depend on appropriations rather than commercial end demand.
- o: A high operator-required share does not mean the current operator identity or staffing model persists.
- o: Import substitution and vertical integration affect domestic operator demand and are primarily reflected in d5.
