# 561910 — Packaging and Labeling Services

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `561910-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 7.23 · L 2.99 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Repetitive physical handling and inspection can be compressed with AI-enabled robotics and machine vision while customers continue outsourcing for equipment, flexibility, and accountable execution.
**Weakness:** Automation requires capital and plant integration, and gains can be stranded by short-run variability, idle capacity, customer concentration, or competitive repricing.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market firms repeatedly packaging client-owned products, including kit assembly, blister packaging, shrink wrapping, folding, labeling, imprinting, inspection, and related handling for external customers.
- Excluded: Manufacturers that process materials into a different product, packaging-material manufacturers, captive in-house packaging lines, pure fulfillment or warehousing providers outside NAICS 561910, one-off consumer gift wrapping without repeat outsourced revenue, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Brand owners and manufacturers outsource runs under per-unit, per-batch, project, minimum-volume, setup, materials-pass-through, and recurring program arrangements; the provider supplies labor, packaging machinery, floor space, quality control, labeling data, and changeover capability around client-owned goods.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Packaging and labeling services offer a meaningful but capital-dependent automation opportunity. Repetitive handling, inspection, labeling, records, and line control are exposed to robotics, machine vision, and AI optimization, while physical execution, changeovers, maintenance, quality accountability, and client-specific compliance keep an operator central.

## How AI changes the work
AI can improve machine vision, defect detection, label verification, production scheduling, demand and material planning, predictive maintenance, work instructions, traceability, and administrative records. AI-enabled robots and autonomous handling can reduce manual picking, packing, palletizing, and line feeding, but diverse shapes, flexible materials, short runs, sanitation, jams, and frequent changeovers preserve hands-on work.

## Value capture
Per-unit and program pricing can retain labor and throughput gains until contracts are rebid, especially when automation avoids scarce hiring or adds sellable capacity. Customer gain-sharing, price competition, materials pass-through, integration and maintenance cost, depreciation, idle capacity, and minimum-volume volatility reduce retained benefit.

## Firm availability
The NAICS definition is inherently outsourced, but eligible firms still need recurring programs, diversified customers, assignable contracts, safe and compliant plants, maintained equipment, and management depth. General succession intentions and strategic packaging consolidation support transfer flow, though direct 561910 transaction evidence is absent and current packaging M&A is selective.

## Demand durability
Brand owners outsource to access specialized equipment and formats, test launches, customize short runs, and handle capacity surges. Broader real-output projections and outsourcing surveys imply growth, while consumer weakness, insourcing, packaging reduction, and shifts to integrated contract manufacturers create downside.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest gaps are six-digit occupation and task-time data, the already-automated baseline, plant-level automation realization, contract pricing, and a qualifying-transfer denominator. A poor outcome would combine expensive or unreliable retrofits with customer concentration, unused capacity, rebid price leakage, regulatory failures, packaging-volume weakness, or clients taking work in-house.

## Caveats
- a: No six-digit occupation mix or task-time study was found for NAICS 561910.
- a: NAICS 561900 includes unrelated other support services, while O*NET occupations span manufacturers and warehouses as well as contract packagers.
- a: The line between conventional packaging automation already in place and incremental AI-enabled substitution is not measured.
- rho: Technology adoption intentions are not realized labor savings.
- rho: The cited surveys cover broader CPG, manufacturing, warehousing, and supply-chain populations and skew larger than the screened firms.
- rho: Flexible short runs and regulated food, pharmaceutical, or medical-device work can make integration and validation slower than broad adoption headlines imply.
- e: No source measures eligibility under the specified earnings and recurring-service lens.
- e: NAICS 561910 ranges from simple gift wrapping and folding to regulated blister packaging and complex multi-SKU kitting.
- e: The frozen firm count is margin-bridged from size classes and may misstate membership for capital-intensive or pass-through-heavy operators.
- s5: Gallup measures intentions rather than completed transfers and covers all employer businesses.
- s5: Capstone combines packaging products, paper, print, equipment, and services and does not provide a 561910 denominator.
- s5: Customer concentration, plant condition, and required reinvestment can prevent an apparent packaging business from completing a durable control transfer.
- q: No source directly measures billing mix or retained automation benefit in lower-middle-market NAICS 561910 firms.
- q: Customer contracts may combine service fees with materials pass-through and capital recovery in ways that obscure the retained share.
- q: Throughput gains do not become cash benefit when released line capacity remains idle or requires additional selling and working capital.
- d5: The BLS output series is four-digit and includes unrelated other support services.
- d5: The PMMI survey combines contract packaging with contract manufacturing and covers North American participants rather than a 561910 probability sample.
- d5: Packaging demand depends on underlying client volumes, while sustainability rules can either create relabeling and reformatting work or reduce material and service intensity.
- o: No source directly measures substitution of external packaging-service purchases after automation.
- o: PMMI combines contract packaging and manufacturing, which may have different switching costs and operator requirements.
- o: Simple labeling, folding, and gift wrapping face more insourcing and self-service risk than regulated blister packaging or complex kitting.
