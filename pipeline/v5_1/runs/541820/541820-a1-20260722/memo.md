# 541820 — Public Relations Agencies

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `541820-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** HIGHEST_PRIORITY · A 8.03 · L 6.56 · interval CONDITIONAL → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Large volumes of writing, research, monitoring, editing, and reporting create implementable AI leverage inside recurring agency relationships.
**Weakness:** Commodity PR production is easy for clients and AI-native competitors to self-serve, constraining both operator-required demand and retained pricing benefit.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market public relations agencies providing recurring or repeat outsourced communications campaigns, media relations, reputation and crisis counsel, public affairs, lobbying, political consulting, executive communications, stakeholder engagement, and related PR consulting to external clients.
- Excluded: Advertising agencies, media buying and representation, direct-mail and display advertising, standalone market-research firms, software vendors, captive in-house communications teams, shells, non-control financing vehicles, solo practitioners below the LMM band, and firms limited to isolated nonrepeat publicity projects.
- Customer and revenue model: Businesses, institutions, nonprofits, public bodies, political interests, and individuals buy ongoing counsel and campaign execution through monthly or annual retainers, hourly fees against budgets, fixed project fees, and hybrids. Repeatability is strongest in corporate reputation, media relations, public affairs, crisis preparedness, executive positioning, and integrated communications.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Public relations agencies combine high labor intensity, broad AI-compatible production work, recurring retainers, and an active small-agency transaction market. The strongest targets have durable sector specialization, multi-person client teams, retained crisis or public-affairs relationships, and value anchored in judgment and access; commodity release-writing and founder-only publicity shops are more exposed.

## How AI changes the work
AI can compress brainstorming, desk research, monitoring synthesis, media-list preparation, first drafts, editing, speeches, releases, social content, reporting, and administrative work. It is much less able to replace trusted media and government relationships, live crisis decisions, political context, spokesperson coaching, stakeholder negotiation, and accountability for reputational consequences.

## Value capture
Flat retainers and fixed projects can retain near-term gains as margin or capacity, while hourly-budget work shares gains through fewer billed hours. Annual reviews, competitive pitches, client transparency expectations, and AI-native competitors should pass through part of the benefit, leaving moderate five-year retention with better economics in differentiated counsel than commodity execution.

## Firm availability
Most LMM agencies should qualify as external repeat-service employers, though political-cycle firms, one-off publicity shops, and adjacent agencies reduce the eligible share. PR-specific M&A is active and includes many small firms, but observed deal counts are global and lack an eligible denominator; founder and rainmaker dependence remains a central transfer risk.

## Demand durability
Reputation, community outreach, crisis response, public affairs, executive visibility, and rapidly changing online opinion support modest demand growth. Discretionary communications budgets, economic cycles, shrinking traditional newsrooms, difficult media placement, and client insourcing create downside risk.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main gaps are a six-digit occupation mix, audited AI labor savings, current fee-model shares, US LMM client retention, and control-deal denominators. Industry adoption surveys may overrepresent technology-forward professionals and agencies. AI errors, confidentiality breaches, plagiarism, undisclosed use, or synthetic-content backlash could damage clients and delay implementation, while autonomous tools may commoditize content and monitoring faster than agencies move toward strategic work.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation mix is for NAICS 541800 and includes advertising and related services with different workflows.
- a: O*NET task importance does not measure time, wage weight, feasibility, or existing automation.
- a: The Muck Rack survey reports individual tool use rather than the share of agency labor technically substitutable.
- a: The injected labor ratio uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and may be distorted where agency receipts include third-party campaign or event pass-through.
- rho: Tool use and self-reported speed or quality are not audited implementation or labor savings.
- rho: Muck Rack mixes agency and in-house professionals and does not isolate LMM firms.
- rho: IPRN covers member agencies across more than 22 countries and does not state a respondent count on the fetched page.
- rho: Current high adoption reduces the remaining not-yet-implemented opportunity even as it supports future workflow acceptance.
- e: No source measures eligibility under the frozen recurring-or-repeat outsourced LMM lens.
- e: The injected firm count uses a 15.65% broad Business & Consumer Services margin and may misclassify high-margin counsel boutiques or lower-margin execution-heavy agencies into the EBITDA band.
- e: The code includes lobbying and political consulting as well as conventional PR, creating customer and cyclicality variation.
- s5: Gallup covers all US employer businesses rather than PR agencies or the LMM band.
- s5: PRovoke's transaction universe is not restricted to the United States, control deals, or the frozen EBITDA band.
- s5: Many observed targets below $6 million in fees are likely below the LMM EBITDA threshold.
- s5: Founder and rainmaker dependence can make nominal agencies difficult to transfer without long earn-outs.
- q: The PRSA guide establishes fee structures but does not measure their prevalence or commercial retention.
- q: The guide predates current AI adoption and modern procurement practices.
- q: Retention varies sharply between hourly media-relations execution, fixed projects, crisis availability retainers, lobbying, and strategic counsel.
- d5: The BLS output forecast is for NAICS 541800, not public relations agencies alone.
- d5: Occupation growth includes in-house PR staff and workers outside agencies.
- d5: IPRN is a global member network rather than a representative US LMM sample.
- d5: The estimate holds quality and the current service basket constant, excluding wholly new AI-visibility services.
- o: No source directly measures the future operator-required share of PR service demand.
- o: Agency survey statements about human judgment and strategic trust may reflect provider optimism.
- o: Rapid improvement in autonomous monitoring, content generation, journalist targeting, sentiment analysis, and crisis simulation could push the value below the range.
- o: Lobbying and political consulting are more operator-dependent than routine publicity, widening intra-code variation.
