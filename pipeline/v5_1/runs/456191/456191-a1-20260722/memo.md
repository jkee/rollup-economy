# 456191 — Food (Health) Supplement Retailers

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `456191-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** no base (evidence-first) · A — · L — · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → LOW_PRIORITY · readiness STRESS_TEST_ONLY · action EVIDENCE_FIRST

**Driver:** A service-bearing recurring relationship can combine modest AI-enabled labor savings with durable supplement demand and initial fixed-price retention.
**Weakness:** Most firms are product merchants, not outsourced-service providers, leaving only a small and poorly measured eligible population.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market food-supplement retailers only where an operating company provides an externally purchased, repeat service around the merchandise, such as subscription replenishment, personalized product curation, adherence support, or managed wellness-product programs.
- Excluded: Ordinary one-off product merchants, marketplaces, manufacturers and brands without a retail operating service, captive channels, shells, non-control financing vehicles, and businesses whose only recurring feature is repeat product purchase without a supplied service.
- Customer and revenue model: Included firms earn primarily fixed-price product revenue from consumers or organizational customers, sometimes through subscriptions or memberships; the lens recognizes only the accompanying recurring curation, support, or managed-replenishment activity as the outsourced service.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Food-supplement retail is principally a merchandise business, making eligibility under an outsourced-service screen the central constraint. A small edge of subscription, managed-replenishment, and substantive curation models can fit, but the opportunity should not be generalized to ordinary vitamin stores or online sellers.

## How AI changes the work
AI can assist product-content maintenance, routine customer questions, promotion and loyalty messaging, transaction review, inventory forecasts, and supervised recommendations. It is less suited to physical merchandising and fulfillment, relationship selling, exception handling, and unsupervised health guidance, so implementation should be meaningful but partial.

## Value capture
Retail prices are not normally cost-plus contracts, so initial savings can remain with the operator. Transparent product comparison, low customer switching costs, online competition, and the need to reinvest in trust and service should pass a substantial portion of gains to customers over five years.

## Firm availability
The eligible pool is far smaller than the NAICS population because repeat merchandise purchase is not itself an outsourced service. Among the qualifying minority, broad U.S. owner-age data indicate succession pressure, but specialist retailers may close or transfer internally rather than complete a market control sale.

## Demand durability
Supplement use is common and adult prevalence has risen, supporting repeat category demand. Yet specialist retailers face self-service, marketplaces, mass merchants, and direct-to-consumer brands; durable operator need rests on trusted curation, supplier diligence, physical fulfillment, and accountable compliance rather than on the commodity product transaction.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainty is whether any claimed subscription or advice is a substantive externally purchased service rather than incidental retail support. Other risks include regulatory and adverse-event exposure, unsafe AI recommendations, unreliable product data, online price competition, private-label concentration, and the absence of 456191-specific transfer or occupation evidence.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS publishes the occupation mix at 456000, which contains pharmacies, optical retailers, and other businesses unlike supplement specialists.
- a: The estimate concerns current not-yet-automated tasks and does not count physical robotics as AI substitution.
- a: Health-claim and adverse-event risk constrains unsupervised product advice.
- rho: No industry-specific implementation cohort was found.
- rho: The estimate excludes pricing, demand, and valuation effects and applies only to the opportunity counted in a.
- e: Eligibility is estimated because public establishment statistics do not identify whether recurring programs constitute an outsourced service.
- e: A subscription for automatic product delivery alone is treated as recurring merchandise, not an outsourced service.
- s5: The Census age statistic covers responding owners of employer businesses across all industries and dates to data year 2018.
- s5: No 456191-specific numerator and denominator for qualifying control transfers was found.
- q: No public study directly measures pass-through of AI-driven cost reductions for supplement retailers.
- q: The range concerns retention of an implemented gross benefit and does not include demand growth or implementation failure.
- d5: NHANES measures use by people, not purchases or specialist-retailer channel share.
- d5: BLS retail-sales projections are occupation-wide and not a direct forecast of supplement demand.
- d5: The low case allows channel migration and self-service to offset category usage growth.
- o: The accountable-operator requirement differs sharply between a retailer that is merely a reseller and one named on labels or acting as distributor.
- o: The estimate applies to year-five quantity after d5, not to current jobs.
