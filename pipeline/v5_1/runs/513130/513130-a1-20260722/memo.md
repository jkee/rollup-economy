**Driver:** Repeatable editorial preparation, production, metadata, marketing, rights administration, and service operations create substantial implementable AI work.
**Weakness:** Most industry firms monetize books and rights as products rather than recurring outsourced services, while the eligible LMM firm count is missing.

## Business-model lens
- Included: United States lower-middle-market book publishers whose operations include repeat design, editing, production coordination, rights administration, marketing, and distribution of print, electronic, or audio books, with eligibility assessed for recurring or repeat outsourced publishing relationships with external authors, institutions, associations, or content owners.
- Excluded: Newspaper and periodical publishers, software publishers, printers without publishing activity, retailers and distributors that do not publish, author-operated single-title vehicles, captive internal presses, shells, non-control financing vehicles, and publishers that only acquire content rights and sell their own books without supplying a recurring or repeat outsourced service to an external customer.
- Customer and revenue model: Industry revenue is primarily title and course-material sales through physical and online retail, direct channels, and print, ebook, and audio formats, plus rights and licensing income. The eligible service subset can earn fixed production fees, royalties, revenue shares, or recurring program and list-management fees from authors, institutions, associations, and other content owners.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Book publishing contains a large base of AI-exposed editorial, production, marketing, and administrative work, but most publishers sell intellectual products on their own account rather than a recurring outsourced service, and the eligible LMM population is not measured.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate manuscript triage, copyediting preparation, metadata, layout, accessibility, translation drafts, marketing variants, sales analysis, rights records, royalty support, and customer service. Editorial taste, commissioning, author development, rights negotiation, pedagogy, legal review, and final accountability remain human-led.

## Value capture
Fixed title pricing, service fees, and owned or licensed backlists can preserve efficiency, but author economics, retailer discounts, procurement, AI assurance costs, competitive advances, and reinvestment in more titles share the benefit.

## Firm availability
Specialist lists, backlists, brands, distribution relationships, and institutional programs can transfer, and niche acquisitions occur. Availability is weakened by missing LMM counts, founder and author dependence, fragmented or nonassignable rights, nonprofit and university ownership, and asset purchases that do not transfer an operating company.

## Demand durability
Recent US publishing revenue growth, continued print importance, and expanding digital audio support a durable service base. Education budgets, consumer attention, self-publishing, open resources, format shifts, and bestseller volatility keep the five-year outlook broad.

## Risks and uncertainty
The principal risks are poor fit to the outsourced-service lens, a missing target-count anchor, broad ancestor labor data, copyright and training-rights uncertainty, author and retailer bargaining, title concentration, self-publishing, quality failures, and overestimating labor substitution in judgment-heavy editorial work.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation mix covers newspapers, periodicals, books, and directories rather than book publishers alone.
- a: Employment shares are not wage-weighted task shares and do not measure current automation.
- a: Editorial and design occupations combine highly automatable preparation with human judgment and accountability.
- rho: The AI-use measure is for the broad Information sector and any business function, not book publishing production deployment.
- rho: Business adoption does not measure labor substitution or the depth of use.
- rho: Copyrightability and training-rights uncertainty may constrain implementation beyond ordinary software adoption.
- e: No direct audit of the missing LMM-band population or its service revenue mix was found.
- e: Traditional author-publisher rights acquisition is not necessarily an outsourced service purchased by the author.
- e: The frozen compensation input is an ancestor-sector proxy, and the dataset provides no defensible n value.
- s5: The source is a qualitative trade-publishing deal review with no eligible-firm denominator.
- s5: It includes international and large-publisher transactions outside the US LMM lens.
- s5: Acquisitions of imprints or title lists may not transfer an operating firm.
- q: No direct study measures post-AI benefit retention by book publishers.
- q: AAP aggregates traditional publishing categories, formats, and channels rather than the eligible outsourced-service subset.
- q: Savings may be competed into author terms, retail pricing, or additional title investment.
- d5: AAP revenue is nominal and broader than the outsourced-service lens.
- d5: One-year revenue growth and format shifts are not a five-year constant-quality volume forecast.
- d5: Bestsellers and education purchasing can cause revenue changes unrelated to publishing workload.
- o: No direct substitution study isolates operator requirement in NAICS 513130.
- o: Operator need differs sharply across trade, education, professional, religious, university-press, and contract publishing.
- o: Copyright rules require human authorship for protection but do not require a publishing firm.
