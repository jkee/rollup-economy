# 561410 — Document Preparation Services

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `561410-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.48 · L 3.35 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Core production consists of digital transcription, editing, proofreading, formatting, and layout tasks that AI can materially compress.
**Weakness:** Customer self-service and rapid repricing can shrink outsourced volume and prevent labor savings from becoming durable operator cash flow.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market firms repeatedly providing outsourced document editing, proofreading, typing, word processing, desktop publishing, transcription, formatting, conversion, and related secretarial document services to external business, professional, nonprofit, and government customers.
- Excluded: Captive internal document teams, one-person or purely episodic consumer resume practices without repeat outsourced revenue, court reporting, translation, printing and copying shops, data-processing firms, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Customers submit text, recordings, templates, or document files and pay by page, word, audio minute, project, hour, service bundle, or recurring volume commitment. Revenue ranges from one-off assignments to repeat workflow outsourcing and master-service arrangements.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Document preparation is labor-intensive digital production with unusually direct exposure to language, speech, proofreading, and layout automation. Implementation can be rapid, but the same tools let customers self-serve and intensify price competition, while the addressable lower-middle-market firm pool is small.

## How AI changes the work
AI can draft transcripts, correct grammar, normalize styles, extract and merge content, reformat files, populate templates, and produce first-pass layouts. People remain important for intake, ambiguous source material, technical and client-specific standards, exception handling, confidentiality, and final quality assurance.

## Value capture
Fixed project and unit pricing can retain near-term productivity gains and support faster turnaround. Over five years, transparent unit economics, buyer insourcing, hourly billing, and competition are likely to share much of the benefit with customers.

## Firm availability
Most scaled firms fit the outsourced-service lens, but the frozen firm count is small and the code includes episodic, owner-dependent practices. Transferability depends on recurring accounts, documented templates and controls, and production capability beyond the founder.

## Demand durability
The need to create and manage documents persists, but demand for the current outsourced service basket is pressured by electronic publishing, software-enabled self-service, and declining employment in core occupations. Complex, confidential, high-volume, and deadline-sensitive work should be more durable than commodity typing or layout.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are service-line mix, repeat revenue, customer insourcing, pricing units, security requirements, offshore and contractor labor, and the review effort needed after automation. Errors in legal, financial, medical, or technical documents can create rework, liability, and customer loss.

## Caveats
- a: O*NET measures an occupation rather than the industry's full wage-weighted task mix.
- a: BLS occupation projections are economy-wide and reflect employment rather than current task exposure.
- a: The frozen compensation-to-receipts input combines different vintages and does not identify controllable labor by workflow.
- rho: Gallup covers all employed U.S. adults rather than document-preparation firms or the lower-middle-market band.
- rho: Office and administrative workers in Gallup report less benefit than technical and professional workers, so broad adoption does not guarantee workflow redesign.
- rho: The estimate assumes usable speech, language, and document models continue to improve over five years without eliminating review obligations.
- e: No source measures repeat outsourced-service eligibility within the frozen lower-middle-market firm population.
- e: The frozen firm count is margin-bridged and may misclassify firms with unusually high subcontractor expense, offshore labor, or owner compensation.
- e: Industry boundaries can blur document preparation with translation, printing, court reporting, virtual assistance, and data processing.
- s5: Gallup is cross-industry and includes employer businesses outside the lower-middle market.
- s5: The survey measures intentions, including transfers that may not satisfy the qualifying-control definition.
- s5: No cited source measures completed transactions in NAICS 561410.
- q: No public source measures the industry's billing-method mix or retained share of automation benefits.
- q: Retention likely differs sharply between commodity transcription and high-complexity or confidential document work.
- q: This range excludes implementation friction and volume loss, which are represented in rho, d5, and o.
- d5: BLS industry employment is published at five-digit 56141 and is not a constant-price output measure.
- d5: Occupation projections span the whole economy and may reflect insourcing, productivity, or reclassification rather than demand for outsourced firms.
- d5: The current payroll series shows a sharp recent decline that may include sampling or classification volatility.
- o: The evidence describes occupations and employment, not buyer substitution decisions in NAICS 561410.
- o: Operator requirement varies widely between commodity text conversion and regulated, technical, or highly formatted documents.
- o: This primitive concerns service displacement and self-service rather than internal labor efficiency.
