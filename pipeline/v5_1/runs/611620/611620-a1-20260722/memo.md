# 611620 — Sports and Recreation Instruction

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `611620-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.29 · L 2.08 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Recurring instruction demand and positive participation trends allow integrated AI administration and analysis to raise coach utilization without removing the live service.
**Weakness:** Safety-critical embodied delivery limits labor substitution, while founder dependence and facility obligations can impair transferability.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market schools, academies, and instruction camps that repeatedly provide paid athletic instruction to external customers, including swimming, gymnastics, martial arts, cheerleading, riding, golf, tennis, and team-sport skills.
- Excluded: Fitness centers whose primary service is facility access, sports leagues and spectator teams, recreational camps where instruction is incidental, academic schools, freelance instructors without transferable operations, captive programs, and sports equipment retailers.
- Customer and revenue model: Primarily household-paid recurring class tuition, memberships, lesson packages, seasonal programs, and repeat camps, with some school, club, or municipal contracts; facilities and equipment may be owned or leased and instructor labor is central to delivery.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Sports and recreation instruction has recurring household revenue, positive participation signals, and a meaningful but bounded workflow opportunity. AI can reduce planning, analysis, enrollment, scheduling, billing, marketing, and parent-communication labor, yet the core service remains embodied, safety-sensitive, and relational. The target population is larger than in some instruction niches, and standardized multi-site schools are demonstrably transferable, but founder dependence and mixed facility economics remain material filters.

## How AI changes the work
Near-term tools can create practice plans, personalize drills, summarize progress, answer routine questions, manage registrations, schedule instructors and facilities, and analyze video. Computer vision already supplies immediate technical metrics in figure skating. Coaches still must observe context, correct movement safely, motivate learners, supervise minors, manage groups, and respond to pain, fear, fatigue, or emergencies. The durable model is coach-plus-software rather than autonomous instruction.

## Value capture
Fixed tuition and lesson fees permit initial retention of avoided hiring and improved utilization. Durable capture depends on keeping service quality and safety visible: competitors can adopt the same software, instructors may capture some productivity through wages, and parents may expect better ratios or more feedback. Schools with waitlists or capacity constraints can retain more by converting workflow time into additional classes without weakening instruction.

## Firm availability
Broad US employer-owner data show meaningful five-year sale or transfer intentions, and the acquisition of AQUAfin by Emler demonstrates an active strategic path for standardized, multi-location swim schools. Transferability is strongest where curriculum, progression, instructor training, billing, safety procedures, and customer relationships are institutional rather than founder-held. One-star-coach academies and operations tied to nonassignable facilities are less likely to close.

## Demand durability
Participation reached a record level in 2025 and relevant coaching and fitness occupations have positive ten-year projections. Health, safety, youth development, competition, and adult recreation support repeat demand, while affordability, declining youth populations in some regions, teen inactivity, and free digital training create downside. AI-only alternatives will capture some self-directed adult use, but operator-led quantity should remain dominant in youth and safety-critical disciplines.

## Risks and uncertainty
Occupation data are available only for the broader 611600 industry, professional-coaching AI evidence is not sports-specific, and transaction evidence is episodic rather than a complete deal census. The code spans pools, mats, courts, fields, stables, camps, and individual instructors, creating wide variation in capital intensity and safety exposure. A bad outcome would combine high facility obligations and instructor turnover with customer migration to cheaper apps or community programs, leaving administrative savings too small to offset demand and fixed-cost pressure.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS is available at NAICS 611600 rather than 611620 and excludes self-employed instructors.
- a: The professional-coaching survey is not limited to athletic coaches or the United States.
- a: Exposure includes partial task assistance and avoidable hiring, not wholesale coach replacement.
- rho: Published evidence demonstrates use and capability rather than sustained labor removal.
- rho: Child privacy, biometric video data, injury risk, and safeguarding require human review and controlled deployment.
- rho: Implementation will vary sharply between standardized technique sports and open-play team sports.
- e: The supplied LMM firm count is an estimate based on size classes and a margin bridge.
- e: NAICS boundaries distinguish instruction from facilities and leagues, but real firms often mix those activities.
- e: A swim-school platform demonstrates eligibility for one facility-intensive segment and may not generalize to all sports.
- s5: Gallup covers all industries and owner-reported intentions rather than completed deals.
- s5: The disclosed swim-school acquisition does not provide a denominator or terms.
- s5: Founder reputation, credentials, and customer relationships can prevent an otherwise planned transfer.
- q: No public source directly measures automation pass-through or renewal repricing in sports instruction.
- q: Private lessons, group classes, memberships, camps, and institutional contracts have different retention dynamics.
- q: Facility-constrained schools may reinvest savings in capacity or safety rather than report them as margin.
- d5: Participation, occupation employment, and broad-industry employment are not direct measures of 611620 service quantity.
- d5: The 1.2% participation increase is a single-year national observation and follows faster post-2020 growth.
- d5: Teen inactivity rose in 2025 even as total participation increased.
- o: No source directly measures operator displacement across 611620.
- o: Operator need is higher for children and safety-critical sports than for healthy adults doing self-directed fitness.
- o: Rapid advances in multimodal motion analysis and connected equipment would push the estimate downward.
