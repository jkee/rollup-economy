# 512240 — Sound Recording Studios

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `512240-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 5.14 · L 2.32 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Software-intensive editing and production workflows offer implementable assistance while premium capture and creative accountability remain valuable.
**Weakness:** Home production and AI-enabled software can eliminate or unbundle commercial studio hours, while many firms depend on a founder's reputation and relationships.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market studios repeatedly providing outsourced facilities and technical expertise for recording, mixing, mastering, remixing, audio postproduction, spoken-word production, and audiovisual sound services to external artists, labels, producers, agencies, and media clients.
- Excluded: Record labels and master-rights owners, record distribution, mass duplication, independent artists recording only themselves, captive broadcast or film facilities, equipment-only rental without an operating service, live-event sound outside the studio-service business, shells, and financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat project, session, hourly, daily, room, engineer, mixing, mastering, and postproduction fees paid by artists, labels, producers, advertising agencies, broadcasters, film and video producers, podcasters, and other media clients.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Sound recording studios have meaningful AI exposure in editing, repair, organization, mastering assistance, and administration, but differentiated work still depends on acoustics, equipment, critical listening, creative collaboration, and accountable delivery. Demand and operator need face genuine pressure from home production and software-first services.

## How AI changes the work
AI can assist noise and dialogue repair, stem separation, take organization, transcription, editing, versioning, mastering suggestions, asset search, scheduling, quoting, and client updates. Humans remain central to capture, room and microphone choices, artistic direction, complex mixing, approvals, and quality responsibility.

## Value capture
Fixed project prices and increased throughput can retain some gains, but hourly billing, competitive pricing, client insourcing, and released capacity without matching demand drive material pass-through.

## Firm availability
The injected estimate identifies 40 firms in the screened band, subject to a broad margin bridge. Staffed studios with repeat artist, label, agency, and media clients fit the lens; room-only, captive, intermittent, and founder-dependent operations require review.

## Demand durability
Recorded-music monetization is growing, yet studio-service quantity may remain roughly flat as home tools, remote collaboration, and AI reduce purchased hours. High-complexity capture and postproduction are more durable than standardized editing or mastering.

## Risks and uncertainty
Major risks include self-service production, synthetic media, low utilization, lease and equipment costs, founder dependence, client concentration, rights and consent disputes, cybersecurity, rapid tool commoditization, and overestimating EBITDA through a broad sector margin.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS covers NAICS 512200 rather than NAICS 512240 and excludes self-employed engineers.
- a: The occupational profile spans broadcast, live-event, motion-picture, and recording contexts.
- a: High-end bespoke sessions and standardized spoken-word editing have very different exposure.
- rho: No source directly measures five-year implementation for LMM studios.
- rho: Tool capability is not the same as dependable workflow adoption under client deadlines.
- rho: Synthetic voice, stem separation, and training-data rights can require consent or restrict use.
- e: The injected firm count uses a broad Information Services margin that may not fit rent-, equipment-, and freelance-intensive studios.
- e: Project revenue can be repeat outsourced service even when individual productions are one-off.
- e: Census product definitions do not reveal client recurrence or normalized EBITDA.
- s5: The proxy is all-industry or broad-sector evidence, not a studio transaction denominator.
- s5: Equipment and property transfers are not qualifying control transfers unless the operating service continues.
- s5: Owner departure can impair customer retention and creative credibility.
- q: No cited source measures five-year benefit pass-through in studio contracts.
- q: Retention differs between room rental, engineer time, fixed-price mastering, and bundled production.
- q: Higher throughput produces value only if demand fills released capacity.
- d5: Recorded-music revenue is downstream of studio services and mixes price with quantity.
- d5: BLS occupation projections cover technicians across multiple industries and confound demand with productivity.
- d5: The 2024 RIAA series added direct reporting from independent labels, affecting comparability.
- o: Operator need varies sharply between orchestral capture, commercial postproduction, music demos, podcasts, and basic mastering.
- o: A project may leave a commercial facility but still use an independent human engineer remotely.
- o: Employment decline does not directly measure the operator-required share of service quantity.
