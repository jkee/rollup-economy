# 923120 — Administration of Public Health Programs

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `923120-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** no base (evidence-first) · A — · L — · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness STRESS_TEST_ONLY · action EVIDENCE_FIRST

**Driver:** Document-heavy, data-heavy public-health administration offers implementable AI assistance while accountable government operation remains necessary.
**Weakness:** The code contains government establishments and provides no defensible population of privately transferable LMM firms.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Government establishments planning, administering, and coordinating public health programs and services, including environmental and mental health programs, health statistics, immunization programs, and public-health inspections.
- Excluded: Government hospitals and clinics, agricultural-product inspection, private consultants, software vendors, laboratories, and nonprofit technical-assistance organizations are excluded because they are classified outside 923120; captive units and non-control financing vehicles are also excluded.
- Customer and revenue model: The establishments are government agencies funded through appropriations, taxes, intergovernmental grants, and program-specific public funding rather than recurring commercial fees paid by external customers.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
The operating work has real AI-assistance potential, especially in documents, communications, coding, data preparation, and routine inquiries. The acquisition lens fails at a more basic level: this NAICS code is defined as government establishments, not privately transferable outsourced-service firms.

## How AI changes the work
AI can shorten drafting, synthesis, translation, communication, coding, surveillance triage, and administrative-support workflows. Scientific interpretation, field inspection, emergency coordination, privacy-sensitive decisions, and public accountability require validation and human ownership, limiting conversion of exposure into implemented substitution.

## Value capture
A public agency can redirect saved staff time, avoid some hiring, or expand service capacity, but it has no ordinary owner margin or customer repricing mechanism. Commercial retention is therefore not measurable for the in-code operator.

## Firm availability
No defensible pool of eligible or transferable LMM firms is established. Private technology, consulting, staffing, laboratory, and technical-assistance vendors may sell recurring services to health departments, but they are outside this industry code.

## Demand durability
Public-health surveillance, program coordination, inspection, preparedness, health statistics, and risk communication remain recurring government functions. Workforce gaps and growing epidemiology needs support durable underlying demand, while appropriations and temporary categorical grants create meaningful quantity volatility.

## Risks and uncertainty
The decisive risk is category mismatch between a government-only industry and a private control-acquisition screen. Operationally, fragmented systems, procurement, privacy, cybersecurity, model errors, scientific-integrity requirements, and inconsistent jurisdictional capacity can slow implementation; funding contraction can reduce realized service volume despite continuing need.

## Caveats
- a: CDC describes use cases among agencies that have adopted AI, not a wage-weighted task census of all 923120 establishments.
- a: Government job classifications and digital maturity vary substantially by jurisdiction.
- a: The frozen labor-share input is missing, so this task-share estimate cannot establish an industry-level labor-benefit magnitude by itself.
- rho: Reported adopters are likely more capable than the median jurisdiction.
- rho: Time savings reported by agencies are not a measured conversion rate from exposed work to sustained operational implementation.
- rho: The estimate excludes pricing, public funding, demand, and valuation effects.
- e: Private contractors serving health departments belong to other NAICS codes and cannot be imported into this record.
- e: The frozen dataset explicitly provides no defensible LMM-band firm count.
- s5: Agency reorganizations, elections, leadership turnover, intergovernmental transfers, and outsourcing contracts are not qualifying control transfers.
- s5: Deaths, internal reorganizations, and transactions involving vendors classified elsewhere must remain excluded.
- q: Budget savings may be retained, reappropriated, or used to expand services, but none maps reliably to owner commercial retention.
- q: Applying vendor contract economics would measure industries outside 923120.
- d5: Epidemiologists are only one occupation within the industry.
- d5: The government-sector projection is much broader than 923120.
- d5: Short-term and categorical funding can make realized service quantity volatile even when underlying public-health need is durable.
- o: This is an accountability judgment, not a measured displacement rate.
- o: Statutory authority differs across federal, state, tribal, territorial, and local jurisdictions.
- o: Outsourcing execution does not necessarily remove the accountable public operator.

## Sources
- **S1** — [North American Industry Classification System: Sector 92, NAICS 923120](https://www.census.gov/naics/resources/archives/sect92.html) (accessed 2026-07-23): Defines 923120 as government establishments planning, administering, and coordinating public-health programs and services, including inspections; also identifies hospitals, clinics, and agricultural-product inspection as classifications elsewhere.
- **S2** — [Considerations for Generative AI in Public Health](https://www.cdc.gov/ai/resources/considerations-for-genai-in-public-health.html) (accessed 2026-07-23): Reports public-health agency uses of generative AI for drafting, summaries, code, communications, and chatbots; describes reported time savings and administrative-burden reduction; requires governance, privacy, security, scientific integrity, and human review.
- **S3** — [Public Health Preparedness: HHS and Jurisdictions Have Taken Some Steps to Address Challenging Workforce Gaps](https://files.gao.gov/reports/GAO-25-107002/index.html) (accessed 2026-07-23): Finds that more than 3,000 jurisdictional health departments have primary public-health responsibility, estimates 239,000 state and local workers in 2022, documents persistent occupational and operational-support gaps, and explains the constraints of short-term and categorical funding.
- **S4** — [Occupational Outlook Handbook: Epidemiologists](https://www.bls.gov/ooh/life-physical-and-social-science/epidemiologists.htm) (accessed 2026-07-23): Projects epidemiologist employment to grow 16 percent from 2024 to 2034 and states that many work in state and local governments providing public-health services and emergency response, with employment affected by government budgets.
- **S5** — [Employment Projections—2024–2034](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/ecopro_08282025.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-23): Projects government employment, excluding state and local education and hospitals, to change by negative 0.1 percent from 2024 to 2034; provides a broad government-sector anchor for demand uncertainty.
