# 541910 — Marketing Research and Public Opinion Polling

**Base tier:** PRIORITY · A 7.36 · L 4.63 · interval CONDITIONAL → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** A large share of research production consists of repeatable text, survey, and data workflows that current AI tools can accelerate under human review.
**Weakness:** The same tools are available to clients and software platforms, creating material self-service and renewal-pricing leakage.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market firms repeatedly supplying external customers with custom market research, brand or customer tracking, survey and polling programs, statistical sampling, qualitative research, data collection, analysis, and research reporting.
- Excluded: Captive corporate insights teams, internal support establishments, shells, non-control financing vehicles, pure software vendors, report publishers without a service operation, advertising agencies whose primary activity is not research, and firms without repeat outsourced-service customers.
- Customer and revenue model: Business, nonprofit, media, campaign, and government customers buy recurring tracking programs, annual or multi-period subscriptions, retainers, and repeat fixed-fee custom projects. Revenue can combine design, fieldwork or panels, analysis, reporting, and advisory interpretation.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Marketing research combines repeat client demand with unusually AI-exposed analytical and production workflows. The opportunity depends on converting faster questionnaire design, coding, analysis, and reporting into reliable delivery while protecting research validity and client trust. Fixed-fee and subscription structures permit some capture, but fragmented competition, renewal repricing, client-side tools, and synthetic data limit how much benefit remains with an agency.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate literature review, questionnaire drafting, data cleaning, segmentation, open-end coding, charting, and first-draft reporting. The highest-value workflow is human-directed automation with documented sources and review gates. Method choice, sample integrity, respondent experience, privacy, bias control, qualitative moderation, exception handling, and executive interpretation remain less substitutable.

## Value capture
Custom projects are often fixed fee and syndicated research can be subscription-based, allowing an operator to retain savings before repricing. Over subsequent renewals, customers can demand faster turnaround, expanded scope, or lower fees, and competitors can bid away part of the gain. Proprietary panels, benchmarks, vertical expertise, and trusted longitudinal data improve retention; commodity survey execution weakens it.

## Firm availability
Most firms classified in this industry provide external research services, but transferability varies with founder relationships, client concentration, and proprietary assets. General employer-owner plans and active small marketing-services add-ons support a meaningful transfer pool, although observed industry-specific control-transfer rates are unavailable.

## Demand durability
Organizations continue to need evidence about customers, markets, public opinion, pricing, and campaign effectiveness. AI may expand the number and speed of studies, but self-service platforms and synthetic respondents can displace traditional fieldwork and routine analysis. Complex methods, regulated data, high-stakes decisions, and longitudinal comparability are more likely to retain an accountable research operator.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are the absence of a six-digit wage-weighted task mix, no direct measure of AI benefit retention, and no denominator-based transfer history for firms in the EBITDA band. Data fraud, biased or nonrepresentative samples, privacy failures, hallucinated interpretation, and undisclosed synthetic data can destroy trust. Client insourcing and software substitution could shrink agency-required quantity even if total research activity grows.

## Caveats
- a: No six-digit occupation-by-wage matrix was located for NAICS 541910.
- a: O*NET covers market research analysts across many industries and does not describe all polling, fieldwork, panel, or agency-support jobs.
- a: MRII reports tool use and perceived benefits, not the share of wages technically substitutable.
- rho: Vendor capability does not establish reliable deployment across heterogeneous client data and methods.
- rho: Global professional survey results may not match smaller U.S. firms.
- rho: The range excludes pricing, customer self-service, and demand effects.
- e: The frozen firm count is margin-bridged and may misclassify firms with margins unlike the supplied benchmark.
- e: Establishment classification does not reveal firm-level repeat revenue or founder dependence.
- e: Ipsos is far larger than the lens and is used only to show that project revenue can recur through retained clients.
- s5: Owner intentions are not completed qualifying transfers.
- s5: The M&A source covers the broader marketing-services sector and does not provide a denominator of eligible firms.
- s5: Undisclosed small transactions and family transfers are difficult to observe.
- q: Public-company contract structures may not represent small custom-research agencies.
- q: No source directly measures five-year sharing of AI-derived gross benefit.
- q: Customer self-service and lost volume are reserved for demand primitives rather than included here.
- d5: No six-digit constant-price demand forecast was found.
- d5: Employment is not service quantity and can diverge as productivity changes.
- d5: The forecast concerns a broader industry and a broader occupation population.
- o: Synthetic-response expectations are opinions rather than observed replacement.
- o: Human oversight can be performed by a client-side team rather than a lens firm.
- o: Vendor product descriptions may emphasize capability and speed more than failure rates or applicability limits.
