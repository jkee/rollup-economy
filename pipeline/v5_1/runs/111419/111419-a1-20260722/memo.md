# 111419 — Other Food Crops Grown Under Cover

**Base tier:** no base (evidence-first) · A — · L — · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness STRESS_TEST_ONLY · action EVIDENCE_FIRST

**Driver:** Continuous environmental and crop data make covered production well suited to AI-assisted climate, irrigation, disease, energy, and scheduling decisions.
**Weakness:** Energy and capital intensity, import competition, biological risk, and missing market-sizing inputs can overwhelm labor automation benefits.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent U.S. operating businesses repeatedly growing and selling vegetables, fruits, melons, sprouts, hydroponic crops, and other food crops except mushrooms under greenhouses, vertical farms, high tunnels, or other protective cover to external customers.
- Excluded: Mushroom production, nursery and floriculture, open-field crops, passive real estate, pure distributors, captive growing units, research facilities, non-control financing vehicles, and operations without transferable control.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat wholesale and direct sales to grocers, foodservice distributors, processors, institutions, produce markets, and consumers, priced by crop, grade, package, season, contract, delivery, freshness, local attributes, and prevailing fresh-produce markets.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Covered food crops are unusually sensor-rich and controllable, making climate, irrigation, disease, scheduling, and energy optimization promising AI uses. Labor-intensive crop handling, harsh greenhouse economics, import competition, and the missing LMM-count and labor-share inputs materially weaken confidence.

## How AI changes the work
AI can optimize climate and fertigation, detect stress and disease, forecast yields, plan crops and labor, manage energy, and automate sales and records. Transplanting, pruning, harvesting, packing, sanitation, repairs, and accountable food-safety decisions remain.

## Value capture
Freshness, local availability, specialty crops, reliable delivery, and direct channels protect value. Wholesale buyers, perishability, seasonal gluts, and greenhouse imports from Mexico and Canada drive pass-through.

## Firm availability
Thousands of protected-crop operations exist, but many are tiny, mixed-category, captive, venture-backed, distressed, or facility rather than operating-company opportunities. No defensible frozen LMM count exists.

## Demand durability
Protected production expanded strongly through 2019, but nominal sales weakened and current outcomes depend on crop, energy, imports, and facility design. Nearly all surviving demand still requires an accountable grower.

## Risks and uncertainty
Risks include electricity and gas costs, crop disease, equipment and climate-control failure, food safety, perishability, retailer concentration, imports, capital intensity, fragile vertical-farm economics, data and controls integration, and missing labor-share and firm-count inputs.

## Caveats
- a: No defensible compensation-to-receipts denominator was provided.
- a: Specialty-crop automation evidence spans outdoor orchards and nurseries as well as covered food crops, while crop labor intensity varies widely.
- rho: Yield, quality, and energy improvements are not all labor substitution.
- rho: Glass greenhouses, low-tech high tunnels, hydroponics, sprouts, and vertical farms have different control and data maturity.
- e: The frozen dataset explicitly provides no defensible LMM firm count, so this conditional share cannot establish target volume.
- e: The 2,994 operations threshold was only $10,000 in horticultural sales and operations are not firms.
- s5: All-farm producer age is not owner age or transaction intent.
- s5: Horticulture ownership data include floriculture and nursery operations beyond 111419.
- q: Vegetable-market evidence includes open-field production and processing vegetables.
- q: Tomatoes, cucumbers, lettuce, herbs, berries, sprouts, and specialty crops have different pricing power and spoilage.
- d5: The physical growth comparison ends in 2019 and sales data are nominal and older.
- d5: Operations, production weight, and constant-quality customer demand are different quantities.
- o: Imports or consolidation can displace domestic operators without eliminating operator-required growing.
- o: Some retail or foodservice buyers may vertically integrate supply, shifting rather than eliminating the operator.

## Sources
- **S1** — [NAICS Definition: 111419 Other Food Crops Grown Under Cover](https://www.census.gov/naics/resources/archives/sect11.html) (accessed 2026-07-23): Defines the industry as food crops except mushrooms grown under glass or protective cover, including hydroponic crops, vegetables, fruits, melons, and sprouts.
- **S2** — [Growth in Greenhouses: Controlled Environment Agriculture Production, Operations on the Rise](https://www.ers.usda.gov/data-products/charts-of-note/109422) (accessed 2026-07-23): Reports protected operations rising from 1,476 in 2009 to 2,994 in 2019 and production increasing 56% to 7.86 million hundredweight.
- **S3** — [Automation for Specialty Crops](https://www.nifa.usda.gov/about-nifa/impacts/automation-specialty-crops) (accessed 2026-07-23): Describes sensors, automated irrigation scheduling, deep-learning disease scanning, and harvest-assistance research addressing labor and quality constraints.
- **S4** — [Integrated Systems Research in Automation and Sensors for Specialty Crops](https://www.nal.usda.gov/research-tools/food-safety-research-projects/integrated-systems-research-and-development-1) (accessed 2026-07-23): Describes sensing, mechanization, and automation systems intended to reduce specialty-crop labor, improve management, and lower production costs.
- **S5** — [Intelligent Optical Nerves for Precision Agriculture](https://www.nal.usda.gov/research-tools/food-safety-research-projects/intelligent-optical-nerves-precision-agriculture) (accessed 2026-07-23): Describes greenhouse sensor networks for micro-environment monitoring, production quality, and energy efficiency.
- **S6** — [2019 Census of Horticultural Specialties](https://www.nass.usda.gov/Publications/AgCensus/2017/Online_Resources/Census_of_Horticulture_Specialties/HORTIC.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-23): Reports 2,994 protected food-crop operations with $703.5 million sales in 2019 and historical operations and sales comparisons.
- **S7** — [2022 Census of Agriculture Farm Producers Demographic Profile](https://data.nass.usda.gov/Publications/AgCensus/2022/Online_Resources/Demographic_Profiles/cpd99000.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-23): Reports average producer age of 58.1, 38% age 65 or older, and broad family ownership and farm structure.
- **S8** — [Vegetables and Pulses Market Outlook](https://ers.usda.gov/topics/crops/vegetables-and-pulses/market-outlook) (accessed 2026-07-23): Reports imports supplied about one-third of U.S. vegetable availability in 2025 and heavy tomato and cucumber import reliance.
- **S9** — [U.S. Fresh Vegetable Imports from Mexico and Canada Continue to Surge](https://ers.usda.gov/amber-waves/2021/november/u-s-fresh-vegetable-imports-from-mexico-and-canada-continue-to-surge) (accessed 2026-07-23): Documents protected-culture import competition and the extension of imported fresh-vegetable market windows.
