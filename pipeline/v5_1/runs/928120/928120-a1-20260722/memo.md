## Business-model lens
- Included: No operating establishment in the defined U.S. NAICS population qualifies. The fixed lens would require a privately transferable lower-middle-market firm supplying recurring outsourced international-affairs services to external customers, while NAICS 928120 comprises establishments of U.S. and foreign governments.
- Excluded: U.S. and foreign ministries, embassies, consulates, diplomatic missions, foreign-assistance agencies, and other government establishments; private international-development contractors, policy consultancies, translation providers, travel processors, nonprofits, and software vendors classified elsewhere; captive units, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Government establishments conduct diplomacy, consular affairs, international assistance, policy, negotiation, representation, analysis, public engagement, and overseas program administration under statutory authority, appropriations, and consular fees rather than commercial recurring-service contracts with external customers.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
International affairs contains meaningful AI-assistance potential in research, multilingual synthesis, document production, consular processing, program administration, and public service. It is nevertheless outside the roll-up lens: the industry consists of government establishments, the dataset has no receipts-based labor share or LMM firm count, and there is no privately transferable operator population.

## How AI changes the work
AI can summarize large multilingual information flows, draft briefs and correspondence, support translation, identify patterns, triage routine cases, answer common questions, and assist grants, budgets, and reporting. Diplomats and accountable officials still must negotiate, represent sovereign positions, cultivate trust, protect sources, manage crises, and make legal and policy judgments.

## Value capture
Benefits may improve timeliness, analytical reach, citizen service, and public-resource use, but they do not become commercial margin for an in-industry owner. Appropriations, fee-funded public accounts, oversight, and policy priorities determine disposition of savings.

## Firm availability
There are no eligible firms under the authoritative industry definition. Acquisition opportunities among international-development contractors, consultancies, data providers, language-service firms, and diplomatic-support vendors must be evaluated in their own industries rather than imported into this record.

## Demand durability
Diplomacy, consular protection, secure travel, international negotiation, and crisis coordination are durable state functions. Passport workload and expanding cyber and technology diplomacy suggest resilient quantity, while foreign-assistance cuts, travel-policy shifts, embassy changes, and government reorganizations can materially alter the service basket.

## Risks and uncertainty
Task exposure is inferred from public roles, which omit classified and sensitive work. Security, privacy, source protection, multilingual reliability, records rules, procurement, and high-consequence decisions can slow implementation. The decisive weakness is structural rather than technical: sovereign government establishments cannot be acquired as LMM operating companies.

## Caveats
- a: State Department roles are a proxy for the wider U.S. and foreign-government industry population.
- a: Public sources omit many classified, sensitive, and national-security workflows.
- a: Exposure is likely much higher in document and consular processing than in political engagement and negotiation.
- rho: The public State inventory excludes or withholds national-security, defense, sensitive law-enforcement, and sensitive-but-unclassified uses.
- rho: Federal adoption evidence does not establish implementation rates for foreign governments.
- rho: Some adoption will expand analytical capacity or improve response time without reducing staffing.
- e: Private international-development, policy, technology, logistics, and language contractors may be acquisition candidates, but they are classified elsewhere.
- e: The injected LMM firm-count input is missing because government establishments are outside the relevant firm datasets; it must not be replaced with contractor counts.
- s5: Zero was not imputed because there is no eligible population on which to define a transfer probability.
- s5: Outsourcing a diplomatic-support contract is not a transfer of the government establishment.
- q: Consular fees are statutory or administratively set public charges, not evidence of a transferable firm's commercial pricing power.
- q: Budget savings may be redeployed or reduce future funding rather than accrue to an owner.
- d5: Passport applications are a narrow U.S. consular indicator, not a complete measure of international-affairs service quantity.
- d5: The 2019-to-2024 comparison spans pandemic disruption and recovery.
- d5: Foreign-government demand may diverge from U.S. workloads, and the estimate concerns constant-quality quantity rather than spending or employment.
- o: Digital self-service can reduce counter and case-processing work without removing governmental adjudication or accountability.
- o: Agency consolidation changes organizational location rather than eliminating the operator requirement.
