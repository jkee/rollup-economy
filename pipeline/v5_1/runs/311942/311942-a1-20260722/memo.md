# 311942 — Spice and Extract Manufacturing

**Driver:** Recurring custom formulations and safety-qualified customer relationships preserve operator demand while creating document-, data-, and knowledge-intensive workflows suited to AI.
**Weakness:** Eligibility and labor exposure are inferred from a large public flavor company and broader-industry occupation data rather than measured for private LMM spice and extract firms.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market U.S. firms repeatedly manufacturing custom or private-label spices, table salt, seasonings, non-coffee and non-meat flavoring extracts, natural food colorings, and qualifying dry food mixes for external food manufacturers, foodservice operators, distributors, or retailers.
- Excluded: Consumer-brand-only businesses without recurring outsourced production, commodity trading without manufacturing, coffee or meat extracts, industrial salts, captive plants, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Recurring business-to-business ingredient and finished-product sales, commonly based on customer formulas, specifications, private labels, or application requirements, with pricing and quantities set through purchase orders, master agreements, or frequently renewed annual arrangements.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
The relevant opportunity is the recurring custom-flavor, seasoning, extract, dry-mix, and private-label manufacturer. These firms sit inside durable food supply chains and perform safety-critical physical work, but AI exposure is concentrated in relatively well-paid formulation-support, sourcing, quality, planning, and commercial workflows rather than the bulk of plant labor.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate formulation search, specification comparison, certificates and supplier-document review, demand forecasting, scheduling, customer-response drafting, quality trend detection, vision inspection, and maintenance triage. Physical sampling, sensory validation, pathogen reduction, milling, blending, cleaning, packing, and final product release remain equipment- and operator-dependent.

## Value capture
Proprietary formulations, customer qualification, sensory expertise, and long relationships support retention, but most arrangements are priced frequently and many customers have substantial procurement leverage. Gains are therefore likely shared at renewal or rebid, especially for standardized ingredients.

## Firm availability
Customized Flavor Solutions and private-label supply are established business models, but the NAICS population also contains branded and commodity firms outside the lens. Aging-owner and marketplace evidence supports transfers, though formula ownership, key-person knowledge, import controls, food-safety history, and customer concentration can block deals.

## Demand durability
Flavor inputs serve cooking at home, packaged foods, snacks, beverages, quick-service restaurants, and other foodservice occasions. That diversification and low ingredient dose make demand resilient, with modest five-year quantity growth plausible, although reformulation, customer insourcing, and mature retail consumption limit upside.

## Risks and uncertainty
The key unknowns are the six-digit wage-weighted task mix, the service-model share of LMM firms, and contract-level pass-through. Raw-material volatility, import dependence, Salmonella or filth events, allergen mistakes, customer concentration, formula loss, and validation failures can dominate modest labor gains. AI use may improve quality or throughput without reducing paid hours.

## Caveats
- a: The closest occupation shares cover five-digit NAICS 31194 rather than 311942 alone.
- a: The source does not identify already-automated tasks, so that exclusion is judgmental.
- a: GenAI exposure research may miss machine vision, predictive maintenance, and process-control AI.
- rho: BTOS covers all industries and business functions, including low-intensity uses that do not yield labor substitution.
- rho: AI-related headcount effects to date need not predict avoidable hiring over five years.
- rho: Spice-specific safety obligations do not bar automation but raise the cost of validation and oversight.
- e: No public source directly measures eligible firms in 311942.
- e: McCormick is global, public, and far larger than the target band.
- e: Customized products qualify only when supplied as a repeat outsourced capability, not merely when sold as catalog ingredients.
- s5: Owner-age evidence is old and not industry-specific.
- s5: BizBuySell reports a broad other-manufacturing category and only transactions disclosed to its marketplace.
- s5: The range excludes internal reorganizations and non-transferable closures.
- q: A public global supplier's contracts and bargaining power may differ from private LMM firms.
- q: Retention should be higher for proprietary custom flavor systems and lower for standard spices or private-label bids.
- q: The estimate excludes demand loss and implementation failure, which belong in other primitives.
- d5: McCormick's geography, product scope, brand mix, and size differ from the lens.
- d5: Real foodservice spending is not a physical-volume measure for spice ingredients.
- d5: Reformulation can reduce ingredient weight while preserving flavor-service value, complicating quantity measurement.
- o: No source measures the share of year-five quantity requiring this operator type.
- o: Accountability may move between competing manufacturers without eliminating the operator role.
- o: Highly standardized spices may be sourced directly, while custom blends and extracts have stronger operator dependence.
