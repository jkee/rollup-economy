# 449122 — Window Treatment Retailers

**Base tier:** no base (evidence-first) · A — · L — · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → STRUCTURAL_OUT · readiness STRESS_TEST_ONLY · action EVIDENCE_FIRST

**Driver:** AI-assisted sales administration and quoting can raise throughput while custom measurement and installation preserve a local accountable operator.
**Weakness:** The eligible recurring-service subset and its transferable firm count are poorly measured inside a NAICS code defined primarily as merchandise retail.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent window-treatment retailers in the lower middle market that repeatedly provide externally accountable specification, in-home or on-site measurement, product selection, project coordination, installation, and post-installation support to property managers, designers, builders, or repeat residential customers.
- Excluded: Pure walk-in or e-commerce product sellers, big-box chains, manufacturers and wholesalers, one-person installers without transferable operations, captive dealer locations, and businesses whose economics are predominantly merchandise resale without a repeat outsourced-service relationship.
- Customer and revenue model: Mostly fixed-price projects combining product markup with design, measurement, installation, and support; recurring economics arise from repeat properties, trade-referral relationships, replacements, and service calls rather than subscriptions.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 449122 is heterogeneous because its official definition is merchandise retailing while only a subset couples the merchandise with a repeat, externally accountable measurement/design/installation service. The lens is narrowed to that service-bearing subset to keep the screen coherent.

## Executive view
The coherent opportunity is not window-covering retail broadly but the minority of dealers that combine product expertise with repeat, accountable site service. AI can remove administrative friction and some selling-support labor, while physical measurement, installation, and local responsibility keep the operator relevant. The largest uncertainty is whether enough scaled, transferable firms actually have repeat service relationships rather than one-off merchandise transactions.

## How AI changes the work
Near-term AI changes lead handling, product and specification retrieval, quote drafting, visualization support, appointment scheduling, follow-up, order entry, and bookkeeping. It is much less able to replace on-site measurement, installation, exception handling, tactile product judgment, or trust-heavy design decisions, so the labor opportunity is meaningful but bounded.

## Value capture
Project pricing allows early savings to remain in gross profit, particularly when faster response and fewer errors improve service. Retention erodes as online sellers, manufacturers, and local rivals copy the tools, and as trade or commercial customers rebid work. Firms with installation accountability and strong referral relationships should retain more than commodity retailers.

## Firm availability
Only a minority of NAICS establishments appear eligible because the code is defined around retailing merchandise and service is incidental. Qualifying targets need staff, repeat trade or property relationships, supplier access, and processes that survive the owner. General small-business succession evidence supports some five-year transfer flow, but direct niche deal data are absent and the supplied LMM count is missing.

## Demand durability
Window coverings continue to serve privacy, light, aesthetics, and energy-management needs across a large installed base. Real quantity demand should be roughly stable to moderately higher over five years, but housing turnover, remodeling cycles, and product mix create a wide range. Custom and multi-site projects retain an operator; stock products are increasingly self-served or bought direct.

## Risks and uncertainty
The case weakens if dealer economics are mostly product markup, if repeat accounts are rare, or if manufacturers and marketplaces take control of configuration and installation. Fragmented catalogs and costly measurement errors may slow AI implementation. Evidence is mostly from broader retail occupations, old channel research, general-owner surveys, and a commercial market forecast rather than direct six-digit operating data.

## Caveats
- a: No six-digit occupation matrix exists in the cited BLS table; 4491 includes furniture and other home-furnishings retailers.
- a: The provided compensation-to-receipts input is LOW quality and measured at ancestor 44-45 with a 2024-wage/2022-receipts vintage mismatch, so it should not be read as dealer-specific labor intensity.
- a: Exposure is current-task opportunity, not evidence that headcount has already been removed.
- rho: Gallup's frontline-worker adoption result is economy-wide rather than dealer-specific.
- rho: Implementation excludes revenue uplift and assumes current exposed tasks have not already been automated.
- e: DOE channel evidence is old and volume-weighted rather than firm-weighted.
- e: The supplied n input is intentionally missing, so this eligibility share cannot produce a defensible eligible-firm count.
- e: Repeat residential referrals alone may not create truly recurring contracted revenue.
- s5: Gallup includes all small-business industries and owner-reported intentions rather than completed transactions.
- s5: The Census age statistic covers responding owners of employer firms and is not specific to window-treatment retailers.
- q: No source directly measures pass-through of automation savings in this industry.
- q: The lens mixes residential projects with trade and commercial accounts, whose rebid cadence and bargaining power differ.
- q: E-commerce growth is total retail, not window-treatment-specific, and is used only as competitive context.
- d5: The market forecast is vendor-produced and revenue-based.
- d5: DOE installed-base research dates to 2013 and does not establish current replacement cycles.
- d5: The range does not assume continued nominal price inflation.
- o: Current retail e-commerce penetration is not a direct forecast of service elimination.
- o: Certified independent installers could preserve physical work while disintermediating the lens firm.
- o: Commercial and residential operator requirements differ substantially.
