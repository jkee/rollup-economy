# 611691 — Exam Preparation and Tutoring

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `611691-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** PRIORITY · A 6.70 · L 4.43 · interval CONDITIONAL → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** A high labor share is paired with directly automatable practice, feedback, preparation, and administrative workflows that can expand tutor capacity.
**Weakness:** The same AI capability that creates labor savings can eliminate routine paid sessions and rapidly transmit savings to customers through abundant low-cost alternatives.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market tutoring and exam-preparation firms that repeatedly sell academic remediation, enrichment, standardized-test preparation, professional-exam review, or related online or center-based instruction to students, families, schools, or employers.
- Excluded: Solo tutors without a transferable operating business, captive school or employer programs, nonprofit or government services without transferable control, educational testing without instruction, degree-granting schools, and software products that do not provide an accountable tutoring or preparation service.
- Customer and revenue model: Families and students commonly buy per-session, monthly, prepaid-package, membership, or fixed-course instruction; schools and organizations may buy cohorts or contracted tutoring. Revenue repeats through weekly sessions, multi-month programs, successive subjects, exam cycles, referrals, and institutional renewals.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Exam preparation and tutoring has unusually direct AI exposure because much of the product is repeated explanation, practice, feedback, and progress tracking. An accountable operator can still matter for motivation, diagnosis, parent trust, safeguarding, and high-stakes outcomes, but firms face a genuine risk that software captures the simplest service volume and forces productivity gains into lower prices or richer delivery.

## How AI changes the work
AI can generate curriculum-aligned questions, personalize practice, explain errors, draft session plans and progress reports, coach less-experienced tutors, schedule sessions, and handle routine inquiries. The operationally credible near-term design is blended: software handles repeatable instruction and administration while tutors intervene on misconceptions, motivation, strategy, relationships, and sensitive student needs.

## Value capture
Session, monthly, package, and fixed-course pricing creates initial room to retain labor savings. Frequent renewals and transparent alternatives shorten that advantage; families and institutions can demand lower prices, tutors can disintermediate the center, and AI-only or free offerings can reset willingness to pay.

## Firm availability
The frozen LMM population is larger than in many niche education categories, but eligibility and transfer evidence remain uncertain. Franchised or multi-instructor centers with documented curriculum, lead flow, and customer records should transfer more readily than founder-star practices; broad education sales and aging-owner transitions show a market without establishing a 611691 rate.

## Demand durability
Academic gaps, college competition, professional credentials, and continued SAT and ACT participation support ongoing need. BLS nevertheless expects only modest tutor employment growth and explicitly anticipates AI substitution, while test-optional admissions, household affordability, free content, and self-service software cap the current service basket.

## Risks and uncertainty
The major risks are rapid AI-only substitution, weak differentiation, tutor disintermediation, dependence on a founder or franchise, customer-acquisition cost, short consumer renewal cycles, school-contract concentration, changing admissions policy, and unequal household ability to pay. Current evidence measures occupations, exams, applicants, and broad education transactions rather than LMM-firm economics, so task, eligibility, transfer, and retention ranges remain judgment-heavy.

## Caveats
- a: No six-digit wage-weighted task study was found.
- a: BLS tutor data cover self-employed, public, and other private settings outside the lens.
- a: Some exposed content and assessment tasks are already automated, so only the residual opportunity belongs in this primitive.
- rho: The Stanford trial measured learning progress from tutor assistance, not labor removed or avoided in LMM firms.
- rho: Implementation may increase service quality or tutor capacity without permitting equivalent payroll reduction.
- rho: Professional-exam, special-needs, and K-12 tutoring have different quality and compliance constraints.
- e: The 2022 NAICS index includes online learning marketplaces as well as direct providers, increasing business-model heterogeneity.
- e: The frozen LMM firm count is itself an estimate and may include firms whose normalized EBITDA falls outside the target band.
- e: Public listings and franchise examples are not representative samples of the full industry.
- s5: No source measures a five-year transfer probability for eligible LMM 611691 firms.
- s5: BizBuySell voluntarily reported sales skew small and combine childcare, schools, test preparation, and other education businesses.
- s5: McKinsey's modeled retirement transitions include closures and firms across industries and size bands.
- q: No direct study measures five-year pass-through of AI-driven tutoring savings.
- q: Exam-prep courses, weekly remediation, professional review, and school contracts differ materially in price elasticity.
- q: Volume displacement is excluded here and reflected in demand and operator-required quantities.
- d5: Employment projections combine demand, productivity, and delivery-model shifts and cover ten rather than five years.
- d5: SAT and ACT takers overlap, and school-day testing does not necessarily generate commercial preparation demand.
- d5: NAEP academic need does not establish ability or willingness to pay for private tutoring.
- o: The BLS statement is directional and does not quantify service-volume substitution.
- o: The Stanford trial studied a Southern school district and math tutoring, not consumer LMM operators across subjects.
- o: Rapid improvements in model reliability, multimodal interaction, and verified curriculum alignment could move this range downward.
