**Driver:** AI addresses high-cost computational, analytical, documentation, and experiment-selection work while specialized facilities and expertise preserve outsourced delivery needs.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market firms in NAICS 541715 that provide recurring or repeat outsourced research and experimental-development services to external commercial or government customers in physical, engineering, agricultural, environmental, computer, chemical, food, geological, health, medical, pharmaceutical, robotics, aerospace, and other covered sciences.
- Excluded: Captive manufacturer or corporate R&D units, firms principally developing their own intellectual property or products rather than serving external customers, shells, non-control financing vehicles, nontransferable owner-only practices, nanotechnology and biotechnology R&D, routine analytical testing, engineering design, prototype production, and social-science or humanities research.
- Customer and revenue model: Customers include manufacturers, pharmaceutical and medical-device companies, aerospace and defense organizations, technology companies, agriculture and food businesses, and federal or other public agencies. Revenue may come from cost-reimbursement and cost-plus contracts, fixed-price or milestone projects, fee-for-service and per-unit work, time and materials, full-time-equivalent arrangements, multi-year programs, and reimbursed pass-through costs.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
This industry combines substantial information-intensive scientific labor with laboratory, field, hardware, safety, and validation work that remains operator-dependent. AI can accelerate research throughput, but the eligible external-service population is much smaller than the full NAICS universe because the code also includes captive and proprietary R&D.

## How AI changes the work
AI compresses literature and prior-art review, candidate generation, simulation, coding, data cleaning and analysis, report drafting, project administration, and experiment selection. Robotics and active learning extend automation into bounded laboratory workflows, while humans retain objective setting, protocol design, physical exceptions, safety, interpretation, reproducibility, and scientific accountability.

## Value capture
Fixed-price, milestone, and per-unit work can retain benefits through lower delivery cost and added capacity. Cost-reimbursement, cost-plus, full-time-equivalent, and time-and-materials structures pass much of the labor benefit to sponsors, while competitive rebids and scope adjustments erode retention over the full horizon.

## Firm availability
General owner succession and active life-sciences services M&A indicate a real transfer market, but exact availability is obscured by institutional labs, nonprofits, venture-backed proprietary R&D, captive units, and businesses whose value depends on founders, patents, clearances, or a small number of sponsors.

## Demand durability
National R&D spending and broader-industry projections support moderate growth, especially where customers need scarce facilities, regulated execution, or specialized teams. Funding volatility, program cancellation, customer insourcing, and uneven government budgets create downside, and AI-driven customer self-service may shift rather than expand outsourced demand.

## Risks and uncertainty
The code spans many sciences and business models, while occupation, contract, AI-adoption, outsourcing, and transaction evidence is mostly broader than 541715. Scientific failure, cyber and intellectual-property risk, specialized capital, regulatory burdens, key-person dependence, and uncertain sponsor funding can overwhelm expected productivity gains.

## Caveats
- a: The occupational distribution is for broader NAICS 541700 and includes biotechnology and social-science R&D outside this code.
- a: Employment shares are not wage shares, although the estimate accounts directionally for different occupational wages.
- a: Published AI research examples may select unusually data-rich or automation-friendly scientific problems.
- a: The injected labor-intensity input combines 2024 QCEW wages with 2022 SUSB receipts and the specified IPS harmonization, so its vintage mismatch is not evidence of task exposure.
- rho: The researcher survey spans countries, institutions, and scientific fields and measures use and perceptions rather than implemented labor savings.
- rho: Autonomous-lab evidence demonstrates technical feasibility in specific materials workflows, not scalable economics across the industry.
- rho: Regulated medical, defense, and safety-critical projects may require slower validation and approval than the central case.
- e: No source measures the share of exact-industry firms that earn recurring or repeat external service revenue.
- e: The code spans radically different scientific fields, capital requirements, customer types, and intellectual-property models.
- e: The injected count of 1,716 firms is margin-bridged from SUSB size classes using a 15.65% EBITDA margin and may misclassify research firms with unusually volatile or nonstandard margins.
- s5: The succession source measures intentions across industries, not completed qualifying control transfers.
- s5: The M&A source combines CROs, CDMOs, and suppliers globally and overlaps only part of exact NAICS 541715.
- s5: Asset purchases of patents, programs, or laboratories may not constitute qualifying transfers of an eligible operating firm.
- s5: The estimated 1,716-firm denominator inherits uncertainty from SUSB size classes and the 15.65% margin bridge.
- q: Federal contracting rules establish permissible and typical structures, not revenue weights for all 541715 firms.
- q: Fortrea is a large global clinical CRO whose customer, scale, and regulatory profile differs from much of the exact industry.
- q: No source directly measures commercial sharing of AI benefits in outsourced scientific R&D.
- d5: BLS output is available at broader NAICS 541700 and employment at 541710, both including nanotechnology and biotechnology or adjacent R&D.
- d5: NSF total R&D spending includes captive business, government, higher-education, and nonprofit performance rather than outsourced services only.
- d5: Current-dollar and modeled spending estimates require care when inferring constant-price service quantity.
- d5: Government funding, commercial research programs, and venture-backed customer demand can change abruptly.
- o: No direct measure exists for the year-five operator-required share of exact-industry demand.
- o: Computational R&D may become software-supplied faster than wet-lab, field, hardware, agricultural, or safety-critical research.
- o: The accountable entity could shift from a lower-middle-market provider to a platform, equipment vendor, hyperscaler, or customer-owned laboratory.
