# 711310 — Promoters of Performing Arts, Sports, and Similar Events with Facilities

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `711310-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 6.86 · L 1.51 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** AI-addressable planning, marketing, customer-service, and settlement work sits around a durable physical venue operation with multiple revenue streams.
**Weakness:** NAICS 711310 mixes repeat outsourced facility management with proprietary promotion and ancillary hospitality, leaving eligibility and transferability poorly observed.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market operators that repeatedly manage and staff arenas, stadiums, theaters, music halls, fairgrounds, and similar facilities for external promoters, event organizers, sponsors, renters, and attendees, including recurring venue booking, ticketing, marketing, event settlement, and facility operations.
- Excluded: One-off project entities, passive real-estate ownership, public or captive facilities without an outsourced customer relationship, sports teams and performing-arts companies classified elsewhere, promoters without facilities, and food-and-beverage operations that are not ancillary to venue management.
- Customer and revenue model: Customers include promoters, artists, corporate and private renters, sponsors, municipalities, and event attendees. Revenue commonly combines management or rental fees with tickets and ticketing fees, sponsorship, premium space, parking, merchandise commissions, food and beverage, and event-profit shares.
- Deviation from default lens: The code combines self-promoted venue economics with repeat outsourced facility management. The lens retains only operators with repeat external-customer service relationships, because self-promoted events alone do not constitute a recurring outsourced service.

## Executive view
The coherent opportunity is repeat third-party venue management and operation, not proprietary event speculation. AI can reduce administrative and commercial workload around the event, while the physical facility and public-assembly obligations preserve the need for an operator. The code is heterogeneous, and eligibility is the largest gating judgment.

## How AI changes the work
The most implementable uses are campaign and proposal drafting, CRM follow-up, customer-service triage, booking support, schedule and staffing suggestions, invoice and settlement reconciliation, contract extraction, reporting, and knowledge retrieval. Contactless entry and self-service continue to reduce front-of-house tasks. Security, maintenance, production, hospitality, crowd management, and live exception handling remain much less substitutable.

## Value capture
Savings in internal overhead can be retained initially, especially where revenue is fixed rental, ticketing, sponsorship, parking, or premium-space income. Over five years, competitive bids, promoter profit shares, and renewal negotiations should transfer a meaningful portion to customers and partners. Multi-stream venue economics provide more levers than a pure hourly model but also make attribution difficult.

## Firm availability
The supplied LMM firm count covers the code, but only a portion has the recurring external-service model. Ownership transfer is plausible in the mid-teens over five years, yet leases, municipal agreements, permits, real estate, and operator relationships can make control transfer more complex than an ordinary service company sale.

## Demand durability
Official projections support modest real growth in the broader promoter-and-agent market. Physical attendance, venue utilization, sponsorship, and ancillary purchases sustain demand for operators even as ticketing and customer interfaces digitize. Downside risk remains high because events are discretionary and local facilities have fixed capacity and cost.

## Risks and uncertainty
The principal research weakness is the lack of six-digit, LMM-specific evidence for outsourced-service eligibility, task mix, transactions, and contract pass-through. A bad case is a capital-heavy, owner-dependent operator whose revenue is mainly self-promoted events or restaurants, with municipal restrictions and little transferable management income. AI may improve service quality without reducing headcount, especially where labor is event-variable or safety-critical.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation table is for combined NAICS 711300 and includes promoters without facilities.
- a: ILO exposure is global and occupation-level, and exposure does not imply feasible substitution.
- a: OEWS employment shares include employers of all sizes rather than the lower-middle-market lens.
- rho: BTOS is economy-wide and its AI question changed in late 2025 to cover any business function.
- rho: Current use is not the same as implemented labor substitution or avoidable hiring.
- rho: Public-assembly safety and contractual approval requirements vary by venue and municipality.
- e: No source measures the eligible share of firms in the specified EBITDA band.
- e: One public operator's revenue mix is illustrative and not representative of LMM firms.
- e: A firm may mix eligible outsourced contracts with ineligible proprietary events.
- s5: Gallup measures stated plans, not completed qualifying transfers.
- s5: The survey is cross-industry and not restricted to the EBITDA band.
- s5: Asset sales, venue closures, and internal reorganizations must be excluded.
- q: No source isolates AI-created gross benefit or its contractual pass-through.
- q: Retention differs sharply between fixed-fee management, rental, self-promotion, and profit-share arrangements.
- q: Ancillary consumer revenue is demand-sensitive and should not be confused with retained cost savings.
- d5: The BLS projection is broader than 711310 and spans ten years rather than five.
- d5: Real output is not a pure constant-quality service-quantity measure.
- d5: Live events are discretionary and exposed to recessions, artist supply, weather, and local competition.
- o: This is a judgment about operator necessity, not a measured automation statistic.
- o: Large standardized arenas may automate more than small heterogeneous theaters and fairgrounds.
- o: Regulation and insurer requirements can preserve human accountability even when tasks are automated.
