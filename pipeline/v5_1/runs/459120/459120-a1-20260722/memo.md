**Driver:** Enthusiast trust, physical custody, specialist service, and recurring community relationships can preserve a local operator while administrative workflows become AI-assisted.
**Weakness:** Recurring outsourced-service firms are a tiny, poorly measured subset of a product-led and highly trend-sensitive retail industry.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market hobby, toy, and game retailers with repeat external-customer services such as paid memberships, grading or authentication intermediation, repair, rental, instruction, institutional programs, or organized-play operations supported by recurring fees or repeat service purchases.
- Excluded: Pure merchandise stores and e-commerce sellers, product manufacturers and publishers, arcades and entertainment venues classified outside retail, captive operations, shells, non-control financing vehicles, and firms without a recurring or repeat outsourced-service relationship.
- Customer and revenue model: Consumer, collector, club, school, and institutional relationships combining merchandise margin with memberships, grading or authentication fees, repair or rental charges, instruction, events, or repeat program supply; much product revenue remains transactional and seasonal.
- Deviation from default lens: The code is predominantly transactional retail but includes a small, distinct subset built around recurring memberships, collector services, repair, rental, instruction, organized play, or institutional programs. The lens is narrowed to that subset for business-model coherence, not attractiveness.

## Executive view
The code is overwhelmingly transactional retail, leaving only a small qualifying set built around memberships, grading or authentication, repair, rental, instruction, organized play, or institutional programs. AI can streamline research, cataloging, customer communications, marketing, ordering, scheduling, and administration, but physical custody, trust, specialist judgment, and community facilitation constrain substitution.

## How AI changes the work
Useful applications include product-description and marketplace content, recommendation and rules support, collectible identification triage, event promotion, membership service, customer messaging, ordering, inventory alerts, bookkeeping, and staff scheduling. Human review remains important for authenticity, condition, safety, nuanced hobby advice, disputes, and live events.

## Value capture
Posted prices and discretionary service fees permit initial retention, particularly in trusted or locally differentiated services. Transparent online comparison, mass merchants, direct publisher and manufacturer channels, digital substitutes, and price-sensitive enthusiasts should force sharing through lower prices, richer memberships, or better service over time.

## Firm availability
Eligible firms are rare within the NAICS code and often owner-dependent. Broad owner aging and an active independent-retail resale market support some transfer flow, but most observed retail deals are below the target EBITDA band and specialized inventory or community goodwill can be difficult to diligence and transfer.

## Demand durability
Toy unit demand returned to growth in 2025, but gains were concentrated in collectibles, licensed properties, and a few supercategories. Physical, trust-based services should retain operator demand, while video-game delivery, product discovery, membership administration, and collectibles trading continue shifting online.

## Risks and uncertainty
The qualifying population is not directly measured and may be too small for a scalable screen. Demand is discretionary, seasonal, fad- and license-driven, and exposed to tariffs, allocation, counterfeit or provenance risk, digital substitution, and rapid category rotation. The occupation evidence is only available at a broader parent-industry level.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation source is the three-digit NAICS 459000 parent and includes several materially different retail industries.
- a: Employment shares are not wage-weighted task shares, and self-employed owners are excluded.
- a: Current automation in POS, marketplaces, online cataloging, and loyalty systems is not separately observed.
- rho: BLS projections span all industries and ten years rather than this lens and five years.
- rho: GameStop is far larger than the target population, and its cost reduction included store closures and divestitures rather than AI alone.
- rho: Some workforce change reflects self-checkout and e-commerce, not AI substitution.
- e: The parent occupation mix includes unrelated retail segments and cannot identify firm-level recurring revenue.
- e: GameStop demonstrates model existence but is not representative of independent LMM firms.
- e: The frozen n input is missing, so this share cannot be converted to an eligible firm count in the packet.
- s5: The Census age statistic is cross-industry, owner-based, and reflects data year 2018 respondents.
- s5: BizBuySell is a marketplace sample and skews toward Main Street transactions below the target EBITDA band.
- s5: Older ownership does not imply an external qualifying sale within five years.
- q: GameStop and toy manufacturers are larger and more channel-diverse than the target firms.
- q: Merchandise margin, licensing economics, and service-fee retention differ substantially.
- q: The estimate cannot fully separate AI-derived savings from inventory mix and store-footprint actions.
- d5: One-year toy unit growth followed two weaker years and may not persist.
- d5: Category growth is highly concentrated in licensed and collectible properties with short or cyclical life spans.
- d5: GameStop's mix is not representative of traditional toys, crafts, models, or independent tabletop stores.
- o: A distressed public chain's footprint changes may not generalize to profitable specialist operators.
- o: Digital substitution differs sharply between video games, tabletop play, models, crafts, toys, and collectibles.
- o: The estimate applies only to the narrowed service-bearing basket, not ordinary product sales.
