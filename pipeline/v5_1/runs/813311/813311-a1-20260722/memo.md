**Driver:** The principal driver is AI exposure in research, writing, fundraising, communications, translation, and reporting workflows that consume substantial staff time.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market organizations classified in 813311 that repeatedly provide human-rights advocacy, research, monitoring, education, legal or policy support, public campaigns, and related programs to external constituencies or funding partners.
- Excluded: Volunteer-only groups, entities without recurring operations, purely grantmaking foundations, political campaign organizations, captive programs, government units, shells, non-control financing vehicles, organizations outside the target normalized-EBITDA band, and activities classified primarily as direct social services rather than human-rights advocacy.
- Customer and revenue model: Revenue is commonly mission funding rather than a conventional customer fee: individual and institutional contributions, foundation and government grants, memberships, and restricted program support, with some contracts, training, publications, events, or professional-service fees. Funders and beneficiaries are often different parties, and restrictions may govern how savings can be redeployed.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Human-rights organizations contain substantial research, writing, reporting, fundraising, and communications work that AI can accelerate, but high-stakes judgment, trust, coalition work, and accountable representation remain human. The central structural constraint is that the category is dominated by mission entities without conventional transferable equity, so operational usefulness does not translate cleanly into an owner-controlled roll-up.

## How AI changes the work
AI can assist policy and document review, monitoring summaries, translation, grant drafting, donor segmentation, campaign content, meeting synthesis, intake triage, and routine reporting. Humans still must validate claims, protect vulnerable sources, choose advocacy strategy, negotiate coalitions, exercise legal judgment, manage crises, and maintain community and funder trust.

## Value capture
Unrestricted donors and fixed grants do not automatically reclaim every saved hour, allowing productivity to become more mission output or avoided hiring. Restricted budgets, reimbursement rules, renewal scrutiny, governance costs, and expectations for expanded impact share the benefit, and any retained value belongs to the mission rather than equity holders.

## Firm availability
A minority of organizations may have recurring contracted programs or separable earned-revenue activities, but most cause-promotion entities are not ordinary owner-controlled service firms. Legal form, governing rights, funding restrictions, tax status, concentration, and asset dedication require firm-level diligence before treating the frozen commercial firm-count bridge as an acquisition population.

## Demand durability
Civil-liberties, justice, equality, migration, and accountability needs are persistent, and broad public-benefit giving has recently grown. Funding remains cyclical, politically exposed, concentrated, and uneven across causes, creating a wide five-year range despite durable underlying need.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are nontransferable governance, restricted assets, donor and grant concentration, political and regulatory shifts, loss of tax status, reputational harm, misinformation, confidentiality breaches, biased outputs, source protection failures, cross-border data exposure, grant nonrenewal, and the mismatch between mission surplus and commercial EBITDA.

## Caveats
- a: BLS describes a broad social and community-service management occupation, not the full 813311 workforce.
- a: Google surveys participants in its nonprofit program across causes, sizes, and countries rather than U.S. human-rights organizations alone.
- a: Exposure to drafting or summarization does not establish safe substitution in sensitive advocacy, legal, or beneficiary-facing work.
- rho: The nonprofit survey measures use and perceived barriers, not realized labor substitution in 813311.
- rho: Informal staff use may not become governed organization-wide workflow adoption.
- rho: High-risk human-rights data and communications may require stricter controls than the average nonprofit workflow.
- e: NAICS classification identifies activity, not legal form or owner-control rights.
- e: Some human-rights organizations may be 501(c)(4), foreign-affiliated, fiscally sponsored, or taxable rather than 501(c)(3).
- e: The frozen n estimate applies a commercial EBITDA-margin bridge to a mission-driven category where surplus and ownership have different meanings.
- s5: Federal tax rules do not measure the frequency of nonprofit mergers or changes in board control.
- s5: A nonprofit merger may transfer programs and governance without providing a qualifying seller-owned control interest.
- s5: The estimate may miss taxable human-rights service firms classified in this code.
- q: No cited source measures savings retention after AI adoption in human-rights organizations.
- q: Restricted grants and cost-reimbursement contracts can prevent savings from becoming freely redeployable capacity.
- q: Mission retention is not equivalent to EBITDA or cash distributable to an acquirer.
- d5: Public-society-benefit giving is broader than human-rights organizations and does not measure service quantity.
- d5: Strong aggregate giving can coexist with severe funding losses for particular causes or organizations.
- d5: Political restrictions, donor fatigue, public controversy, and changes in government funding create asymmetric downside.
- o: Continued social need does not guarantee demand for the same organization or service basket.
- o: BLS occupational duties are broader than human-rights advocacy.
- o: Digital organizing and direct-to-public AI tools may reduce intermediary information work faster than the estimate assumes.
