# 611699 — All Other Miscellaneous Schools and Instruction

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `611699-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.71 · L 2.52 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Recurring credential renewal and employer compliance demand support a standardized workflow in which AI can remove cognitive and administrative labor while practical verification persists.
**Weakness:** The official code is highly heterogeneous, so the narrowed segment may contain relatively few of the supplied LMM firms and lacks direct transaction or product-volume data.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Private firms repeatedly providing outsourced health, safety, emergency-response, and compliance-oriented instruction to employers and other organizations, including first-aid, CPR/AED, lifeguard, and closely related skills courses when classified in 611699.
- Excluded: Consumer yoga and spiritual schools, Bible schools, public-speaking and speed-reading programs, hobby or self-development instruction, military-combat schools, captive internal training departments, software-only course publishers, and firms whose principal activity belongs in a more specific education NAICS code.
- Customer and revenue model: Employer and institutional customers buy scheduled public seats or on-site group classes, commonly priced per participant or per cohort, with repeat demand driven by employee turnover, credential expiration, and regulatory or workplace policy requirements.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 611699 combines materially different models, including yoga schools, public-speaking and speed-reading instruction, survival training, Bible schools, and first-aid/CPR instruction. The screen is narrowed to recurring outsourced health, safety, emergency-response, and compliance instruction because those firms share credential cycles, employer buyers, practical-skill assessment, and cohort delivery; combining them with consumer spiritual, fitness, hobby, and personal-development schools would make the primitives incoherent.

## Executive view
The useful coherent slice of this residual NAICS category is recurring outsourced health, safety, and emergency-skills training for employers. It has a substantial digitalizable administration and cognitive-learning layer, but its durable core is verified practical instruction delivered under recognizable credential and record controls.

## How AI changes the work
AI can support enrollment, customer service, renewal reminders, scheduling, roster reconciliation, localized examples, instructor preparation, written testing, remediation prompts, and audit-ready records. It is less able to replace hands-on demonstration, psychomotor observation, equipment management, identity assurance, or accountable credential issuance.

## Value capture
Per-participant and cohort pricing creates room to retain productivity gains, particularly through higher class fill, fewer cancellations, and less administrative labor. Standardized course products and employer procurement also make prices comparable, so some savings will migrate to customers or credential owners.

## Firm availability
The headline industry is too heterogeneous to interpret as one acquisition population. After narrowing, the eligible set is likely a minority of the supplied 70 LMM-band firms, and transferability depends on whether credential affiliations, instructors, customer contracts, and operating records survive the founder.

## Demand durability
Two-year credential cycles, employee turnover, workplace rules, and employer safety policies produce repeat demand. Digital learning can remove classroom time, but practical skills and verified completion preserve an operator role. Broad industry projections imply moderate growth, with segment-specific volume still unobserved.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest risks are residual-code misclassification, dependence on credential bodies, low barriers for instructors to form competing local businesses, procurement price pressure, customer insourcing, and faster approval of automated skills verification. The weakest evidence concerns segment size, firm eligibility, owner succession, and actual course-volume trends.

## Caveats
- a: The BLS staffing pattern is for 611600, which includes fine arts, sports, language, tutoring, driving, and other schools beyond both 611699 and the narrowed lens.
- a: O*NET self-enrichment teachers are broader than compliance and emergency-skills instructors.
- a: The supplied compensation ratio uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and may be distorted by the industry's heterogeneous business mix.
- rho: Existing e-learning proves digital delivery feasibility, not that generative AI is approved for controlled course content or final assessment.
- rho: Affiliation rules and credentialing platforms differ across AHA, Red Cross, HSI, state, and specialty programs.
- rho: The estimate excludes pricing, demand, and valuation effects.
- e: No current 611699 product-line firm-count table was found that isolates the narrowed lens.
- e: The supplied 70-firm LMM count is itself an EBITDA-band estimate derived from size classes and a sector margin.
- e: Firms may report neighboring NAICS codes, especially professional training, technical schools, fitness instruction, or educational support services.
- s5: No national transfer-rate series for 611699 or the narrowed segment was located.
- s5: Owner age alone does not establish willingness to sell or operational transferability.
- s5: Deaths, internal reorganizations, credential-only changes, and asset sales without a continuing operation are excluded.
- q: One small-provider rate card is not a national pricing sample.
- q: Credential bodies may capture part of the savings through course materials, card, platform, or affiliation fees.
- q: Volume effects are assigned to d5 and o, and implementation difficulty to rho.
- d5: Employment is an input and not a direct measure of constant-quality training quantity.
- d5: BLS 611600 includes multiple industries excluded from the narrowed lens.
- d5: AHA card validity and OSHA rules establish recurrence for portions of demand but not a national volume growth rate.
- o: The estimate is most applicable to CPR, first aid, and similar psychomotor credentials, not every health or safety course.
- o: Some cognitive-only compliance modules can already be supplied entirely by software.
- o: Credential bodies could approve automated skills stations more quickly than assumed.
