**Driver:** A large analytical and engineering work share, paired with demonstrated machine-learning reductions in seismic interpretation time, creates material productivity potential.
**Weakness:** Commodity-linked cyclicality and the industry's mixture of field-intensive services and software-like data products make both automation exposure and value capture highly heterogeneous.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market firms providing recurring or repeat outsourced geophysical acquisition, processing, interpretation, mapping, monitoring, data management, and related consulting, including seismic, magnetic, gravity, electrical, and electromagnetic methods.
- Excluded: Captive operator teams; core sampling, drilling, and mine-development services; software-only publishers; equipment manufacturers, resellers, or rental businesses without a service operation; and one-off owner-only practices without repeat outsourced demand.
- Customer and revenue model: Customers include oil and gas, mining and minerals, utilities, infrastructure and civil engineering, environmental users, and government. Revenue is earned through project or fixed-fee work, time-and-materials or day rates, per-unit surveys, monitoring contracts, data licenses, and consulting.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Geophysical surveying combines high labor intensity with a wage-heavy analytical workforce and proven opportunities to accelerate seismic interpretation. Its attractiveness is tempered by physical field execution, expert quality control, heterogeneous revenue models, and commodity-sensitive demand.

## How AI changes the work
The clearest near-term effect is augmentation and partial automation of processing, horizon picking, image handling, GIS work, reporting, and data search. Acquisition planning, field operations, exception handling, validation, geological judgment, and customer accountability remain durable human tasks.

## Value capture
Providers with fixed-fee work, proprietary datasets, recurring monitoring, or differentiated interpretation can retain gains through throughput and margin. Day-rate and time-and-materials models, rebids, and sophisticated customers are more likely to pass savings through.

## Firm availability
A general employer-owner succession pool and visible specialist consolidation suggest a meaningful acquisition pipeline, but the exact count is uncertain and many firms may be small, key-person dependent, internally transferred, or outside the lower-middle-market band.

## Demand durability
Subsurface information remains necessary across energy, minerals, infrastructure, environmental work, and government. Diversification supports durability, while exploration budgets and commodity cycles can make individual firms and end markets volatile.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are exact-industry occupational payroll, actual production AI penetration among smaller providers, contract mix, transaction completion, and the use of broader or global proxies. Mixed acquisition, processing, licensing, software, and consulting models also widen every operating estimate.

## Caveats
- a: Pell's exact-industry occupation shares are commercial estimates and are not wage-weighted.
- a: The SLB case may reflect unusually suitable data, tooling, and expert support.
- a: The injected labor-intensity input uses 2024 QCEW wages over 2022 SUSB receipts with the specified harmonization, so its vintage mismatch should not be confused with direct evidence for task exposure.
- rho: Adoption surveys cover customer verticals and global populations, not the exact U.S. service industry or lower-middle-market subset.
- rho: Reported use does not measure depth, production reliability, or realized labor substitution.
- e: The product taxonomy establishes what can be sold but does not disclose the industry's revenue weights.
- e: The injected firm-count estimate of 78 depends on SUSB size classes and a 15.65% margin bridge; uncertainty in that denominator does not provide direct evidence for this primitive.
- s5: Succession intentions are general small-business evidence and are not completed transactions.
- s5: One consolidator's activity demonstrates buyer appetite but cannot establish an industry-wide transfer rate.
- s5: The injected estimate of 78 firms is itself model-derived from SUSB size classes and a 15.65% margin assumption, so both the count and the available-firm implication are uncertain.
- q: No observed exact-industry billing-model or post-automation margin distribution was found.
- q: The official product list identifies offerings, not their revenue shares or contractual pricing terms.
- d5: The BLS output projection is for four-digit NAICS 5413, not six-digit NAICS 541360.
- d5: Customer digital-spending growth is not equivalent to demand growth for independent geophysical service providers.
- d5: Commodity prices and exploration budgets can create outcomes outside the stated range.
- o: The BLS surveying-technician evidence is adjacent to, rather than identical with, geophysical surveying.
- o: Remote sensing, autonomous instruments, and standardized processing could lower operator dependence faster than assumed.
