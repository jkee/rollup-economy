# 541430 — Graphic Design Services

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `541430-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** HIGHEST_PRIORITY · A 7.78 · L 6.53 · interval CONDITIONAL → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Digitally native creative production exposes a large share of design and adaptation work to AI embedded directly in existing software.
**Weakness:** Low switching barriers and improving self-service tools make it difficult to retain productivity gains or preserve external-firm demand for commodity design.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US graphic-design firms in the $1-10M normalized EBITDA band that repeatedly provide outsourced visual-communication planning, brand identity, layout, illustration, production design, packaging, digital content, and related design management to external customers.
- Excluded: Captive in-house creative departments, shell entities, non-control financing vehicles, standalone advertising agencies classified elsewhere, software-only products, individual art sales without an outsourced service relationship, and practices whose client relationships or delivery capability are not transferable.
- Customer and revenue model: Business and institutional customers buy discrete projects, fixed-scope campaigns and brand systems, hourly production, or ongoing retainers for recurring creative needs; repeat demand is generated by new campaigns, channels, products, variants, and brand maintenance rather than a mandatory subscription.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Graphic design has a large implementable AI surface because its core production is already digital and generative tools are native to incumbent workflows. The economic challenge is not technical adoption but protecting revenue as customers expect faster, cheaper, and more abundant output and increasingly self-serve commodity work. Durable value is concentrated in client interpretation, distinctive creative direction, brand governance, rights review, and reliable multi-channel production.

## How AI changes the work
AI accelerates ideation, first drafts, image generation and editing, layout alternatives, resizing, copy support, proofing, and repetitive adaptation. Designers shift toward briefing, curation, comparison, refinement, brand consistency, client collaboration, and production judgment. Because current professional tools still struggle with complex layouts, typography, structured vector work, and consistent production-ready outputs, human review and craft remain necessary for demanding work.

## Value capture
Fixed-fee projects and fixed retainers can retain early productivity gains, while hourly work exposes savings directly through fewer billed hours. Over five years, competition, self-service tools, and client demands for more variants and shorter cycles should transfer a large share of benefits to buyers. Firms with specialized brand knowledge, controlled scope, and outcome-oriented pricing should retain more than undifferentiated production shops.

## Firm availability
The frozen dataset estimates 370 firms in the EBITDA band. Many should have repeat business clients and team-based delivery, but founder reputation, creative style, client concentration, and mixed-agency classification reduce true eligibility. Digital-agency leaders show substantial openness to selling and the wider marketing-services market is consolidating, although expressed interest is much higher than completed qualifying transfers.

## Demand durability
Demand for visual communication should be supported by more digital channels, faster campaigns, personalization, and continuing brand competition. That output growth does not guarantee demand for an external graphic-design firm: simple assets and adaptations are increasingly generated in-house or through software. Complex brand systems, packaging, production standards, and high-stakes campaigns retain a stronger operator need than commodity social and presentation graphics.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main risks are rapid commoditization, self-service substitution, downward fee resets, scope expansion that consumes productivity gains, copyright and provenance disputes, inconsistent AI output, and the loss of clients after a founder exits. The largest evidence gaps are wage-weighted task time, AI savings net of rework, pure-play graphic-design pricing, and completed LMM control-transfer incidence.

## Caveats
- a: There is no direct wage-weighted task-exposure measure for this six-digit industry.
- a: Adobe samples span graphic design, illustration, photography, video, and other creative roles rather than only LMM graphic-design firms.
- a: The frozen compensation ratio uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and a harmonization adjustment; that vintage mismatch may distort the labor base even though it is not re-estimated here.
- rho: Tool use and faster output do not prove durable labor substitution or avoided hiring.
- rho: Digital agencies include development and marketing firms whose workflows differ from pure graphic design.
- rho: Commercial-use rights, client restrictions, training, and inconsistent outputs may slow implementation.
- e: The frozen n of 370 is already a margin-bridged estimate and is not re-estimated here.
- e: NAICS self-classification may include mixed creative or marketing agencies.
- e: No fetched source directly measures repeat revenue or transferability among EBITDA-band graphic-design firms.
- s5: Openness to selling is not observed deal incidence and the survey population is broader than graphic design.
- s5: Marketing-services M&A includes advertising, martech, digital marketing, and large transactions outside the lens.
- s5: Founder-dependent firms may close or lose clients rather than complete a transferable sale.
- q: Pricing evidence covers digital agencies broadly rather than only NAICS 541430.
- q: Fixed-fee projects retain savings only if scope and revision demands remain controlled.
- q: Demand volume and self-service are reflected in d5 and o, and implementation friction is reflected in rho, not double-counted here.
- d5: Employment is an input proxy and embeds productivity and outsourcing shifts rather than directly measuring output quantity.
- d5: Adobe's content-demand evidence spans marketing and creative work beyond the current NAICS service basket.
- d5: Print decline, recessions, in-housing, and AI-generated asset proliferation can move different submarkets in opposite directions.
- o: Graphic design has no licensure or statutory accountability barrier comparable with regulated professions.
- o: Creator preference for a human final decision is not proof that customers will continue buying an external firm.
- o: Operator-required share should be higher for brand systems and packaging than for routine social, presentation, and promotional assets.
