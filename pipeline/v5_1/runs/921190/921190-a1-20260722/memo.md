# 921190 — Other General Government Support

**Driver:** Document-heavy recurring government administration gives AI a credible role in avoiding hiring and improving throughput while statutory demand persists.
**Weakness:** NAICS 921190 is structurally a government-establishment category, leaving no verified transferable LMM firm population or commercial retention model.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Any establishment correctly classified in NAICS 921190 that provides recurring general-government support, including government personnel services, election boards, civil-rights or civil-service commissions, general-services departments, and government supply agencies, and that also satisfies the default external-customer and LMM control-investment requirements.
- Excluded: Government establishments that do not sell recurring services to external customers; private HR, election-technology, consulting, procurement, staffing, and facilities-support vendors classified outside NAICS 921190; captive internal units; financing vehicles; and operations outside the roughly $1-10 million normalized EBITDA band.
- Customer and revenue model: The official industry is composed of government establishments that primarily support government and generally does not have a commercial external-customer revenue model. Any qualifying fringe case would need recurring arm's-length fees from external government customers, but no defensible population of such firms was identified.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
There is meaningful AI-assistable administrative work inside 921190, but the industry definition creates a fundamental mismatch with a private-firm roll-up screen. Census places government personnel offices, election boards, commissions, general-services departments, and supply agencies here; these are government establishments, not transferable LMM firms serving external customers. The operating-improvement thesis may matter to public-sector modernization, yet the investable population is unverified and likely empty.

## How AI changes the work
AI can support correspondence drafting, policy and record search, classification, procurement-document preparation, HR case triage, scheduling, reporting, and program-status tracking. Sensitive records, due process, cybersecurity, auditability, public-records duties, and accountable statutory decisions constrain substitution. GAO's rapid growth in reported use cases supports applicability, while its documented policy and resource obstacles support a slower implementation fraction than raw technical exposure.

## Value capture
Commercial retention cannot be estimated for this code because the official establishments generally operate through appropriations and internal government budgets rather than arm's-length recurring customer contracts. Efficiency may appear as avoided hiring, redeployed staff, faster service, or lower appropriations, but those outcomes are not equivalent to margin retained by an acquired operator. Importing contract economics from adjacent private vendors would change the industry being screened.

## Firm availability
The firm-availability case is the central problem. The official definition specifies government establishments, and the frozen firm-count input is missing. Rare directories or self-reported records bearing 921190 would require manual confirmation because coding anomalies, public authorities, and quasi-government entities need not have transferable equity, external recurring revenue, or LMM EBITDA. No qualifying control-transfer probability is defensible until a real eligible cohort exists.

## Demand durability
The underlying service need is durable: governments will continue to employ people, run elections, enforce civil-service and civil-rights rules, purchase supplies, and coordinate shared services. Broad BLS projections point to nearly flat government employment over a decade, while recent real government spending and population data allow modest five-year growth. Workflow volume can shift to self-service or software, but formal authority and accountability should keep most of the service basket operator-required.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainty is not AI performance but category validity: the code may contain no firms that satisfy the acquisition lens. Other risks include public-sector budget cuts, long procurement cycles, fragmented legacy systems, data-quality problems, privacy and cybersecurity rules, union or civil-service constraints, election-security requirements, and the loss of institutional knowledge during workforce reductions. This industry would be a bad roll-up candidate if registry-level diligence confirms that every correctly classified establishment is governmental or nontransferable.

## Caveats
- a: No published occupation mix specific to NAICS 921190 was located, so this is not an observed wage-weighted share.
- a: Federal AI inventories do not represent state and local election boards, commissions, personnel offices, or supply agencies.
- a: The estimate concerns government work even though the default commercial-firm lens appears nearly empty.
- rho: Reported use cases are not equivalent to scaled production deployments or realized labor savings.
- rho: Federal evidence may overstate capability and understate fragmentation among smaller state and local governments.
- rho: Implementation in election, personnel, and civil-rights workflows can be delayed by due-process, records, cybersecurity, accessibility, and privacy requirements.
- e: The frozen dataset provides no defensible LMM firm-count band, so the denominator for an empirical eligibility rate is absent.
- e: Directory listings can contain self-assigned or stale NAICS codes and should not be treated as proof of industry eligibility.
- e: This is a structural classification judgment, not an observed census of every entity using the code.
- s5: Treating elections, appointments, agency reorganizations, or outsourcing awards as firm control transfers would violate the primitive definition.
- s5: The frozen LMM firm-count input is missing.
- q: Internal government budget retention or appropriations are not equivalent to commercial benefit retention.
- q: Adjacent private government contractors belong to other NAICS industries and cannot be imported into this record merely to supply pricing evidence.
- d5: Government employment and aggregate spending are broad inputs rather than direct measures of this six-digit service basket.
- d5: Policy changes, fiscal stress, federal workforce reductions, election cycles, and intergovernmental shifts can move workloads sharply without changing underlying public need.
- d5: Recent spending growth includes investment and functions outside general government support.
- o: The boundary between operator-required quantity and automated workflow steps is not reported consistently by agencies.
- o: The estimate applies to government operators performing the current service basket, not to a proven population of acquisitive commercial firms.
- o: Future legislation could either mandate human review or authorize more end-to-end automated decisions.
