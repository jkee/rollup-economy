# 336390 — Other Motor Vehicle Parts Manufacturing

**Driver:** Required safety and increasingly important thermal-management content provides durable recurring vehicle-program demand plus addressable quality and plant-support workflows.
**Weakness:** The residual code's extreme product heterogeneity makes a single industry estimate fragile, and combustion-specific components face secular displacement even as customer price-downs absorb much of the AI upside.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market independent OEM and Tier automotive suppliers making recurring-program components captured by the residual 336390 category, including thermal and HVAC systems, fluid and air-management parts, filters, airbags and restraint components, wheels, frames, bumpers, radiators, fuel tanks, exhaust-system parts, and related assemblies.
- Excluded: Captive OEM operations, global integrated megasuppliers, aftermarket brands and distributors, remanufacturers, prototype-only shops, businesses primarily classified in another named motor-vehicle-parts category, and firms without repeat production-program revenue.
- Customer and revenue model: Suppliers earn per-part revenue under OEM or Tier customer platform awards and production releases, often with separate or negotiated tooling, engineering, testing, and launch economics. Awards can last a vehicle program but quantities are not guaranteed; customer quality, traceability, warranty, delivery, and annual productivity expectations govern the relationship.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed to independent recurring-production OEM and Tier suppliers because this residual code spans dissimilar products, aftermarket and rebuilding activities, captive and global operations, and suppliers whose AI exposure and demand outlook—especially between combustion-specific and powertrain-neutral parts—are not economically comparable.

## Executive view
336390 contains a real LMM recurring-supplier opportunity, but it is among the least internally coherent automotive manufacturing codes. AI can improve inspection, testing, planning, maintenance, quality documentation, and engineering support across the category; physical conversion and safety validation remain dominant. The investment result depends heavily on product selection because safety and thermal content is durable or growing while exhaust, fuel-tank, and engine-filter exposure faces electrification pressure.

## How AI changes the work
The common AI layer is plant support rather than a single production technology: vision inspection, anomaly detection, test analytics, predictive maintenance, schedule and inventory optimization, quotation, traceability, root-cause analysis, and quality-document preparation. Addressability is bounded by highly varied physical processes and must exclude conventional automation already embedded in molding, forming, sewing, assembly, and test equipment.

## Value capture
OEM and Tier customers typically hold substantial bargaining power, and annual productivity expectations erode visible gains over a vehicle program. Suppliers can retain more value when improvements reduce hidden scrap, warranty risk, downtime, or launch failure, or when proprietary safety technology and scarce qualifications limit alternatives. The base nevertheless assumes most gross AI benefit is shared over five years.

## Firm availability
The frozen broad-code count overstates the coherent target universe because Census explicitly places many unlike parts in this residual category. Large transactions in thermal and fluid management prove strategic transferability, but they are poor scale matches for US LMM assets. Family ownership, product obsolescence, customer concentration, warranty exposure, and capital needs make attractive independent suppliers only moderately available.

## Demand durability
Demand is bifurcated. Airbags, restraints, frames, bumpers, thermal systems, and other powertrain-neutral parts remain essential, and EV battery thermal management can add content. Fuel tanks, exhaust parts, and some engine filters decline with electrification. Flat broad vehicle production plus changing content yields modest aggregate real growth but a much wider supplier-level outcome range.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainty is composition: eligibility, AI exposure, demand, and outside replacement all change by product family. Other risks include program concentration, launch and warranty failures, annual price-downs, raw-material and tariff volatility, customer insourcing, legacy plant data, safety validation, environmental liabilities, electrification timing, and the absence of a disclosed LMM transaction denominator.

## Caveats
- a: A single addressability estimate masks very different airbag, thermal, exhaust, wheel, and fluid-component processes.
- a: Published AI cases skew toward larger and better-instrumented plants.
- a: The range measures current technical exposure, not realized labor elimination.
- rho: Realization varies substantially by product family and installed equipment.
- rho: Safety-critical decisions may retain human review even after successful AI deployment.
- rho: Weak MES, sensor, and defect-label data are common adoption bottlenecks.
- e: The residual nature of the code creates unusually high classification heterogeneity.
- e: Establishment counts do not map cleanly to firms or acquisition platforms.
- e: A supplier may mix eligible OEM production with aftermarket, rebuilding, or non-automotive work.
- s5: Both observed precedents are substantially above the LMM target scale.
- s5: Veritas was a selected-business acquisition rather than a simple whole-company sale.
- s5: Combustion-specific and powertrain-neutral suppliers likely have different buyer pools.
- q: Lear is much larger and more diversified than the target lens.
- q: Retention varies by customer, product criticality, patent position, and capacity scarcity.
- q: Input-cost pass-throughs can obscure underlying productivity sharing.
- d5: The residual code combines product families with opposite powertrain transitions.
- d5: A single program award or loss can dominate an LMM supplier's result.
- d5: Tariffs and localization may move production among plants without changing end demand.
- o: The low case is concentrated in combustion-specific suppliers rather than evenly spread across the category.
- o: This primitive excludes ordinary unit-cycle demand already represented in d5.
- o: New regulation can add safety or emissions content even as other parts disappear.
