# 488390 — Other Support Activities for Water Transportation

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `488390-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.33 · L 2.70 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Document- and evidence-heavy inspection workflows can be accelerated while qualified independent accountability remains valuable.
**Weakness:** The 488390 firm denominator mixes incompatible business models, and the eligible survey-firm share is not observed.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent, repeat marine cargo checking, quantity and condition surveying, loading observation, hold-readiness inspection, cargo-damage documentation, and related compliance certificates for shipowners, charterers, cargo interests, insurers, and government shippers.
- Excluded: Floating drydock operation, ship dismantling, ship scaling and physical hull maintenance, shipyard repair, captive carrier or insurer inspection teams, classification-society statutory surveys outside this NAICS activity, shells, and firms outside the roughly $1-10M normalized EBITDA band.
- Customer and revenue model: Customers buy per-call, per-shipment, per-day, or master-service-agreement inspection and survey work, often around loading, discharge, loss prevention, claims, quantity verification, or regulatory evidence; repeat port and commodity relationships provide recurring demand even when individual assignments are episodic.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 488390 combines asset-heavy floating drydocks and physical ship scaling with professional marine cargo checking and surveying. Those models have fundamentally different occupation mixes, capital intensity, contracting, and substitution paths, so the lens retains the coherent independent cargo-inspection and survey segment rather than blending unlike businesses.

## Executive view
Independent marine cargo survey and checking firms are a report-intensive, safety- and evidence-driven segment within a much broader residual NAICS code. AI can compress document review, calculations, photo handling, reporting, and coordination, while qualified human observation and accountability remain important. The opportunity is therefore an augmented-inspector model, not unattended certification. Firm availability is the most uncertain part because public data do not separate this segment from floating drydocks and ship scaling.

## How AI changes the work
AI can ingest bills of lading, stowage plans, prior surveys, stability calculations, photos, and sensor readings; flag inconsistencies; draft structured reports; index evidence; translate communications; and automate scheduling and invoicing. Drones, crawlers, and remote video can reduce hazardous access and travel. Inspectors still need to witness loading, measure quantities and conditions, preserve evidentiary integrity, resolve exceptions, and sign accountable conclusions.

## Value capture
Per-job and fixed-fee work can retain gains through faster turnaround and higher inspector utilization, especially when the firm wins on responsiveness and report quality. Retention weakens at competitive rebid, under transparent day rates, or when a concentrated insurer, government shipper, or commodity customer demands savings. A proprietary evidence workflow and consistent multi-port service matter more than access to a generic model.

## Firm availability
The narrowed segment likely represents less than half of modeled LMM firms in this residual code, but there is no definitive roster. Survey firms may be transferable when they have multiple credentialed inspectors, repeat customers, standardized quality systems, and brand equity; single-principal practices may simply wind down. General small-business data show retirement-driven sale intent, but no marine-surveyor transfer series exists.

## Demand durability
Demand should track bulk and breakbulk port calls, regulated cargoes, claims, and the need for independent evidence more than aggregate tonnage. Safety and certification frameworks continue to require qualified judgment, while remote capture changes delivery rather than eliminating most assignments. Downside comes from trade contraction, containerization, larger vessels with fewer calls, customer insourcing, or classification bodies absorbing adjacent work.

## Risks and uncertainty
The main risks are code heterogeneity, owner and credential dependence, sparse six-digit occupation data, customer and port concentration, exposure to commodity cycles, liability for erroneous reports, cyber and evidence-integrity failures, and rapid remote-survey adoption by larger recognized organizations. A poor target would be a founder-led practice whose clients and signing authority do not survive the founder's exit.

## Caveats
- a: The BLS industry proxy includes port operations, cargo handling, navigational services, drydock work, and other occupations outside this lens.
- a: O*NET describes transportation inspectors across modes and does not report wage-weighted time by task.
- a: Self-employed surveyors are excluded from OEWS, and small firms may have owners performing both field and administrative work.
- rho: Remote class surveys are adjacent to, but not identical with, independent cargo condition and quantity surveys.
- rho: Observed technology availability does not measure implementation share in U.S. LMM 488390 firms.
- rho: High-consequence or disputed cargo assignments may require conservative review and physical attendance.
- e: No public source reports the share of 488390 LMM firms by cargo surveying, floating drydock, scaling, or other activity.
- e: The 38-firm count is a frozen model estimate and may combine establishments belonging to larger firms.
- e: Delegated survey and certification work may sit in nonprofit or recognized-organization structures unavailable for control acquisition.
- s5: Neither proxy isolates NAICS 488390, marine surveyors, or the $1-10M EBITDA band.
- s5: A retiring principal may close a personal practice or sell a client list without transferring an operating firm.
- s5: Reported marketplace deals skew smaller and exclude many private strategic acquisitions.
- q: No source directly measures AI-benefit pass-through in marine cargo surveying.
- q: P&I, government, commodity, and private shipper assignments have different pricing power and procurement intensity.
- q: Faster work can create extra capacity that is valuable only if local assignment demand is available.
- d5: Water-transportation employment is not a direct measure of survey assignments.
- d5: Containerized cargo may require less independent checking than bulk, project, food-aid, or disputed cargo.
- d5: Trade policy, commodity cycles, larger vessels, and fewer port calls can make national tonnage misleading.
- o: The lens includes voluntary commercial surveys as well as compliance-adjacent certificates, so operator requirements vary.
- o: Remote inspection can preserve the accountable firm while reducing local field labor, which belongs in exposure and implementation rather than demand elimination.
- o: Classification-society surveys are adjacent and may compete with independent cargo surveyors rather than support them.
