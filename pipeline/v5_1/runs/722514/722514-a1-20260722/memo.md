# 722514 — Cafeterias, Grill Buffets, and Buffets

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `722514-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 6.42 · L 1.69 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Repeat meal demand plus packaged digital tools creates a credible efficiency opportunity in transaction, planning, and administrative workflows.
**Weakness:** Most work is physical and safety-critical, while qualifying firm transfers and durable margin retention are only indirectly evidenced.

## Business-model lens
- Included: U.S. lower-middle-market operators of cafeterias, grill buffets, and buffets that repeatedly prepare and sell meals to external patrons using cafeteria lines, buffet stations, display grills, and related self-service equipment.
- Excluded: Captive internal food operations, shells, non-control financing vehicles, food-service contractors classified outside 722514, full-service restaurants, conventional limited-service restaurants, and snack or nonalcoholic beverage bars.
- Customer and revenue model: Mostly repeat but noncontractual sales of prepared meals to individual patrons at posted prices, with some group, institutional-location, or event-related traffic; revenue depends on visits, check size, throughput, and local competitive positioning.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
This is a physical food-production and hospitality format with a real but bounded digital labor opportunity. The most credible gains sit around ordering, payment, scheduling, purchasing, forecasting, training, and administrative control, while cooking, buffet replenishment, cleaning, food safety, and exception handling remain operator-led. Repeat patron demand supports continuity, but local price competition and a thin population of transferable firms limit how much benefit persists and how quickly a scaled portfolio can be assembled.

## How AI changes the work
AI can improve demand forecasts, labor schedules, purchasing, waste control, menu analysis, marketing, training, compliance documentation, and guest communications. Kiosks and electronic payment already remove parts of the transaction workflow at many 722514 establishments. The remaining task base is less digitally substitutable because food must be prepared, held at safe temperatures, replenished, cleaned up, and adapted to real-time customer and kitchen exceptions.

## Value capture
Posted prices and spot transactions let an operator retain savings initially without a contractual rebate. Retention erodes as competitors adopt similar tools, customers respond to price and promotions, and operators reinvest savings in service, portion value, or staffing. The central uncertainty is not whether efficiency is possible, but how much remains in margin after several menu and competitive cycles.

## Firm availability
The format is generally eligible because it repeatedly sells prepared meals to outside patrons, although captive, owner-dependent, or nontransferable operations should be excluded. Broad owner-exit intent and an active restaurant resale market support control-transfer opportunities, but restaurant owners are younger than private owners overall and reported marketplace sales skew far below the target earnings band.

## Demand durability
Prepared-meal demand appears stable to modestly growing, with BLS specifically expecting more meals from cafeterias in healthcare and residential-care settings. Buffet concepts still face traffic cyclicality, price sensitivity, format shifts, and competition from takeout, grocery-prepared food, and conventional limited-service restaurants. Even when ordering becomes self-service, accountable operation remains necessary for physical production and food safety.

## Risks and uncertainty
The narrow code lacks direct occupation, transfer, and real-output series, so several conclusions bridge from broader restaurant evidence. The largest risks are overstating labor removal from tools that merely shift bottlenecks, assuming intended technology spending becomes realized savings, relying on owner exit intent that does not become a qualifying sale, and underestimating local price competition. Food-safety incidents, wage regulation, commodity volatility, leases, and franchise or location agreements can dominate the modeled AI benefit.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS publishes the usable occupation mix at broader NAICS 722500 rather than 722514.
- a: Electronic ordering or payment is not the same as AI task substitution and may leave labor in guest assistance or exception handling.
- a: The frozen compensation-to-receipts input uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and a cross-code harmonization, so it may not perfectly represent the current operating model.
- rho: The Census measure combines several forms of electronic ordering and payment and does not isolate AI.
- rho: The association survey covers restaurants broadly, while Deloitte surveyed 375 executives across 11 countries and is tilted toward brands and operating groups larger than the target firms.
- rho: Investment intention does not establish successful implementation or labor removal.
- e: No source directly enumerates eligibility within the frozen EBITDA-band population.
- e: NAICS is assigned at the establishment level, while the primitive is firm-level and some firms span multiple restaurant formats.
- e: The frozen firm count is itself an estimate bridged from receipts and a sector EBITDA margin.
- s5: The exit survey is cross-industry and may overrepresent owners already engaged with exit-planning professionals.
- s5: Restaurant owner age data cover the broad restaurant industry, not 722514 or the target size band.
- s5: BizBuySell covers reported marketplace transactions and its median restaurant is materially smaller than the frozen lens.
- q: The Federal Reserve pass-through estimate is cross-industry and primarily concerns cost increases, not AI-driven cost reductions.
- q: The restaurant pricing evidence is from an Ohio independent-restaurant survey and is not specific to buffets or cafeterias.
- q: Retention will vary materially with local concentration, brand strength, franchise restrictions, and whether savings improve service rather than reduce cost.
- d5: The primary source projects occupations across multiple food-service settings rather than output for NAICS 722514.
- d5: Employment growth embeds productivity and occupational-mix changes and therefore is not a direct measure of real meal quantity.
- d5: The base extrapolates a ten-year national projection to five years and may miss near-term cycles.
- o: The FDA Food Code is a model adopted and modified by jurisdictions, not a single directly binding national restaurant rule.
- o: The kiosk labor study uses Korean restaurants and may not transfer to U.S. wages, service expectations, or regulation.
- o: Operator-required demand is distinct from labor intensity: a highly automated location may still require an accountable operator.
