# 311340 — Nonchocolate Confectionery Manufacturing

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `311340-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** STRUCTURAL_OUT · A 6.07 · L 0.86 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → LOW_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** A real and recurring contract-manufacturing niche benefits from gummy-led demand, innovation cadence, and information-heavy customer and quality workflows.
**Weakness:** Eligibility and contract economics are poorly enumerated, and much factory labor remains physical while sophisticated customers can reclaim savings at renewal.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent US lower-middle-market contract and private-label manufacturers repeatedly formulating, producing, and packaging nonchocolate confections such as gummies, hard candy, mints, chews, caramels, lozenges, and taffy for external brands and retailers.
- Excluded: Manufacturers primarily selling their own brands, retail candy shops making on premises, supplement-only plants whose primary classification is outside 311340, captive facilities, pure co-packers without confectionery manufacturing, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Recurring product-development, batch-conversion, and packaging revenue from brands and retailers, generally priced per unit, case, or production run under purchase orders, fixed-term supply agreements, or periodically rebid private-label programs.
- Deviation from default lens: The NAICS code includes branded manufacturers, private-label producers, contract manufacturers, and on-premises makers. It is narrowed to contract and private-label manufacturing because only that subset supplies a recurring outsourced service to external customers.

## Executive view
Nonchocolate confectionery contains a visible contract and private-label manufacturing niche with recurring customer production, a sizable estimated firm base, and category demand supported by gummies and innovation. AI opportunity is concentrated in planning, quality, customer, and process-information workflows rather than the core physical craft.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate quoting, formulation search, demand forecasting, scheduling, specification and artwork review, QA documentation, customer service, machine vision, predictive maintenance, and root-cause analysis. Cooking, depositing, curing, coating, sanitation, changeovers, packaging interventions, and maintenance remain substantially physical.

## Value capture
Formulation know-how, validated facilities, tooling, audit history, and scarce seasonal capacity support retention. Per-unit bidding, customer-owned recipes, cost transparency, concentrated buyers, and renewal competition should return a meaningful share of gains to brands and retailers over five years.

## Firm availability
Supplier directories and operating company sites confirm many contract and private-label offerings, but they also mix chocolate, supplements, packaging, and foreign capacity. A firm-by-firm screen is necessary before applying the 120-firm frozen estimate to the target lens.

## Demand durability
Nonchocolate candy has recently outgrown chocolate in dollar terms and gained seasonal volume share, aided by gummies, novelty, and relative affordability. Constant-price quantity growth should be more modest, while physical outsourced production remains strongly operator-required.

## Risks and uncertainty
Key risks are uncertain eligible share, customer concentration, private-label rebids, food-safety and allergen failures, seasonal working capital, formula and trend obsolescence, sugar and ingredient volatility, imported competition, and overestimating labor savings in manual small-batch plants.

## Caveats
- a: The occupation source is four-digit NAICS and includes chocolate and sugar manufacturing.
- a: Small-batch and artisan operations may have substantially more manual work than scaled gummy or hard-candy lines.
- a: Existing recipe, depositing, wrapping, inspection, and warehouse automation is unknown by firm.
- rho: Survey use may include pilots or individual productivity tools with no labor substitution.
- rho: Gummy, hard-candy, panning, and lozenge processes have different technical constraints.
- rho: Food and supplement gummies may face different quality and regulatory systems.
- e: Supplier directories are promotional, may duplicate facilities, and include chocolate or nutraceutical firms outside 311340.
- e: Retail private-label share understates branded products made by third-party contract manufacturers.
- e: The frozen n estimate is margin-bridged and does not enumerate actual firms.
- s5: No source directly measures qualifying transfers for the lens.
- s5: Owner intentions do not equal closed control transactions.
- s5: Supplement-gummy deals may be classified outside 311340.
- q: The contract evidence is from adjacent food manufacturing rather than candy.
- q: Seasonal capacity scarcity and customer power vary sharply by product and run size.
- q: Demand-volume effects are excluded and captured in d5 and o.
- d5: Published growth is primarily dollar based and includes price and mix.
- d5: Halloween share shifts may reflect cocoa inflation rather than durable preference.
- d5: The current outsourced-service basket may grow faster or slower than branded retail sales.
- o: Regulation ensures an accountable facility, not survival of a particular operator.
- o: Supplement gummies may shift to differently regulated or classified plants.
- o: Automation can reduce staffing without eliminating the operator entity.
