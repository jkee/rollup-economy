## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent US merchant wholesalers in the EBITDA band that repeatedly source, hold, sell, and coordinate delivery of miscellaneous durable goods for external business customers, including recurring account service and order administration.
- Excluded: Consumer-only resellers, digital-media businesses with no physical distribution role, captive manufacturer distribution, shells, commission-only agents, financing vehicles, and firms whose economics are principally one-off liquidation or commodity speculation.
- Customer and revenue model: Revenue is principally product resale to business accounts; the recurring outsourced service is supplier aggregation, availability, ordering, inventory coordination, and delivery support, monetized through gross margin rather than a separately billed fee.
- Deviation from default lens: The code is a residual collection of unrelated durable products, so the lens retains repeat B2B merchant-distribution firms with a common inventory-and-account-service model and excludes one-off or nonphysical models to keep the screen coherent.

## Executive view
The coherent opportunity is the repeat B2B distributor, not every establishment in this catch-all code. Its administrative workflows offer a meaningful but bounded labor opportunity, while physical handling, commercial judgment, and customer relationships keep the accountable operator central. The largest uncertainty is not whether AI can draft and classify documents, but whether a fragmented small wholesaler can integrate those capabilities into reliable order-to-cash work and retain the resulting economics.

## How AI changes the work
AI can structure inbound orders, retrieve product information, draft quotes and customer replies, flag replenishment and invoice exceptions, summarize account history, and support sales preparation. Humans remain responsible for ambiguous specifications, supplier negotiation, credit, key-account trust, inventory commitments, warehouse work, driving, and service recovery. Implementation therefore looks like targeted workflow automation and avoided administrative hiring rather than autonomous distribution.

## Value capture
Benefits embedded in faster response, fewer errors, and better availability are less visible and potentially more retainable than an explicit labor surcharge. Pure cost reduction is vulnerable to negotiated discounts and competitive imitation, especially for transparent products. Durable capture depends on turning lower service cost into a differentiated response-time and reliability advantage while maintaining commercial discipline.

## Firm availability
The supplied firm count indicates a sizable potential population, but eligibility is reduced by the residual code's mixed models and by firms dependent on a single owner, customer, supplier, or fading product line. Public marketplace data demonstrate that durable-goods distributors do transfer, yet do not establish the probability for this exact band. Broker and lending records are the next practical route to a denominator-based transfer estimate.

## Demand durability
Underlying demand appears roughly stable rather than structurally high growth. Physical-product procurement, inventory coordination, and delivery remain necessary, but standardized categories face manufacturer-direct, marketplace, and customer self-service substitution. The strongest operators will likely retain an accountable role where assortment, availability, technical ambiguity, local stock, or exception resolution matters.

## Risks and uncertainty
The central risks are residual-code heterogeneity, poor item and customer data, thin integration capacity, cyber and hallucination errors entering orders or quotes, customer and supplier concentration, inventory obsolescence, direct-channel bypass, and aggressive price competition. Public evidence is much broader than six digits, so occupation, eligibility, transfer, pass-through, and operator-requirement judgments need primary diligence before capital is committed.

## Caveats
- a: Public occupation evidence is broader than this residual six-digit code.
- a: Anthropic usage is platform-specific and is not a measure of removable payroll.
- a: The frozen compensation-to-receipts input mixes 2024 wages with 2022 receipts and is harmonized to the IPS basis.
- rho: The estimate assumes disciplined workflow redesign rather than stand-alone chatbot deployment.
- rho: Small firms may lack clean item masters and integration staff.
- rho: Observed historical technology adoption predates the full capability expected within the horizon.
- e: The Census industry definition measures establishments, while the frozen opportunity count is firm based.
- e: The frozen count is an estimate derived from size-class receipts and a broad distributor margin bridge.
- e: Public sources do not quantify the share with genuinely recurring B2B accounts.
- s5: Marketplace sales are voluntary reports and skew toward brokered small businesses.
- s5: Owner-age evidence covers small businesses broadly, not this industry.
- s5: The proxy cannot distinguish every qualifying control transfer from asset-only sales.
- q: No public source directly measures pass-through of AI savings in this industry.
- q: Product and customer concentration can dominate bargaining power.
- q: The estimate excludes volume effects, which belong in demand primitives.
- d5: Employment is not demand and may diverge with productivity.
- d5: The BLS detailed series is at the four-digit residual group, not six digits.
- d5: Constant-quality adjustment is unavailable for the heterogeneous product basket.
- o: The code mixes products with very different direct-to-customer feasibility.
- o: E-commerce can either bypass the wholesaler or be operated by the wholesaler.
- o: The estimate addresses operator requirement, not headcount intensity.
