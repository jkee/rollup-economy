# 711110 — Theater Companies and Dinner Theaters

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `711110-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 5.79 · L 1.85 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** AI can compress a meaningful layer of marketing, fundraising, administration and pre-production while the live experience remains differentiated and operator-led.
**Weakness:** Only a small, uncertain share of theater companies fits a transferable recurring outsourced-service lens, and national demand remains fragile outside premium markets.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market theater and dinner-theater companies that repeatedly supply live theatrical productions or integrated dinner-and-show experiences to external audiences, presenters, venues, institutions, or event buyers, with transferable operations, rights, customer channels and production capability.
- Excluded: Nonprofit or community organizations without transferable control economics, one-off special-purpose production entities, independent performers classified elsewhere, venue landlords without a theater-company operation, businesses centered on film exhibition or concerts, shells, captive units, and companies whose artistic identity or rights cannot survive a control transfer.
- Customer and revenue model: Revenue is commonly earned from single tickets, subscriptions, contracted performances, venue or presenter guarantees, education and community programs, food and beverage at dinner theaters, sponsorship, donations and grants. The qualifying lens emphasizes repeat paid productions and transferable customer relationships rather than temporary show-level investment vehicles.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Theater companies have a real but bounded AI labor opportunity concentrated in administration, marketing, fundraising, research and pre-production. The live product itself is embodied, collaborative and rights-intensive. More importantly, the default outsourced-service lens fits only a minority of the industry because many entities are nonprofits, direct-to-consumer venues or temporary production vehicles rather than transferable repeat-service firms.

## How AI changes the work
AI can support campaign creation, audience segmentation, donor and grant communications, budgeting, scheduling, customer service, translation, research, concept art and first-pass production planning. It is far less able to replace acting, rehearsal, direction, live technical execution, hospitality, construction, safety and audience management. Performer-consent, digital-replica, copyright, authorship and union constraints add review and negotiation costs around creative uses.

## Value capture
Ticket, subscription and contracted-performance prices let operators retain some within-season savings, but benefits can be reinvested in production quality, audience growth and mission activity rather than held as margin. Competitive entertainment markets, union minimums, royalties and renewal negotiations erode five-year retention. Commercial firms with distinctive programming, owned audience relationships and disciplined capacity pricing have more capture potential than grant-dependent nonprofits.

## Firm availability
Marketplace evidence confirms that some theater and entertainment venues transfer, but the observed businesses are small and the category is contaminated by clubs. Eligible LMM theater platforms are scarce after excluding nonprofits without control economics, property-only deals, show vehicles and key-person-dependent artistic organizations. Rights portability, leases, real estate, donor restrictions, unions and the continuity of artistic leadership are central diligence issues.

## Demand durability
Demand evidence is polarized. Broadway attendance recovered to near-record levels by 2024-2025, while national nonprofit-theater real ticket income and attendance remained below 2019 and the pandemic-affected 2022 NEA survey showed steep declines in stage-play participation. BLS nevertheless projects slight employment growth through 2034. A flat central five-year demand case with a wide downside and upside range is more defensible than extrapolating either Broadway's rebound or regional distress.

## Risks and uncertainty
Principal risks are weak default-lens fit, nonprofit and show-entity contamination in the firm count, volatile discretionary demand, high fixed production costs, short hit windows, union and rights constraints, real-estate entanglement and artistic key-person dependence. Evidence on AI savings, recurring contracted revenue and control transfers is indirect. The dataset labor ratio has a 2024-wage/2022-receipts mismatch, and the 117-firm count is a recreation-margin estimate rather than observed EBITDA-band firms.

## Caveats
- a: The BLS matrix is employment-weighted, not wage-weighted, and includes both theater companies and dinner theaters with very different occupation mixes.
- a: O*NET producer/director coverage includes film, television and radio as well as stage work.
- a: The dataset labor ratio combines 2024 wages with 2022 receipts and is harmonized to the IPS basis, so vintage and normalization uncertainty remain.
- rho: No source measures five-year AI implementation in NAICS 711110.
- rho: Broadway, regional nonprofit, touring and dinner-theater firms have materially different budgets, systems, unions and risk tolerances.
- rho: Current legal and collective-bargaining treatment of AI-generated material and digital replicas may change within the horizon.
- e: No dataset measures default-lens eligibility for firms in the $1-10M normalized EBITDA band.
- e: The provided firm count is an ESTIMATE bridged from receipts using a recreation-sector margin and may misclassify nonprofit surplus or show-level economics as EBITDA.
- e: Nonprofit governance transfers, asset sales and artistic-leadership succession generally are not qualifying control transfers.
- s5: The source category is not NAICS-pure and does not disclose a transaction denominator.
- s5: Reported sold businesses are far smaller than the target EBITDA band on average.
- s5: The estimate excludes closures and artistic succession that do not preserve a transferable operating business.
- q: There is no industry measure of contractual pass-through or retained AI benefit.
- q: TCG data describe nonprofit theaters, while Broadway is an unusually large and high-demand commercial submarket.
- q: Demand volume and implementation difficulty are excluded here to avoid double counting.
- d5: BLS provides detailed employment rather than real output for 711110 and begins from 2024.
- d5: The 2022 NEA survey period retained substantial pandemic effects, while Broadway recovery is not representative of the national sector.
- d5: The wide range reflects unresolved divergence between premium urban, regional nonprofit and dinner-theater demand.
- o: No source directly measures operator elimination for live theater.
- o: Digital replicas and virtual productions may improve quickly, but legal permission, audience acceptance and willingness to pay remain uncertain.
- o: This estimate concerns the operator requirement, not the separate amount of labor that AI can reduce inside an operator.
