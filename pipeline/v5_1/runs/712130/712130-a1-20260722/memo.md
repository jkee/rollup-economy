# 712130 — Zoos and Botanical Gardens

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `712130-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 5.85 · L 1.39 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Repeat posted-price visitor and membership revenue can retain savings from automating administrative and commercial workflows.
**Weakness:** Most recognizable institutions are not conventionally transferable private companies, and the live-collection core is difficult to automate.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market operators whose recurring or repeat external-customer service is paid access, membership, educational programming, events, or related visitor services centered on live animal or plant collections.
- Excluded: Government departments, captive exhibits, non-operating foundations, conservation land without a live public collection, shell entities, and facilities whose economics cannot support a transferable operating enterprise.
- Customer and revenue model: Primarily consumer admissions and memberships, supplemented by school and public programs, events, retail and food, donations, grants, and public support; prices are generally posted per visit or membership rather than cost-plus.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Zoos and botanical gardens combine a physically indispensable core with a narrower digital administrative layer. AI can reduce labor in admissions, membership, communications, scheduling, analytics, and reporting, but most collection care and on-site delivery remains human and facility intensive. The central commercial limitation is that many industry entities are public or nonprofit and therefore not ordinary transferable firms.

## How AI changes the work
Near-term change is concentrated in integrated ticketing and CRM, first-line guest support, donor and member communications, scheduling, marketing production, knowledge retrieval, and routine reporting. Animal and plant care may gain decision support and better records, but feeding, cleaning, horticulture, observation, training, safety, and public accountability still require people.

## Value capture
Posted admission and membership prices allow more retention than hourly or cost-plus billing. Savings may nonetheless be reinvested in animal welfare, collections, wages, access discounts, and visitor experience, especially where missions, donors, grants, or public oversight constrain margin extraction.

## Firm availability
A private, transferable subset exists among wildlife attractions, gardens, and specialized exhibitors, yet public and nonprofit governance dominates much of the recognizable institutional landscape. Specialized assets, permits, stewardship obligations, and a thin buyer universe reduce the conversion of owner exit intentions into completed control transfers.

## Demand durability
Physical visitation, education, memberships, events, and conservation engagement should remain durable, and recent industry commentary points to strong attendance recovery. Demand is still discretionary and locally exposed to household budgets, weather, tourism, disease, and competing leisure choices.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest evidence gaps are a current six-digit wage-weighted occupation mix, current legal-form ownership by EBITDA band, closed transaction incidence, and US private-operator revenue contracts. The available ownership evidence is particularly old, and multiple sources describe accredited or nonprofit populations rather than the eligible LMM subset.

## Caveats
- a: The available industry-specific occupation profile is old, covers NAICS 71213 rather than a modern six-digit private-only population, and reports broad EEO categories rather than task shares.
- a: ILO exposure is not adoption or job displacement and is not specific to US visitor attractions.
- a: The injected compensation-to-receipts input uses 2024 wages over 2022 receipts and is harmonized to the IPS basis, as stated in the assignment.
- rho: The clearest zoo implementation source is an AZA commercial-member feature and may select successful adopters.
- rho: General occupational exposure does not establish feasibility under zoo accreditation, animal-welfare, or public-sector controls.
- e: The ownership statistic is decades old and based on an acknowledged incomplete national universe.
- e: AZA membership is neither a census of NAICS 712130 nor an ownership classification.
- e: The injected firm count is a margin-bridged ESTIMATE; eligibility is applied to that uncertain base without re-estimating it.
- s5: The owner-readiness survey is cross-industry, self-reported, and not representative of this NAICS.
- s5: The sell-through statistic refers to businesses that go to market and is not a direct annual hazard for eligible firms.
- s5: Internal succession is excluded unless it constitutes a qualifying control transfer under the screen.
- q: The most detailed revenue mix is Canadian and nonprofit, not the US private eligible subset.
- q: The estimate separates price retention from attendance volume, but actual institutions may reinvest savings in animal welfare or visitor experience rather than report margin.
- d5: BLS employment projections are not demand projections and cover many animal-care settings outside zoos.
- d5: AZA attendance commentary is not a quantified, private-only longitudinal series and may reflect large accredited institutions.
- d5: Demand is discretionary and vulnerable to recession, weather, disease, travel disruption, and competing leisure formats.
- o: This is bounded judgment rather than a measured operator-requirement share.
- o: The estimate could be lower for education-only and simple exhibit formats than for complex living collections.

- **S7** — [Museums and Related Institutions: A Basic Program Survey](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED037983.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-22): Historical US survey reports roughly 8% of covered botanical gardens and aquariums/zoos operated by companies, businesses, or individuals, with most under government, nonprofit, or educational auspices; explicitly notes incomplete universe coverage.
- **S8** — [2023 National State of Owner Readiness Report](https://exit-planning-institute.org/hubfs/Member%20Center%20Resources/2023%20National%20State%20of%20Owner%20Readiness%20Report.pdf) (accessed 2026-07-22): Surveys 1,162 US owners across more than 25 industries; reports 49% want to exit within five years and documents owner age, family ownership, readiness, and preferences for internal exits.

- **S10** — [Cost of Admissions and Museum Visitation: A 2024 Annual Survey of Museum-Goers Data Story](https://www.aam-us.org/2024/10/11/cost-of-admissions-and-museum-visitation-a-2024-annual-survey-of-museum-goers-data-story/) (accessed 2026-07-22): Documents that some museums rely materially on admissions to support mission delivery and that admission cost affects visitation, supporting pricing-retention and elasticity considerations.
