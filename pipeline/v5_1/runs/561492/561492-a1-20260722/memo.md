# 561492 — Court Reporting and Stenotype Services

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `561492-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** CONDITIONAL · A 6.21 · L 3.52 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** AI can compress transcript drafting, scoping, quality control, scheduling, and administration while certified record accountability remains valuable.
**Weakness:** Per-page and hourly pricing is transparent and labor-linked, so customers and AI-enabled competitors can capture a large share of productivity gains.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market firms providing recurring or repeat outsourced court reporting, deposition reporting, legal transcription, public stenography, realtime captioning, scheduling, transcript production, exhibit handling, and related record services to law firms, courts, agencies, corporations, and institutions.
- Excluded: Captive court or corporate reporting units, government-employed official reporters, solo nonemployers, shells, non-control financing vehicles, general document-preparation firms, postproduction captioning firms, and firms outside the lower-middle-market band.
- Customer and revenue model: Law firms, courts, agencies, corporations, and institutions purchase attendance, capture, transcription, certification, copies, realtime feeds, expedited delivery, exhibits, repositories, and related litigation support. Billing commonly combines hourly or appearance charges with per-page transcript, copy, rush, and package fees.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Court reporting combines a large AI-assistable transcript-production workflow with an unusually durable need for accuracy, security, and accountable certification. The best platform owns customer relationships, scheduling and quality systems, repositories, and a broad reporter and transcription network rather than depending on one founder's personal book or stenographic output.

## How AI changes the work
AI can generate first drafts from audio, flag names and terminology, suggest speaker labels, proofread, format, index, summarize, route assignments, answer scheduling requests, prepare invoices, and search repositories. A trained person must still monitor consequential proceedings, resolve ambiguity, preserve context and exhibits, correct the record, protect confidential data, and certify or supervise final output where rules require it.

## Value capture
Hourly, appearance, per-page, copy, realtime, package, and rush pricing lets an operator retain some early productivity gain when internal hours fall. Retention is constrained because page and delivery schedules are transparent, output is readily comparable, procurement is sophisticated, and human-verified AI services market themselves as lower-cost alternatives. Security, accuracy, nationwide coverage, and rush reliability remain defensible service attributes.

## Firm availability
A visible specialist consolidator repeatedly adds local court-reporting firms, confirming a working transaction channel for customer books, staff, contractor networks, and operating systems. The general employer-owner succession base also supports supply. Diligence must distinguish a transferable agency from a founder-dependent professional practice and verify contractor retention, client portability, transcript custody, pricing obligations, and state compliance.

## Demand durability
Accurate legal records and accessibility services remain necessary, while BLS expects occupation employment to be flat rather than collapse. Demand is not mechanically tied to one caseload series: depositions, hearings, trials, arbitrations, captioning, and transcript-copy demand move differently. Digital recording and AI reduce labor per unit and can replace premium service in lower-risk settings, but they also create more recordings that may require verified transcription.

## Risks and uncertainty
The thesis fails if certification rules liberalize quickly, law firms self-serve routine recordings, AI vendors capture the customer interface, or national platforms compress page rates before smaller firms integrate automation. Other risks include transcript errors, model bias, confidentiality breaches, reporter and contractor shortages, client concentration, nonportable founder relationships, uneven state licensing, price regulation, and litigation cycles.

## Caveats
- a: The occupational sources cover government employees, self-employed reporters, and simultaneous captioners as well as employer firms in the screened lens.
- a: No source measures wage-weighted, not-yet-automated task exposure for six-digit NAICS 561492.
- a: AI transcription product claims do not isolate labor hours avoided after human verification and exception handling.
- rho: Vendor product availability is not evidence of sustained adoption or realized labor avoidance across smaller firms.
- rho: The largest provider reports that stenographic reporters still cover nearly all of its work, but that statement concerns capture method rather than downstream AI assistance.
- rho: Legal and accessibility work varies in tolerance for delayed correction, automation, and remote production.
- e: Public sources do not measure lens eligibility among the supplied lower-middle-market firms.
- e: The NAICS definition includes realtime captioning outside legal proceedings, which can have different customers and transferability.
- e: The supplied firm count is itself margin-bridged and may misclassify firms near the economic band.
- s5: Gallup measures stated plans across all employer industries rather than completed court-reporting transactions.
- s5: The Veritext archive is buyer-authored and does not disclose transaction structures or consideration.
- s5: Some listed firms are outside the United States, and the archive does not supply a market denominator.
- q: Federal maximum rates are regulated public-sector schedules rather than negotiated private-market prices.
- q: Per-page billing does not reveal how agencies split revenue with reporters, scopists, or transcription contractors.
- q: AI-assisted competitors may accelerate price compression faster than historical contract renewal cycles.
- d5: The BLS projection concerns employment, not constant-price, constant-quality service demand.
- d5: Federal caseload is only one portion of the market and recent totals were distorted by multidistrict litigation.
- d5: No source measures national deposition volume or transcript pages across state courts and private proceedings.
- o: Certification and recording rules vary materially by jurisdiction and proceeding type.
- o: The NCRA position is an industry advocacy statement rather than a neutral forecast of legal requirements.
- o: A physical stenographer is not synonymous with an accountable operator; digital capture can preserve the operator role with fewer labor hours.
