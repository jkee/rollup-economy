# 313220 — Narrow Fabric Mills and Schiffli Machine Embroidery

*v5.1 Stage 1 research memo. Run `313220-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 5.84 · L 1.04 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → LOW_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Repeat specification-based physical products create recurring relationships and information workflows around a still-essential operator.
**Weakness:** The labor base is predominantly physical, and six-digit evidence on AI adoption, demand, and qualifying transfers is sparse.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market mills repeatedly supplying external customers with woven or braided narrow fabrics, elastic narrow fabrics, labels, ribbons, tape, webbing, covered rubber thread, and Schiffli machine embroidery.
- Excluded: Captive internal production, shell entities, non-control financing vehicles, firms outside the normalized EBITDA band, and businesses without repeat external-customer orders.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat B2B manufacture sold by specification, lot, length, production run, or contract order, often with customer-specific material, performance, design, or finishing requirements.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Narrow-fabric and Schiffli operations provide repeat physical inputs whose specifications, short runs, and customer qualification can support sticky relationships. AI opportunity exists mainly in design/specification handling, planning, quoting, quality analytics, maintenance, and administration; the core manufacturing work remains physical.

## How AI changes the work
AI can parse customer specifications, prepare or check design files, support demand and production scheduling, recommend material purchases, flag visual anomalies, summarize maintenance history, and automate routine service and back-office work. It should augment rather than replace most loom, braiding, embroidery, setup, material-handling, and final inspection roles.

## Value capture
Custom or qualified work may preserve benefits through faster quotes, lower minimum runs, better lead times, and fewer defects. Commodity products face quicker price sharing through rebids and imports, while limited current PPI coverage makes direct pass-through unobservable.

## Firm availability
The frozen dataset supplies 56 candidate-band firms before eligibility. Repeat external orders are common to the product model, but captive plants, larger-group divisions, weak or distressed mills, and non-transferable customer relationships reduce the actionable set. Broad manufacturing owner-age evidence supports succession pressure but not observed sale flow.

## Demand durability
The output remains physical and operator-required, with product breadth across elastic, labels, tapes, webbing, braids, covered thread, and embroidery. Domestic demand is nevertheless exposed to imports, offshore downstream manufacturing, substitution, and consolidation; broader 3132 output has stabilized recently after a sharp decline.

## Risks and uncertainty
Evidence is mostly broader than 313220: occupations and output are available at 3132, adoption at manufacturing-wide level, and succession only at broad owner populations. Legacy controls, inconsistent design data, customer-specific qualification, small technical teams, and costly false quality decisions can limit implementation, while product heterogeneity widens the demand range.

## Caveats
- a: No six-digit OEWS occupation table is published for 313220.
- a: The product mix spans commodity tape and elastic through engineered webbing and decorative embroidery, so task exposure varies.
- a: Existing computer-controlled production is part of the baseline and is not credited as AI exposure.
- rho: The manufacturing AI evidence is economy-wide and includes establishments much larger than the target band.
- rho: Some exposed office tasks may be easier to implement than production-facing inspection or optimization.
- e: The estimate is applied to frozen n=56 and does not re-estimate that dataset input.
- e: Firm and establishment boundaries can differ materially for multi-plant textile groups.
- e: Schiffli embroidery and engineered narrow fabrics can have different customer and asset profiles.
- s5: The proxy covers women-owned manufacturing employer firms, not 313220 specifically.
- s5: Owner age creates succession pressure but does not establish transaction probability or transferability.
- q: PPI measures output price, not retention of a gross AI benefit.
- q: BLS announced discontinuation of selected 313220 PPIs, limiting ongoing price visibility.
- q: Bargaining power varies between commodity ribbon or tape and highly qualified webbing.
- d5: Both output series are 3132 rather than 313220.
- d5: The NAICS combines product categories with materially different end markets and substitution risks.
- d5: Pandemic-era comparisons distort the 2021 starting point.
- o: Operator requirement is a bounded judgment, not a directly measured share.
- o: Some decorative embroidery or label demand could be eliminated digitally or shifted into printed substitutes; that effect overlaps with d5 and is not double counted.
