# 482111 — Line-Haul Railroads

**Driver:** The physical, network-controlled nature of line-haul freight creates very high operator persistence while inspection, planning, maintenance, and administrative workflows offer meaningful but bounded AI-assisted productivity upside.
**Weakness:** The eligible lower-middle-market operator population and representative five-year transfer history cannot be measured from the available evidence, so firm availability and sale incidence are missing rather than estimable.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Privately operated U.S. line-haul freight rail services moving cargo over main and branch lines between terminals, with repeat shipper relationships and recurring transportation demand.
- Excluded: Public intercity passenger operations, local switching and terminal railroads classified in NAICS 482112, tourist or club operations, and software-only logistics coordination.
- Customer and revenue model: Freight shippers buy recurring physical transportation capacity through negotiated contracts and tariffs; revenue varies with carloads, lanes, commodity mix, core pricing, service commitments, and fuel-surcharge mechanisms.
- Deviation from default lens: The default U.S. lower-middle-market recurring outsourced-service lens is narrowed to freight because intercity passenger operations have structurally different public ownership, consumer-fare, and policy economics. The narrowing is for analytical coherence, not attractiveness.

## Executive view
The physical line-haul railroad operator is highly durable and has credible opportunities for AI-assisted workflow compression, but the industry is network- and asset-intensive and dominated by Class I systems. The decisive diligence gap is whether a measurable population of independent NAICS 482111 businesses exists in the requested $1 million to $10 million EBITDA band.

## How AI changes the work
The most credible five-year path is augmentation and selective labor compression through automated track inspection, machine vision, predictive maintenance, dispatch and network-planning support, document processing, billing, customer communications, and exception triage. Physical train operation, field repair, track maintenance, safety accountability, and regulated crew activity remain much less substitutable.

## Value capture
Network access and captive lanes can protect pricing, while trucking and intermodal alternatives, service reliability, shipper bargaining, contract renewals, incentives, fuel mechanisms, and regulatory remedies constrain capture. AI-derived savings should split among carrier margins, improved service, and shipper pricing rather than accrue entirely to the operator.

## Firm availability
No defensible roster or denominator of independent 482111 operators in the specified EBITDA band was established. Short-line operators are separately classified in 482112, and public carrier or establishment counts cannot be treated as lower-middle-market acquisition targets. Both eligibility and five-year sale incidence therefore remain MISSING.

## Demand durability
A modest positive freight backdrop is plausible, but commodity mix, export exposure, industrial production, service quality, and modal competition make the five-year range wide. Software does not replace the physical rail movement, so operator persistence remains very high even under aggressive workflow automation.

## Risks and uncertainty
The central risks are absence of a size-qualified target population, concentration in large carriers, commodity and lane volatility, regulatory and labor constraints, legacy-system integration, modal substitution, and reliance on four-digit occupational evidence. Public Class I disclosures may overstate both technology capacity and pricing power for any smaller operator.

## Caveats
- a: The BLS table covers NAICS 482100 and therefore can include employment outside 482111.
- a: Occupational shares are not task shares; even exposed occupations contain physical, regulated, and exception-heavy work.
- a: Automated inspection evidence establishes technical capability, not realized labor-hour displacement.
- rho: The estimate applies to the automatable task share, not total employment or total cost.
- rho: Class I adoption experience may overstate the integration capacity of smaller operators.
- rho: Regulatory and labor arrangements can vary by operation and may change during the horizon.
- e: STB carrier classes are defined by operating revenue, not EBITDA.
- e: Establishments are not interchangeable with independent acquisition targets.
- e: The frozen dataset provides no usable value for the related population primitive.
- s5: Rail asset transfers and trackage-rights arrangements are not necessarily business sales.
- s5: Observed large-carrier transactions would not represent lower-middle-market turnover.
- s5: Regulatory filings may omit small private transactions or encode transaction type inconsistently.
- q: Public Class I economics may not transfer cleanly to any smaller eligible operator.
- q: Fuel surcharges and commodity mix can obscure underlying price realization.
- q: Captive-shipper conditions vary materially by origin, destination, commodity, and access to alternative modes.
- d5: National freight forecasts do not map directly to a specific operator's traffic mix.
- d5: Tonnage, ton-miles, carloads, and revenue are different measures of service quantity.
- d5: Public Class I results can mask divergent commodity and regional trends.
- o: Operator survival is distinct from demand retention; customers can switch carriers or modes.
- o: Automation could alter staffing and asset intensity without eliminating the licensed network operator.
- o: The estimate assumes the service remains physical freight movement rather than a software-only coordination product.
