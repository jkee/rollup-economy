# 541511 — Custom Computer Programming Services

*v5 Stage 1 research memo. Run `541511-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** PRIORITY · A 7.11 · L 4.36 · interval CONDITIONAL → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** Whether an operator can convert client billing from hourly/time-and-materials to fixed-fee or retainer pricing faster than clients and AI-native competitors compete that value away.
**Weakness:** Value capture (q) is structurally weak: time-and-materials billing ties revenue directly to labor hours, so AI-driven efficiency gains show up by default as fewer billable hours rather than retained operator margin.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Firms whose primary NAICS 541511 activity is writing, modifying, testing, and supporting custom application and software code under ongoing or renewing engagements with external, unaffiliated clients: bespoke application development, systems customization/integration coding, dedicated or embedded development-team (staff-augmentation-with-delivery-accountability) arrangements, and post-launch maintenance/enhancement contracts that follow an initial build.
- Excluded: Packaged/off-the-shelf software publishing (NAICS 5112, out of scope by definition); genuinely one-off single-project engagements with no expectation of a renewing client relationship; in-house/captive corporate development units (not separately incorporated or classified under 541511 in any case); shell entities; and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: External business and government clients purchasing custom development on a time-and-materials/hourly basis, a dedicated-team retainer, or (a growing minority) a fixed-fee/outcome-based basis. Revenue is services fees for labor and delivery rather than product licensing. At LMM scale a meaningful share of revenue recurs through multi-phase or maintenance follow-on work with existing clients rather than single discrete projects, but the industry also contains a genuine spectrum from pure staff-augmentation body shops to retained development partners.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Custom Computer Programming Services (541511) is a large, fragmented, labor-intensive professional-services industry now visibly reshaped by AI coding tools, but the reshaping cuts against a simple AI-labor-arbitrage roll-up thesis more than it supports one. Developer-side AI tool adoption is near-universal and productivity gains on core coding tasks are well documented and real. But three findings complicate the picture. First, the exposed and implementable share of the wage bill is concentrated in mid-level coding work (the narrow 'Computer Programmers' category scores highest of any US occupation on observed AI exposure), while a large share of industry payroll sits in more judgment-heavy developer, systems-analyst, architecture, and management roles that are comparatively insulated. Second, the dominant billing model — time-and-materials/hourly — means AI-driven efficiency gains flow toward clients as fewer billable hours or renegotiated fees rather than accruing to the operator, unless an acquirer converts client relationships to fixed-fee/retainer pricing faster than clients and AI-native competitors force the issue. Third, firm eligibility and ownership-transfer dynamics look like ordinary lower-middle-market services M&A base rates: nothing found here suggests unusually favorable succession or deal-flow conditions specific to this NAICS code, and PE/strategic buyer enthusiasm in tech M&A skews heavily toward SaaS/product companies rather than services shops like these. The more defensible upside case rests on durable demand for accountable delivery of complex, compliance- and integration-heavy custom work, not on capturing AI margin directly, and even that durable-demand case is under real (if imprecisely measured) pressure from low-code/citizen development and embedded AI agents.

## How AI changes the work
AI substitution potential is real but concentrated, not industry-wide. Using the nearest published occupation mix (BLS OEWS, NAICS 5415, since 541511 is not separately broken out) wage-weighted against the best available occupation-level AI exposure evidence, roughly a third to a bit under half of current industry payroll sits in tasks meaningfully exposed to AI substitution or avoidable hiring. The narrowly defined 'Computer Programmers' occupation is the single highest-scoring occupation in the economy on Anthropic's observed-exposure ranking, but it is a small slice of industry payroll; the much larger 'Software Developers' category scores lower precisely because the work is described as more complex, collaborative, and judgment-intensive, and management, sales, and project-oversight roles (a fifth of payroll) score lower still. On implementation timing, developer AI-tool adoption is already near-saturated and Anthropic's own Claude Code telemetry shows a much higher automation share than general-purpose AI chat use, which is encouraging for near-term implementability — but that reflects self-selected, already-delegated tasks (front-end/UI work dominates; harder backend/database work lags), enterprise adoption trails startup adoption by a wide margin, and credible forecasters expect a meaningful share of enterprise agentic AI projects to be scrapped rather than sustained through 2027.

## Value capture
Retention of AI-driven gains looks structurally weak for this industry's dominant billing model. Time-and-materials/hourly billing is common industry practice, and it mechanically converts labor-hour reductions into fewer billable hours rather than retained margin. Outcome-based and fixed-fee contracting is growing but remains a minority of new deals. An IT-services M&A advisory analysis explicitly frames AI coding productivity gains as a structural headwind for traditional software-development engagements specifically (as distinct from managed services, cybersecurity, or data/cloud infrastructure segments, which the same analysis frames as AI tailwinds). The clearest path to retaining value is repositioning client relationships toward fixed-fee, retainer, or outcome-based pricing ahead of clients and competitors doing the repricing for the operator — a race the operator is not guaranteed to win, and where visible public discourse about AI coding productivity makes clients better-informed negotiating counterparties than in a typical opaque professional-services market.

## Firm availability
The lower-middle-market population here (firms in the $1-10M normalized-EBITDA band, injected as n) is, almost by construction, made up of employer firms with material, ongoing external-client operations rather than freelancers or shells, so the share of that population plausibly eligible under the lens (recurring/repeat outsourced service to external customers) is estimated to be high. The bigger open question is ownership-transfer likelihood: no 541511-specific succession or deal-flow data was found, so this packet anchors on a general US employer-business-owner base rate (roughly a fifth of owners report five-year plans to sell or transfer), adjusted only qualitatively for two offsetting factors — tech/software founders skew younger than the average small-business owner (fewer near-retirement sellers than the base rate implies), while PE and strategic buyer interest in tech/IT services has been rising (more demand-side transfer activity than the base rate implies). Available PE/M&A statistics for tech services, however, are dominated by SaaS/product-company transactions and larger deal sizes, and are only weak evidence for what happens among $1-10M-EBITDA custom-programming-services sellers specifically.

## Demand durability
BLS employment projections rank Computer Systems Design and Related Services among the fastest-growing and highest-job-adding industries in the economy through 2034, and Software Developers specifically are projected to see the second-largest employment increase of any US occupation — used here as an imperfect labor-input proxy for real, constant-price service-quantity growth, prorated to a five-year horizon. That said, meaningful headwinds exist to how much of that future quantity still requires an accountable outsourced operator (as opposed to being self-served or software-supplied): enterprise applications are projected to rapidly increase their embedded task-specific AI-agent content over the next several years, and low-code/citizen-development platforms are widely (if not rigorously) reported to already account for a majority of new internally-built custom business applications. Durable demand for this industry's accountable-vendor model is most plausible in the more complex slice of the work — legacy-system integration, regulated/compliance-heavy builds, and multi-system judgment calls — rather than in the simpler custom-app work most exposed to citizen development and agentic substitution.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest uncertainties are, in rough order of how much they could move this screen: (1) whether AI capability progress since the 2023-24 vintage of the wage, occupation, and employment-projection data used here has already outpaced what these primitives assume, in either direction; (2) whether operators can reposition billing models fast enough to retain AI-driven value before clients force repricing at renewal; (3) whether low-code/citizen-development and embedded agentic AI erode the addressable, accountable-operator-required demand faster than legacy-modernization and compliance-driven work replaces it; and (4) whether PE/strategic buyer interest in tech services — currently concentrated in SaaS/product companies — extends meaningfully to $1-10M-EBITDA custom-programming-services shops specifically, an assumption this packet could not verify with segment-specific deal data. Separately, the injected labor-share ratio l mixes 2024 wage data with 2022 receipts data (a stated vintage mismatch), and the injected firm count n rests on a generic cross-industry EBITDA margin assumption that may not fit this industry's actual margin structure well; both add uncertainty upstream of the primitives researched here.

## Caveats
- a: Occupation mix is measured at NAICS 5415 (4-digit aggregate), not 541511 specifically; 541511 is very likely more coding-intensive than the aggregate, biasing this estimate downward.
- a: Only 4 of 13 occupation categories in the mix have a directly sourced exposure figure; the rest (including the single largest category, Software Developers, at ~45% of payroll) are analogical judgment.
- a: The injected labor-share ratio l (0.6736, QCEW 2024 wages / SUSB 2022 receipts, stated MED quality) predates the visible 2025-2026 AI-tooling adoption wave described in this packet; if compensation share is already compressing due to AI-assisted delivery, l may overstate the wage base to which a and rho apply going forward.
- rho: Adoption and automation-share figures come from AI-vendor usage telemetry and developer-tool market surveys — self-selected users and self-selected tasks, not a representative sample of delivered 541511 work.
- rho: The Gartner project-cancellation figure is a forward-looking prediction as of 2025, not a realized 2027 outcome.
- e: No direct survey of 541511 LMM firms' revenue-recurrence or client-renewal rate was found; this is industry-structure judgment, not a measured share.
- e: The injected firm count n (5,767) is itself a margin-bridged estimate using a generic Damodaran 'Business & Consumer Services' 15.65% EBITDA margin assumption; if actual margins in custom programming services (often higher, given labor-arbitrage-based staffing/consulting models) differ materially from 15.65%, the revenue band mapped to '$1-10M EBITDA' — and therefore which firms populate the eligible-firm population e is applied to — could be mis-sized in either direction.
- s5: No 541511-specific succession or deal-count data was found; this is a cross-industry base rate applied by analogy.
- s5: The PE/strategic tech-M&A statistics found were dominated by SaaS/product-company deals and large-cap transactions, not representative of $1-10M-EBITDA custom-programming-services sellers specifically.
- q: The ~70% time-and-materials billing-share figure is sourced from an industry blog aggregator rather than a primary billing-practice survey.
- q: No direct measurement exists of realized post-AI-adoption repricing or margin retention specific to 541511 sellers; this is inferred from the industry's billing structure and one advisory firm's qualitative framing.
- d5: Employment growth is a labor-input proxy, not a direct constant-price/quality demand-quantity measure.
- d5: BLS projection vintage (base year 2024) may not fully reflect the AI-adoption acceleration visible through 2026.
- d5: Commercial market-size forecasts (~22% nominal CAGR) were not used directly because they blend price inflation, a broader product/services scope, and vendor-report incentives to describe large addressable markets.
- o: The widely circulated 'majority of new custom business apps built by non-IT employees' figure originates in low-code-vendor-ecosystem marketing content without a verifiable primary citation; treated as a weak, directional data point only.
- o: Gartner's agentic-enterprise-application figures measure AI features embedded within applications, not whether an accountable human operator remains responsible for the engagement — a related but distinct question from o.
- o: Internal citizen-development substitution primarily displaces simpler internal workflow apps that may never have been outsourced spend in the first place, which could mean o for the specific externally-contracted 541511 basket is understated by these figures, not overstated.
