# 541890 — Other Services Related to Advertising

**Driver:** Repeat client programs can pair AI-assisted coordination with physical delivery that still requires an accountable operator.
**Weakness:** The six-digit code is heterogeneous, and the most relevant transfer and labor evidence is not measured for its retained lower-middle-market service subset.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market external providers of recurring or repeat promotional-product coordination, product demonstrations, and display-lettering services, where the provider remains accountable for client service, creative or campaign coordination, and delivery.
- Excluded: One-off sign fabrication or installation projects, pure merchandise resale without an outsourced service relationship, captive marketing teams, internal units, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Business customers buy recurring campaign, promotional-program, demonstration, or display-support work under repeat orders, projects, retainers, or account arrangements; merchandise and fabrication can be pass-through components.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed for coherence because the code spans advertising-specialty coordination, public merchandise demonstrations, and display lettering. The lens retains only repeat outsourced client-service relationships and excludes project-only fabrication and pure resale.

## Executive view
This is a heterogeneous advertising-services code with a viable repeat-service subset, but its mix of tangible, field, and creative work limits a uniform roll-up thesis. The central view is modest AI-enabled labor improvement in a largely flat demand setting, with transfer availability supported only by a cross-industry owner-intent proxy (S1, S6).

## How AI changes the work
AI is most useful for artwork variants, proofs, sales and proposal preparation, order communications, and reporting. It is less substitutive for client persuasion, demonstrations, vendor exceptions, physical lettering, and delivery. The broad 541800 occupation data confirm a mix of management, business, arts/design, sales, office, production, and transport work rather than a purely digital workflow (S2).

## Value capture
Repeat accounts and fixed-price work can let a provider retain part of lower coordination cost at first, but customer marketing teams can see and demand a share of the benefit in renewals or competitive bids. Merchandise and fabrication pass-through also mean that labor productivity may be a limited part of the client invoice (S1, S3).

## Firm availability
The retained activities are plausible outsourced services, but the code also contains project-only fabrication and pure resale, reducing eligibility. Gallup found 14% of primary-work business owners planned a sale, public offering, or transfer within five years, rising to 17% for owners 55 and older; it is evidence of intent, not realized 541890 control transfers (S6).

## Demand durability
Physical merchandise, demonstrations, and installed graphics keep an operator in the loop for many customers. Offsetting this, BLS projects declines for advertising sales agents and advertising and promotions managers, while graphic designers show only 2% projected growth; digital channel shift and automated placement are explicit pressures (S3, S4, S5).

## Risks and uncertainty
The principal uncertainty is composition: promotional-product coordination, public demonstrations, and display lettering have different labor mixes, billing models, and demand paths. Six-digit occupation, AI-adoption, transfer, and customer-retention data were not located; the ranges therefore rely on structured estimates and broader-industry or cross-industry proxies.

## Caveats
- a: No published six-digit occupation-by-wage matrix was located for 541890.
- a: The 541800 mix includes advertising and public-relations businesses that are more desk-intensive than much of the retained physical-service work.
- a: Exposure describes task substitution or avoidable hiring, not immediately realized savings.
- rho: There is no observed 541890 implementation rate.
- rho: The range assumes use of commercially available tools and does not assume a proprietary data advantage.
- e: NAICS descriptions identify activities, not the share of firms or revenue in each business model.
- e: Eligibility is assessed at the firm level and may differ from establishment coding.
- s5: The source is a 2024 survey, not an industry transaction census.
- s5: A qualifying transfer excludes closures and internal reorganizations, which the survey does not cleanly separate.
- q: No industry-specific evidence was found for the portion of automation benefits passed through to customers.
- q: Retention can differ sharply between a retainer-led demonstration provider and a transaction-led promotional-products distributor.
- d5: Occupation projections are not constant-price, constant-quality demand measures for 541890 and are used only as directional context.
- d5: The code's component activities may have materially different demand trajectories.
- o: This is a judgment about the retained lens, not an observed industry survival share.
- o: The value is lower for digital-first promotional programs and higher for field demonstrations and installed physical graphics.
