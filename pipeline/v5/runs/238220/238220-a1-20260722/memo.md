# 238220 — Plumbing, Heating, and Air-Conditioning Contractors

*v5 Stage 1 research memo. Run `238220-a1-20260722`, model `claude-sonnet-5`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** STRUCTURAL_OUT · A 7.47 · L 0.79 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → LOW_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** An unusually intense, multi-year private-equity buyer pool actively bidding for an aging, under-succession-planned owner base in a still-mostly-independent, recurring-service segment of the trade.
**Weakness:** The AI-labor thesis is thin: roughly 70% of the wage bill is licensed, on-site, physical trade work that is structurally low-exposure to AI substitution, so any operational-efficiency case rests on a comparatively small back-office slice of the cost base.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Residential and light-commercial plumbing, heating, and air-conditioning service, repair, replacement, and maintenance-contract work performed by contractors for external homeowner and business customers who return to the same operator across repeat calls, seasonal maintenance visits, and eventual equipment replacement. Includes flat-rate diagnostic/repair visits, sold service/maintenance agreements, and one-time system replacements sold directly to the end user (e.g., furnace or water-heater swap-outs).
- Excluded: New-construction plumbing and HVAC/mechanical installation performed as a subcontractor to a general contractor or homebuilder on a single build project, where the contractual and billing relationship is with the GC/builder rather than the eventual occupant and ends at project handoff with no expectation of a repeat service relationship. Also excluded: large industrial/commercial process-piping and mechanical contracting let as one-off bid capital projects (e.g., new hospital or plant mechanical systems), and any captive in-house maintenance staff of non-contractor firms. Shells and non-control financing vehicles are excluded per the default lens.
- Customer and revenue model: Three billing structures coexist under NAICS 238220 and were not separated by the data injected for this packet: (1) flat-rate/fixed-price pricing for residential diagnostic and repair visits, now the dominant and fastest-growing model (industry commentary cites roughly nine in ten homeowners preferring an upfront flat price over hourly billing); (2) sold annual or multi-year maintenance/service agreements that bundle tune-ups, priority scheduling, and discounted repair rates, reported to capture on the order of a third to two-fifths of total industry service revenue at the highest margins in the trade; and (3) time-and-materials or cost-plus billing, concentrated in larger commercial/light-industrial jobs and unpredictable diagnostic work where scope cannot be fixed in advance. Customer acquisition runs substantially through paid digital channels (Google Local Services Ads, organic/SEO, Angi-type marketplaces), with reported cost-per-lead in the $40-150 range and wide variance in close rate by channel, indicating real local price/lead competition for one-time repair work even where flat-rate pricing insulates the price of a given job.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed to exclude new-construction/GC-subcontracted mechanical installation and one-off large commercial/industrial mechanical project work. NAICS 238220 by definition mixes two economically distinct models: (a) trade subcontractors bidding project-by-project into homebuilders and general contractors, whose customer relationship is transactional and ends at project completion, and (b) residential/light-commercial service-and-replacement contractors who cultivate a repeat relationship directly with the equipment owner via maintenance agreements and recall service. Only (b) meets the default lens's requirement of a recurring or repeat outsourced service to an external customer; (a) is a construction subcontracting relationship, not a repeat-service relationship, regardless of firm size or profitability. This narrowing was made for coherence (the two models have different billing structures, different demand drivers tied to housing starts vs. equipment replacement cycles, and different M&A treatment in the market, where private-equity platforms specifically target service-and-replacement revenue mix and value it at a premium multiple to installation-heavy revenue) and was not selected because it screens more favorably.

## Executive view
NAICS 238220 is a large, structurally fragmented trade (roughly 1.24M industry employees per BLS OEWS) where the physical work itself -- soldering pipe, brazing refrigerant lines, running duct, replacing a furnace under a licensed and inspected permit -- sits at the bottom of every published AI-exposure ranking and is not going to be automated away over a five-year horizon. The real AI story here is a back-office one: dispatch, scheduling, customer communication, estimating, and invoicing are meaningfully exposed and are the explicit product roadmap of every major field-service software vendor selling into this industry, but actual embedded adoption today is low (about 12% of surveyed contractors) and concentrated in scale/PE-backed platforms rather than the median independent firm. Separately and largely independently of the AI thesis, this is one of the most actively consolidating trades in the US economy right now, with an aging, under-succession-planned owner base and more than two dozen active private-equity platforms bidding for service-and-replacement-revenue businesses -- a firm-availability dynamic that would exist with or without any AI angle. The honest picture is two overlapping opportunities (labor-cost efficiency and firm-transfer availability) that only partially reinforce each other, sitting inside a lens that had to be narrowed because the raw NAICS code also contains a materially different new-construction-subcontracting business that this analysis deliberately excludes.

## How AI changes the work
The industry's wage bill is dominated (roughly 70%) by field trade labor -- plumbers, pipefitters, HVAC/refrigeration mechanics, sheet-metal workers, and their helpers -- performing licensed, inspected, physically on-site work that current and near-term AI cannot substitute or eliminate; national occupation-level AI-exposure research consistently places construction/extraction and installation/maintenance/repair occupations in the least-exposed tier of the entire US labor market. The exposed opportunity instead lives in the roughly 12% of the wage bill sitting in office/administrative support (dispatch, customer service, billing) plus smaller slices of business/financial operations (cost estimating, bookkeeping) and sales -- functions where AI-assisted scheduling, automated invoicing, and AI voice/chat customer service are already being marketed directly at contractors. Actual adoption lags awareness badly: roughly six in ten contractors say they're familiar with AI and seven in ten see it as relevant, but only about one in ten have embedded it into their workflow, and small-firm field-service software adoption generally (29% for sub-100-employee firms versus 76% for 1,000+-employee firms) suggests the long tail of independent operators will adopt slowly even where the tooling exists and works.

## Value capture
Billing structure is the swing factor for whether an operator keeps an efficiency gain. Flat-rate pricing, now the dominant and still-growing model for residential repair and replacement work, lets an operator retain a productivity gain within a fixed job price rather than passing it through the way hourly or cost-plus billing would; sold annual maintenance agreements (roughly a third to two-fifths of service revenue, the highest-margin work in the trade) add further insulation from job-by-job renegotiation. Against that, this is a locally competitive, low-switching-cost, price-transparent market -- digital lead-generation costs of $40-150 per lead and thin per-job profitability thresholds indicate real competitive pressure that would compete away a visible efficiency gain over time, particularly on one-off repair and replacement jobs quoted competitively against several local providers. Net retention looks real but partial and uneven across the revenue mix, not a clean pass-through in either direction.

## Firm availability
This segment of the trade is being actively bid for by an unusually large and well-capitalized buyer pool: more than two dozen active private-equity-backed consolidation platforms, roughly 32 disclosed HVAC-services add-on transactions in just the first half of 2025 (up 88% year over year), and service-and-replacement-revenue businesses reportedly trading at multiples north of 10x EBITDA at the platform level. The seller side is aging and under-planned -- owners in their high 50s on average, with widely cited estimates that a majority of US small-business owners generally have no documented succession plan. Despite the deal intensity, the eligible population remains largely untapped: independent operators are still estimated to hold roughly three-quarters of the broader home-services market and about seventy percent of plumbing-repair market share specifically, meaning the buyer pool has consumed a minority of the available firms to date and meaningful further supply should exist over a five-year window, even as competition among buyers for the best assets intensifies.

## Demand durability
Structural demand supports are real: an aging equipment base moving through its 15-20 year replacement cycle, rising cooling-degree-day exposure pushing air-conditioning penetration higher, and a persistent and worsening skilled-trades shortage (an estimated 225,000-technician HVAC shortfall within five years) that constrains supply rather than signaling weak demand. Working against that: the federal Section 25C heat-pump/efficiency tax credit was repealed effective the end of 2025, removing a direct subsidy that had been pulling replacement demand forward, and 2025 equipment-unit shipments were already down sharply (roughly 20% year to date) -- a decline confounded by both the credit expiration and a concurrent refrigerant-standard (A2L) transition, so it is not a clean read on the underlying trend. On balance I expect modest real, constant-price demand growth over five years, well below the pace suggested by nominal-dollar market-size forecasts that embed price inflation from the equipment transition.

## Risks and uncertainty
The single biggest source of uncertainty in this packet is that NAICS 238220 mixes a recurring-service residential/light-commercial business with a project-based new-construction-subcontracting business, and no source directly measures what share of LMM-band firms sit in each; the eligibility estimate is inferred from adjacent revenue-mix commentary, not counted. A second material risk is policy-driven demand volatility: the heat-pump tax-credit repeal and the ongoing A2L refrigerant transition are live, recent shocks whose medium-term demand effect is not yet cleanly observable in the data. A third risk is that the entire firm-availability thesis depends on an already-crowded buyer pool of 27+ PE platforms continuing to bid aggressively for a finite set of quality assets; if that competitive intensity causes acquirers to bid multiples up further or to exhaust the best-positioned targets first, the remaining available pool could skew toward weaker assets over the horizon. Finally, the persistent skilled-labor shortage that supports demand durability and operator-necessity (`o`) is a double-edged fact: it also means any acquired platform's growth is capacity-constrained by the same labor market this analysis says AI cannot substitute for.

## Caveats
- a: Pew's exposure classification is from 2023 (pre-agentic-AI) and is a national cross-industry study, not specific to NAICS 238220 or to small/mid-size contractor firms.
- a: The occupation-to-exposure-bucket mapping used here is my own bounded judgment layered on top of Pew's category-level findings, not a published number for this NAICS code.
- a: Does not capture within-occupation task exposure for field trades (e.g., AI-assisted fault diagnosis tools used by a technician in the field), which would push the estimate up modestly if quantified.
- rho: The ServiceTitan survey's sample and methodology (how contractors were recruited, survey date) are not disclosed in the source consulted, and ServiceTitan is itself a vendor with an interest in the AI narrative.
- rho: Excludes pricing, demand, and valuation effects per the primitive definition; this is implementation likelihood only, not payoff.
- e: This is an ESTIMATE built from adjacent revenue-mix commentary, not a direct firm-count measurement.
- e: Firm-level blending (a single firm doing both new-construction subcontracting and residential service work) makes a clean binary eligibility split inherently fuzzy; the true construct is closer to a firm-level revenue-share threshold than a yes/no eligibility flag.
- s5: The 92%-closure/5%-sale/3%-transfer baseline (S15) is for all US small businesses, not this trade, and likely understates transfer probability here given the unusually active buyer pool -- I have adjusted upward from that baseline rather than using it directly.
- s5: Deal-count sources (S5, S6, S7) are industry/advisory-firm marketing content, not audited transaction databases, and likely undercount smaller, non-press-released add-on deals in one direction while over-representing PE-platform activity relative to family-to-family or search-fund transfers in the other.
- s5: "Qualifying control transfer" excludes deaths without transferable operations and internal reorganizations, which the general small-business closure statistics do not cleanly separate out.
- q: No source directly measures retained benefit after AI-driven efficiency gains specifically; this is inferred from billing-structure and competitive-intensity evidence, not observed margin data tied to an AI implementation event.
- q: Commercial/light-industrial work billed time-and-materials or cost-plus (a real but unquantified share of this lens) would show materially lower retention than the residential flat-rate/maintenance-agreement estimate used here.
- d5: No unit-volume (as opposed to nominal-dollar) forecast for the full five-year window was located; the deflation applied here is my own judgment calibrated against one year of actual shipment data, not a published real-growth forecast.
- d5: Plumbing-specific demand is not separately sourced and is assumed rather than measured.
- d5: 2025-26 shipment weakness is unusually confounded by two distinct one-time shocks (tax-credit expiration and refrigerant-standard transition), making it hard to read as a clean trend signal in either direction.
- o: Licensing-requirement variation by state (a few states have no state-level HVAC license requirement, per S16) means the true national share is somewhat lower than a fully-licensed-everywhere assumption would imply, though local permitting/inspection requirements still generally apply.
- o: Does not separately quantify the demand-reduction effect of connected/self-diagnosing equipment, which is a real but currently unmeasured channel distinct from full elimination of the operator.
