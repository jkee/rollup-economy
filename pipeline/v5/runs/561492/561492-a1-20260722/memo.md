# 561492 — Court Reporting and Stenotype Services

**Base tier:** LOW_PRIORITY · A 5.33 · L 1.20 · interval STRUCTURAL_OUT → CONDITIONAL · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** AI-assisted drafting and production can reduce portions of transcript and agency workflow while preserving a certified human quality-control role.
**Weakness:** The core legal-record service has limited observable demand growth and meaningful risk of digital-recording substitution and price competition.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Private agencies furnishing reporter-staffed verbatim deposition, arbitration, hearing, and related certified-transcript services to law firms and corporate legal departments.
- Excluded: Official court employees and other captive government units, standalone broadcast or meeting-captioning providers, document-preparation businesses, and firms outside the lower-middle-market band.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat and referral-based outsourced legal-service bookings, billed through attendance or appearance charges, per-page transcript charges, and optional expedited, realtime, exhibit, and digital-delivery services.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 561492 combines legal proceeding reporting with live captioning. The legal reporting agency segment is retained because its legal-record requirements, buyers, and repeat booking model are coherent; standalone captioning has different accessibility and media demand drivers.

## Executive view
Private legal court-reporting agencies provide repeat outsourced records for law firms and corporate legal departments. AI can improve the production workflow, but the service remains constrained by live-record accountability, certified accuracy, confidentiality, and jurisdiction-specific rules. [S1, S4]

## How AI changes the work
The work includes live verbatim capture, proofreading, transcript production, filing, exhibits, and client delivery. Hybrid use can accelerate draft creation and post-event production, while a human reporter still monitors the proceeding and performs final review and certification. [S2, S3]

## Value capture
Public service descriptions show appearance charges, per-page transcript rates, and separately priced expedited, realtime, exhibit, and delivery services. These components allow agencies to retain some workflow gains, but client procurement and lower-cost digital alternatives can pressure pricing. [S5]

## Firm availability
A disclosed acquisition of a longstanding Dallas-Fort Worth reporting agency demonstrates strategic buyer interest in established local operations. It does not reveal sector-wide sale frequency, and reliable ownership-age and closed-sale denominators remain missing. [S6, S8]

## Demand durability
BLS projects flat employment for the related occupation and says detailed, accurate records will still be needed even as technology makes transcription more efficient. Demand is exposed to court budgets and recording-method substitution, but legal and arbitration proceedings retain a need for reliable records. [S4]

## Risks and uncertainty
The NAICS code includes live captioning as well as legal reporting, so the packet excludes captioning-led firms. Evidence on AI rollout, realized margin retention, and five-year transfer rates is thin; provider and association sources may also emphasize their own preferred reporting model. [S1, S3, S7]
