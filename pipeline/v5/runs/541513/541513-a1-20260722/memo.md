## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market providers that operate and manage clients' computer systems or data-processing facilities on a recurring outsourced basis.
- Excluded: Captive client IT units, software publishers, systems integrators that do not operate the environment, hardware resellers, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Business customers buy continuing operational coverage, monitoring, maintenance, incident response, and facility or systems administration under managed-service, support, or staffing contracts.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Computer facilities management is a recurring outsourced operating service with a meaningful but bounded opportunity to reduce administrator effort. Its core trade-off is that automation can improve delivery while the same software and cloud trends can reduce the need for the incumbent operating model.

## How AI changes the work
Monitoring, log interpretation, ticket triage, documentation, routine configuration, and guided remediation are practical AI-assisted targets. Security decisions, production changes, novel incidents, physical infrastructure, and accountable escalation constrain full substitution.

## Value capture
Commercial retention is likely partial because customers can benchmark managed-service prices and observe changes in staffing. Operators retain more value where they control specialized runbooks, integrations, response quality, and SLA performance.

## Firm availability
The industry definition is oriented to operating clients' environments, making the external-service lens broadly applicable. Transfer availability remains uncertain because no verified six-digit sale-rate series was found and technical customer relationships can be founder or key-person dependent.

## Demand durability
BLS projects constant-dollar output growth for the broader computer systems design and related services industry, but it is an imperfect proxy. On-site client operations can be displaced by cloud platforms, automation, or customer self-service even as broader technical-services demand grows.

## Risks and uncertainty
The biggest evidence gap is six-digit data on contract mix, buyer behavior, ownership age, and completed transfers. Security and outage accountability make operational changes risky, while cloud migration and platform-managed services can erode both demand and the need for a standalone operator.
