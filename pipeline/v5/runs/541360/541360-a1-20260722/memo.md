## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market firms providing repeat outsourced geophysical data acquisition, processing, interpretation, and mapping for resource, engineering, environmental, and public-sector customers.
- Excluded: Captive units, shells, controlled financing vehicles, owned-data-library businesses, standalone software publishing or custom software development, equipment resale, and training businesses are excluded.
- Customer and revenue model: External customers buy accountable field and technical services on repeat programs or discrete contracted assignments; revenue is principally service revenue rather than software licenses or equipment margin.
- Deviation from default lens: The code's product list includes service work alongside standalone software, equipment resale, and training. The lens retains repeat outsourced geophysical service providers because those businesses share field-data and professional-interpretation delivery economics; the excluded activities do not.

## Executive view
The narrowed industry supplies specialized outsourced geophysical field and interpretation services. It has credible AI-enabled workflow improvement, but its small addressable service-only population, end-market cyclicality, and uncertain price retention warrant careful diligence.

## How AI changes the work
AI is most useful for data conditioning, pattern recognition, map and report production, and first-pass interpretation. O*NET task evidence also points to field acquisition, physical instruments, accuracy checks, technical judgment, and client communication that constrain full substitution. USGS is actively applying machine learning to accelerate comparable mineral-assessment work.

## Value capture
The benefit is likely to appear as faster turnaround and lower internal production effort, but buyers can demand some of that benefit through competitive bids or repricing. Retention should be tested from actual contracts, bid tabs, and renewal outcomes rather than inferred from technical feasibility.

## Firm availability
The code contains more than the target service model: Census product materials associate it with consulting, training, software, and equipment resale as well as geophysical work. Eligible firms may transfer, but specialist relationships, key-person expertise, equipment condition, and exposure to resource cycles can limit a qualifying sale.

## Demand durability
Critical-mineral mapping provides a positive public-demand channel, including USGS investment in airborne geophysical surveys. Counterbalancing that support, EIA expects lower US crude production through the mid-2030s in most cases, so demand from exploration-oriented customers is not durable by default.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest unknowns are the share of firms that are true repeat outsourced providers, their revenue mix by end market, the share of work that remains field-bound, and contract-level ability to retain efficiency gains. Commodity cycles, procurement concentration, professional liability, data quality, and customer acceptance can each change the result materially.
