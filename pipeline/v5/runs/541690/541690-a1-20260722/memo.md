# 541690 — Other Scientific and Technical Consulting Services

**Driver:** Repeat advisory workflows combine substantial information-processing work with specialist services that customers continue to buy from accountable providers.
**Weakness:** The six-digit code is heterogeneous, and public evidence does not isolate the recurring, transferable lower-middle-market provider population.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent providers of recurring outsourced scientific and technical advisory services, including safety, security, energy-management, agricultural, biological, chemical, geological, and clinical advisory work where customers repeatedly retain the provider for monitoring, assessment, reporting, or specialized guidance.
- Excluded: One-off expert opinions, litigation support, episodic project consulting without repeat service, motion-picture consulting, captive internal advisory units, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Business, farm, public-sector, and nonprofit customers buy specialist advice through recurring retainers, periodic assessments, monitoring programs, compliance cycles, or repeat engagements; revenue may be fixed-fee, retainer, or time-and-materials.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed for coherence because the code combines recurring technical advisory providers with episodic expert and project work; the screen retains only the recurring or repeat outsourced-service population specified by the default lens.

## Executive view
NAICS 541690 is a broad container for non-environmental scientific and technical advice. The coherent population is recurring outsourced specialist providers, not one-off expert work. Its labor intensity creates workflow opportunity, but technical accountability and the code's mixed revenue models make the evidence less uniform than for a narrow service category.

## How AI changes the work
AI can shorten research, document review, evidence extraction, report drafting, data classification, knowledge retrieval, and coordination. It is less suited to field observations, validated measurements, specialist interpretation, relationship management, and recommendations carrying safety, security, clinical, or professional-liability consequences. S3's task catalog makes that division visible, though it is an occupation proxy rather than a code-level measure.

## Value capture
Retention is conditional on contracts. Retainers and fixed-fee recurring programs give the operator more room to keep efficiency gains; hourly work, procurement-driven renewals, and AI-enabled competitors increase pass-through pressure. A transaction should therefore test rate cards, scope creep, utilization, and customer concentration before treating labor savings as durable margin.

## Firm availability
The code contains many owner-led specialist practices, but only a subset has repeat revenue and transferable operations at the lens scale. Public data do not observe qualifying-control-transfer probabilities for that subset. Census owner-age data and code-tagged closed-deal records are the highest-value next evidence; S6 only establishes broader service-sector deal activity.

## Demand durability
BLS projects broader management, scientific, and technical consulting wage-and-salary employment to rise 9.4 percent from 2024 to 2034, supporting a modest-growth proxy rather than a declining-demand assumption. The mapping is imperfect because 541690 combines disparate end markets. Human accountability should remain important even as lower-complexity information work becomes software-assisted.

## Risks and uncertainty
The chief risk is aggregation: economic, energy, safety, security, clinical, agricultural, and scientific advisory providers can have very different compliance burdens, work locations, billing terms, and repeat-revenue patterns. BLS OEWS excludes self-employed people, and no public dataset jointly measures the code's occupation mix, AI adoption, repeat revenue, profitability, and transfer outcomes. Underwriting should be subsegment- and contract-specific.

## Caveats
- a: OEWS excludes self-employed people, an important potential component of consulting firms.
- a: No public source measured AI-exposed task share specifically for the retained 541690 population.
- rho: This is a five-year operating judgment, not an observed adoption rate.
- rho: Safety, security, and clinical work may require more human review than economic or report-heavy advisory work.
- e: No public dataset reports repeat-revenue share and normalized EBITDA jointly for NAICS 541690.
- e: The injected firm-count estimate already supplies the size bridge and is not re-estimated here.
- s5: Service-sector transactions in S6 include businesses outside this NAICS code and may be much smaller than the lens population.
- s5: The estimate excludes closures and reorganizations without a transferable operating business.
- q: No public source provides billing-model or realized pass-through shares for the frozen lens.
- q: This estimate excludes volume loss, which belongs in demand durability.
- d5: Employment is not a direct measure of constant-price, constant-quality service quantity.
- d5: The BLS projection is broader than 541690 and spans ten years rather than the exact five-year horizon.
- o: This is not a prediction of firm survival; it is the share of current service quantity still needing an accountable provider.
- o: The retained subsegments have materially different regulatory and liability requirements.
