## Business-model lens
- Included: Lower-middle-market firms in NAICS 541614 that provide repeat outsourced operating, process-improvement, inventory, distribution, warehouse-utilization, transportation, or materials-management advice to external customers.
- Excluded: Captive internal advisory units, shell entities, non-control financing vehicles, one-off engagements without a repeat-service relationship, engineering design, computer-systems integration, freight arrangement, and warehousing operations.
- Customer and revenue model: External business customers; revenue is principally project, retainer, recurring assessment, or repeat advisory fees rather than freight, warehouse, or software-license revenue.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
This industry supplies external process and logistics advice, with work that combines AI-addressable analysis and documentation with client-specific operational judgment. The opportunity depends on preserving repeat-service relationships while changing how analysis is produced. [S1][S2]

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate information gathering, data analysis, draft recommendations, manuals, and report review. It is less able to replace onsite observation, stakeholder interviews, accountability for operating changes, and the validation required when recommendations affect customer operations. [S2]

## Value capture
Commercial retention is constrained by proposal-based, hourly, and project billing: customers may receive more deliverables or seek renewal repricing. Fixed scope, repeat assessments, proprietary benchmarks, and execution support should retain more benefit than interchangeable analytical projects. [S4]

## Firm availability
The NAICS definition is broadly external advisory work, but the screen excludes nonrepeat, captive, and adjacent operating businesses. Broad small-business succession evidence indicates owner-age pressure, while also warning that many firms lack a plan or buyer; a NAICS-specific transaction series is the critical missing evidence. [S1][S3]

## Demand durability
Demand is supported by the continuing need to improve efficiency and by logistics complexity associated with e-commerce, inventory, multiple supply chains, and delivery timeliness. These are occupational proxies rather than a direct forecast of outsourced 541614 service quantity. [S4][S5]

## Risks and uncertainty
The code spans process improvement and logistics advice with varying repeatability, data access, and billing. AI may reduce billable hours faster than it reduces needed expertise, and software may displace standardized diagnostic work. Firm-level service mix, contracts, and transaction data would materially narrow the uncertainty.
