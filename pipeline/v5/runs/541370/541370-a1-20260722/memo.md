## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent US firms providing repeat or recurring land, boundary, construction, topographic, aerial, GIS, photogrammetric, or mapping services to external customers in the lower-middle-market band.
- Excluded: Captive owner, contractor, utility, or government units; non-control financing vehicles; geophysical surveying; software-only mapping vendors; and firms outside the lower-middle-market band.
- Customer and revenue model: Project, task-order, hourly, unit, and occasionally retainer revenue from developers, contractors, utilities, governments, and property owners; repeat work is retained only where a firm continues to be outsourced.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Surveying and mapping combines repeat outsourced professional services with significant office-processing work, but its core legal and field-accountability functions constrain full automation. The evidence supports a measured opportunity rather than a purely software-substitution thesis.

## How AI changes the work
AI and adjacent automation can assist record extraction, field-data coding, point-cloud and imagery processing, drafting, map revision, QA triage, reporting, and scheduling. Precise field measurement, resolving conflicts among records and site conditions, and licensed certification remain key human tasks.

## Value capture
A mixed project, task-order, hourly, and unit-priced book permits retention of some efficiency on fixed-price work, while competitive bids, public procurement, and renewals pressure the operator to share savings with customers. Contract review is essential before treating technical savings as margin.

## Firm availability
Surveyor openings attributable in part to retirement and recent practice acquisitions indicate succession and transaction activity, but neither is a denominator for LMM control transfers. The transfer estimate therefore remains deliberately broad.

## Demand durability
Boundary certification, construction-site review, infrastructure maintenance, and mapping needs create durable operator demand. BLS projects 4 percent surveyor employment growth from 2024 to 2034, while explicitly noting that drones and related technology may limit employment growth.

## Risks and uncertainty
The best available occupation mix is a four-digit architecture-and-engineering proxy rather than 541370. Construction and property-market cyclicality, state licensing differences, mixed-firm classification, subcontractor use, and scarce contract-form and transaction data are material uncertainties.

## Caveats
- a: The available industry occupation mix is NAICS 541300, not 541370.
- a: No source measures current task-level AI substitution for the frozen lens.
- a: The injected compensation-to-receipts ratio may be distorted because owner labor and subcontractors are not consistently represented in payroll.
- rho: State licensing and client acceptance differ materially.
- rho: This excludes demand, pricing, and valuation effects by construction.
- e: No public source measures lens eligibility among the injected LMM count.
- e: Six-digit classification may not isolate mixed engineering-and-survey practices cleanly.
- s5: Occupation exits are not business-owner retirements.
- s5: Press releases identify completed transactions but do not provide a denominator or represent the LMM population.
- s5: Deaths, closures, internal reorganizations, and minority investments are excluded from the construct but not measured here.
- q: No public industry source reports contract-form shares or AI-related repricing.
- q: Retention will vary sharply between regulated boundary work, construction support, government work, and commodity mapping.
- d5: The source is an occupation projection, not six-digit industry output or quantity demand.
- d5: The source's horizon is ten years and includes employment in other industries.
- o: Licensure protects only work within regulated scope and does not guarantee the same firm type supplies it.
- o: Noncertified aerial, GIS, and visualization work may face faster software substitution than boundary surveys.
