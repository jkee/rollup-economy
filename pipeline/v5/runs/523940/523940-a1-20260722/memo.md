## Business-model lens
- Included: LMM operating firms supplying recurring outsourced portfolio-management or investment-advice services to external investor clients.
- Excluded: Captive family offices and internal investment units, shells, control-financing vehicles, execution-only broker activity, and firms without a recurring or repeat external service relationship.
- Customer and revenue model: External individual or institutional clients receiving recurring advice, monitoring, discretionary management, or planning; revenue is commonly an advisory fee, AUM-based fee, retainer, flat fee, commission, or performance-based fee where permitted.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
This is a recurring, relationship-heavy financial service with a large small-adviser population, active transfer market, and constrained but real workflow automation potential. The code combines retail and institutional practices, so LMM diligence must distinguish portable recurring client relationships from founder-dependent books and non-target entities.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate investment research, meeting preparation, draft communications, account monitoring, documentation, reporting, and internal knowledge retrieval. It is less able to replace accountable, client-specific recommendations, relationship trust, sales, and supervised fiduciary judgment; SEC actions over false AI claims reinforce the need for controlled deployment.

## Value capture
Advisers must disclose compensation, fee schedules, and fee negotiability in Form ADV materials. That supports retention where recurring fees are set contractually, but visible pricing, competitive alternatives, and renewal discussions can return some savings to clients or require reinvestment in service and compliance.

## Firm availability
The IAA reports that 92.8 percent of advisers had 100 or fewer employees in 2025 and that individual-client advisers averaged eight employees, indicating a deep small-practice population. DeVoe recorded active RIA transactions, but its data show buyer attention and deal volume concentrating above smaller sellers, so transferability for the target LMM band is uncertain.

## Demand durability
BLS projects 10 percent growth in personal-financial-advisor employment from 2024 to 2034, citing aging clients, longer retirements, and individual retirement saving. It also expects robo-advisors to temper routine demand while complex and specialized advice continues to use human advisers, supporting a modest-growth demand view rather than a software-elimination view.

## Risks and uncertainty
No public source directly measures 523940 LMM EBITDA, AI-exposed wage-weighted tasks, implementation, qualifying control transfers, savings pass-through, or constant-quality service quantity. Regulatory supervision, client portability, market-sensitive AUM fees, heterogeneous business models, and an M&A market favoring larger sellers can all materially change the outcome.

- a: No source measures current AI exposure or wage shares for the frozen LMM lens.
- rho: This is an operational-adoption judgment, not a forecast of AI vendor revenue or client acceptance.

- e: Employee count and AUM are weak substitutes for normalized EBITDA.
- e: S1 is an investment-adviser population, whereas NAICS 523940 also includes portfolio-management businesses with different economics.
- s5: AUM thresholds are not EBITDA thresholds.
- s5: Transaction announcements can omit private, internal, and failed processes and do not by themselves identify control transfers.
- q: The SEC disclosure source identifies fee schedules and negotiability but does not measure savings pass-through.

- d5: The forecast is national and not limited to LMM firms or outsourced providers.
