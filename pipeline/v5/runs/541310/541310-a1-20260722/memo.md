## Business-model lens
- Included: US architectural-services firms in the lower-middle-market band that provide recurring or repeat external design, documentation, permitting, contract-administration, and site-observation services to commercial, institutional, public, or repeat residential-development customers.
- Excluded: One-off consumer design work without repeat-service characteristics, captive owner/developer design teams, pure drafting businesses, landscape architecture, engineering, construction contracting, software vendors, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Project fees, retainers, and repeat engagements from external building owners, developers, institutions, and public-sector customers; revenue is commonly tied to scoped design and construction-administration phases rather than a perpetual software subscription.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Architectural services combines digital production work with licensed, client-facing, and site-accountable professional services. The credible AI opportunity is meaningful but constrained by professional review and a currently soft project pipeline. S3, S4, S7.

## How AI changes the work
AI can assist drawings, specifications, cost and schedule information, contract documents, reports, and proposals. It is less able to replace the accountable judgment embedded in client consultation, code compliance, consultant coordination, contract administration, and site observation. S2, S3.

## Value capture
Scoped project fees leave room to retain capacity and rework gains, but competitive procurement and future fee negotiations can pass productivity to customers. The evidence found does not quantify that pass-through for lower-middle-market architectural firms.

## Firm availability
Succession and ownership transition are established architecture-firm concerns, and AEC M&A activity was active in 2025. However, aggregate AEC evidence is not a reliable measurement of qualifying control transfers among architectural-services firms in the specified EBITDA band. S5, S6.

## Demand durability
BLS projects modest architect employment growth over 2024 to 2034, but AIA reported below-neutral billings, weak new work, and delayed or canceled projects. Demand appears operator-required in many regulated and complex projects, while the near-term cycle remains a risk. S3, S7.

## Risks and uncertainty
The principal uncertainties are project-cycle exposure, the extent of customer price pass-through, principal dependence in small practices, and the lack of public NAICS-specific data on AI task savings, eligible firm recurrence, and qualifying transfers. AIA’s current adoption survey also shows that staffing effects have been limited so far. S4.

## Caveats
- a: No source measures AI-substitutable, wage-weighted tasks for NAICS 541310.
- a: The task source is for architects rather than the full industry staffing mix, which also includes administrative and technical roles.
- a: Current staffing effects do not measure the five-year technical potential.
- rho: The AIA statistic is a respondent survey and is about past-year staffing, not five-year workflow implementation.
- rho: State licensing rules and customer acceptance differ materially across projects and jurisdictions.
- e: No public source was located that measures lens eligibility for the injected lower-middle-market firm population.
- e: NAICS classification does not identify customer recurrence or transferable operating independence.
- s5: AEC deal counts and valuations are not a NAICS 541310 lower-middle-market transfer probability.
- s5: An internal employee purchase can preserve operations but may not be a qualifying external control transfer.
- q: No public, industry-wide evidence was located that measures contractual pass-through of AI productivity for the frozen lens.
- q: Billing model and customer bargaining power vary sharply by specialty, geography, and project phase.
- d5: Architect employment is not identical to industry output or demand and the BLS horizon is ten years, not five.
- d5: ABI is a near-term diffusion survey and does not supply a constant-price quantity forecast.
- o: No source directly measures the future share of NAICS 541310 service quantity that still requires an external accountable operator.
- o: The result can differ greatly between code-intensive institutional work and low-complexity residential projects.
