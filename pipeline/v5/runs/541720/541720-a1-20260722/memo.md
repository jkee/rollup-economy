**Driver:** A large share of the operating work is digital research production, data handling, analysis, coordination, and reporting rather than physical delivery.
**Weakness:** The NAICS code is heterogeneous and no observed LMM data establish either transferable-firm supply or durable retention of AI-derived savings.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent firms conducting repeat commissioned social-science, behavioral, economic, policy, survey, evaluation, and humanities research for external government, nonprofit, and commercial customers.
- Excluded: University and captive research units, grant-funded research without an external service customer, clinical or counseling service delivery, one-off self-directed research, shells, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: Repeat external research engagements, task orders, panels, program evaluations, surveys, and analytical deliverables, commonly priced as fixed-fee, time-and-materials, or reimbursable projects.
- Deviation from default lens: Narrowed to repeat commissioned research contractors because the code's observed occupation mix includes counseling, clinical, education, fundraising, and health-service roles alongside research; these are not one coherent outsourced research-service model.

## Executive view
The narrowed opportunity is repeat commissioned research contractors, not the full code. The work is labor-intensive and digitally mediated enough for AI assistance, but transferability and value retention are constrained by project contracting, customer procurement, sensitive data, key personnel, and a heterogeneous source industry.

## How AI changes the work
OEWS reports a research-heavy occupational mix: life, physical, and social-science occupations account for 31.50 percent of employment, business and financial operations 15.17 percent, computer and mathematical occupations 10.15 percent, and office support 12.70 percent. O*NET identifies data preparation, statistical analysis, report production, database work, data entry, and quality procedures in social-science research assistance. AI can accelerate these tasks, but field collection, research design, inference, and accountable review remain human-led.

## Value capture
Project-based, hourly, and reimbursable contracts create material customer pass-through and rebidding risk. Fixed-fee repeat work offers more temporary retention, but a buyer should underwrite value only from contract-level evidence on price form, renewal dates, labor categories, and customer concentration.

## Firm availability
The injected LMM count should not be treated as the number of eligible targets. The code contains work outside repeat external research services, and transferable contractors must also carry durable customer relationships, operational documentation, and staff beyond a founder. A focused firm-level screen is necessary before treating succession as an observed pipeline.

## Demand durability
BLS projects NAICS 541720 employment to increase from 70.0 thousand in 2024 to 72.6 thousand in 2034, indicating modest underlying support rather than a large demand surge. Outsourced demand should persist where customers need independent methods, regulated or sensitive data handling, stakeholder collection, and accountable interpretation; routine research and analytics can migrate to client teams or software.

## Risks and uncertainty
The largest evidence gaps are transaction incidence, eligibility at the LMM firm level, contract pass-through, and realized AI savings after validation. The BLS and O*NET evidence covers broader populations than the lens, while AI adoption evidence is not industry-specific. Government contract consents, privacy restrictions, human-subject protocols, and key-person dependency can alter both implementation and transfer outcomes.
