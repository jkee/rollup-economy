**Driver:** AI can streamline a meaningful share of documentation, visualization, specification, and coordination work while repeat-client firms retain an accountable design role.
**Weakness:** The industry's project-based, sometimes owner-personal revenue and lack of direct firm-level transfer evidence limit confidence in target availability and benefit retention.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent lower-middle-market interior-design firms that sell repeat or recurring outsourced planning, design, specification, and project-administration services to external residential, commercial, hospitality, healthcare, or institutional clients.
- Excluded: One-off decorating-only engagements without a repeat-service relationship; captive in-house design teams; furniture retailers' internal design desks; architectural firms classified elsewhere; and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: External clients purchase project-based professional services, commonly billed as fixed or staged design fees, hourly consulting, procurement-related fees, or a combination; repeat work comes from developers, operators, architects, and returning homeowners rather than a subscription model.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Interior Design Services contains repeat-service firms, but the industry also contains episodic decorating and owner-personal practices. AI is more likely to compress production and coordination work than to replace accountable design leadership, making the opportunity dependent on disciplined workflow adoption and client portability.

## How AI changes the work
BLS lists client discovery, preliminary plans, material specification, cost estimates, ordering, installation oversight, and contractor coordination as core duties (S3). Generative and workflow tools can assist with drafts, visualizations, specifications, research, and routine communication, while code awareness, site conditions, aesthetic judgment, and stakeholder alignment retain a human control point.

## Value capture
Professional design fees can allow a firm to hold productivity gains within an agreed scope, but competitive bidding and renewal repricing can return part of the benefit to clients. Procurement and installation-administration work further make capture engagement-specific rather than uniform.

## Firm availability
The relevant target set is limited to firms with repeat external clients and transferable operating practices. BLS reports that self-employed workers account for 21.2 percent of interior designers, a signal of owner-led work but not evidence that a particular firm is saleable or will transfer (S3).

## Demand durability
BLS projects national interior-designer employment to grow 3 percent from 2024 to 2034 and cites renovation, building codes, and accessibility as demand supports (S3). That is a broad occupation proxy, not a direct forecast of six-digit industry demand, and discretionary projects remain cyclically exposed.

## Risks and uncertainty
The evidence is strongest on occupational duties and broad employment; it is weak on firm-level recurring revenue, transaction rates, contractual pass-through, and realized AI labor savings. Six-digit firms also vary from aesthetic decorating to complex code-sensitive project administration, so diligence must segment revenue and workflow rather than rely on the NAICS label.

## Caveats
- a: The OEWS source is NAICS 541400, Specialized Design Services, rather than six-digit NAICS 541410 and excludes self-employed workers.
- a: No source directly measures task-level AI substitution for this industry, so the allocation is bounded judgment rather than an observed automation rate.
- rho: Implementation will differ sharply between decorating-led residential studios and firms that produce permit or construction documents.
- rho: This estimate excludes demand, pricing, and valuation effects.
- e: No public source found that reports recurring or repeat revenue specifically for six-digit NAICS 541410 lower-middle-market firms.
- e: Eligibility is a firm characteristic and should not be inferred from establishment counts alone.
- s5: Self-employment is an occupation-wide statistic, not a measure of ownership or sale propensity for qualifying firms.
- s5: The estimate excludes closures without transferable operations and internal reorganizations by definition.
- q: No public, industry-specific evidence was located for fee pass-through or realized pricing after AI adoption.
- q: The estimate concerns retention of an implemented gross benefit and does not include volume loss or implementation difficulty.
- d5: Employment is not a direct measure of constant-price, constant-quality service quantity.
- d5: The occupation projection spans employers outside NAICS 541410 and runs over ten years rather than the specified horizon.
- o: The operator-required boundary varies by state licensing rules, project risk, client sophistication, and whether the work is decorating or construction administration.
- o: This is a forward-looking judgment; no identified source directly quantifies future displacement by software.
