# 541320 — Landscape Architectural Services

**Driver:** A substantial share of documentation and design-production work can be assisted while accountable site and client work remains operator-led.
**Weakness:** The service is project-based and principal-dependent, making transferability and realized retention difficult to establish without firm-level diligence.

## Business-model lens
- Included: US lower-middle-market firms providing repeat or recurring outsourced landscape architecture, site planning, land-use planning, garden design, golf-course design, or landscape consulting to external customers.
- Excluded: Landscape maintenance and installation, nurseries and garden centers, captive design units, shells, non-control financing vehicles, and firms without a repeat or recurring external-service relationship.
- Customer and revenue model: Professional design, planning, consulting, documentation, and construction-administration services sold to public agencies, developers, institutions, businesses, and repeat private clients through project, term, hourly, fixed-fee, or hybrid engagements.
- Deviation from default lens: none

## Executive view
Landscape architectural services combine repeatable professional production work with a licensed, site-specific accountability layer. The code includes landscape design, planning, consulting, and golf-course design, while maintenance and installation sit outside it (S1).

## How AI changes the work
AI can assist drawings, specifications, visualizations, estimates, research, and proposals, but it does not remove the need to assess a site, coordinate stakeholders, inspect work, or make accountable design decisions (S2, S3).

## Value capture
Retention is constrained by the contract book. A current municipal term solicitation requires an hourly-rate schedule whose rates remain fixed during each project, illustrating customer visibility into labor pricing; fixed-fee scopes can retain more productivity until repricing (S5).

## Firm availability
Transferability is uncertain because client relationships, licensed principals, and small-team reputation can be central to delivery. The Annual Business Survey provides a route to owner-characteristic diligence, but this packet does not treat it as direct transfer evidence (S4).

## Demand durability
BLS projects positive real output for the broader architectural, engineering, and related-services industry and positive employment for landscape architects. Demand also remains connected to outdoor development, water conservation, and environmental planning, although project cycles are material (S3, S6).

## Risks and uncertainty
The evidence is strongest on task content and weakest on exact firm transfer rates, contract-mix shares, and NAICS-specific real demand. Broader-industry and occupation sources can misstate the economics of small landscape architecture practices.
