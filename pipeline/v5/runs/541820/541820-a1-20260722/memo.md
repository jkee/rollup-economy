# 541820 — Public Relations Agencies

*v5 Stage 1 research memo. Run `541820-a1-20260722`, model `gpt-5.6-terra`, 2026-07-22, attempt 1.*

**Base tier:** PRIORITY · A 6.36 · L 5.51 · interval LOW_PRIORITY → HIGHEST_PRIORITY · readiness MODEL_CONDITIONED · action VALIDATE_ASSUMPTIONS

**Driver:** A large share of recurring PR workflow is information production and coordination that can be AI-assisted, while reputation-sensitive advisory work preserves an operator role.
**Weakness:** Hourly and project pricing can transfer AI productivity to clients, and the code's political and one-off work complicates both recurring-revenue eligibility and transferability.

## Business-model lens
- Included: Independent commercial, nonprofit, and public-affairs agencies providing repeat outsourced reputation, earned-media, stakeholder, crisis-communications, and lobbying counsel to external clients.
- Excluded: Election-cycle political campaign consulting, isolated one-off campaigns, captive communications teams, media buying, advertising placement, and non-control financing vehicles.
- Customer and revenue model: External organizations buy advisory and execution services through agency-of-record retainers, monthly fees, hourly engagements, or projects; the screen retains only firms with recurring or repeat service revenue and roughly $1–10M normalized EBITDA.
- Deviation from default lens: NAICS 541820 explicitly combines public relations, lobbying, and political consulting. Election-cycle political consulting has a materially different buyer, timing, and repeat-revenue pattern, so it is excluded to make the recurring outsourced-service screen coherent.

## Executive view
The recurring outsourced slice of public-relations agencies combines meaningful AI exposure in communication-production workflows with a client-facing advisory role that remains accountable to reputation and stakeholder outcomes. The code requires a narrow lens because political campaign consulting has different cyclicality and economics.

## How AI changes the work
AI can accelerate drafting, research, pitch preparation, monitoring, summaries, distribution, and administration. BLS's core duty list shows why those workflow components matter, but the same list also highlights client representation, sensitive-issue judgment, speaking, and relationship work that remain hard to substitute (S2, S3).

## Value capture
AI savings are vulnerable where compensation is tied to hours: the cited PR-industry evidence describes time-based revenue and Axios reports 1,685 billed hours per employee in 2022 (S3). Long-lived AOR and retainer relationships can support some retention, but renewal negotiations and competing agencies can pass savings to clients (S4, S7).

## Firm availability
Gallup reports that 22% of employer-business owners intended a sale, transfer, or public offering within five years, while 52.3% of employer businesses had owners at least 55 (S5). PR transaction activity exists, but PRovoke identified fewer deals in 2025 than the prior year and describes a larger-agency tilt, so the relevant LMM control-transfer rate remains uncertain (S6).

## Demand durability
BLS projects public-relations-specialist employment growth over the next decade and connects it to outreach, customer relations, rapid information spread, and social media (S2). That is directional support for demand, not proof that external agencies will retain the work; buyers can move routine tasks in-house or to software.

## Risks and uncertainty
The evidence base does not isolate LMM NAICS 541820 agencies by task mix, contract type, EBITDA, transfer completion, or outsourced demand. The chief commercial risk is that AI compresses billable effort faster than agencies can repackage strategy and accountable counsel into priced outcomes.

## Caveats
- a: No source measures wage-weighted task exposure specifically for NAICS 541820 LMM agencies.
- a: The cited AI evidence is a leader survey and describes potential task efficiency, not realized labor removal.
- rho: Expected task efficiency is not equivalent to operational implementation or avoidable hiring.
- rho: Regulated public affairs and crisis work may have lower adoption than general commercial PR.
- e: The Census definition does not publish the revenue share of political consulting or one-off work.
- e: Eligibility is conditional on the injected LMM-band count and is not an establishment-count estimate.
- s5: Intentions are not completed transfers and Gallup is not industry specific.
- s5: PRovoke's identified-deal count has unknown geographic coverage, firm-size coverage, and denominator.
- q: The pricing survey covers branding, creative, digital, marketing, PR, social-media, and web agencies rather than PR alone.
- q: Relationship tenure does not prove that an agency retains AI-created savings at renewal.
- d5: Occupational employment is not PR-agency revenue or constant-quality demand.
- d5: Only 13% of public-relations specialists worked in the broader advertising, public relations, and related-services industry in the cited BLS profile.
- o: No source measures the share of NAICS 541820 service quantity that will remain operator supplied after five years.
- o: Crisis, public-affairs, and commodity-content engagements can have materially different substitution rates.
