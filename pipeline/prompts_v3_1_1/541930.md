## What you author

Write one JSON object conforming exactly to `pipeline/build/schemas/research_draft_v3_1_1.schema.json`. You author reusable atomic `evidence_facts` and selected research/judgment inputs. You do **not** author final provenance, deterministic dataset values, role weights, role contributions, factor scores, `S`, uplift or a verdict. Plain Python owns those fields.

Every selected input has a `method`:

- `OBSERVED` — `value` repeats a value stated by a cited fact exactly. Include the identical `observed_value`. No interpretation, proxy, normalization or judgmental bridge is permitted.
- `CALCULATED` — `value` is fully reproducible arithmetic over named numeric operands, every operand is backed by a cited fact, and the expression uses only numeric constants, parentheses and `+ - * /`. Include every operand's `name`, `value` and `fact_id`. Python recomputes the result. Any judgmental mapping makes this method invalid.
- `ESTIMATE` — required whenever any bridge from facts to the selected value is judgmental, including proxy choice, population mapping, normalization assumptions or an opinion. Its structured `estimate_basis` must enumerate every starting fact ID, every scope mismatch, each ordered proxy/normalization/population step, and the explicit judgmental step reaching the selected value. A cited background fact does not turn an estimate into `OBSERVED` or `CALCULATED`.

Python maps `OBSERVED → DIRECT`, `CALCULATED → DERIVED`, and `ESTIMATE → ESTIMATE`. Never author `provenance_type` in the draft. `evidence_quality` is a separate reliability axis: `HIGH | MED | LOW | NONE`. Empty `fact_ids` requires `NONE`; non-empty `fact_ids` requires non-`NONE` quality.

## Citation discipline — C1 to C4

1. **C1 — Fetch before cite.** Open and inspect the exact page before creating a fact. Search snippets, titles and memory are not evidence.
2. **C2 — Quote what you saw.** Each fact contains one short exact contiguous quote or table figure. Compact (`...`), spaced (`. . .`) and Unicode (`…`) ellipses are forbidden. Never splice separated passages.
3. **C3 — Attribute only what the URL establishes.** Never use a URL as support for a claim it does not establish. If no fetched source supports a background fact, omit the fact and label the selected value honestly as an estimate.
4. **C4 — Reopen and verify.** Before submission, enumerate and reopen every URL and verify its quote, attribution and all structured scope fields against the live page.

`evidence_facts[]` is the only place URLs may appear. Use stable unique IDs `F1`, `F2`, ... and reuse a fact by ID instead of duplicating its URL/quote. Every scope must state `population`, `geography`, `unit`, `period` and `industry_mismatch`. If a source omits any scope element, write exactly `not reported` for that field; never infer it silently.

## Frozen research rules

1. PE/M&A activity informs only B and M, never V or A.
2. Multiples are EV/EBITDA after market-rate owner compensation. State unit, target size, source population and every normalization. A normalization or market mapping that requires judgment is `ESTIMATE`.
3. A zero consolidator count is an absence-of-evidence estimate, not an observation. State the searches performed; do not cite a topical page as proving zero.
4. `current_adoption_pct` means the share of US firms in this NAICS materially using AI in automatable functions. Tool use, worker use and broad-sector use are different populations. Any judgmental mapping to the target population is `ESTIMATE`.
5. `t50_years` means years from today until 50% material firm adoption. If current adoption is already at least 50%, set it to `0`; otherwise it must be positive. Forecasting or analog-based bridging is normally `ESTIMATE`, even when supported by observed adoption facts.
6. Payer/regulatory repricing belongs in C when it determines who retains cost savings. Terminal class separately answers whether paid end demand survives mature AI; do not count the same clawback twice.
7. A statutory human-in-the-loop boundary affects terminal class only to the extent it preserves paid demand. It does not make automatable tasks non-automatable.
8. If a W-2 dataset labor share is distorted by 1099-heavy labor, use the injected value unchanged, flag `dataset_mismatch`, lower confidence as appropriate and show sensitivity. Do not silently adjust it.
9. Catch-all codes: identify at least the top three revenue subsegments, score the dominant subsegment, set `heterogeneous=true`, and complete the structured market-boundary disclosure.
10. `succession_shortage_documented` changes B. It is a full boolean selection under the same method/fact contract. `true` requires `OBSERVED`, at least one fact that directly documents a shortage, and exact live verification. A search-based `false` is normally `ESTIMATE` + `NONE`, with the searches and absence judgment fully disclosed in `estimate_basis`.

## Industry-specific guidance — hints, not facts

**Likely role structure:**
- **Staff interpreters and translators** — ~20-30% of W-2 wages (hypothesis; freelance linguists sit in COGS) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 27-3091 Interpreters and Translators 2.1% of wage bill in the record's 4-digit 5419 mix — but that mix is dominated by veterinary occupations from sibling 541940 (veterinarians 17.1%, vet techs 9.2%) and is unusable as a proxy; within an actual LSP the linguist share of the W-2 wage bill is far higher, and most production linguists are 1099 freelancers who never appear in OEWS wages at all); automatable: partial. Written translation is the most AI-displaced task in the economy — MT post-editing (MTPE) is already the default workflow at documented 30-50% per-word discounts; what persists is certified/sworn translation, high-liability legal/medical work, and transcreation. On-site and live interpretation is physical/real-time and holds longer, though AI speech translation is closing in on remote modalities.
- **Project managers and localization engineers** — ~25-35% (hypothesis) of wage bill (OEWS basis: not in the contaminated top-10 — PMs are the operational core of every language services company; hypothesis est. ~25-35% of W-2 wages); automatable: partial. File prep, TM/termbase management, vendor assignment, QA passes and delivery are being automated end-to-end by TMS platforms with AI orchestration; client-side program management and escalation persist.
- **Interpretation scheduling and operations (OPI/VRI/on-site dispatch)** — ~10-15% (hypothesis) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-4051 Customer Service Representatives 2.3% in the contaminated mix; real interpreting firms carry large scheduling/dispatch teams — hypothesis est. ~10-15%); automatable: yes. - **Sales and account management** — ~10-15% (hypothesis) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-3091 Sales Reps of Services 3.3% in the contaminated mix; hypothesis est. ~10-15%); automatable: partial. RFP responses and quoting automate; healthcare-system, court and enterprise localization relationships persist and are the firm's real asset.
- **Management** — ~8-10% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 6.6% (one of the few plausible signals in the 5419 proxy)); automatable: partial. Capacity planning and margin management automate substantially; compliance ownership (HIPAA, court certification) persists.
- **Admin and back office** — ~10-15% of wage bill (OEWS basis: long tail of admin occupations, est. remainder ~10-15%); automatable: yes. Invoicing (per-word/per-minute reconciliation), freelancer payables and compliance paperwork automate near-fully.

**Capture questions:**
- Translation is priced per word and interpreting per minute/hour — MTPE discount schedules (30-50% off per-word rates) already show AI savings being passed to clients as lower prices, not kept as margin; what evidence shows any LSP holding price while its production cost collapses, and does interpreting's per-minute pricing follow the same deflation path as AI speech translation matures?
- Who owns the client relationship — the LSP (contracted language-access programs with hospital systems, courts, insurers) or the freelance linguist the end user actually sees? Which contracts (statewide court contracts, health-system language-access agreements) are institutional and transferable in an acquisition?
- What stops enterprise clients from bypassing LSPs with LLM translation plus an in-house reviewer — and for which revenue lines (certified translations for USCIS/courts, sworn documents, regulated medical device labeling under ISO 17100-type requirements) does a certification or liability gate genuinely block the bypass?
- In mandated interpreting (Section 1557 healthcare, court access), the payer often reimburses per encounter at set rates — when AI cuts the LSP's delivery cost, do public payers and hospital procurement re-rate the contracts downward at renewal, clawing back the savings?

**Terminal-value question:**
This is the canonical melter test: LLMs produce the product itself — translated text — at near-zero marginal cost, so the question is precisely which revenue survives as paid demand: statutorily mandated live interpretation (Section 1557 healthcare encounters, court certification requirements, ADA/deaf services), certification-gated document translation (USCIS, sworn/legal), and liability-bearing regulated content — versus ordinary business translation, which is already collapsing into MT-with-review at falling per-word rates. A researcher who scores this code on current LSP revenue growth (interpreting demand is still rising on demographics and enforcement) without separating the mandate-protected floor from the substitutable bulk will get terminal value wrong in either direction. What fraction of this operator's revenue sits behind a statute or certification, and what happens to the rest at mature AI?

**Known traps:**
- Role-mix contamination is total: the 4-digit 5419 proxy is veterinary-dominated (541940 is the giant sibling); 27-3091 Interpreters and Translators shows only 2.1%. Rebuild the V breakdown from LSP structure (linguists/PMs/scheduling), and note that most production linguists are 1099 freelancers — their cost sits in COGS, not the wage bill, so the 0.4178 labor share understates the true language-labor content AI attacks. This materially changes V: AI savings land partly in gross margin (freelance spend), not just opex.
- Pricing-model deflation is documented, not hypothetical: per-word pricing with MTPE discount tiers is the industry standard, and per-word rates have been falling for a decade. Model pi_dist with the per-word/per-minute rate-card channel explicit; the operator keeps savings only where contracts are fixed-fee or mandate-protected.
- Segment split is everything: interpreting (esp. healthcare OPI/VRI and court) is growing, mandate-protected and physically/real-time delivered; written translation is the melting half. Blended firm-level financials mask this — insist the run separates the two before scoring V, A or terminal value.
- Consolidation is active and well covered (Propio, Sorenson Communications, Big Language Solutions, TransPerfect, LanguageLine/Teleperformance, court-contract roll-ups) — usable for B and M only, never as evidence for V or A (rule 2). Slator reports deal multiples; use those rather than generic services comps.
- EBITDA normalization: LSP gross margins run 40-50% over freelance COGS; small-agency 'profit' includes owner comp (SDE). Also watch revenue recognition on interpreting minutes vs. translation projects when comparing margins across firms.
- Boundary confusion: sign-language interpreting providers (Sorenson) straddle this code and telecom relay services funded by the FCC TRS fund; localization technology platforms (TMS vendors) are software 511210/513210; captioning/subtitling straddles 541930 and 512191 — Nimdzi/Slator market sizes include tech revenue; strip it before mapping to the code's $6.1B receipts.
- Structure note: 2,193 of 2,674 firms are <5 employees and n_band is only 28 (the 100-499 class) — the roll-up universe is thin, and the biggest firms (15 firms, 500+, avg $211M revenue) are already consolidator-owned. Golden-set melter anchor: be conservative about cost-savings framing; substitution dominates.

## Frozen formulas — Python calculates them

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`
- `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)`
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = 10` when `t50_years=0`, otherwise `min(10, 10 / t50_years)`
- `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)`
- `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; add 0.1 for documented succession shortage, cap at 1
- `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
- `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`
