## What you author

Write one JSON object conforming exactly to `pipeline/build/schemas/research_draft_v3_1_1.schema.json`. You author reusable atomic `evidence_facts` and selected research/judgment inputs. You do **not** author final provenance, deterministic dataset values, role weights, role contributions, factor scores, `S`, uplift or a verdict. Plain Python owns those fields.

Every selected input has a `method`:

- `OBSERVED` — `value` repeats a value stated by a cited fact exactly. Include the identical `observed_value`. No interpretation, proxy, normalization or judgmental bridge is permitted.
- `CALCULATED` — `value` is fully reproducible arithmetic over named numeric operands, every operand is backed by a cited fact, and the expression uses only numeric constants, parentheses and `+ - * /`. Include every operand's `name`, `value` and `fact_id`. Python recomputes the result. Any judgmental mapping makes this method invalid.
- `ESTIMATE` — required whenever any bridge from facts to the selected value is judgmental, including proxy choice, population mapping, normalization assumptions or an opinion. Its structured `estimate_basis` must enumerate every starting fact ID, every scope mismatch, each ordered proxy/normalization/population step, and the explicit judgmental step reaching the selected value. A cited background fact does not turn an estimate into `OBSERVED` or `CALCULATED`.

Python maps `OBSERVED → DIRECT`, `CALCULATED → DERIVED`, and `ESTIMATE → ESTIMATE`. Never author `provenance_type` in the draft. `evidence_quality` is a separate reliability axis: `HIGH | MED | LOW | NONE`. Empty `fact_ids` requires `NONE`; non-empty `fact_ids` requires non-`NONE` quality.

## Citation discipline — C1 to C4

1. **C1 — Fetch before cite.** Open and inspect the exact page before creating a fact. Search snippets, titles and memory are not evidence.
2. **C2 — Quote what you saw.** Each fact contains one short exact contiguous quote or table figure. Compact (`...`), spaced (`. . .`) and Unicode (`…`) ellipses are forbidden. Never splice separated passages.
3. **C3 — Attribute only what the URL establishes.** Never use a URL as support for a claim it does not establish. If no fetched source supports a background fact, omit the fact and label the selected value honestly as an estimate.
4. **C4 — Reopen and verify.** Before submission, enumerate and reopen every URL and verify its quote, attribution and all structured scope fields against the live page.

`evidence_facts[]` is the only place URLs may appear. Use stable unique IDs `F1`, `F2`, ... and reuse a fact by ID instead of duplicating its URL/quote. Every scope must state `population`, `geography`, `unit`, `period` and `industry_mismatch`. If a source omits any scope element, write exactly `not reported` for that field; never infer it silently.

## Frozen research rules

1. PE/M&A activity informs only B and M, never V or A.
2. Multiples are EV/EBITDA after market-rate owner compensation. State unit, target size, source population and every normalization. A normalization or market mapping that requires judgment is `ESTIMATE`.
3. A zero consolidator count is an absence-of-evidence estimate, not an observation. State the searches performed; do not cite a topical page as proving zero.
4. `current_adoption_pct` means the share of US firms in this NAICS materially using AI in automatable functions. Tool use, worker use and broad-sector use are different populations. Any judgmental mapping to the target population is `ESTIMATE`.
5. `t50_years` means years from today until 50% material firm adoption. If current adoption is already at least 50%, set it to `0`; otherwise it must be positive. Forecasting or analog-based bridging is normally `ESTIMATE`, even when supported by observed adoption facts.
6. Payer/regulatory repricing belongs in C when it determines who retains cost savings. Terminal class separately answers whether paid end demand survives mature AI; do not count the same clawback twice.
7. A statutory human-in-the-loop boundary affects terminal class only to the extent it preserves paid demand. It does not make automatable tasks non-automatable.
8. If a W-2 dataset labor share is distorted by 1099-heavy labor, use the injected value unchanged, flag `dataset_mismatch`, lower confidence as appropriate and show sensitivity. Do not silently adjust it.
9. Catch-all codes: identify at least the top three revenue subsegments, score the dominant subsegment, set `heterogeneous=true`, and complete the structured market-boundary disclosure.
10. `succession_shortage_documented` changes B. It is a full boolean selection under the same method/fact contract. `true` requires `OBSERVED`, at least one fact that directly documents a shortage, and exact live verification. A search-based `false` is normally `ESTIMATE` + `NONE`, with the searches and absence judgment fully disclosed in `estimate_basis`.

## Industry-specific guidance — hints, not facts

**Likely role structure:**
- **Court reporters / stenographers (the production core)** — est. 30-50% of W-2 wages, more of true production cost including 1099 payouts — flagged as hypothesis of wage bill (OEWS basis: 23-2011 Court Reporters and Simultaneous Captioners — NOT in the record's listed top-10 because role_mix is a 4-digit 5614 proxy dominated by contact-center occupations; role_mix is effectively unrepresentative for this code — hypothesis: reporters plus freelance equivalents are the dominant production cost, and many are 1099 freelancers outside the W-2 wage bill entirely); automatable: partial. AI speech recognition now produces a rough verbatim draft at near-zero cost; what it cannot yet do is swear witnesses, certify the record, or carry the officer-of-the-court role. The paid human element is compressing toward capture-plus-certification; realtime stenographic skill still commands a premium where rules require it.
- **Transcriptionists, scopists and proofreaders** — est. 10-15% of production cost — hypothesis of wage bill (OEWS basis: 31-9094 Medical Transcriptionists 1.25% appears in the 4-digit proxy; general transcription/scoping sits in the tail and in 1099 freelance networks); automatable: yes. Cleaning ASR drafts into certified transcripts is exactly what current AI does well; human review shrinks to a QA pass.
- **Scheduling, calendar and production coordination** — est. 15-25% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-4051 Customer Service Representatives 25.9% in the proxy overstates this via contact-center contamination; at 561492 these are calendar coordinators, production and exhibit staff); automatable: yes. Job scheduling, reporter assignment, transcript production tracking and delivery are routine coordination — near-fully automatable.
- **Management and sales** — est. 10-15% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 7.5% + 41-3091 Sales Representatives of Services 4.0% (proxy shares)); automatable: partial. Law-firm relationship management and litigation-support selling persist; internal ops reporting automates.
- **Captioning / CART realtime staff** — est. 5-10% — hypothesis of wage bill (OEWS basis: within 23-2011 (captioners) — not separately listed; this code also houses realtime captioning services); automatable: partial. ADA-driven live captioning demand is growing and quality-sensitive; ASR captioning is already substituting at the low end (education, webinars) while legal/broadcast realtime holds longer.

**Suggested sources:**
- (adoption) National Court Reporters Association (NCRA) — workforce and industry data — The stenographic trade association; shortage statistics, certification counts, and its advocacy materials reveal where ASR/digital reporting is displacing stenographers.
- (adoption) AAERT (American Association of Electronic Reporters and Transcribers) — The digital-reporting association — its certification growth is direct evidence of the product-replacement channel (digital capture + transcription vs live steno).
- (succession_owners) Ducker Worldwide Court Reporting Industry Outlook (the 'Ducker Report', 2013-14, NCRA-commissioned) — The standard source for reporter age structure and the projected shortage — dated; verify whether NCRA has published updated workforce numbers before relying on 2014 figures.

- (capture_pricing) State statutory transcript page-rate schedules and federal court reporter fee schedules (Judicial Conference maximum transcript rates) — Primary documents for the per-page pricing mechanism — statutory rates cap official-work pricing and anchor freelance depo rates.
- (other) Speech-to-Text Institute (STTI) publications on the reporter shortage and digital transition — Vendor-side coalition material quantifying the shortage-driven shift to digital; the organization may have wound down — confirm it still exists before citing. *(existence unverified — confirm this source still exists/publishes before relying on it)*

**Capture questions:**
- Transcripts are priced per page (statutory schedules for official/court work, market rates plus appearance fees for freelance deposition work) — when AI transcription collapses production cost per page, do agencies keep the spread as margin, or do law-firm procurement and digital-first competitors (already marketing lower page rates) bid the page rate down toward the new cost floor?
- Who owns the client: the agency holds the law firm's calendar and preferred-vendor status, but the certified reporter is the officer who appears — freelance reporters routinely take relationships with them. In an acquisition, what book actually transfers, and what does reporter (not client) churn do to it?
- What stops a litigation client from bypassing agencies entirely — remote deposition platforms with built-in ASR plus an AAERT-certified digital reporter, or in some jurisdictions a notarized recording — and which state court rules still make that impossible vs merely unconventional?
- Which states still require a licensed stenographic CSR for depositions vs permit digital/voice-writer reporting, and how fast are those rules moving given the documented reporter shortage — is the regulatory moat widening or draining?

**Terminal-value question:**
The paid product here is a certified verbatim record of a legal proceeding. Mature ASR produces the verbatim text at near-zero marginal cost; what remains purchasable is the oath, the certification, the chain-of-custody liability, and realtime delivery where rules demand it. Do state CSR statutes and court rules keep a paid human certifier in the loop — shrinking the product to a thin certification wrapper around AI transcription — or do rule changes (accelerated by the stenographer shortage itself) let certified-AI/digital workflows replace the product outright? Answer by naming the actual rule mechanism per major jurisdiction, and distinguish agencies cutting their transcription costs (margin story) from clients no longer needing what agencies sell (melt story).

**Known traps:**
- The record's role_mix (4-digit 5614) is dominated by contact-center occupations and omits court reporters entirely — treat it as unrepresentative; build V from the hypothesis structure above and say so in the output.
- Freelance economics: a large share of deposition reporters are 1099 freelancers paid per page/appearance through agencies — the 0.2728 labor_share captures only W-2 staff, and agency P&Ls are intermediary spreads on freelance production; automating production changes the freelancer's income before the agency's.
- Statutory page rates on official/court work cap upside there; the commercial deposition market is where pricing (and deflation) is set.
- Tiny industry: n_total 3,004 firms, $3.1B receipts, n_band only 60 (51 in the 20-99 class, 9 in 100-499) — a single Veritext/USLS acquisition materially changes the universe; single-year data is noisy.
- Captioning/CART (ADA-driven) demand inside this code is growing while deposition steno shrinks — the two sub-segments have opposite trajectories; identify the mix before scoring one number.
- Consolidation by Veritext and US Legal Support is well documented — usable for B and M only, never as evidence of automatability or adoption (rule 2).

## Frozen formulas — Python calculates them

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`
- `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)`
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = 10` when `t50_years=0`, otherwise `min(10, 10 / t50_years)`
- `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)`
- `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; add 0.1 for documented succession shortage, cap at 1
- `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
- `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`
