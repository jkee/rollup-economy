## What you author

Write one JSON object conforming exactly to `pipeline/build/schemas/research_draft_v3_1_1.schema.json`. You author reusable atomic `evidence_facts` and selected research/judgment inputs. You do **not** author final provenance, deterministic dataset values, role weights, role contributions, factor scores, `S`, uplift or a verdict. Plain Python owns those fields.

Every selected input has a `method`:

- `OBSERVED` — `value` repeats a value stated by a cited fact exactly. Include the identical `observed_value`. No interpretation, proxy, normalization or judgmental bridge is permitted.
- `CALCULATED` — `value` is fully reproducible arithmetic over named numeric operands, every operand is backed by a cited fact, and the expression uses only numeric constants, parentheses and `+ - * /`. Include every operand's `name`, `value` and `fact_id`. Python recomputes the result. Any judgmental mapping makes this method invalid.
- `ESTIMATE` — required whenever any bridge from facts to the selected value is judgmental, including proxy choice, population mapping, normalization assumptions or an opinion. Its structured `estimate_basis` must enumerate every starting fact ID, every scope mismatch, each ordered proxy/normalization/population step, and the explicit judgmental step reaching the selected value. A cited background fact does not turn an estimate into `OBSERVED` or `CALCULATED`.

Python maps `OBSERVED → DIRECT`, `CALCULATED → DERIVED`, and `ESTIMATE → ESTIMATE`. Never author `provenance_type` in the draft. `evidence_quality` is a separate reliability axis: `HIGH | MED | LOW | NONE`. Empty `fact_ids` requires `NONE`; non-empty `fact_ids` requires non-`NONE` quality.

## Citation discipline — C1 to C4

1. **C1 — Fetch before cite.** Open and inspect the exact page before creating a fact. Search snippets, titles and memory are not evidence.
2. **C2 — Quote what you saw.** Each fact contains one short exact contiguous quote or table figure. Compact (`...`), spaced (`. . .`) and Unicode (`…`) ellipses are forbidden. Never splice separated passages.
3. **C3 — Attribute only what the URL establishes.** Never use a URL as support for a claim it does not establish. If no fetched source supports a background fact, omit the fact and label the selected value honestly as an estimate.
4. **C4 — Reopen and verify.** Before submission, enumerate and reopen every URL and verify its quote, attribution and all structured scope fields against the live page.

`evidence_facts[]` is the only place URLs may appear. Use stable unique IDs `F1`, `F2`, ... and reuse a fact by ID instead of duplicating its URL/quote. Every scope must state `population`, `geography`, `unit`, `period` and `industry_mismatch`. If a source omits any scope element, write exactly `not reported` for that field; never infer it silently.

## Frozen research rules

1. PE/M&A activity informs only B and M, never V or A.
2. Multiples are EV/EBITDA after market-rate owner compensation. State unit, target size, source population and every normalization. A normalization or market mapping that requires judgment is `ESTIMATE`.
3. A zero consolidator count is an absence-of-evidence estimate, not an observation. State the searches performed; do not cite a topical page as proving zero.
4. `current_adoption_pct` means the share of US firms in this NAICS materially using AI in automatable functions. Tool use, worker use and broad-sector use are different populations. Any judgmental mapping to the target population is `ESTIMATE`.
5. `t50_years` means years from today until 50% material firm adoption. If current adoption is already at least 50%, set it to `0`; otherwise it must be positive. Forecasting or analog-based bridging is normally `ESTIMATE`, even when supported by observed adoption facts.
6. Payer/regulatory repricing belongs in C when it determines who retains cost savings. Terminal class separately answers whether paid end demand survives mature AI; do not count the same clawback twice.
7. A statutory human-in-the-loop boundary affects terminal class only to the extent it preserves paid demand. It does not make automatable tasks non-automatable.
8. If a W-2 dataset labor share is distorted by 1099-heavy labor, use the injected value unchanged, flag `dataset_mismatch`, lower confidence as appropriate and show sensitivity. Do not silently adjust it.
9. Catch-all codes: identify at least the top three revenue subsegments, score the dominant subsegment, set `heterogeneous=true`, and complete the structured market-boundary disclosure.
10. `succession_shortage_documented` changes B. It is a full boolean selection under the same method/fact contract. `true` requires `OBSERVED`, at least one fact that directly documents a shortage, and exact live verification. A search-based `false` is normally `ESTIMATE` + `NONE`, with the searches and absence judgment fully disclosed in `estimate_basis`.

## Industry-specific guidance — hints, not facts

**Likely role structure:**
- **Building and home inspectors (owners and staff)** — ~65-75% (hypothesis; record's 4-digit mix unrepresentative) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 47-4011 Construction and Building Inspectors 2.05% of the 4-digit 5413 wage bill — but 5413 is engineering-dominated; 47-4011 is this code's signature occupation and within 541350 proper inspectors are plausibly ~65-75% of wages (hypothesis; 6,523 of 7,623 firms are under-5-employee owner-operator shops)); automatable: partial. The physical walk-through — attic, crawlspace, roof, systems testing — cannot be done by AI and is the product's legal/contractual core. But report writing, photo annotation, defect narration and code citation are a large share of the hours and are near-fully automatable; the within-role fraction should come from the field-time vs. report-time split, plausibly 0.2-0.35.
- **Owner-managers / multi-inspector firm operators** — ~10-15% (hypothesis) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 5.72% at 4-digit; owner comp largely SDE outside wages; hypothesis ~10-15%); automatable: partial. Realtor-relationship marketing and inspector recruiting persist; scheduling and dispatch automate.
- **Scheduling / call center / admin staff** — ~10-15% (hypothesis) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 43-xxxx office/admin occupations in the 5413 tail; hypothesis ~10-15%); automatable: yes. Booking, confirmations, report delivery and payment collection are already platform tasks (ISN-style inspection software).
- **Specialty technical staff (commercial PCA, engineers on staff)** — ~5% (hypothesis) of wage bill (OEWS basis: 17-2051 Civil Engineers 12.8% at 4-digit is parent contamination, but a small real engineering presence exists in commercial property-condition-assessment firms; hypothesis ~5%); automatable: partial. Commercial PCA report production (ASTM E2018) is document-heavy and AI-exposed; field verification is not.

- (consolidators_multiples) Franchise disclosure documents (Pillar To Post, WIN Home Inspection, AmeriSpec, HouseMaster/Neighborly) — Franchise systems are the visible consolidation pattern — unit economics, royalty loads and resale values; Neighborly's HouseMaster acquisition shows strategic interest.
- (consolidators_multiples) BizBuySell Insight Report / BVR DealStats — Small inspection-business comps; expect SDE-based listings.

**Capture questions:**
- Pricing is a flat per-inspection fee (a few hundred dollars) paid by the homebuyer in a one-shot transaction: when AI cuts report-writing time roughly in half, does the operator keep that as margin (fee is set by local going rates, not hours) — or does inspector oversupply and realtor fee-shopping pass it through as lower fees? Note the fee was never hours-priced, which argues for margin retention.
- Who owns demand — the buyer hires once, but the realtor referral relationship drives most flow (subject to state RESPA-adjacent rules on referral steering): is the durable asset the firm's realtor network, and does it survive an acquisition and the founding inspector's exit?
- What stops buyers from relying on AI photo/video analysis plus seller disclosures instead of a paid inspection — is the moat the physical access and testing, the liability-backed written opinion (E&O), state licensing requirements, or lender/insurer requirements, and which of those actually binds in the dominant residential segment?
- Is inspection volume itself the bigger risk — waived inspections in competitive markets and falling existing-home sales — such that AI economics are second-order to transaction-volume exposure?

**Terminal-value question:**
A physical human walk-through cannot be done by AI, and most states license home inspectors — but the paid product is an opinion attached to a real-estate transaction, and demand is set by whether buyers (and their agents and insurers) still commission it. The deciding mechanisms: state licensing plus contract contingency norms hold the human requirement in place, while inspection waivers, iBuyer/institutional purchasing and AI-assisted seller disclosure could shrink the commissioned base. Does mature AI cut the inspector's report-production cost (margin gain on an intact product), or erode the share of transactions that buy a human inspection at all? Answer with the waiver-rate and transaction-volume trend lines, not capability speculation.

**Known traps:**
- Role-mix warning: the provided role_mix is 4-digit 5413 (engineering-dominated; 47-4011 only 2.05% at parent level) and misstates this code — inspectors dominate the real wage bill; rebuild and flag as hypothesis.
- Demand is derivative of existing-home transaction volume — the 2021-2022 boom then 2023-2025 slump moved inspection counts massively for reasons unrelated to AI; normalize any revenue evidence against NAR existing-home sales, and treat inspection-waiver rates as a separate structural headwind.
- EBITDA normalization: overwhelmingly SDE owner-operator businesses (6,523 of 7,623 firms under 5 employees); n_band is just 40 firms — the acquirable pool is multi-inspector regional firms and franchise operators, and single-inspector books have minimal transferable value since the realtor relationships are personal.
- Sub-segment mix: residential home inspection (dominant, licensed in most states, consumer-paid) vs. commercial property condition assessment (ASTM E2018, lender-driven, engineering-adjacent) vs. specialty niches (radon, sewer scope, energy audits — often upsells). Exposure and pricing differ; score the dominant residential segment and note the mix.
- Boundary confusion: municipal/code-enforcement inspectors are government employees (not in this code); engineering firms doing structural inspections are 541330; pest/termite inspection is 561710; home energy raters may sit in 541690 — association data (InterNACHI) spans licensed individuals, not firms, so firm-level economics need care.
- Watch for state licensing variation: a handful of states still have no home-inspector license — the human-requirement moat is contract-and-custom in those markets, not statute.

## Frozen formulas — Python calculates them

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`
- `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)`
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = 10` when `t50_years=0`, otherwise `min(10, 10 / t50_years)`
- `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)`
- `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; add 0.1 for documented succession shortage, cap at 1
- `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
- `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`
