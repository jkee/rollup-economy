**Capture questions:**
- Brokerage revenue is a percentage commission on transaction value, but 70-90%+ of gross commission passes straight through to 1099 agents under commission splits (100%-split and cap models like eXp/RE-MAX leave the brokerage a fee, not a margin) â€” if AI removes labor from the transaction, trace precisely who keeps the saving: the brokerage (whose retained 'company dollar' is thin), the individual agent (whose split is contractual), or the consumer via post-settlement commission-rate compression. Which line item on a brokerage P&L actually gets bigger?
- Who owns the client and the lead flow: the agent's book is personal and fully portable across brokerages, and upstream, Zillow/Realtor.com/Homes.com and the MLSs control search traffic and increasingly sell the leads back to agents â€” when AI improves search, matching and transaction automation, does the value accrue to portals and MLS platforms rather than to any brokerage an acquirer could buy?
- What does an acquirer of a brokerage own after the deal â€” agents can leave with their books the next day, the brand is often franchised (a separate royalty stream), and the office itself is increasingly virtual. Is there any durable asset here beyond a recruiting treadmill?
- What stops sellers and buyers from bypassing the traditional agent entirely â€” flat-fee MLS listing plus AI-drafted disclosures plus a transaction attorney â€” and has the post-2024 commission decoupling measurably increased that bypass or compressed effective commission rates?

**Terminal-value question:**
The brokerage's product is licensed intermediation of a home sale: access, pricing judgment, negotiation, and paperwork shepherding, paid as a percentage commission. Mature AI makes listing data, valuation, marketing, scheduling and document preparation self-serve; the statutory holdout is that state license law requires a licensed agent/broker to collect a commission, and the practical holdout is consumer trust in the largest transaction of their lives. Does paid full-commission intermediation survive â€” or does it compress to a low-fee licensed-execution wrapper while portals and AI capture the discovery and decision value? Answer with the post-settlement commission-rate evidence, and keep separate the two ways this fails: AI cutting brokerage office costs (a small W-2 base) versus AI plus fee compression shrinking the commission pool that is the industry's entire revenue.
