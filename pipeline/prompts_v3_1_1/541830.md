## What you author

Write one JSON object conforming exactly to `pipeline/build/schemas/research_draft_v3_1_1.schema.json`. You author reusable atomic `evidence_facts` and selected research/judgment inputs. You do **not** author final provenance, deterministic dataset values, role weights, role contributions, factor scores, `S`, uplift or a verdict. Plain Python owns those fields.

Every selected input has a `method`:

- `OBSERVED` — `value` repeats a value stated by a cited fact exactly. Include the identical `observed_value`. No interpretation, proxy, normalization or judgmental bridge is permitted.
- `CALCULATED` — `value` is fully reproducible arithmetic over named numeric operands, every operand is backed by a cited fact, and the expression uses only numeric constants, parentheses and `+ - * /`. Include every operand's `name`, `value` and `fact_id`. Python recomputes the result. Any judgmental mapping makes this method invalid.
- `ESTIMATE` — required whenever any bridge from facts to the selected value is judgmental, including proxy choice, population mapping, normalization assumptions or an opinion. Its structured `estimate_basis` must enumerate every starting fact ID, every scope mismatch, each ordered proxy/normalization/population step, and the explicit judgmental step reaching the selected value. A cited background fact does not turn an estimate into `OBSERVED` or `CALCULATED`.

Python maps `OBSERVED → DIRECT`, `CALCULATED → DERIVED`, and `ESTIMATE → ESTIMATE`. Never author `provenance_type` in the draft. `evidence_quality` is a separate reliability axis: `HIGH | MED | LOW | NONE`. Empty `fact_ids` requires `NONE`; non-empty `fact_ids` requires non-`NONE` quality.

## Citation discipline — C1 to C4

1. **C1 — Fetch before cite.** Open and inspect the exact page before creating a fact. Search snippets, titles and memory are not evidence.
2. **C2 — Quote what you saw.** Each fact contains one short exact contiguous quote or table figure. Compact (`...`), spaced (`. . .`) and Unicode (`…`) ellipses are forbidden. Never splice separated passages.
3. **C3 — Attribute only what the URL establishes.** Never use a URL as support for a claim it does not establish. If no fetched source supports a background fact, omit the fact and label the selected value honestly as an estimate.
4. **C4 — Reopen and verify.** Before submission, enumerate and reopen every URL and verify its quote, attribution and all structured scope fields against the live page.

`evidence_facts[]` is the only place URLs may appear. Use stable unique IDs `F1`, `F2`, ... and reuse a fact by ID instead of duplicating its URL/quote. Every scope must state `population`, `geography`, `unit`, `period` and `industry_mismatch`. If a source omits any scope element, write exactly `not reported` for that field; never infer it silently.

## Frozen research rules

1. PE/M&A activity informs only B and M, never V or A.
2. Multiples are EV/EBITDA after market-rate owner compensation. State unit, target size, source population and every normalization. A normalization or market mapping that requires judgment is `ESTIMATE`.
3. A zero consolidator count is an absence-of-evidence estimate, not an observation. State the searches performed; do not cite a topical page as proving zero.
4. `current_adoption_pct` means the share of US firms in this NAICS materially using AI in automatable functions. Tool use, worker use and broad-sector use are different populations. Any judgmental mapping to the target population is `ESTIMATE`.
5. `t50_years` means years from today until 50% material firm adoption. If current adoption is already at least 50%, set it to `0`; otherwise it must be positive. Forecasting or analog-based bridging is normally `ESTIMATE`, even when supported by observed adoption facts.
6. Payer/regulatory repricing belongs in C when it determines who retains cost savings. Terminal class separately answers whether paid end demand survives mature AI; do not count the same clawback twice.
7. A statutory human-in-the-loop boundary affects terminal class only to the extent it preserves paid demand. It does not make automatable tasks non-automatable.
8. If a W-2 dataset labor share is distorted by 1099-heavy labor, use the injected value unchanged, flag `dataset_mismatch`, lower confidence as appropriate and show sensitivity. Do not silently adjust it.
9. Catch-all codes: identify at least the top three revenue subsegments, score the dominant subsegment, set `heterogeneous=true`, and complete the structured market-boundary disclosure.
10. `succession_shortage_documented` changes B. It is a full boolean selection under the same method/fact contract. `true` requires `OBSERVED`, at least one fact that directly documents a shortage, and exact live verification. A search-based `false` is normally `ESTIMATE` + `NONE`, with the searches and absence judgment fully disclosed in `estimate_basis`.

## Industry-specific guidance — hints, not facts

**Likely role structure:**
- **Media planners and buyers** — ~30-40% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 13-1161 Market Research Analysts and Marketing Specialists 6.6% + 41-3011 Advertising Sales Agents 8.2% in the 4-digit 5418 proxy — real media-buying firms are dominated by planner/buyer/investment staff largely spread across these SOCs and the tail; est. ~30-40% of actual wage bill); automatable: partial. Plan building, audience research, RFPs to vendors, flighting, pacing, optimization and reporting are heavily AI-exposed — platforms automate much of this natively; vendor negotiation, principal deal-making and client strategy persist. Plausible within-role fraction 0.4-0.6.
- **Ad operations, trafficking and reconciliation** — ~10-15% of wage bill (OEWS basis: not in listed top-10; ad-ops/billing-reconciliation staff in the tail, est. ~10-15%); automatable: yes. Trafficking, tag management, make-goods, discrepancy reconciliation and media billing are the most directly automatable cost lines in the business.
- **Owners and management** — ~15-17% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 11-1021 General and Operations Managers 9.3% + 11-2021 Marketing Managers 5.2% (proxy)); automatable: partial. Client relationships and media-vendor relationships (rate leverage) persist; internal management automates. Only 663 firms in the code — many are founder-led.
- **Analytics and data staff** — ~6-10% of wage bill (OEWS basis: not in listed top-10; analysts/data engineers in the tail, est. ~6-10%); automatable: partial. Attribution, MMM-lite reporting and dashboarding automate heavily; measurement design judgment persists at the margin.
- **Client service** — ~8-10% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 41-3091 Sales Representatives of Services 3.0% + 43-xx tail (proxy)); automatable: partial. Status reporting and plan presentations automate; relationship ownership persists.
- **Back office and remaining tail (incl. proxy contaminants)** — ~15-20% of wage bill (OEWS basis: 4-digit top-10 covers ~51% of wage bill and includes non-media-buying occupations (27-3031 PR Specialists 8.3%, 27-1024 Graphic Designers 3.3%, 41-9011 Demonstrators 1.6%, 27-1026 Merchandise Displayers 1.2%) — treat as contamination; true remainder is finance/admin); automatable: yes. Finance/billing admin in a business that reconciles high-volume media invoices — strongly automatable.

**Suggested sources:**
- (other) COMvergence media agency billings, pitch and new-business tracking — The standard tracker of media-buying account movements and billings — market structure and client-stickiness evidence.
- (capture_pricing) ANA media transparency studies (2016 K2 report and successor programmatic-transparency work) — Primary documentation of principal-based buying, rebates and undisclosed margin — the capture mechanism (and its client-side backlash) in this industry.
- (adoption) IAB and eMarketer programmatic/automation research — Quantifies how much media buying is already automated/programmatic — the baseline for A.
- (consolidators_multiples) JEGI Clarity media/marketing M&A reports — Deal flow and multiples for media-services transactions.
- (adoption) Madison and Wall (Brian Wieser) advertising market analyses — Independent analyst coverage of agency economics and AI's effect on media-buying revenue models. *(existence unverified — confirm this source still exists/publishes before relying on it)*

**Capture questions:**
- Media buying is priced as a % of media spend (commission), fixed fees benchmarked to spend, or undisclosed principal-trading margin — none of which are hour-based, so the operator keeps AI savings as margin UNTIL clients or auditors reprice: does fee benchmarking (auditors like Ebiquity-type firms) and transparency pressure convert AI efficiency into commission compression — and how fast does the operator's keep erode?
- Who owns the client relationship — the firm's media-vendor rate leverage and buying clout (scale-based, survives acquisition) or individual principals? Does a roll-up actually aggregate buying clout across acquired shops?
- What stops SMB and mid-market clients from self-serving through Google Performance Max / Meta Advantage+ and retail media consoles — the exact client tier independent media shops serve — and what does client-count attrition data show?
- Is principal-based buying margin available to small independents at all, or does it require holdco-scale inventory commitments — i.e., is the highest-capture pricing mechanism structurally out of reach for the band population?

**Terminal-value question:**
The deciding mechanism is platform self-serve automation: Google, Meta, Amazon and retail media networks are building AI that plans, buys and optimizes natively, aimed exactly at the SMB/mid-market clients of the ~600 firms in this code. Commission pricing means the operator keeps AI's cost savings — but only on spend that still flows through them. Is the terminal state a smaller number of firms arbitraging cross-platform complexity and principal deals, or near-total disintermediation of the independent media buyer? Distinguish falling operating cost per dollar managed (margin story) from falling dollars managed (product replacement).

**Known traps:**
- Capture is unusually pricing-model-favorable (commission/% of spend, not hours) — the deflation risk arrives as commission-rate compression via fee auditors and transparency reviews, not as hour rescoping; model pi_dist through that channel and cite the ANA transparency lineage.
- Revenue accounting trap (severe here): gross media billings vs net revenue — the record's labor_share of 0.2759 and revenue/employee of $435k suggest some firms' SUSB receipts include media pass-through; any revenue-based multiple or market-size figure must be normalized to net revenue/commission income.
- Principal-based buying and rebates distort reported margins: some firms' EBITDA includes undisclosed media margin that transparency-era clients claw back — normalize and probe sustainability before applying multiples.
- Small-N caution: n_total is 663 and n_band 121 — the roll-up universe is thin; a few large independents (Horizon-scale) dominate industry statistics and are not comps for band-sized targets.
- Role-mix contamination: the 4-digit 5418 proxy imports PR specialists, designers, demonstrators and display staff that do not exist in media-buying shops — use the adjusted structure and say so.
- Boundary confusion: full-service agencies with media departments are 541810, media reps (selling side) are 541840, programmatic consultancies straddle 541830/541613/541511 — check what any survey or comp actually covers.

## Frozen formulas — Python calculates them

- `V_raw = labor_share × ai_replaceable_share`
- `V = 10 × min(1, V_raw / 0.25)`
- `C = 10 × pi_dist × pi_moat`
- `A = 10` when `t50_years=0`, otherwise `min(10, 10 / t50_years)`
- `TD = clamp(log10(n_band) / 4, 0, 1)`
- `OW = clamp((owners_60plus_pct − 0.10) / 0.35, 0.1, 0.9)`; add 0.1 for documented succession shortage, cap at 1
- `CFD = min(0.9, log10(1 + active_consolidators) / 2)`
- `B = 10 × sqrt(TD × OW) / (1 + 0.3 × CFD)`
- `M = clamp(4 × (exit_mult − buy_mult) / buy_mult, 0, 10)`
- `S = (V × C × A × B × M)^(1/5)`
